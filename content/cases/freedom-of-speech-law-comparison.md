## Summary

Freedom of speech -- the right to express opinions, ideas, and information without governmental restraint -- is widely regarded as a cornerstone of democratic governance, yet its scope, limitations, and enforcement mechanisms vary dramatically across legal systems. South Korea protects freedom of speech under Article 21 of the Constitution (대한민국 헌법 제21조), which guarantees freedom of speech and the press while simultaneously prohibiting speech that infringes upon the honor or rights of others or undermines public morals; the Constitutional Court of Korea (헌법재판소) has played a pivotal role in defining boundaries, notably striking down the unconstitutionality of certain internet censorship provisions while upholding the National Security Act (국가보안법) restrictions on pro-North Korean expression. The United States provides what is often considered the broadest protection of free speech globally through the First Amendment to the Constitution, as interpreted by the Supreme Court in landmark decisions from Schenck v. United States (1919) through Brandenburg v. Ohio (1969) and New York Times Co. v. Sullivan (1964), establishing that government may not restrict speech based on content unless it falls within narrow, historically recognized categories such as incitement to imminent lawless action, true threats, obscenity, and defamation. Germany takes a more restrictive but rights-balancing approach through Article 5 of the Basic Law (Grundgesetz), which guarantees freedom of expression, information, the press, and artistic and academic freedom, but subjects these rights to explicit limitations including general laws, provisions for the protection of youth, and the right to personal honor; Germany's unique historical context has led to specific prohibitions on Holocaust denial under Section 130(3) StGB and the display of symbols of unconstitutional organizations under Section 86a StGB. At the European supranational level, Article 10 of the European Convention on Human Rights guarantees freedom of expression subject to limitations prescribed by law and necessary in a democratic society, with the European Court of Human Rights having developed an extensive body of jurisprudence applying the proportionality principle and margin of appreciation doctrine to balance free speech against competing interests including national security, public safety, and the protection of the reputation or rights of others.

## South Korea

### Legal Framework

Freedom of speech in South Korea is guaranteed by Article 21 of the Constitution of the Republic of Korea (대한민국 헌법 제21조), which provides: "(1) All citizens shall enjoy the freedom of speech and the press, and the freedom of assembly and association. (2) Licensing or censorship of speech and the press, and licensing of assembly and association shall not be recognized." However, Article 21(4) qualifies this by stating that "neither speech nor the press shall violate the honor or rights of other persons nor undermine public morals or social ethics."

The Constitutional Court (헌법재판소) has been the primary arbiter of free speech boundaries. In its landmark 2002 decision on the film rating system (2001헌가27), the Court drew a critical distinction between censorship (검열) -- which is absolutely prohibited -- and content rating or classification systems, which may be constitutionally permissible.

### National Security Act (국가보안법)

The most controversial restriction on free speech in South Korea is the National Security Act (NSA), originally enacted in 1948 and subsequently amended multiple times. Article 7 of the NSA criminalizes praising, encouraging, or sympathizing with anti-state organizations (primarily North Korea), with penalties of up to seven years' imprisonment. The Constitutional Court has upheld the constitutionality of Article 7 on multiple occasions, most recently in 2015, while narrowing its application to expression that poses an "active and aggressive" danger to the existence and security of the state or the free democratic basic order.

### Criminal Defamation

South Korea maintains a robust criminal defamation regime. Article 307 of the Criminal Act (형법 제307조) criminalizes defamation through public allegation of facts, imposing up to two years' imprisonment or a fine of up to five million KRW. Notably, Article 307(1) criminalizes defamation even when the alleged facts are true, making South Korea one of the few democracies where truth is not an absolute defense to defamation. Article 309 imposes enhanced penalties for defamation through publications, with up to seven years' imprisonment.

The Act on Promotion of Information and Communications Network Utilization and Information Protection (정보통신망 이용촉진 및 정보보호 등에 관한 법률), commonly known as the Information and Communications Network Act, previously contained Article 70, which imposed enhanced criminal penalties for online defamation. The Constitutional Court upheld its constitutionality in 2016 (2013헌바105) but emphasized the need for strict interpretation.

### Internet Regulation

The Korea Communications Standards Commission (방송통신심의위원회, KCSC) exercises administrative authority to order deletion or blocking of online content deemed illegal or harmful. The Deliberation Regulation on Communications (통신심의에 관한 규정) grants the KCSC broad discretion, leading to criticism from organizations including the UN Special Rapporteur on Freedom of Opinion and Expression.

## United States

### Legal Framework

The First Amendment to the United States Constitution, ratified in 1791, provides: "Congress shall make no law respecting an establishment of religion, or prohibiting the free exercise thereof; or abridging the freedom of speech, or of the press; or the right of the people peaceably to assemble, and to petition the Government for a redress of grievances." The Fourteenth Amendment, through the incorporation doctrine established in Gitlow v. New York (1925), extends this prohibition to state and local governments.

The U.S. Supreme Court has developed a content-based versus content-neutral framework for analyzing speech restrictions. Content-based restrictions are subject to strict scrutiny, requiring the government to demonstrate that the restriction is narrowly tailored to serve a compelling governmental interest. Content-neutral time, place, and manner restrictions are evaluated under intermediate scrutiny.

### Categories of Unprotected or Less-Protected Speech

| Category | Leading Case | Standard |
|----------|-------------|----------|
| Incitement | Brandenburg v. Ohio, 395 U.S. 444 (1969) | Speech directed to inciting imminent lawless action and likely to produce such action |
| True threats | Virginia v. Black, 538 U.S. 343 (2003); Counterman v. Colorado, 600 U.S. 66 (2023) | Statements through which the speaker means to communicate a serious expression of intent to commit violence; recklessness standard |
| Obscenity | Miller v. California, 413 U.S. 15 (1973) | Three-part test: appeals to prurient interest, patently offensive, lacks serious literary, artistic, political, or scientific value |
| Defamation | New York Times Co. v. Sullivan, 376 U.S. 254 (1964) | Public officials must prove actual malice (knowledge of falsity or reckless disregard for truth) |
| Fighting words | Chaplinsky v. New Hampshire, 315 U.S. 568 (1942) | Words that by their very utterance tend to incite an immediate breach of the peace |
| Child pornography | New York v. Ferber, 458 U.S. 747 (1982) | Strict prohibition; not subject to Miller obscenity test |

### Modern Challenges

The Supreme Court has addressed the application of First Amendment principles to new technologies and platforms. In Packingham v. North Carolina, 582 U.S. 98 (2017), the Court struck down a law prohibiting registered sex offenders from accessing social media, recognizing social media as a modern public forum. In Moody v. NetChoice (2024) and NetChoice, LLC v. Paxton (2024), the Court addressed state laws regulating content moderation by social media platforms, examining the tension between platform editorial discretion and state interests in preventing censorship of user speech.

## Germany

### Legal Framework

Article 5 of the Basic Law (Grundgesetz, GG) provides: "(1) Every person shall have the right freely to express and disseminate his opinions in speech, writing, and pictures and to inform himself without hindrance from generally accessible sources. Freedom of the press and freedom of reporting by means of broadcasts and films shall be guaranteed. There shall be no censorship. (2) These rights shall find their limits in the provisions of general laws, in provisions for the protection of young persons, and in the right to personal honour."

The Federal Constitutional Court (Bundesverfassungsgericht, BVerfG) has interpreted Article 5 through the lens of the Lüth decision (BVerfGE 7, 198 (1958)), which established that fundamental rights create an objective system of values (objektive Wertordnung) that radiates into all areas of law. The Lüth framework requires courts adjudicating private disputes to consider the effect of their decisions on fundamental rights, including freedom of expression.

### Specific Restrictions

**Volksverhetzung (Section 130 StGB -- Incitement to Hatred):** This provision criminalizes inciting hatred against segments of the population, calling for violence or arbitrary measures against them, or attacking their human dignity. Penalties range from three months to five years' imprisonment.

**Holocaust Denial (Section 130(3) StGB):** Publicly approving of, denying, or downplaying the genocide committed under National Socialist rule is punishable by up to five years' imprisonment. The Federal Constitutional Court upheld this provision in its 2018 decision (1 BvR 673/17), reasoning that Holocaust denial does not constitute a protected opinion under Article 5 GG because it constitutes a factually false assertion about a historically established event.

**Insult (Beleidigung, Section 185 StGB):** German law criminalizes insult, defined as an attack on another person's honor. This provision is frequently invoked against offensive speech and has no direct equivalent in U.S. law. Penalties include a fine or imprisonment up to two years.

### Network Enforcement Act (NetzDG)

The Netzwerkdurchsetzungsgesetz (NetzDG), enacted in October 2017, requires social media platforms with more than two million registered users in Germany to remove "manifestly unlawful" content within 24 hours of receiving a complaint and other unlawful content within seven days. Non-compliance can result in fines of up to 50 million euros. The NetzDG has been partially superseded by the EU Digital Services Act (Regulation 2022/2065), which entered into force in February 2024.

## ECHR & EU

### Article 10 ECHR

Article 10 of the European Convention on Human Rights provides: "(1) Everyone has the right to freedom of expression. This right shall include freedom to hold opinions and to receive and impart information and ideas without interference by public authority and regardless of frontiers... (2) The exercise of these freedoms, since it carries with it duties and responsibilities, may be subject to such formalities, conditions, restrictions or penalties as are prescribed by law and are necessary in a democratic society, in the interests of national security, territorial integrity or public safety, for the prevention of disorder or crime, for the protection of health or morals, for the protection of the reputation or rights of others, for preventing the disclosure of information received in confidence, or for maintaining the authority and impartiality of the judiciary."

### Key ECHR Jurisprudence

**Handyside v. United Kingdom (Application No. 5493/72, 1976):** The Court's foundational statement on freedom of expression, holding that Article 10 protects not only information and ideas that are favorably received or regarded as inoffensive, but also those that "offend, shock or disturb the State or any sector of the population." The Court simultaneously established the margin of appreciation doctrine.

**Lingens v. Austria (Application No. 9815/82, 1986):** The Court held that criminal defamation convictions for criticism of politicians violated Article 10, distinguishing between statements of fact and value judgments and holding that the limits of acceptable criticism are wider for public figures than for private individuals.

**Delfi AS v. Estonia (Application No. 64569/09, Grand Chamber, 2015):** The Grand Chamber upheld Estonia's finding that an internet news portal was liable for defamatory user comments, establishing that intermediary liability for third-party content does not necessarily violate Article 10.

**Perinçek v. Switzerland (Application No. 27510/08, Grand Chamber, 2015):** The Court held that Switzerland's criminal conviction of a Turkish politician for denying the Armenian genocide violated Article 10, distinguishing denial of the Armenian genocide from Holocaust denial based on the specific historical and legal context.

### EU Charter and Digital Services Act

Article 11 of the EU Charter of Fundamental Rights mirrors Article 10 ECHR. The Digital Services Act (DSA, Regulation 2022/2065), which became fully applicable in February 2024, establishes a comprehensive framework for content moderation by online platforms across the EU, requiring transparency in content moderation decisions, providing for user redress mechanisms, and imposing enhanced obligations on very large online platforms and search engines.

## Comparative Analysis

### Structural Approaches to Limitations

The four jurisdictions represent fundamentally different philosophical approaches to limiting speech:

**United States -- Categorical Approach:** The First Amendment creates a presumption of protection, with narrow, historically defined categories of unprotected speech. Outside these categories, content-based restrictions face strict scrutiny and are nearly always unconstitutional. This approach maximizes individual expressive liberty at the potential cost of inadequate protection against harmful speech.

**Germany -- Balancing Approach with Historical Constraints:** Article 5 GG explicitly recognizes limitations through general laws, youth protection, and personal honor. Germany's history of Nazi-era propaganda has produced specific prohibitions on hate speech and extremist symbols that would be unconstitutional in the United States. The Federal Constitutional Court balances competing constitutional values through proportionality analysis.

**South Korea -- Hybrid Approach:** The Constitution simultaneously guarantees free speech and explicitly restricts it to protect honor and public morals. The National Security Act adds a unique geopolitical dimension absent in other democracies. Criminal defamation provisions, particularly the truth-is-not-a-defense regime, represent one of the most restrictive approaches among democratic nations.

**ECHR -- Proportionality Framework:** The Convention permits restrictions that are prescribed by law, pursue a legitimate aim, and are necessary in a democratic society. The margin of appreciation doctrine gives states varying degrees of latitude depending on the type of expression and the nature of the restriction, creating a flexible but less predictable framework.

### Hate Speech Regulation Spectrum

The jurisdictions fall along a clear spectrum. The United States provides the broadest protection for hate speech, requiring incitement to imminent lawless action before speech can be prohibited. Germany imposes the most detailed restrictions, criminalizing hate speech, Holocaust denial, and extremist symbols. South Korea falls between these poles, with criminal defamation and insult laws serving a function analogous to hate speech regulation. The ECHR framework permits member states to restrict hate speech under Article 10(2) and to invoke the abuse clause of Article 17 against speech that seeks to destroy Convention rights.

### Digital Speech Governance

All four jurisdictions are actively grappling with the regulation of online speech. Germany was an early mover with the NetzDG in 2017. The EU Digital Services Act now provides a comprehensive framework that partially supersedes national laws. South Korea's KCSC exercises broad content-removal authority. The United States remains an outlier, with Section 230 of the Communications Decency Act providing broad intermediary immunity, though legislative and judicial challenges to this framework continue to mount.

### Emerging Issues: AI-Generated Content and Deepfakes

The proliferation of AI-generated content and deepfakes presents novel challenges for all four jurisdictions. South Korea enacted the Act on the Punishment of Deepfake-Related Sexual Offenses in 2024. The EU's AI Act (Regulation 2024/1689) requires transparency labeling for AI-generated content. The United States has no comprehensive federal deepfake legislation, though several states have enacted laws. Germany addresses deepfakes through existing criminal provisions (insult, defamation, data protection) while the EU AI Act provides supplementary obligations.

### Academic Freedom

The relationship between freedom of speech and academic freedom represents an important subsidiary issue. South Korea's Constitution (Article 22) guarantees academic freedom, and the Higher Education Act protects university autonomy. The United States protects academic freedom through the First Amendment, with the Supreme Court recognizing academic freedom as "a special concern of the First Amendment" in Keyishian v. Board of Regents (1967). Germany's Article 5(3) GG provides a separate guarantee that "art and scholarship, research, and teaching shall be free." The ECHR protects academic freedom under Article 10, with the Court's Kula v. Turkey (2018) decision addressing the dismissal of an academic for political speech.

### Corporate Speech and Commercial Expression

Commercial speech receives varying levels of protection. The United States extends significant First Amendment protection to commercial speech under the Central Hudson test (Central Hudson Gas & Electric v. Public Service Commission, 1980), and the Citizens United v. FEC (2010) decision extended full political speech protections to corporate expenditures. Germany distinguishes between political and commercial expression, with commercial speech receiving less protection under Article 5 GG. South Korea regulates advertising through the Act on Fair Labeling and Advertising and permits greater regulation of commercial speech. The EU framework addresses commercial expression primarily through consumer protection and advertising regulation directives.

### Social Media and Platform Governance

The governance of social media platforms raises distinct issues in each jurisdiction. South Korea's real-name verification system for online comments, upheld by courts but subsequently relaxed, represented a unique approach to online accountability. The United States' Section 230 debate pits platform immunity against calls for greater content moderation. Germany's NetzDG pioneered mandatory content removal by platforms. The EU's Digital Services Act creates a comprehensive framework for platform governance, including transparency requirements, user complaint mechanisms, and risk assessments for very large platforms.
