## Summary

Supply chain due diligence regulation -- the legal frameworks requiring companies to identify, prevent, mitigate, and account for actual and potential adverse human rights and environmental impacts in their global value chains -- represents one of the most significant developments in international business regulation, transforming voluntary corporate social responsibility commitments into binding legal obligations with enforcement consequences. South Korea does not yet have a comprehensive supply chain due diligence law but addresses certain aspects through the Serious Accidents Punishment Act (중대재해 처벌 등에 관한 법률, effective January 27, 2022, for businesses with 50+ employees, and January 27, 2024, for businesses with 5-49 employees), which imposes criminal liability on business owners and responsible officers for serious industrial accidents including those involving subcontractors and supply chain workers, supplemented by the Framework Act on International Development Cooperation (국제개발협력기본법) and various sector-specific regulations on product safety and labor standards; the National Human Rights Commission of Korea (국가인권위원회) has recommended comprehensive human rights due diligence legislation, and a draft bill was introduced in the National Assembly in 2023 but has not yet been enacted. The United States pursues supply chain due diligence through a patchwork of targeted laws including the Uyghur Forced Labor Prevention Act (UFLPA, Pub. L. 117-78, effective June 21, 2022), Section 307 of the Tariff Act of 1930 (19 U.S.C. Section 1307) banning imports of goods produced with forced labor, the California Transparency in Supply Chains Act (2010), and enforcement of the Trafficking Victims Protection Reauthorization Act (18 U.S.C. Sections 1589-1592); no comprehensive federal supply chain due diligence legislation exists, though the proposed Supply Chain Due Diligence Act has been introduced in multiple Congressional sessions. Germany enacted the Lieferkettensorgfaltspflichtengesetz (LkSG, Supply Chain Due Diligence Act) on July 16, 2021, effective January 1, 2023, for companies with 3,000+ employees and January 1, 2024, for companies with 1,000+ employees, requiring covered companies to establish risk management systems, conduct regular risk analyses, implement preventive and remedial measures, establish complaint mechanisms, and report annually on due diligence compliance, with enforcement by the Federal Office for Economic Affairs and Export Control (BAFA, Bundesamt für Wirtschaft und Ausfuhrkontrolle). The EU adopted the Corporate Sustainability Due Diligence Directive (CSDDD, Directive 2024/1760) on July 5, 2024, establishing the most comprehensive mandatory human rights and environmental due diligence framework globally, applying to EU companies with 1,000+ employees and EUR 450 million+ net worldwide turnover and non-EU companies with EUR 450 million+ net turnover generated in the EU, requiring identification and mitigation of adverse impacts throughout the value chain, climate transition plan adoption, and -- critically -- establishing civil liability for failure to conduct adequate due diligence, with member states required to transpose the Directive by July 26, 2026.

## South Korea

### Legal Framework

South Korea does not yet have dedicated supply chain due diligence legislation comparable to Germany's LkSG or the EU's CSDDD. However, several existing laws address aspects of supply chain responsibility, and legislative proposals for a comprehensive framework are under active consideration.

### Serious Accidents Punishment Act (중대재해 처벌 등에 관한 법률)

Enacted on January 26, 2021, the Serious Accidents Punishment Act (SAPA) represents Korea's most significant supply chain-adjacent legislation by imposing criminal liability on business owners and responsible officers for failures to establish safety management systems that lead to serious industrial or public accidents:

| Provision | Details |
|-----------|---------|
| **Scope** | Businesses with 50+ employees (effective Jan 27, 2022); 5-49 employees (effective Jan 27, 2024) |
| **Criminal Penalties** | Business owner/responsible officer: 1+ year imprisonment or fine up to KRW 1 billion for death; up to 7 years imprisonment or KRW 100 million fine for serious injury |
| **Corporate Penalties** | Fine up to KRW 5 billion for death; up to KRW 1 billion for serious injury |
| **Supply Chain Element** | Applies when the business owner or responsible officer exercises substantial control over third-party workers (subcontractors, dispatched workers) at the workplace |
| **Duty of Care** | Obligation to establish and implement safety and health management systems, allocate dedicated safety budgets, and appoint safety officers |

The SAPA's application to subcontractor and supply chain workers was confirmed in a landmark Seoul Central District Court decision (2023) holding that a principal contractor's CEO was criminally liable for the death of a subcontractor's employee at a construction site where the principal exercised control over working conditions.

### National Human Rights Commission Recommendations

The National Human Rights Commission of Korea (NHRCK, 국가인권위원회) has been a driving force behind proposed due diligence legislation:
- In 2018, the NHRCK published the National Action Plan on Business and Human Rights (기업과 인권 국가행동계획), endorsing the UN Guiding Principles on Business and Human Rights (UNGPs)
- In 2022, the NHRCK formally recommended that the National Assembly enact comprehensive human rights due diligence legislation
- The Ministry of Justice established a Business and Human Rights Policy Division in 2023 to develop legislative proposals

### Proposed Legislation

In 2023, the Human Rights Due Diligence Act (인권실사법) was introduced in the National Assembly by multiple sponsors. The proposed bill would:
- Require companies of a specified size to conduct human rights and environmental due diligence across their supply chains
- Mandate establishment of grievance mechanisms
- Require annual due diligence reporting
- Establish administrative enforcement by a designated regulatory body
- Create civil liability for damages caused by due diligence failures

The bill remains under committee review and faces opposition from business groups (particularly the Federation of Korean Industries, 한국경영자총협회) who argue it would impose excessive compliance burdens on Korean companies competing globally.

### Sector-Specific Regulations

Korea has sector-specific supply chain requirements including the Occupational Safety and Health Act (산업안전보건법) imposing safety obligations on principal contractors for subcontractor workers, the Product Safety Act (제품안전기본법) requiring supply chain traceability for consumer products, and the Fair Transactions in Subcontracting Act (하도급거래 공정화에 관한 법률) protecting subcontractors from unfair practices by principal contractors.

## United States

### Legal Framework

The United States has adopted a targeted, issue-specific approach to supply chain due diligence rather than a comprehensive framework. The most significant laws address forced labor in supply chains, with no federal legislation requiring general human rights or environmental due diligence.

### Uyghur Forced Labor Prevention Act (UFLPA)

The UFLPA, signed into law on December 23, 2021, and effective June 21, 2022, establishes a rebuttable presumption that goods mined, produced, or manufactured wholly or in part in the Xinjiang Uyghur Autonomous Region (XUAR) of China are produced with forced labor and are therefore prohibited from entry into the United States under Section 307 of the Tariff Act:

**Rebuttable Presumption:** All goods from Xinjiang are presumed to be produced with forced labor unless the importer can demonstrate by "clear and convincing evidence" that forced labor was not used at any stage of production.

**UFLPA Entity List:** The Forced Labor Enforcement Task Force (FLETF), chaired by the Department of Homeland Security, maintains an Entity List of companies operating in Xinjiang or sourcing from Xinjiang that use or facilitate forced labor. As of 2024, the Entity List includes 60+ entities spanning cotton, polysilicon, tomatoes, and other sectors.

**Enforcement Statistics (through 2024):**

| Metric | Number |
|--------|--------|
| Shipments reviewed under UFLPA | 7,500+ |
| Shipments detained or denied entry | 4,100+ |
| Value of detained/denied goods | $2.8+ billion |
| Top affected sectors | Apparel, electronics (polysilicon/solar panels), cotton products, tomato products |

### Section 307 of the Tariff Act (19 U.S.C. Section 1307)

Section 307 provides the broader legal foundation, prohibiting the importation of goods "mined, produced, or manufactured wholly or in part" by forced or indentured labor. U.S. Customs and Border Protection (CBP) enforces this through Withhold Release Orders (WROs) and Findings:

- **WROs:** CBP issued 56 WROs between 2016 and 2024, targeting goods from China, Malaysia, Brazil, and other countries across sectors including seafood, palm oil, rubber gloves, tobacco, gold, and diamonds
- **Notable WROs:** Hoshine Silicon Industry (polysilicon, 2021); Natchi Apparel (Indian garments, 2022); various Malaysian palm oil producers

### California Transparency in Supply Chains Act (2010)

California's TISCA (Cal. Civ. Code Section 1714.43) requires retail sellers and manufacturers doing business in California with annual worldwide gross receipts exceeding $100 million to disclose their efforts to eradicate slavery and human trafficking from their direct supply chains, including verification, auditing, certification, internal accountability, and training. The law requires disclosure but does not mandate specific due diligence actions.

### Other Federal Provisions

- **Trafficking Victims Protection Reauthorization Act (TVPRA):** 18 U.S.C. Sections 1589-1592 provide criminal penalties for forced labor and trafficking, including extraterritorial application
- **Trade Facilitation and Trade Enforcement Act (2015):** Repealed the "consumptive demand" loophole in Section 307 that had previously exempted forced-labor goods where domestic production was insufficient to meet demand
- **Dodd-Frank Section 1502:** Requires SEC registrants to disclose use of conflict minerals (tin, tantalum, tungsten, gold) from the DRC and adjoining countries (discussed in separate analysis)

## Germany

### Legal Framework

Germany enacted the Lieferkettensorgfaltspflichtengesetz (LkSG, Act on Corporate Due Diligence Obligations in Supply Chains) on July 16, 2021, making it the first major EU member state to adopt comprehensive supply chain due diligence legislation. The LkSG reflects Germany's implementation of the UN Guiding Principles on Business and Human Rights and the OECD Guidelines for Multinational Enterprises.

### Scope and Application

| Phase | Effective Date | Companies Covered |
|-------|---------------|-------------------|
| Phase 1 | January 1, 2023 | Companies with 3,000+ employees and headquarters, principal place of business, or administrative headquarters in Germany (~900 companies) |
| Phase 2 | January 1, 2024 | Companies with 1,000+ employees (~4,800 companies total) |

The LkSG applies to companies regardless of legal form, including subsidiaries of foreign parent companies if the subsidiary itself meets the employee threshold. Employees of affiliated companies (Konzernzurechnung) are counted toward the threshold.

### Due Diligence Obligations

The LkSG establishes a graduated system of due diligence obligations:

**Risk Management System (Section 4):** Companies must establish an adequate and effective risk management system, including appointment of a human rights officer or department, integration of due diligence into internal processes, and regular effectiveness monitoring.

**Risk Analysis (Section 5):** Companies must conduct annual and event-driven risk analyses covering:
- Their own business operations
- Direct suppliers' operations
- Indirect suppliers' operations (upon substantiated knowledge of potential violations)

**Preventive Measures (Section 6):**
- Policy statement on human rights strategy
- Procurement policies incorporating human rights and environmental expectations
- Training programs for relevant employees
- Risk-based control mechanisms (audits, certifications)
- Contractual assurances from direct suppliers

**Remedial Measures (Section 7):** Upon identification of a violation or imminent violation, companies must take remedial action immediately, develop a remediation concept, and, if the violation cannot be ended in the foreseeable future, consider suspension or termination of the business relationship as a last resort.

**Complaint Mechanism (Section 8):** Companies must establish an accessible complaint procedure enabling persons to report human rights and environmental risks or violations along the supply chain. The mechanism must be publicly accessible, confidential, and protect whistleblowers from retaliation.

**Documentation and Reporting (Section 10):** Annual report on due diligence fulfillment, published on the company's website, retained for seven years.

### Protected Rights and Environmental Interests

The LkSG references 11 international conventions and instruments defining the protected human rights:

| Category | Protected Interests |
|----------|-------------------|
| **Labor Rights** | Prohibition of child labor (ILO Conventions 138, 182); forced labor (ILO Conventions 29, 105); discrimination (ILO Conventions 100, 111); freedom of association (ILO Conventions 87, 98); adequate wages; occupational safety |
| **Human Rights** | Prohibition of torture; right to life and health; prohibition of unlawful land seizure; prohibition of hiring private security forces that violate human rights |
| **Environmental** | Mercury (Minamata Convention); persistent organic pollutants (Stockholm Convention); hazardous waste (Basel Convention) |

### Enforcement by BAFA

The Bundesamt für Wirtschaft und Ausfuhrkontrolle (BAFA) enforces the LkSG with significant powers:
- Request information and documents
- Conduct on-site inspections
- Issue orders to comply with specific obligations
- Impose fines up to EUR 8 million or up to 2% of average annual worldwide turnover for companies with annual turnover exceeding EUR 400 million
- Exclude non-compliant companies from public procurement for up to three years

As of 2024, BAFA had received over 400 complaints, conducted initial reviews of all covered companies' annual reports, and initiated formal proceedings in approximately 30 cases.

### Exclusion of Civil Liability

Section 3(3) of the LkSG explicitly provides that a breach of the due diligence obligations under the Act does not give rise to civil liability. This exclusion was a politically driven compromise during the legislative process, and it distinguishes the LkSG from the EU CSDDD, which establishes civil liability.

## ECHR & EU

### Corporate Sustainability Due Diligence Directive (CSDDD)

The CSDDD (Directive 2024/1760), adopted on July 5, 2024, after contentious negotiations that nearly collapsed in February 2024 when several member states (including Germany, which abstained) threatened to block the proposal, establishes mandatory human rights and environmental due diligence across the EU.

### Scope

| Category | Criteria |
|----------|----------|
| **EU Companies** | 1,000+ employees AND EUR 450M+ net worldwide turnover (phased: applicable from 2027 for companies with 5,000+ employees/EUR 1.5B turnover; 2028 for 3,000+/EUR 900M; 2029 for 1,000+/EUR 450M) |
| **Non-EU Companies** | EUR 450M+ net turnover generated in the EU (same phased thresholds) |
| **Financial Sector** | Initially excluded from downstream due diligence obligations; subject to review |
| **Estimated Coverage** | ~5,500 EU companies; ~900 non-EU companies |

### Due Diligence Obligations

The CSDDD requires covered companies to:

1. **Integrate due diligence into policies** (Article 7): Adopt a due diligence policy describing the company's approach, code of conduct, and due diligence process
2. **Identify and assess adverse impacts** (Article 8): Map the chain of activities, identify actual and potential adverse human rights and environmental impacts
3. **Prevent and mitigate potential impacts** (Article 10): Develop and implement prevention action plans, seek contractual assurances, make necessary investments, provide support to SME business partners
4. **Bring actual adverse impacts to an end** (Article 11): Take appropriate measures to bring actual impacts to an end, including remediation
5. **Establish complaint mechanisms** (Article 14): Provide accessible grievance mechanisms for affected persons, trade unions, and civil society organizations
6. **Monitor effectiveness** (Article 15): Regular assessment of due diligence measures
7. **Communicate publicly** (Article 16): Annual statement on due diligence

### Climate Transition Plans

Article 22 of the CSDDD requires covered companies to adopt and implement a climate transition plan ensuring, through best efforts, compatibility of the business model and strategy with the transition to a sustainable economy and the limiting of global warming to 1.5 degrees Celsius in line with the Paris Agreement. The transition plan must include time-bound targets for 2030 and in five-year steps up to 2050, a description of decarbonization levers, and investment plans.

### Civil Liability (Article 29)

The CSDDD establishes civil liability for companies that fail to comply with due diligence obligations and thereby cause damage to natural or legal persons. Key provisions:
- Member states must ensure effective access to justice, including reasonable limitation periods (minimum 5 years), reasonable cost of proceedings, and access to evidence
- Trade unions and civil society organizations may bring actions on behalf of affected persons
- The burden of proof follows national law, but courts must be able to order disclosure of evidence
- Companies cannot avoid liability by delegating due diligence to contractual partners

### Relationship with German LkSG

The CSDDD will require Germany to amend the LkSG to comply with the Directive's broader requirements, most significantly:
- Extending the scope to the entire value chain (not just direct and substantiated-knowledge indirect suppliers)
- Introducing civil liability (which the LkSG currently excludes)
- Adding climate transition plan requirements
- Broadening the range of protected human rights and environmental interests

### French Loi de Vigilance (Law No. 2017-399)

France's Duty of Vigilance Law, enacted on March 27, 2017, was the first mandatory supply chain due diligence law in the world, applying to French companies with 5,000+ employees in France or 10,000+ employees worldwide. The law requires covered companies to establish, implement, and publish a vigilance plan (plan de vigilance) addressing human rights, health, safety, and environmental risks. Notably, the French law includes civil liability, and several landmark cases have been brought:
- TotalEnergies (Tribunal judiciaire de Paris, 2023): Environmental and human rights impacts of fossil fuel projects
- Suez (2022): Water access and human rights in Chile
- Casino (2021): Deforestation in supply chains

### UN Guiding Principles on Business and Human Rights

The CSDDD and national due diligence laws are rooted in the UN Guiding Principles on Business and Human Rights (UNGPs), endorsed by the Human Rights Council in 2011, which establish a three-pillar framework: (1) the state duty to protect human rights; (2) the corporate responsibility to respect human rights; and (3) access to remedy. The UNGPs introduced the concept of human rights due diligence as a process to identify, prevent, mitigate, and account for adverse human rights impacts.

## Comparative Analysis

### Scope and Ambition

The EU's CSDDD represents the most ambitious supply chain due diligence framework, covering the entire "chain of activities" (upstream and downstream) and including both human rights and environmental impacts plus climate transition plans. Germany's LkSG has narrower scope, focusing primarily on direct suppliers with a "substantiated knowledge" trigger for indirect suppliers. The United States has no comprehensive framework, relying on targeted forced labor laws. South Korea is in the earliest stage, with workplace safety criminal liability but no dedicated supply chain due diligence law.

### Civil Liability

The most consequential distinction is civil liability. The CSDDD and France's Loi de Vigilance establish civil liability for due diligence failures, creating a private enforcement mechanism and potential litigation exposure for covered companies. Germany's LkSG explicitly excludes civil liability -- a deliberate political compromise that weakens the law's deterrent effect. The United States does not have a due diligence-specific civil liability regime, though victims may pursue claims under tort law and the Alien Tort Statute (28 U.S.C. Section 1350), the scope of which was significantly narrowed by the Supreme Court in Kiobel v. Royal Dutch Petroleum (2013) and Nestlé USA v. Doe (2021).

### Enforcement Architecture

Germany's BAFA-based administrative enforcement model provides centralized, systematic oversight but has been criticized for limited resources. The EU's CSDDD relies on member state supervisory authorities, creating potential divergence in enforcement intensity. The United States uses customs enforcement (CBP) and criminal prosecution (DOJ) for forced labor, resulting in punitive but narrowly targeted enforcement. South Korea's prosecutorial approach under the Serious Accidents Act represents the most severe individual consequences (criminal imprisonment) but the narrowest scope.

### SME Impact

All frameworks raise concerns about the cascading effect on small and medium enterprises. Large covered companies will inevitably pass due diligence requirements to their smaller suppliers through contractual terms, creating de facto obligations for SMEs without the compliance resources of large corporations. The CSDDD partially addresses this by requiring covered companies to provide support to SME business partners and prohibiting unfair cost-shifting, but implementation remains challenging.

### Global Supply Chain Restructuring

The combination of supply chain due diligence laws, forced labor import bans, and conflict mineral regulations is driving significant restructuring of global supply chains. Companies are nearshoring production, diversifying away from high-risk regions, investing in supply chain mapping and traceability technology, and developing alternative sourcing strategies. This restructuring has particular implications for developing countries that may lose orders from risk-averse buyers.
