## Summary

Private military contractors (PMCs) -- commercial entities providing armed security, military training, logistical support, or combat-related services to governments, international organizations, or private clients -- occupy one of the most contested legal spaces in contemporary military and international humanitarian law, with regulatory frameworks ranging from comprehensive prohibition to de facto tolerance. South Korea does not have dedicated PMC legislation but regulates private security activities through the Security Services Industry Act (경비업법) and restricts military-related private activities through the Military Service Act (병역법) and the Criminal Act provisions on unauthorized armed organizations; Korean nationals participating in foreign armed conflicts may face prosecution under the Passport Act (여권법) and the Act on the Protection of Military Bases and Installations. The United States is the world's largest employer of private military contractors, with the legal framework centered on the Military Extraterritorial Jurisdiction Act (MEJA, 18 U.S.C. Section 3261), the Special Maritime and Territorial Jurisdiction Act (18 U.S.C. Section 7), the Defense Federal Acquisition Regulation Supplement (DFARS), and the Uniform Code of Military Justice as amended to cover certain contractor personnel, with the Nisour Square massacre by Blackwater contractors in Baghdad in 2007 serving as the watershed case that exposed accountability gaps. Germany addresses PMCs through the Grundgesetz prohibition on aggressive war preparation (Article 26), the War Weapons Control Act (Kriegswaffenkontrollgesetz), and the Gewerbeordnung (Trade Regulation Act) provisions on private security, while participating in EU-level discussions on PMC regulation. At the international level, the Montreux Document on Private Military and Security Companies (2008) establishes a non-binding framework of good practices, the International Code of Conduct for Private Security Service Providers (ICoC, 2010) creates an industry self-regulation mechanism, and the UN Working Group on the use of mercenaries has pursued a binding international convention, while the ECHR has addressed state responsibility for outsourced military functions.

## South Korea

### Legal Framework

South Korea lacks a dedicated legal framework for private military contractors, reflecting both the limited scale of domestic PMC activity and the country's reliance on its own armed forces and the U.S. military alliance for security needs. The relevant legal provisions are dispersed across several statutes.

**Security Services Industry Act (경비업법):** This Act regulates private security companies providing facility security, personal protection, and security consulting services within South Korea. Licensed security companies must register with the National Police Agency (경찰청) and meet requirements for personnel qualifications, insurance, and operational standards. The Act does not extend to military or combat-related services.

**Criminal Act (형법):** Articles 114 and 115 of the Criminal Act prohibit the formation of armed organizations without state authorization. Participation in unauthorized armed groups is punishable by imprisonment of up to 10 years. These provisions could apply to persons organizing or joining private military formations.

**Passport Act (여권법):** The Ministry of Foreign Affairs may restrict passport issuance or validity for persons seeking to travel to conflict zones, effectively preventing Korean nationals from participating in foreign armed conflicts as private contractors.

### Korean Nationals in Foreign Conflicts

South Korea's approach focuses primarily on preventing its citizens from participating in foreign military activities rather than regulating an export PMC industry. The Military Service Act (병역법) imposes strict obligations on male citizens regarding military service, and unauthorized departure from the country to avoid service or engage in foreign military activity constitutes a criminal offense.

### Penalties

| Violation | Penalty |
|-----------|---------|
| Operating unlicensed security business (경비업법) | Up to 3 years imprisonment or fine up to 30 million KRW |
| Formation of unauthorized armed organization (형법 Art. 114) | Up to 10 years imprisonment |
| Participation in unauthorized armed group (형법 Art. 115) | Up to 7 years imprisonment |
| Violation of passport travel restrictions | Up to 1 year imprisonment or fine up to 10 million KRW |

## United States

### Legal Framework

The United States has developed the world's most extensive framework for regulating private military contractors, driven largely by the massive scale of PMC deployment in Iraq and Afghanistan, where contractor personnel at times outnumbered uniformed military.

**Military Extraterritorial Jurisdiction Act (MEJA, 18 U.S.C. Section 3261):** Enacted in 2000 and amended in 2004, MEJA extends federal criminal jurisdiction to felony offenses committed by persons "employed by or accompanying the Armed Forces outside the United States." The 2004 amendment expanded coverage beyond Department of Defense contractors to include contractors of any federal agency supporting the DoD mission.

**Uniform Code of Military Justice (UCMJ):** The 2006 NDAA amendment to UCMJ Article 2(a)(10) expanded military jurisdiction to cover persons "serving with or accompanying an armed force in the field" during "contingency operations," not just declared wars. This theoretically allows courts-martial of contractor personnel, though no contractor has been tried under this provision.

**Defense Federal Acquisition Regulation Supplement (DFARS):** DFARS Part 252.225 imposes contractual requirements on private security contractors, including mandatory reporting of incidents involving weapons discharge, mandatory pre-deployment training, and compliance with applicable laws of armed conflict.

### Nisour Square Incident (2007)

On September 16, 2007, employees of Blackwater Worldwide (later renamed Xe Services, then Academi) opened fire in Nisour Square, Baghdad, killing 17 Iraqi civilians and wounding 20 others. The incident became the defining accountability case for private military contractors.

The legal proceedings exposed multiple jurisdictional gaps:
- Iraqi courts lacked jurisdiction under Coalition Provisional Authority Order No. 17 (subsequently rescinded)
- Military courts had questionable jurisdiction over civilian contractors
- FBI investigation was complicated by initial State Department grants of limited-use immunity

Four Blackwater guards were eventually tried in U.S. federal court under MEJA. In 2014, Nicholas Slatten was convicted of first-degree murder (sentenced to life), while Paul Slough, Evan Liberty, and Dustin Heard were convicted of voluntary manslaughter and weapons charges (sentenced to 30 years each). In December 2020, President Trump pardoned all four defendants, provoking significant international criticism including from the UN Working Group on the use of mercenaries.

## Germany

### Legal Framework

Germany takes a restrictive approach to private military activity, rooted in its constitutional framework and historical experience.

**Grundgesetz Article 26:** The Basic Law declares preparations for aggressive war unconstitutional and requires federal legislation to regulate the manufacture, transport, and marketing of weapons designed for warfare. While not explicitly addressing PMCs, this provision establishes a constitutional framework hostile to private military enterprise.

**Kriegswaffenkontrollgesetz (KrWaffKontrG):** The War Weapons Control Act requires government authorization for the manufacture, acquisition, transfer, and transport of war weapons. PMC activities involving controlled weapons require licensing that is effectively unobtainable for private military use.

**Gewerbeordnung (GewO -- Trade Regulation Act):** Private security companies must obtain a trade license (Gewerbeerlaubnis) under Section 34a GewO, which imposes requirements for personal reliability (Zuverlässigkeit), professional qualification (Sachkunde), and insurance coverage. The regulatory framework is designed for domestic security services and does not contemplate military operations.

### German PMSCs

Germany hosts several private military and security companies that provide services including maritime security, security consulting, and training. Companies such as Asgaard German Security Group have attracted controversy for operations in conflict zones, including allegations of activities in Kurdish regions of Iraq. German authorities have investigated but rarely prosecuted PMSC personnel for overseas activities.

### Academic and Policy Debate

German legal scholars and policymakers have engaged extensively with PMC regulation questions. The Bundestag's Scientific Service (Wissenschaftlicher Dienst) has published analyses examining the constitutional compatibility of PMC deployment with Article 26 GG and the state monopoly on force (Gewaltmonopol des Staates). The prevailing view maintains that outsourcing core military functions to private entities is constitutionally problematic, though ancillary logistical and training services occupy a gray area.

### Penalties

| Violation | Penalty |
|-----------|---------|
| Unauthorized weapons transfer (KrWaffKontrG) | 1-5 years imprisonment; aggravated: up to 15 years |
| Operating security business without license (GewO Section 34a) | Fine up to 25,000 EUR |
| Preparation for aggressive war (Section 80 StGB, repealed 2017; now Section 13 VStGB) | Up to 10 years imprisonment |
| Recruiting for foreign military service (Section 109h StGB) | Up to 5 years imprisonment or fine |

## ECHR & EU

### Montreux Document (2008)

The Montreux Document on Pertinent International Legal Obligations and Good Practices for States Related to Operations of Private Military and Security Companies during Armed Conflict was finalized on September 17, 2008, through a Swiss government initiative with ICRC participation. As of 2026, 58 states and 3 international organizations have endorsed it.

The Document affirms:
- States retain their obligations under international humanitarian law and human rights law when outsourcing military and security functions
- PMSCs and their personnel are bound by applicable international humanitarian law
- States have an obligation to ensure accountability for PMC misconduct

### International Code of Conduct (ICoC)

The International Code of Conduct for Private Security Service Providers, opened for signature on November 9, 2010, establishes a self-regulatory framework for the private security industry. The International Code of Conduct Association (ICoCA), established in 2013 in Geneva, provides oversight, certification, and complaint mechanisms. Over 700 companies have signed the Code, though enforcement remains dependent on voluntary compliance and client contracting requirements.

### European Court of Human Rights

The ECtHR has addressed state responsibility for outsourced security functions in several contexts:

**Al-Skeini v. United Kingdom (Application No. 55721/07, Grand Chamber Judgment of July 7, 2011):** While not directly addressing PMCs, the Grand Chamber established that the ECHR can apply extraterritorially where a state exercises authority and control over individuals. This jurisdiction analysis is relevant to situations where states deploy PMCs exercising governmental authority.

**Costello-Roberts v. United Kingdom (Application No. 13134/87, Judgment of March 25, 1993):** The Court established that states cannot absolve themselves of Convention obligations by delegating functions to private actors. This principle has implications for state responsibility when military functions are outsourced to PMCs.

### UN Draft Convention

The UN Working Group on the use of mercenaries, established by the Human Rights Council, has pursued a binding international convention on PMCs since 2005. A draft convention was submitted to the Human Rights Council in 2010 but has not been adopted, facing opposition from states with significant PMC industries, particularly the United States and United Kingdom. The draft would require state licensing and oversight of PMCs, prohibit core military functions from being outsourced, and establish liability mechanisms for PMC violations of international humanitarian and human rights law.

### EU Initiatives

The European Parliament has passed several non-binding resolutions on PMC regulation, including the Resolution of May 17, 2017, calling for an EU-wide regulatory framework. The European Commission has not proposed binding legislation, though EU crisis management missions under the Common Security and Defence Policy (CSDP) routinely contract private security providers, raising questions about accountability within EU operations.

### Maritime Private Security

A distinct subset of PMC activity involves armed private security on commercial vessels transiting piracy-prone areas, particularly the Gulf of Aden and Indian Ocean:

**South Korea:** The Ship Safety Act and related regulations permit the use of privately contracted armed security personnel (PCASP) on Korean-flagged vessels in designated high-risk areas. The Ministry of Oceans and Fisheries regulates licensing and operational standards for maritime security companies.

**United States:** The Coast Guard Authorization Act authorizes the use of armed security on U.S.-flagged vessels. MARAD (Maritime Administration) has issued guidelines for the use of PCASP. However, most U.S.-owned vessels operate under flags of convenience, complicating regulatory oversight.

**Germany:** The Gewerbeordnung requires licensing for maritime security companies operating from Germany. The Federal Maritime and Hydrographic Agency (BSH) issues guidance on armed security aboard German-flagged vessels. The legal framework addresses weapons storage, rules of engagement, and incident reporting.

**International:** The IMO's Revised Interim Guidance on the use of PCASP (MSC.1/Circ.1405/Rev.2) provides the international framework. The International Organization for Standardization (ISO) published ISO/PAS 28007-1 on guidelines for private maritime security companies, which has become the industry standard.

### Wagner Group and the Evolution of State-Linked PMCs

The Wagner Group, a Russian state-linked PMC that operated extensively in Syria, Libya, Central African Republic, Mali, Sudan, and Ukraine before its 2023 mutiny and subsequent reorganization as "Afrika Corps" under GRU control, highlighted fundamental challenges in the existing regulatory framework:

- Wagner operated as a nominally private entity but functioned as an arm of Russian state power
- Its operations included direct combat, political influence operations, and natural resource extraction
- None of the existing international instruments (Montreux Document, ICoC) effectively addressed state-directed PMC operations
- The 2023 mutiny demonstrated the destabilizing potential of powerful PMCs even to their sponsoring states

The Wagner experience has renewed calls for a binding international convention on PMCs and highlighted the inadequacy of frameworks designed for Western-style corporate PMCs when applied to state-directed paramilitary organizations.

## Comparative Analysis

### Regulatory Philosophy

The jurisdictions exhibit fundamentally different regulatory philosophies. The United States accepts PMCs as legitimate participants in military operations and focuses on regulating their conduct through contractual mechanisms, criminal jurisdiction, and oversight. Germany views private military activity as constitutionally suspect and maintains restrictive controls. South Korea's minimal framework reflects the absence of a significant domestic PMC industry. The international framework remains fragmented, with non-binding instruments predominating.

### Accountability Gaps

The central challenge across all jurisdictions is the accountability gap -- the difficulty of holding private military personnel criminally or civilly liable for misconduct in overseas operations. The United States' experience with the Nisour Square prosecutions illustrated both the possibility and the fragility of criminal accountability: convictions required years of complex litigation and were ultimately undone by presidential pardons. MEJA's jurisdictional reach is limited, military jurisdiction over contractors remains largely untested, and host-state immunity provisions can create complete impunity.

### State Responsibility

International law increasingly recognizes that states cannot escape their obligations under international humanitarian law and human rights law by outsourcing military functions. The Montreux Document and ECHR jurisprudence reinforce this principle, but practical enforcement mechanisms remain weak. The attribution of PMC conduct to the contracting state under the International Law Commission's Articles on State Responsibility requires proof of effective control or direction, a standard that is difficult to meet when PMCs operate with significant autonomy.

### Industry Self-Regulation

The ICoC/ICoCA mechanism represents an innovative approach to PMC accountability through industry self-regulation, but its effectiveness depends on voluntary participation and client government requirements. The absence of binding international standards means that the PMC industry operates under a patchwork of national regulations with significant gaps in coverage.
