## Summary

Contempt of parliament -- conduct that obstructs, impedes, or undermines the authority, dignity, or functioning of a legislative body, including refusal to comply with parliamentary orders, providing false testimony to legislative committees, disrupting parliamentary proceedings, and insulting or threatening legislators -- occupies a distinctive position at the intersection of legislative sovereignty, executive accountability, and individual rights. South Korea addresses contempt of legislative authority through the National Assembly Act (국회법) and the Act on Testimony and Appraisal Before the National Assembly (국회에서의 증언·감정 등에 관한 법률), which provides penalties of up to five years' imprisonment for witnesses who refuse to testify, provide false testimony, or fail to produce documents before National Assembly committees, with enforcement shaped by the politically charged nature of parliamentary investigations in Korea's adversarial political culture. The United States vests contempt power in each house of Congress, exercised through three mechanisms: inherent contempt (direct enforcement by the chamber's sergeant-at-arms), statutory criminal contempt under 2 U.S.C. Section 192 carrying penalties of up to one year imprisonment and fines of $1,000-$100,000, and civil contempt through the courts; the contempt power has been tested in recent high-profile cases including Bannon v. United States and the January 6th Committee proceedings. Germany does not recognize a general parliamentary contempt power comparable to the Anglo-American tradition; instead, the Grundgesetz provides specific investigative powers to parliamentary committees under Article 44 (Untersuchungsausschüsse), with the Untersuchungsausschussgesetz (Parliamentary Investigation Committee Act) of 2001 establishing procedures for compelling testimony and documents, and refusal to comply prosecuted as contempt of court (Ungebühr) or obstruction. At the European level, the European Parliament possesses limited investigative powers under Article 226 TFEU and Regulation (EU, Euratom) No 2020/2092, while the ECHR has addressed the tension between parliamentary privilege and individual rights under Articles 6, 10, and 13.

## South Korea

### Legal Framework

South Korea's National Assembly possesses inspection and investigation powers under Article 61 of the Constitution: "The National Assembly may inspect affairs of state or investigate specific matters of state affairs, and may demand production of documents directly related thereto, the appearance of a witness, and the furnishing of testimony or statements of opinion."

### Act on Testimony and Appraisal Before the National Assembly

| Offense | Penalty |
|---------|---------|
| Perjury (위증) before committee | Up to 5 years imprisonment |
| Refusal to testify without justification | Up to 1 year imprisonment or fine up to 10 million KRW |
| Refusal to produce documents | Up to 1 year imprisonment or fine up to 10 million KRW |
| Failure to appear as summoned | Up to 3 million KRW fine |
| Contemptuous conduct during testimony | Removal from hearing; potential criminal referral |

### Political Context

South Korea's parliamentary investigations have been highly politicized, reflecting the country's adversarial political culture. Notable investigations include:

**Choi Soon-sil Scandal Investigation (2016-2017):** The National Assembly's investigation into the influence-peddling scandal involving President Park Geun-hye's confidante Choi Soon-sil featured dramatic confrontations. Several witnesses refused to testify or provided evasive answers, leading to contempt referrals. The investigation culminated in President Park's impeachment.

**Sewol Ferry Disaster Investigation (2014-2015):** The parliamentary investigation into the sinking of the MV Sewol, which killed 304 persons (mostly high school students), involved protracted disputes over document production and witness testimony. Government officials were accused of obstructing the investigation, though criminal contempt charges were limited.

### Limitations on Contempt Power

The Constitutional Court of Korea has established limits on the National Assembly's investigative powers. In Decision 2009헌라6, the court held that parliamentary investigations must be directed at matters within the National Assembly's legislative competence and cannot be used for purely political purposes or to circumvent the jurisdiction of the courts. The court also held that witnesses retain the right against self-incrimination.

## United States

### Legal Framework

Congress possesses three distinct contempt enforcement mechanisms:

**Inherent Contempt:** The oldest mechanism, recognized by the Supreme Court in Anderson v. Dunn, 19 U.S. 204 (1821), allows each chamber to direct its sergeant-at-arms to arrest and detain contumacious witnesses. This power has not been exercised since 1935 and is considered largely obsolete, though proposals to revive it have resurfaced in recent years.

**Criminal Contempt (2 U.S.C. § 192):** "Every person who having been summoned as a witness by the authority of either House of Congress to give testimony or to produce papers upon any matter under inquiry before either House... willfully makes default, or who, having appeared, refuses to answer any question pertinent to the question under inquiry, shall be deemed guilty of a misdemeanor, punishable by a fine of not more than $100,000 or imprisonment in a common jail for not less than one month nor more than twelve months."

**Civil Contempt:** Congress may authorize its legal counsel to seek a civil judgment compelling compliance, enforceable through the courts.

### Recent High-Profile Cases

**Bannon v. United States (D.C. Cir. 2023):** Steve Bannon was convicted of two counts of criminal contempt of Congress for failing to comply with a subpoena from the House Select Committee investigating the January 6th attack. Bannon was sentenced to four months' imprisonment. The D.C. Circuit upheld the conviction, rejecting Bannon's claims of executive privilege and advice of counsel defenses.

**Peter Navarro (2023-2024):** Former White House trade advisor Peter Navarro was similarly convicted of contempt of Congress for defying a January 6th Committee subpoena, receiving a four-month sentence.

**Eric Holder (2012):** Attorney General Eric Holder was held in contempt by the House of Representatives for refusing to produce documents related to the "Fast and Furious" gun-walking operation. The criminal contempt referral was not prosecuted by the Department of Justice, and the civil enforcement action was litigated for years.

### Executive Privilege Limitations

The Supreme Court addressed the limits of executive privilege in relation to congressional investigations in Trump v. Mazars USA, LLP, 591 U.S. 848 (2020), establishing a four-part test for evaluating congressional subpoenas directed at the executive branch, requiring consideration of the legislative purpose, the breadth of the subpoena, alternative sources of information, and the burden on the executive.

### Penalty Structure

| Mechanism | Maximum Penalty | Enforcement |
|-----------|----------------|-------------|
| Inherent contempt | Detention until end of session | Sergeant-at-arms |
| Criminal contempt (2 U.S.C. § 192) | 1-12 months imprisonment + $100,000 fine | DOJ prosecution; D.C. District Court |
| Civil contempt | Court-ordered compliance; daily fines | Federal courts |

## Germany

### Legal Framework

Germany does not recognize a general parliamentary contempt power in the Anglo-American sense. Instead, the Grundgesetz provides specific mechanisms for parliamentary investigations.

**Article 44 GG (Untersuchungsausschüsse -- Investigation Committees):** "(1) The Bundestag shall have the right, and on the motion of one quarter of its members the duty, to set up an investigation committee. (2) The rules of criminal procedure shall apply mutatis mutandis to the taking of evidence."

### Untersuchungsausschussgesetz (PUAG, 2001)

The Parliamentary Investigation Committee Act establishes the procedural framework:

| Power | Legal Basis | Enforcement |
|-------|-------------|-------------|
| Summoning witnesses | PUAG Section 20 | Court-enforced compliance |
| Compelling testimony | PUAG Section 22 | Ordnungsgeld (fines) up to 10,000 EUR |
| Document production | PUAG Section 18 | Court order |
| Coercive detention (Ordnungshaft) | PUAG Section 27 | Up to 6 months by court order |
| Search and seizure | PUAG Section 29 | Requires court authorization |

### Key Distinction from Anglo-American Systems

The critical difference is that German parliamentary committees cannot independently sanction contempt. All enforcement must be channeled through the courts. The committee requests judicial assistance, and a court (typically the Bundesgerichtshof for federal matters) determines whether compliance can be compelled. This reflects the separation of powers principle (Gewaltenteilung) as understood in German constitutional law.

### Notable Investigations

**NSA Investigation Committee (2014-2017):** The Bundestag established an investigation committee to examine NSA surveillance activities in Germany following the Edward Snowden revelations. The committee encountered significant resistance from the executive branch in obtaining classified documents. The Bundesverfassungsgericht was called upon to adjudicate disputes between the committee and the government regarding document access, holding in BVerfGE 143, 101 (2016) that the government's obligation to cooperate with investigation committees is a constitutional duty, though subject to legitimate national security restrictions.

**Wirecard Investigation Committee (2020-2021):** Following the collapse of Wirecard AG, the Bundestag investigation committee summoned former and current government officials, including the former Finance Minister. Several witnesses invoked their right to refuse testimony on grounds of potential self-incrimination, highlighting the tension between parliamentary investigation and individual rights.

### Bundesverfassungsgericht Jurisprudence

The Federal Constitutional Court has established important principles regarding parliamentary investigation powers. In BVerfGE 67, 100 (Flick Committee Decision, 1984), the court held that the minority right to establish investigation committees is a fundamental element of parliamentary opposition rights, and that the majority cannot obstruct investigations through procedural manipulation.

## ECHR & EU

### European Parliament

The European Parliament possesses limited investigative powers under Article 226 TFEU, which permits the establishment of temporary committees of inquiry "to investigate alleged contraventions or maladministration in the implementation of Union law."

**Regulation (EU, Euratom) No 2020/2092:** The Rule of Law Conditionality Regulation, while not directly a contempt mechanism, allows the EU to withhold funds from member states that undermine the rule of law, including obstruction of legislative oversight.

### ECHR Jurisprudence

**A v. United Kingdom (Application No. 35373/97, 2002):** The Court addressed the scope of parliamentary privilege under Article 6 (right to a fair trial), holding that absolute parliamentary immunity for statements made in Parliament did not violate the Convention, given the legitimate aim of protecting parliamentary debate. However, the scope of immunity cannot extend to acts outside the legislative process.

**Karácsony and Others v. Hungary [GC] (Application No. 42461/13, 2016):** The Grand Chamber found a violation of Article 10 where Hungarian parliamentarians were sanctioned (fined) for displaying banners during parliamentary proceedings. The Court held that while Parliament has the right to maintain order, sanctions against members for political expression within Parliament must be proportionate and subject to adequate procedural safeguards.

**Kart v. Turkey [GC] (Application No. 8917/05, 2009):** The Grand Chamber addressed the tension between parliamentary immunity and the right of access to a court under Article 6, establishing that parliamentary immunity must pursue a legitimate aim and be proportionate.

### Council of Europe Venice Commission

The Venice Commission has published opinions and reports on parliamentary investigation powers, recommending that investigation committees should have legally defined powers, that minority rights to establish committees should be protected, and that adequate procedural safeguards should govern the compulsion of testimony and documents.

## Comparative Analysis

### Enforcement Models

The three national jurisdictions represent distinct enforcement models. The United States combines inherent (self-enforcement), criminal (prosecution), and civil (court-ordered) mechanisms, giving Congress the broadest range of options but creating political complications when the executive branch controls prosecution decisions. South Korea relies primarily on criminal referral to prosecutors, which can be influenced by political relationships. Germany channels all enforcement through the courts, providing the strongest procedural protections but potentially limiting the speed and effectiveness of investigations.

### Scope of Contempt

The definition of contemptuous conduct varies significantly. The United States recognizes the broadest range of contumacious behavior, including refusal to appear, refusal to testify, and refusal to produce documents. South Korea similarly covers refusal and perjury. Germany is more limited, with enforcement focused on specific non-compliance with investigation committee orders rather than a generalized contempt power.

### Political Independence of Enforcement

A recurring challenge across all jurisdictions is the political independence of contempt enforcement. In the United States, criminal contempt referrals depend on the Department of Justice for prosecution, creating a structural problem when the executive branch is the subject of investigation. South Korea's prosecution service, while nominally independent, operates within a political environment. Germany's judicial enforcement model provides the greatest structural independence but may be slower.
