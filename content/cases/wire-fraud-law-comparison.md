## Summary

Wire fraud -- the use of electronic communications to execute a scheme to defraud -- is one of the most versatile and frequently prosecuted financial crimes in the United States, yet the concept does not translate directly into other legal systems. The United States treats wire fraud as a standalone federal offense under 18 U.S.C. section 1343, carrying penalties of up to 20 years imprisonment (or 30 years when a financial institution is affected), making it one of the broadest tools in the federal prosecutor's arsenal. South Korea does not have a direct "wire fraud" statute but addresses equivalent conduct through Article 347-2 of the Criminal Act (형법 제347조의2, "Fraud by the Use of Computer, etc."), punishing computer-facilitated fraud with up to 10 years imprisonment, supplemented by the Act on Aggravated Punishment of Specific Economic Crimes (특정경제범죄 가중처벌 등에 관한 법률) which imposes sentences of life imprisonment or a minimum of five years for fraud exceeding 5 billion won. Germany criminalizes comparable conduct through Section 263a of the Strafgesetzbuch (StGB) -- Computerbetrug (computer fraud) -- with penalties of up to five years imprisonment in standard cases and up to ten years in aggravated cases. At the supranational level, the European Union harmonizes member state approaches through Directive 2013/40/EU on attacks against information systems, while the Budapest Convention on Cybercrime (2001), ratified by all four jurisdictions or their member states, establishes baseline obligations for criminalizing computer-related fraud under its Article 8. These divergent approaches reflect fundamentally different legal traditions: the U.S. system emphasizes the use of interstate communications as the jurisdictional hook, while civil law systems in South Korea, Germany, and the EU framework focus on the manipulation of data processing systems as the core criminal act.

## South Korea

### Legal Framework

South Korea addresses wire-type fraud through multiple overlapping statutes rather than a single dedicated provision. The core offense is found in Article 347-2 of the Criminal Act (형법), titled "Fraud by the Use of Computer, etc." (컴퓨터등 사용사기죄), which was added by Act No. 5057 on December 29, 1995 to fill a gap left by traditional fraud law's requirement of human deception.

**Article 347-2 -- Fraud by the Use of Computer, etc.:** Any person who acquires any benefits to property or has a third person acquire them, by making any data processed after inputting false information or improper orders, or inputting or altering data without authority into a data processor such as a computer, shall be punished by imprisonment with prison labor for not more than 10 years, or a fine not exceeding 20 million won (approximately $15,000 USD).

Supporting statutes include:

- **Article 347(1) -- General Fraud (사기죄):** Any person who deceives another and thereby obtains any property or pecuniary advantage shall be punished by imprisonment for not more than 10 years or a fine not exceeding 20 million won. This covers fraud committed through telephone, email, or other electronic communication where a human victim is directly deceived.
- **Electronic Financial Transactions Act (전자금융거래법):** Regulates the safety and reliability of electronic financial transactions and imposes criminal penalties for unauthorized electronic fund transfers, hacking of financial systems, and misuse of electronic payment instruments.
- **Act on Aggravated Punishment of Specific Economic Crimes (특정경제범죄 가중처벌 등에 관한 법률), Article 3:** Applies aggravated penalties to fraud offenses based on the amount of illicit gain:
  - Gain of 5 billion won or more: imprisonment for an indefinite term or not less than 5 years.
  - Gain of 500 million won to less than 5 billion won: imprisonment for not less than 3 years.
- **Act on the Protection of Consumers in Electronic Commerce (전자상거래 등에서의 소비자보호에 관한 법률):** Addresses fraud in online commercial transactions and e-commerce platforms.

### Key Distinctions from U.S. Wire Fraud

Unlike the U.S. approach, South Korean law does not use the electronic transmission medium as a jurisdictional element. Instead, Article 347-2 focuses on the manipulation of a data-processing system. Traditional fraud (Article 347) still requires the deception of a human being, so Article 347-2 was introduced to cover situations where no human is deceived but a computer system is manipulated to produce an unauthorized financial benefit.

### Voice Phishing (보이스피싱) and Electronic Fraud

Voice phishing (vishing) has become one of South Korea's most prevalent forms of electronic fraud. Organized criminal networks, often based in China or Southeast Asia, use phone calls, text messages, and messaging apps to impersonate government officials, financial institutions, or family members to extract money from victims.

The prosecution service has adopted an aggressive enforcement posture:

- Voice phishing ringleaders face sentences under the Act on Aggravated Punishment of Specific Economic Crimes, with courts imposing sentences exceeding 20 years.
- In March 2025, the Supreme Court of Korea upheld a 23-year prison sentence against a voice phishing conspirator who used drugged beverages and extortion threats in a scheme targeting teenagers and their parents.
- The Telecommunications Business Act (전기통신사업법) criminalizes the use of altered caller ID and spoofed phone numbers, which are key tools in voice phishing schemes.

### Landmark Cases

**Supreme Court Decision on Voice Phishing Extortion (2025):** The Supreme Court upheld a 23-year sentence against Lee, a 28-year-old who conspired with a Chinese voice phishing gang. Lee convinced students to consume beverages laced with methamphetamine, then extorted their parents by threatening to report the drug use to police. The scheme targeted 13 teenagers and 6 parents. This decision signaled the judiciary's willingness to treat organized voice phishing as severely as violent crime.

**Woori Bank Embezzlement Case (2025):** The Supreme Court finalized criminal sentences in a 70 billion won ($53 million) embezzlement case involving Woori Bank employees who exploited internal banking systems for unauthorized transfers -- a case prosecuted under both Article 347-2 (computer fraud) and the Act on Aggravated Punishment of Specific Economic Crimes.

## United States

### Legal Framework

Wire fraud is codified at 18 U.S.C. section 1343 and is one of the most frequently charged federal offenses. Originally enacted in 1952 to extend the mail fraud statute (18 U.S.C. section 1341, dating to 1872) to cover wire communications, it has become the federal prosecutor's "Swiss Army knife" due to its breadth.

**18 U.S.C. section 1343 -- Fraud by Wire, Radio, or Television:** Whoever, having devised or intending to devise any scheme or artifice to defraud, or for obtaining money or property by means of false or fraudulent pretenses, representations, or promises, transmits or causes to be transmitted by means of wire, radio, or television communication in interstate or foreign commerce, any writings, signs, signals, pictures, or sounds for the purpose of executing such scheme or artifice, shall be fined under this title or imprisoned not more than 20 years, or both. If the violation occurs in relation to, or involving any benefit authorized, transported, transmitted, transferred, disbursed, or paid in connection with, a presidentially declared major disaster or emergency, or affects a financial institution, such person shall be fined not more than $1,000,000 or imprisoned not more than 30 years, or both.

### Elements of the Offense

The Department of Justice identifies the following elements (Criminal Resource Manual section 941):

1. The defendant voluntarily and intentionally devised or participated in a scheme to defraud another out of money or property.
2. The defendant did so with the intent to defraud.
3. It was reasonably foreseeable that interstate wire communications would be used.
4. Interstate wire communications were in fact used.

Notably, the scheme need not succeed, and the government need not prove actual financial loss. Each individual wire transmission can constitute a separate count, enabling prosecutors to stack charges.

### Penalties

| Circumstance | Maximum Imprisonment | Maximum Fine |
|-------------|---------------------|-------------|
| Basic wire fraud | 20 years | $250,000 |
| Affecting a financial institution | 30 years | $1,000,000 |
| In connection with a presidentially declared disaster or emergency | 30 years | $1,000,000 |
| Each wire transmission is a separate count | Consecutive sentences possible | Per count |

### Landmark Cases

**Carpenter v. United States, 484 U.S. 19 (1987):** The Supreme Court upheld the wire fraud conviction of a Wall Street Journal columnist who leaked advance information about his investment column to a stockbroker. The Court held that confidential business information is "property" within the meaning of the fraud statutes, even when the information belongs to the employer rather than a client.

**Pasquantino v. United States, 544 U.S. 349 (2005):** The Supreme Court held 5-4 that a scheme to defraud a foreign government (Canada) of tax revenue by smuggling liquor across the border violated the wire fraud statute. The decision expanded the reach of section 1343 to protect foreign governments' rights to collect lawful tax revenue.

**Kelly v. United States, 590 U.S. ___ (2020):** In the "Bridgegate" case, the Supreme Court unanimously reversed wire fraud convictions of associates of New Jersey Governor Chris Christie who orchestrated lane closures on the George Washington Bridge for political retaliation. The Court held that the scheme did not aim to obtain money or property -- the lanes were reallocated, not stolen -- and therefore fell outside the wire fraud statute.

**Ciminelli v. United States, 598 U.S. 306 (2023):** The Supreme Court unanimously rejected the "right to control" theory of wire fraud, holding that section 1343 criminalizes only schemes to deprive people of traditional property interests, not intangible rights such as the right to control the use of one's assets.

**Kousisis v. United States (2025):** The Supreme Court held that a defendant who intentionally lies to induce a victim into a transaction that will cost money or property violates the wire fraud statute, affirming the government's "fraudulent inducement" theory as consistent with the text of section 1343.

### Wire Fraud as a Predicate Offense

Wire fraud serves as a predicate offense for several other federal statutes:

- **RICO (18 U.S.C. section 1961):** Wire fraud can establish a "pattern of racketeering activity."
- **Money Laundering (18 U.S.C. section 1956):** Wire fraud proceeds can trigger money laundering charges.
- **Forfeiture (18 U.S.C. section 981):** Property involved in wire fraud is subject to civil and criminal forfeiture.

## Germany

### Legal Framework

Germany does not have a "wire fraud" offense in the American sense. Instead, the Strafgesetzbuch (StGB) addresses fraud through two primary provisions: Section 263 (Betrug -- general fraud) and Section 263a (Computerbetrug -- computer fraud). Section 263a was introduced by the Second Act to Combat Economic Crime (Zweites Gesetz zur Bekampfung der Wirtschaftskriminalitat, 2. WiKG) in 1986 to address the gap created by the fact that traditional fraud under Section 263 requires the deception of a human being.

**Section 263a StGB -- Computer Fraud (Computerbetrug):**

(1) Whoever, with the intent of obtaining an unlawful pecuniary advantage for themselves or a third party, damages the assets of another by influencing the result of a data-processing operation through:
- incorrect configuration of a program,
- use of incorrect or incomplete data,
- unauthorized use of data, or
- other unauthorized influence on the course of the processing,

shall be punished with imprisonment for a term not exceeding five years or a fine.

(2) Section 263 subsections (2) through (6) apply mutatis mutandis. This includes the aggravated cases provision.

(3) Whoever prepares an offense under subsection (1) by producing, procuring for themselves or another, offering for sale, storing, or providing to another computer programs the purpose of which is the commission of such an offense shall be punished with imprisonment for a term not exceeding three years or a fine.

### Penalties

| Category | Maximum Imprisonment | Fine |
|----------|---------------------|------|
| Basic computer fraud (section 263a(1)) | 5 years | Day-fine system (unlimited theoretical maximum) |
| Aggravated cases (section 263a(2) via section 263(3)) | 10 years | Day-fine system |
| Preparatory offenses (section 263a(3)) | 3 years | Day-fine system |
| Gang-related or commercial fraud (section 263(5)) | 10 years (minimum 1 year) | Day-fine system |

Aggravating circumstances under Section 263(3) include: acting on a commercial basis, causing a large-scale financial loss, acting as a member of a gang, exploiting a position of trust, or pretending that an insurance event has occurred.

### Key Distinctions from U.S. Wire Fraud

German computer fraud law differs from U.S. wire fraud in several fundamental respects:

- **No communications-based jurisdictional hook:** German law focuses on the manipulation of a data-processing operation, not the use of wire communications. The medium of communication is irrelevant; what matters is interference with data processing.
- **Pecuniary advantage required:** Section 263a requires intent to obtain an unlawful pecuniary advantage, which is narrower than the U.S. requirement of a "scheme to defraud" that encompasses intangible interests.
- **Direct causation of asset damage:** The statute requires that the data-processing manipulation directly damages another's assets, establishing a closer causal nexus than U.S. wire fraud.
- **General fraud still available:** Where a human being is deceived via electronic means (e.g., phishing emails), Section 263 (general fraud) applies rather than Section 263a, since a person -- not a computer -- was deceived.

### Landmark Cases

**OLG Braunschweig -- Bitcoin Theft Decision (2025):** The Braunschweig Higher Regional Court ruled that Bitcoin is not "property" within the meaning of Section 242 StGB (theft) but that unauthorized acquisition of Bitcoin may constitute computer fraud under Section 263a StGB if data-processing operations were manipulated. This landmark decision clarified the application of computer fraud law to digital assets and has significant implications for cryptocurrency-related crime throughout Germany.

**BGH Decision, 4 StR 194/16 (2016):** The Federal Court of Justice (Bundesgerichtshof) addressed the scope of "unauthorized use of data" under Section 263a, holding that the unauthorized use of payment card data in online transactions constitutes computer fraud even where no physical card is presented and no point-of-sale terminal is manipulated.

**LG Duisburg, 33 KLs - 111 Js 32/16 (2017):** The Duisburg Regional Court applied Section 263a in a case involving manipulation of computer programs to generate unauthorized financial transactions, reinforcing the statute's applicability to sophisticated technical manipulation beyond simple data input fraud.

### Relationship to EU Law

As an EU member state, Germany has transposed Directive 2013/40/EU on attacks against information systems into national law. German criminal provisions generally exceed the Directive's minimum requirements, as the Directive mandates maximum penalties of at least two years for basic offenses and at least five years for aggravated offenses, while German law provides for up to ten years in aggravated cases.

## ECHR & EU

### Supranational Framework

The European framework for addressing wire-type fraud operates at multiple levels: the Council of Europe (which includes non-EU members), the European Union, and the European Court of Human Rights (ECHR). Unlike national legal systems, these institutions do not directly prosecute individuals but establish binding obligations for member states to criminalize certain conduct and cooperate in enforcement.

### Budapest Convention on Cybercrime (2001)

The Convention on Cybercrime, opened for signature in Budapest on November 23, 2001 (ETS No. 185), is the first and most influential multilateral treaty on cybercrime. It has been ratified by 68 states as of 2025, including all EU member states, the United States, South Korea (acceded 2014), and Japan.

**Article 8 -- Computer-Related Fraud:** Each Party shall adopt such legislative and other measures as may be necessary to establish as criminal offences under its domestic law, when committed intentionally and without right, the causing of a loss of property to another person by:
- any input, alteration, deletion or suppression of computer data,
- any interference with the functioning of a computer system,

with fraudulent or dishonest intent of procuring, without right, an economic benefit for oneself or for another person.

The Convention does not specify penalties but requires that offenses be "punishable by effective, proportionate and dissuasive sanctions, which include deprivation of liberty" (Article 13).

### EU Directive 2013/40/EU on Attacks Against Information Systems

Adopted on August 12, 2013, and replacing Council Framework Decision 2005/222/JHA, this Directive harmonizes the criminal law of EU member states regarding attacks on information systems. While primarily focused on unauthorized access, system interference, and data interference rather than fraud per se, it establishes a graduated penalty framework:

| Offense Category | Minimum Maximum Penalty Required |
|-----------------|--------------------------------|
| Basic offenses (Articles 3-7) | At least 2 years imprisonment |
| Offenses affecting significant number of systems via specialized tools | At least 3 years imprisonment |
| Offenses committed by criminal organizations or causing serious damage | At least 5 years imprisonment |
| Offenses against critical infrastructure systems | At least 5 years imprisonment |

Member states were required to transpose the Directive into national law by September 4, 2015.

### EU Directive 2017/1371 (PIF Directive)

The Directive on the Fight against Fraud to the Union's Financial Interests, adopted July 5, 2017, harmonizes definitions and sanctions for criminal offenses affecting the EU budget. It mandates:

- Maximum penalties of at least 4 years imprisonment for fraud involving damage exceeding 100,000 euros.
- Member states must provide for effective, proportionate, and dissuasive criminal penalties, including imprisonment.
- Covers fraud committed through any means, including electronic communications.

### ECHR Jurisprudence

The European Court of Human Rights does not adjudicate fraud cases directly but has developed jurisprudence relevant to the prosecution of wire-type fraud:

- **Article 6 (Right to a Fair Trial):** The ECHR has established procedural safeguards applicable to fraud prosecutions, including the right to adequate time to prepare a defense and the right to examine digital evidence.
- **Article 8 (Right to Privacy):** The Court has addressed tensions between law enforcement surveillance of electronic communications for fraud investigations and the right to privacy. States must demonstrate that surveillance measures are "necessary in a democratic society" and subject to adequate safeguards.
- **Article 1 of Protocol 1 (Protection of Property):** The ECHR has recognized that states have positive obligations to protect individuals' property rights, which may extend to establishing effective criminal frameworks against electronic fraud.

### Eurojust and Europol

Cross-border enforcement of wire-type fraud within the EU is facilitated by:

- **Eurojust:** The EU Agency for Criminal Justice Cooperation coordinates joint investigations and prosecutions of cross-border fraud, including cases involving electronic communications.
- **Europol's European Cybercrime Centre (EC3):** Provides operational support, strategic analysis, and coordination for member state investigations into cyber-enabled fraud.
- **European Arrest Warrant (EAW):** Enables expedited extradition between EU member states for fraud offenses, eliminating the double criminality requirement for offenses carrying a maximum penalty of at least 3 years.

## Comparative Analysis

### Conceptual Divergences

The four jurisdictions reveal fundamentally different approaches to criminalizing fraud conducted through electronic means:

1. **Communications-based vs. data-manipulation-based:** The United States uniquely anchors its wire fraud statute to the use of interstate wire communications, making the communication medium itself the jurisdictional element. South Korea, Germany, and the EU framework instead focus on the manipulation of data-processing systems, treating the communication channel as incidental.

2. **Breadth of "scheme to defraud":** U.S. wire fraud encompasses any "scheme or artifice to defraud," which courts have interpreted to include schemes involving intangible rights (subject to recent Supreme Court narrowing in Ciminelli and Kelly). By contrast, South Korean Article 347-2, German Section 263a, and the Budapest Convention all require that the fraud result in a concrete economic loss or property benefit.

3. **Role of human deception:** Traditional fraud in all jurisdictions requires the deception of a human being. Computer fraud provisions (Article 347-2, Section 263a, Budapest Convention Article 8) were introduced precisely because manipulating a computer does not involve deceiving a person. U.S. wire fraud, however, does not distinguish between deceiving a person and manipulating a system -- it focuses on the use of wire communications to further any fraudulent scheme.

4. **Penalty severity:** The United States imposes the harshest basic penalties (20 years), followed by South Korea (10 years for basic fraud, life for aggravated cases involving more than 5 billion won), and Germany (5 years basic, 10 years aggravated). The EU Directive sets minimum maximums (2-5 years) that member states must meet or exceed.

### Prosecutorial Strategy

- **United States:** Wire fraud is often charged alongside other offenses (mail fraud, bank fraud, money laundering, RICO) to maximize leverage. Each wire transmission constitutes a separate count, enabling prosecutors to seek substantial cumulative sentences.
- **South Korea:** Prosecutors frequently invoke the Act on Aggravated Punishment of Specific Economic Crimes to elevate penalties for large-scale fraud, and have shown particular aggressiveness against voice phishing networks.
- **Germany:** The principle of proportionality (Verhaltnismassigkeitsprinzip) constrains prosecutorial discretion. Computer fraud is typically prosecuted as a single course of conduct rather than as multiple separate offenses.
- **EU:** The emphasis is on cross-border cooperation through Eurojust and the European Arrest Warrant rather than on harmonizing substantive penalties beyond minimum thresholds.

### Emerging Challenges

All four jurisdictions face common challenges in adapting their fraud frameworks to evolving technology:

- **Cryptocurrency fraud:** The German OLG Braunschweig decision (2025) illustrates the difficulty of applying traditional property concepts to digital assets. U.S. prosecutors routinely charge cryptocurrency fraud under section 1343.
- **AI-enabled fraud:** Deepfake technology and AI-generated communications create new vectors for fraud that challenge existing legal definitions of deception and manipulation.
- **Cross-border enforcement:** Wire-type fraud increasingly involves actors, victims, and infrastructure in multiple jurisdictions, straining mutual legal assistance frameworks.
