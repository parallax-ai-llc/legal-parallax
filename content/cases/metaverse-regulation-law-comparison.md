## Summary

Metaverse regulation addresses the governance of persistent, immersive virtual environments where users interact through avatars, own digital assets, and engage in economic, social, and creative activities. As metaverse platforms have grown in scale and complexity, jurisdictions have confronted novel legal questions regarding virtual property rights, avatar identity and rights, platform liability for in-world conduct, virtual real estate transactions, and the application of existing laws to conduct occurring entirely within digital spaces. South Korea has been a global pioneer in metaverse governance, enacting the Act on the Development and Support of Virtual Convergence Economy (가상융합경제 발전 및 지원에 관한 법률, 2023) and establishing a dedicated regulatory framework. The European Union addresses metaverse issues through the intersection of the Digital Services Act (Regulation (EU) 2022/2065), the Digital Markets Act (Regulation (EU) 2022/1925), the AI Act, and GDPR, without a single unified metaverse statute. The United States has no federal metaverse legislation, relying on existing intellectual property, consumer protection, and securities law frameworks applied analogically to virtual environments. Germany operates within the EU framework while developing national guidance on virtual world governance through the Federal Network Agency (BNetzA) and data protection authorities.

## South Korea

### Legal Framework

South Korea has positioned itself as a global leader in metaverse regulation, driven by the country's significant metaverse industry (including platforms such as Zepeto, operated by Naver Z, with over 400 million registered users globally) and the government's Digital New Deal strategy.

Key statutory provisions include:

- **Virtual Convergence Economy Act (가상융합경제 발전 및 지원에 관한 법률, 2023):** Enacted to promote the metaverse industry while establishing user protection frameworks. The Act defines "virtual convergence" (가상융합) as the integration of physical and virtual worlds through extended reality (XR) technologies, and establishes a comprehensive governance framework.
  - **Article 2 -- Definitions:** Defines virtual convergence services, virtual convergence content, and virtual convergence platforms, providing a technology-neutral framework applicable to current and future metaverse platforms.
  - **Article 10 -- User Protection:** Requires platform operators to establish fair terms of service, provide transparent information about virtual asset transactions, implement dispute resolution mechanisms, and protect user-generated content rights.
  - **Article 12 -- Virtual Asset Protection:** Establishes that users have protectable interests in virtual assets acquired or created within metaverse platforms, requiring platforms to maintain asset registries and provide reasonable notice before service termination or asset deletion.
  - **Article 15 -- Content Moderation:** Requires platforms to implement content moderation systems addressing harmful content, including harassment, hate speech, and sexually explicit material within virtual environments.
- **Game Industry Promotion Act (게임산업진흥에 관한 법률):** Applies to metaverse platforms that incorporate game elements, requiring age ratings and prohibiting minors from accessing age-restricted virtual environments. The Act's mandatory shutdown system (강제적 셧다운제) was repealed in 2021, but age verification requirements remain.
- **Personal Information Protection Act (PIPA, 개인정보보호법):** Applies to all personal data collected through metaverse platforms, including biometric data from VR headsets (eye tracking, facial expressions, body movement data), avatar-linked behavioral data, and spatial mapping data.
- **Act on the Consumer Protection in Electronic Commerce (전자상거래 등에서의 소비자보호에 관한 법률):** Applies to virtual commerce conducted within metaverse environments, requiring disclosure obligations, cooling-off periods for digital purchases, and dispute resolution mechanisms.

### Key Principles

South Korea's regulatory approach to the metaverse emphasizes the "metaverse ethics principles" (메타버스 윤리원칙) published by the Ministry of Science and ICT in 2022, establishing three core principles: authentic identity (진정한 자아 구현), safe experience (안전한 경험), and sustainable prosperity (지속가능한 번영). These principles underpin regulatory guidance addressing avatar impersonation, virtual harassment, data portability between platforms, and economic fairness in virtual marketplaces.

The Korean Communications Commission (KCC) has issued guidance classifying immersive metaverse platforms as "value-added telecommunications services" under the Telecommunications Business Act, subjecting them to registration requirements and content regulation obligations. The Personal Information Protection Commission (PIPC) has published guidelines specifically addressing biometric data collection through VR/AR devices, requiring explicit consent for collection of eye-tracking data, gait patterns, and physiological responses captured by immersive hardware.

### Notable Cases

- **Zepeto Virtual Sexual Harassment Case (2022-2023):** South Korean police investigated reports of sexual harassment within the Zepeto metaverse platform, where users' avatars were subjected to unwanted sexual contact. The case raised fundamental questions about whether existing sexual harassment laws (under the Criminal Act, 형법 Article 298, forcible indecency) could apply to avatar-to-avatar conduct. The prosecution ultimately declined to prosecute, citing the absence of physical contact, prompting legislative proposals to extend harassment protections to virtual environments.
- **PIPC VR Biometric Data Enforcement (2024):** The PIPC fined a VR gaming company KRW 150 million for collecting and processing users' eye-tracking and facial expression data without obtaining separate, specific consent as required under PIPA Article 23 (sensitive information processing).
- **Virtual Real Estate Fraud Prosecution (2023):** The Suwon District Court convicted a defendant of fraud (Criminal Act Article 347) for selling fictitious virtual real estate parcels in a purported metaverse platform that did not exist, establishing that virtual property can constitute a subject of fraud even in the absence of statutory recognition of virtual property rights.

## United States

### Federal Framework

The United States has no federal legislation specifically addressing metaverse governance. Instead, existing legal frameworks are applied analogically to virtual environments:

- **Section 230 of the Communications Decency Act (47 U.S.C. § 230):** Provides broad immunity to interactive computer service providers for third-party content, including content within metaverse platforms. Section 230(c)(1) protects platforms from liability for user-generated virtual environments, avatar conduct, and in-world communications, while Section 230(c)(2) protects good-faith content moderation efforts. The scope of Section 230 in immersive environments remains untested at the appellate level.
- **Children's Online Privacy Protection Act (COPPA, 15 U.S.C. §§ 6501-6506):** Applies to metaverse platforms that collect personal information from children under 13, requiring verifiable parental consent. The FTC has signaled that biometric data collected through VR headsets (including iris scans, voice prints, and physical movement data) constitutes "personal information" under COPPA. The FTC proposed updated COPPA rules in 2024, specifically addressing immersive environments.
- **Federal Trade Commission Act Section 5 (15 U.S.C. § 45):** The FTC's authority over unfair or deceptive trade practices applies to misleading representations in virtual marketplaces, deceptive pricing of virtual goods, and unfair terms of service governing virtual asset ownership.
- **Securities Laws (Securities Act of 1933; Securities Exchange Act of 1934):** The SEC has taken the position that certain virtual assets, including virtual real estate tokens and in-world currency, may constitute securities under the Howey test (SEC v. W.J. Howey Co., 328 U.S. 293 (1946)), particularly when marketed as investment opportunities with expected profits derived from the efforts of others.
- **Copyright Act (17 U.S.C. §§ 101-1511):** User-generated content within metaverse platforms raises complex copyright issues, including the copyrightability of virtual architectures, avatar designs, and in-world creations.

### State Variations

Several states have enacted or proposed legislation addressing aspects of metaverse governance:

- **California Age-Appropriate Design Code Act (AB 2273, effective 2024):** Requires online services likely to be accessed by children to conduct data protection impact assessments and implement age-appropriate default privacy settings, with direct implications for metaverse platforms.
- **Utah Social Media Regulation Act (SB 152, 2023):** Requires age verification for social media platforms, with implications for metaverse platforms that incorporate social media features.
- **Texas Securing Children Online through Parental Empowerment Act (HB 18, 2023):** Prohibits social media platforms from allowing minors to create accounts without parental consent, applicable to metaverse platforms classified as social media.
- **Illinois Biometric Information Privacy Act (BIPA, 740 ILCS 14):** Requires informed consent before collecting biometric identifiers, directly applicable to VR headset manufacturers and metaverse platforms collecting facial geometry, voice prints, and other biometric data. BIPA's private right of action has generated significant metaverse-related litigation.

### Notable Cases

- **Linden Research, Inc. v. Bragg (E.D. Pa. 2007):** An early virtual property case where the court held that Second Life's terms of service, which purported to grant users property rights in virtual land, created enforceable contractual obligations and that the platform's unilateral seizure of virtual property could constitute a breach.
- **FTC v. Fortnite (Epic Games) (2022):** The FTC imposed a USD 520 million settlement on Epic Games for COPPA violations and dark patterns in Fortnite, including deceptive practices in virtual currency purchases and inadequate privacy protections for minors in the game's virtual environment. This remains the largest FTC enforcement action involving a virtual environment.
- **Roblox COPPA Litigation (2024):** Multiple class action lawsuits alleged that Roblox's metaverse platform collected personal information from children under 13 without verifiable parental consent, including voice recordings and behavioral data, in violation of COPPA and state privacy laws.
- **SEC v. Decentraland (Investigation, 2023-2024):** The SEC investigated whether virtual real estate sales in the Decentraland metaverse constituted unregistered securities offerings, focusing on the marketing of MANA tokens and virtual land parcels as investment vehicles.

## Germany

### Legal Framework

Germany addresses metaverse regulation through the intersection of EU-wide legislation and national legal frameworks:

- **Digital Services Act (Regulation (EU) 2022/2065):** Directly applicable in Germany, the DSA imposes graduated obligations on metaverse platforms based on their size and role. Very Large Online Platforms (VLOPs) with over 45 million monthly EU users must conduct systemic risk assessments addressing, inter alia, the dissemination of illegal content, effects on fundamental rights, and risks to minors within their virtual environments (Article 34). Metaverse platforms designated as VLOPs must implement risk mitigation measures and submit to independent auditing.
- **Digital Markets Act (Regulation (EU) 2022/1925):** Potentially applicable to metaverse gatekeepers that control core platform services, including virtual marketplace services, social networking within virtual worlds, and operating system-level access for VR/AR hardware.
- **Digital Services Act Implementation Act (Digitale-Dienste-Gesetz, DDG, 2024):** Germany's national implementation law designating the Federal Network Agency (BNetzA) as the Digital Services Coordinator responsible for DSA enforcement, including enforcement in virtual environments.
- **Youth Protection Act (Jugendschutzgesetz, JuSchG) and Interstate Treaty on the Protection of Minors in the Media (JMStV):** The JuSchG was amended in 2021 to address "interaction risks" (Interaktionsrisiken) in digital media, including contact risks and economic risks posed to minors in virtual environments. The Federal Agency for the Protection of Children and Young People in the Media (BzKJ) has authority to list harmful metaverse content and impose access restrictions.
- **German Civil Code (BGB) §§ 145-157 (Contract Law):** Virtual asset transactions within metaverse platforms are governed by general contract law principles, with terms of service functioning as general terms and conditions (Allgemeine Geschäftsbedingungen) subject to the fairness controls of BGB §§ 305-310.
- **GDPR and BDSG:** The extensive processing of personal data in immersive environments -- including biometric data, behavioral profiling, and spatial data -- triggers comprehensive GDPR obligations. German data protection authorities have been particularly attentive to the "surveillance" potential of VR/AR technologies.

### Key Principles

Germany's approach to metaverse regulation is characterized by a strong emphasis on user protection, data privacy, and youth safety. The Hamburg Commissioner for Data Protection published a position paper in 2024 on "Data Protection in the Metaverse," identifying key risks including the involuntary collection of biometric data, behavioral profiling through avatar interactions, and the challenge of ensuring meaningful consent in immersive environments where users may not be aware of the extent of data collection.

The BNetzA, as Germany's Digital Services Coordinator, has established a dedicated unit for platform regulation that includes expertise in immersive technologies. The agency has published guidance clarifying that metaverse platforms offering user-to-user interactions fall within the DSA's definition of "online platform" (Article 3(i)) and are subject to corresponding obligations regarding content moderation, transparency reporting, and complaint handling.

### Notable Cases

- **BzKJ Metaverse Content Listing (2024):** The Federal Agency for the Protection of Children and Young People (BzKJ) listed a VR-based social platform on its index of media harmful to young persons (Liste jugendgefährdender Medien) after finding that the platform failed to implement adequate safeguards against minors' exposure to violent and sexually explicit user-generated content in virtual environments.
- **Hamburg DPA VR Data Collection Decision (2024):** The Hamburg Commissioner for Data Protection imposed a fine of EUR 1.2 million on a VR fitness application for collecting and processing detailed biometric data -- including heart rate variability, body composition estimates, and movement patterns -- without a lawful basis under GDPR Article 9(2) for processing special categories of data.
- **Bundesgerichtshof (BGH) Virtual Currency Decision (2023):** The Federal Court of Justice ruled in a consumer dispute that virtual currency purchased within an online game constitutes a contractual entitlement subject to BGB contract law protections, and that the platform operator's unilateral alteration of virtual currency value through game mechanic changes could constitute an unfair contract term under BGB § 307.

## ECHR / EU Framework

### Convention Articles

The European Convention on Human Rights provides fundamental principles applicable to metaverse governance:

- **Article 8 -- Right to Respect for Private and Family Life:** The extensive data collection inherent in immersive virtual environments -- including biometric data, behavioral patterns, social interactions, and spatial awareness data -- implicates Article 8's protection of private life. The ECtHR's established principle that the concept of "private life" extends to activities in public spaces (Peck v. United Kingdom, 2003, App. No. 44647/98) suggests that activities within shared virtual spaces also attract Article 8 protection.
- **Article 10 -- Freedom of Expression:** Content creation and communication within metaverse environments are protected by Article 10, while platform content moderation measures must comply with the proportionality requirements established in the Court's jurisprudence. The question of whether avatar expression constitutes "expression" within the meaning of Article 10 has not been directly addressed by the Court.
- **Article 11 -- Freedom of Assembly and Association:** Virtual gatherings within metaverse platforms may constitute assemblies protected under Article 11, particularly in light of the Court's recognition in Navalnyy v. Russia (2018) that Article 11 protects the right to choose the time, place, and modalities of peaceful assembly.
- **Article 1 of Protocol No. 1 -- Protection of Property:** Whether virtual assets constitute "possessions" within the meaning of Protocol No. 1 remains an open question. The ECtHR's broad interpretation of "possessions" -- encompassing any asset with economic value, including claims and legitimate expectations (Öneryildiz v. Turkey, 2004) -- suggests that virtual property with demonstrated economic value could attract Convention protection.
- **Article 14 -- Prohibition of Discrimination:** Applicable to discriminatory practices within metaverse platforms, including algorithmic bias in avatar representation, discriminatory access to virtual spaces, and unequal treatment based on protected characteristics.

### EU Legislative Framework

- **Digital Services Act (Regulation (EU) 2022/2065):** The DSA is the primary EU instrument governing platform obligations in the metaverse. Key provisions applicable to metaverse platforms include: transparency in content moderation (Article 14); internal complaint handling (Article 20); out-of-court dispute settlement (Article 21); trusted flaggers (Article 22); measures against misuse (Article 23); and, for VLOPs, systemic risk assessment and mitigation (Articles 34-35) and independent auditing (Article 37).
- **Digital Markets Act (Regulation (EU) 2022/1925):** The DMA's gatekeeper obligations -- including interoperability requirements, prohibition of self-preferencing, and data portability -- could significantly reshape the metaverse ecosystem if immersive platforms are designated as gatekeepers. The European Commission's designation process for core platform services will determine the DMA's application to specific metaverse platforms.
- **Data Act (Regulation (EU) 2023/2854):** The Data Act's provisions on data sharing and portability have direct implications for metaverse platforms, requiring that users be able to access and port their data (including user-generated content and virtual assets) between platforms.
- **European Commission's Virtual Worlds Initiative (2023):** The Commission published a communication on "An EU initiative on Web 4.0 and virtual worlds: a head start in the next technological transition" (COM(2023) 442 final), outlining a strategy for European governance of virtual worlds based on openness, interoperability, and protection of fundamental rights. The initiative included a Citizens' Panel on Virtual Worlds (2023-2024) that produced recommendations on governance principles.

### Key Principles and Guidance

- **EDPB Guidelines on Virtual Reality and Data Protection (2024):** The European Data Protection Board adopted guidelines addressing the specific data protection challenges posed by immersive technologies, including the classification of VR/AR-collected biometric data as special category data under GDPR Article 9, the application of data minimization principles to immersive environment data collection, and guidance on valid consent in virtual environments.
- **Fundamental Rights Impact:** The EU Agency for Fundamental Rights (FRA) published a report on "Metaverse and Fundamental Rights" (2024), identifying key risks including immersive manipulation, virtual harassment, exclusion from virtual economic opportunities, and the potential for metaverse platforms to create "parallel legal orders" operating outside national regulatory frameworks.

## Key Regulatory Challenges

### Jurisdictional Complexity in Borderless Virtual Worlds

Metaverse platforms operate across national borders without physical localization, creating fundamental jurisdictional questions. When a user in South Korea interacts with a user in Germany within a metaverse platform operated by a US company, multiple legal systems potentially apply simultaneously. The EU's DSA addresses this through the "country of origin" principle (Article 3) modulated by the VLOP regime, but the application of criminal law to conduct within virtual environments remains largely unresolved. South Korea's Virtual Convergence Economy Act applies to platforms that provide services to users in South Korea, while US Section 230 immunity may shield platforms from liability that would otherwise attach under Korean or EU law.

### Virtual Harassment and Criminal Law Application

The extension of criminal law concepts to virtual environments remains one of the most contested areas. Traditional criminal law requires physical elements (actus reus) that are absent in avatar-to-avatar interactions. South Korea's Zepeto case demonstrated the limitations of applying physical assault and harassment statutes to virtual conduct. Germany's Criminal Code (StGB) § 185 (insult) and § 238 (stalking) could potentially apply to persistent virtual harassment, as these offences do not require physical contact. The EU Victims' Rights Directive (2012/29/EU) may require member states to extend victim support to those experiencing severe harassment in virtual environments.

### Economic Regulation of Virtual Economies

Virtual economies within metaverse platforms can reach substantial scale, raising questions about monetary regulation, consumer protection, and taxation. South Korea's approach of extending e-commerce consumer protection to virtual transactions provides the most comprehensive framework. The EU Data Act's provisions on data portability may enable users to transfer virtual assets between platforms, potentially disrupting closed virtual economies.
