## Summary

Unlawful assembly -- the gathering of persons in a manner that threatens or results in public disorder, violence, or breach of the peace -- lies at one of the most contested intersections of criminal law and constitutional rights, as every legal system must balance the fundamental right to peaceful assembly against the state's legitimate interest in maintaining public order and preventing violence. South Korea regulates assemblies through the Assembly and Demonstration Act (집회 및 시위에 관한 법률), which requires advance notification for outdoor assemblies, prohibits assemblies near certain government buildings, and penalizes participation in unlawful assemblies with up to six months' imprisonment or a fine, with the Constitutional Court of Korea having repeatedly addressed the boundaries between protected protest and criminalized assembly, notably striking down blanket nighttime assembly bans in its 2009 decision (2008헌가25). The United States protects the right of peaceful assembly under the First Amendment but permits regulation of the time, place, and manner of assemblies, with unlawful assembly typically defined under state law as the gathering of three or more persons with a common intent to engage in conduct that creates a clear and present danger of violence; the Supreme Court has established stringent standards for restricting assembly rights in cases including DeJonge v. Oregon (1937) and Brandenburg v. Ohio (1969). Germany protects the right of assembly under Article 8 of the Grundgesetz (Versammlungsfreiheit) and regulates assemblies through the federal Versammlungsgesetz (Assembly Act) and increasingly through Länder-level assembly laws, with the Bundesverfassungsgericht's landmark Brokdorf decision (BVerfGE 69, 315, 1985) establishing that the right to demonstrate is an essential element of democratic governance and that restrictions must be narrowly tailored. At the European level, Article 11 of the ECHR protects the right to freedom of peaceful assembly, with the European Court of Human Rights developing extensive jurisprudence requiring states to protect peaceful assemblies and prohibiting blanket bans, while the EU Charter of Fundamental Rights mirrors these protections under Article 12.

## South Korea

### Legal Framework

South Korea's Assembly and Demonstration Act (집회 및 시위에 관한 법률, commonly abbreviated 집시법), originally enacted in 1962 and substantially amended multiple times, provides the primary regulatory framework for public assemblies.

### Key Provisions

| Provision | Content |
|-----------|---------|
| Article 5 | Prohibition of assemblies inciting violence or crime |
| Article 6 | 48-hour advance notification requirement for outdoor assemblies |
| Article 8 | Prohibition of assemblies obstructing traffic |
| Article 10 | Prohibition of assemblies near National Assembly, courts, Blue House |
| Article 11 | Restrictions on nighttime assemblies (partially unconstitutional) |
| Article 12 | Prohibition of assemblies with weapons or dangerous objects |

### Penalty Structure

| Offense | Penalty |
|---------|---------|
| Participation in prohibited assembly | Up to 6 months imprisonment or fine up to 500,000 KRW |
| Organizing prohibited assembly | Up to 2 years imprisonment or fine up to 2 million KRW |
| Failure to provide advance notification | Up to 2 years imprisonment or fine |
| Armed assembly | Up to 3 years imprisonment |

### Constitutional Court Decisions

**2008헌가25 (2009) -- Nighttime Assembly Ban:** The Constitutional Court declared unconstitutional the blanket prohibition on outdoor assemblies between midnight and sunrise under former Article 10 of the Assembly Act. The court held that while nighttime assemblies may create heightened public order concerns, a blanket prohibition was disproportionate and violated the right to assembly under Article 21 of the Constitution. The court noted that many significant democratic protests -- including the 2008 candlelight vigils against U.S. beef imports -- necessarily extended into nighttime hours.

**2003헌바457 (2005) -- Buffer Zone Near National Assembly:** The Constitutional Court upheld the prohibition on assemblies within 100 meters of the National Assembly building, finding that the restriction served the legitimate purpose of ensuring the legislature's ability to function without external physical pressure. However, the court emphasized that the restriction must be interpreted narrowly to apply only to assemblies that could interfere with legislative functions.

### Candlelight Vigils and Democratic Protest

South Korea has a distinctive tradition of mass democratic protest, from the 1987 June Democracy Movement (6월 민주 항쟁) to the 2016-2017 candlelight revolution (촛불혁명) that led to the impeachment of President Park Geun-hye. These movements have shaped the legal framework, with courts increasingly recognizing the democratic significance of large-scale peaceful assemblies and restraining police from dispersing peaceful gatherings.

## United States

### Legal Framework

The First Amendment provides: "Congress shall make no law... abridging... the right of the people peaceably to assemble." This protection has been incorporated against state governments through the Fourteenth Amendment.

### Constitutional Doctrine

**DeJonge v. Oregon, 299 U.S. 353 (1937):** The Supreme Court held that the right of peaceable assembly is cognate to those of free speech and free press, and is equally fundamental. The state may not make criminal the mere participation in a peaceful assembly called by an organization that has also engaged in unlawful conduct.

**Brandenburg v. Ohio, 395 U.S. 444 (1969):** The Court held that the government cannot punish advocacy of the use of force or of law violation except where such advocacy is directed to inciting or producing imminent lawless action and is likely to incite or produce such action. This standard effectively limits the government's ability to declare an assembly unlawful based on the content of the participants' speech.

**Cox v. Louisiana, 379 U.S. 536 (1965):** The Court upheld the principle that states may impose reasonable time, place, and manner restrictions on assemblies, provided they are content-neutral, narrowly tailored to serve a significant governmental interest, and leave open ample alternative channels for communication.

### State Unlawful Assembly Statutes

| State | Definition | Classification |
|-------|-----------|---------------|
| California (PC § 407-408) | Assembly of 2+ persons to commit unlawful act or lawful act in violent manner | Misdemeanor |
| New York (PL § 240.10) | Assembly of 4+ persons with intent to engage in tumultuous conduct likely to cause alarm | Class B misdemeanor |
| Texas (PC § 42.02) | Assembly of 7+ persons with intent to obstruct lawful process | Class B misdemeanor |
| Georgia (OCGA § 16-11-33) | Assembly of 2+ persons for purpose of committing unlawful act | Misdemeanor |

### Recent Controversies

The 2020 racial justice protests following the killing of George Floyd brought renewed scrutiny to unlawful assembly laws. Several jurisdictions faced legal challenges to their enforcement of assembly restrictions:

**Black Lives Matter protests:** Federal courts in multiple jurisdictions, including Don't Shoot Portland v. City of Portland (D. Or. 2020), issued temporary restraining orders limiting the use of tear gas and crowd-control munitions against protesters, finding that indiscriminate use of such measures violated the First and Fourth Amendment rights of peaceful protesters who happened to be present at assemblies where some individuals engaged in unlawful conduct.

### Anti-Protest Legislation

Since 2017, numerous states have enacted or proposed legislation increasing penalties for protest-related offenses, including laws targeting pipeline protests (critical infrastructure protection acts), highway blocking, and organized protest activities. Civil liberties organizations have challenged several of these statutes as unconstitutionally overbroad.

## Germany

### Legal Framework

Germany provides robust constitutional protection for the right of assembly under Article 8 of the Grundgesetz: "(1) All Germans shall have the right to assemble peacefully and without arms, without prior notification or permission. (2) For outdoor assemblies, this right may be restricted by or pursuant to a law."

### Brokdorf Decision (BVerfGE 69, 315, 1985)

The Bundesverfassungsgericht's Brokdorf decision is the foundational ruling on assembly rights in German law. The case arose from a ban on anti-nuclear demonstrations near the Brokdorf nuclear power plant. The Court held:

- The right to demonstrate is not merely an individual right but a collective exercise essential to democratic participation.
- The state has a duty to protect peaceful assemblies, not merely to tolerate them.
- Restrictions on assemblies must be based on concrete evidence of danger, not mere suspicion.
- The principle of cooperation (Kooperationsgebot) requires authorities to seek dialogue with assembly organizers before resorting to restrictions or bans.
- An assembly does not lose its protected status merely because individual participants engage in unlawful conduct.

### Versammlungsgesetz (Assembly Act)

The federal Versammlungsgesetz, originally enacted in 1953, establishes the regulatory framework:

| Provision | Content |
|-----------|---------|
| Section 1 | Everyone has the right to organize and participate in public assemblies |
| Section 14 | Duty to register outdoor assemblies 48 hours in advance |
| Section 15 | Authority to impose conditions or ban assemblies based on direct danger to public safety |
| Section 17a | Prohibition on face coverings (Vermummungsverbot) at assemblies |
| Section 21 | Penalties: fine or up to 1 year for violations |
| Section 27 | Penalties: up to 3 years for carrying weapons at assemblies |

### Bannmeile (Restricted Zones)

The Bannmeilengesetz establishes restricted zones around the Bundestag and certain other constitutional organs, within which assemblies require special authorization. The Bundesverfassungsgericht has interpreted these restrictions narrowly, requiring that any ban within the Bannmeile be justified by a concrete threat to the functioning of the protected institution.

### Right-Wing Assembly Controversies

German law faces particular challenges regarding far-right assemblies, especially those near Holocaust memorial sites. The Wunsiedel decision (BVerfGE 124, 300, 2009) addressed the tension between assembly rights and the prohibition on glorifying National Socialism, upholding restrictions on assemblies that serve to glorify the Nazi regime. Section 130(4) StGB criminalizes public disturbance of peace by glorifying National Socialist rule in a manner violating the dignity of victims.

## ECHR & EU

### European Court of Human Rights

Article 11 of the ECHR protects the right to freedom of peaceful assembly. The Court has developed extensive jurisprudence:

**Kudrevičius and Others v. Lithuania [GC] (Application No. 37553/05, 2015):** The Grand Chamber held that farmers' road blockade protests were protected under Article 11, even though the blockades caused significant disruption. The Court found that the criminal conviction of protest organizers was disproportionate, emphasizing that some degree of disruption is inherent in the exercise of the right to peaceful assembly.

**Lashmankin and Others v. Russia (Application No. 57818/09, 2017):** The Court found that Russia's extensive notification requirements and geographic restrictions on assemblies violated Article 11. The Court held that blanket bans on assemblies in certain locations, without case-by-case assessment, are incompatible with the Convention.

**Navalnyy and Yashin v. Russia (Application No. 76204/11, 2014):** The Court found a violation of Article 11 where Russian authorities arrested participants in a peaceful assembly that had not received official authorization, holding that the penalization of an unauthorized but peaceful demonstration constitutes a disproportionate restriction on freedom of assembly.

### OSCE/ODIHR Guidelines on Freedom of Peaceful Assembly

The OSCE Office for Democratic Institutions and Human Rights (ODIHR) and the Venice Commission published the Guidelines on Freedom of Peaceful Assembly (3rd edition, 2019), establishing best practices:

- Peaceful assemblies should be governed by a notification rather than authorization regime.
- Blanket prohibitions on assemblies in specific locations are inconsistent with international standards.
- Spontaneous assemblies should be accommodated.
- The "polluter principle" should apply: violent individuals should be removed without dispersing the entire assembly.

### EU Charter of Fundamental Rights

Article 12 of the EU Charter mirrors ECHR Article 11: "Everyone has the right to freedom of peaceful assembly and to freedom of association at all levels." The CJEU has invoked this provision in cases involving labor disputes and political protests, though the primary jurisprudence on assembly rights remains with the ECtHR.

## Comparative Analysis

### Notification vs. Authorization

All four jurisdictions require some form of advance notice for outdoor assemblies, but the legal nature of the requirement differs. South Korea and Germany require notification (Anmeldepflicht) -- the assembly may proceed even without official approval. The United States permit system, by contrast, functions as an authorization regime in some localities, though courts have held that permit requirements cannot function as prior restraints on speech.

### Buffer Zones

All jurisdictions maintain some form of buffer zone around government buildings or sensitive locations. South Korea prohibits assemblies near the National Assembly and courts. The United States has upheld buffer zones around abortion clinics (McCullen v. Coakley, 573 U.S. 464 (2014)). Germany maintains Bannmeile zones around parliament. The ECHR requires that all buffer zones be proportionate and subject to case-by-case assessment.

### Individual vs. Collective Responsibility

A critical question is whether the unlawful conduct of some participants renders the entire assembly unlawful. Germany's Brokdorf principle clearly states that an assembly does not lose protection merely because individuals within it engage in violence. The ECHR's Kudrevičius decision supports a similar view. South Korean courts have moved in this direction. U.S. law varies, with some jurisdictions allowing dispersal orders that apply to all participants.
