## Summary

Equal pay law -- the legal framework requiring that men and women receive equal remuneration for equal work or work of equal value -- represents a fundamental pillar of gender equality that has evolved from simple prohibitions on pay differentials to sophisticated frameworks addressing structural causes of pay gaps, including pay transparency requirements, employer reporting obligations, and salary history bans. South Korea, despite having enacted the Act on Equal Employment and Support for Work-Family Reconciliation (남녀고용평등과 일·가정 양립 지원에 관한 법률) in 1987 with an explicit equal pay provision in Article 8, maintains one of the largest gender pay gaps in the OECD at approximately 31.2 percent as of 2023, driven by occupational segregation, the disproportionate impact of career interruptions for childcare, and the concentration of women in non-regular employment; the government introduced the Affirmative Action program (적극적 고용개선조치) in 2006 requiring large employers to report workforce gender composition and develop improvement plans when female representation falls below industry averages. The United States addresses equal pay through the Equal Pay Act of 1963 (29 U.S.C. Section 206(d)), which prohibits sex-based pay differentials for equal work on jobs requiring equal skill, effort, and responsibility under similar working conditions, and Title VII of the Civil Rights Act of 1964 as interpreted in cases such as County of Washington v. Gunther, 452 U.S. 161 (1981), which recognized comparable worth claims; the gender pay gap was approximately 16 percent in 2023 (full-time workers), and reform efforts have focused on the Paycheck Fairness Act (repeatedly introduced but not enacted) and state-level salary history bans. Germany implements the EU's equal pay framework through the Entgelttransparenzgesetz (EntgTranspG, Pay Transparency Act) of 2017, which grants employees the right to request information about comparative median pay of the opposite sex, requires employers with more than 500 employees to publish gender pay reports, and reflects Article 157 TFEU and the recast Gender Equality Directive 2006/54/EC; the gender pay gap was approximately 18 percent (unadjusted) in 2023. At the European level, the EU Pay Transparency Directive 2023/970, adopted on May 10, 2023, represents the most ambitious equal pay reform in EU history, mandating pay range disclosure in job postings, banning salary history questions, requiring employers with 100+ workers to report gender pay gaps, granting workers access to comparative pay data, and shifting the burden of proof to employers in equal pay disputes, with transposition due by June 7, 2026.

## South Korea

### Legal Framework

Equal pay in South Korea is guaranteed by Article 8 of the Act on Equal Employment: "An employer shall provide equal pay for work of equal value in the same business." The provision covers base wages, bonuses, allowances, and all other forms of compensation.

### Definition of Work of Equal Value

Article 8(2) provides that work of equal value is assessed by considering skill, effort, responsibility, and working conditions -- criteria substantially similar to those in the U.S. Equal Pay Act. The Ministry of Employment and Labor has issued guidelines elaborating on these factors, but judicial application has been limited due to the relatively small number of equal pay cases reaching the courts.

### Gender Pay Gap

South Korea has consistently maintained the largest gender pay gap among OECD member states:

| Year | Gender Pay Gap (OECD measure) |
|------|------------------------------|
| 2010 | 39.6% |
| 2015 | 36.7% |
| 2018 | 34.1% |
| 2020 | 31.5% |
| 2023 | 31.2% |

The persistent gap is attributed to several structural factors:

**Occupational Segregation:** Women are concentrated in lower-paying service and clerical occupations, while men dominate higher-paying manufacturing, technology, and management positions.

**Career Interruptions:** The "M-shaped" female labor force participation curve, in which women exit the workforce in their late 20s to mid-30s for childcare and re-enter in lower-paying positions in their 40s, creates a substantial lifetime earnings penalty.

**Non-Regular Employment:** Women constitute approximately 45 percent of non-regular workers (비정규직), who earn on average 65 percent of regular workers' wages.

**Corporate Culture:** The seniority-based wage system (연공서열제) prevalent in Korean corporations amplifies pay gaps because women's career interruptions reduce their tenure.

### Affirmative Action Program (적극적 고용개선조치)

Established by the 2006 amendment to the Equal Employment Act, this program requires employers with 500 or more employees (300+ in certain sectors) to report their workforce composition by gender and occupational level to the Ministry of Employment and Labor. If female representation in management or specific occupational categories falls below 60 percent of the industry average, the employer must develop and implement an improvement plan. As of 2023, approximately 2,400 employers are covered. The Ministry publishes annual lists of compliant and non-compliant employers.

### Legislative Proposals

Bills have been introduced to require pay transparency reporting (similar to the UK's gender pay gap reporting requirement) and to mandate gender pay gap audits for large employers. As of early 2026, none had been enacted.

## United States

### Legal Framework

The United States addresses equal pay through two overlapping federal statutes and numerous state laws.

### Equal Pay Act of 1963

The EPA (29 U.S.C. § 206(d)), an amendment to the FLSA, prohibits sex-based pay differentials for equal work on jobs requiring equal skill, effort, and responsibility performed under similar working conditions. The employer may assert four affirmative defenses: seniority system, merit system, system measuring earnings by quality or quantity of production, and any factor other than sex.

**Corning Glass Works v. Brennan, 417 U.S. 188 (1974):** The Supreme Court established that the EPA requires comparison of jobs, not job titles, and that a "going market rate" defense based on historically lower wages for women does not constitute a "factor other than sex."

### Title VII -- Comparable Worth

While the EPA requires "equal work," Title VII's broader prohibition on sex discrimination in compensation has been interpreted to reach "comparable worth" claims -- situations where different jobs of comparable value are compensated unequally.

**County of Washington v. Gunther, 452 U.S. 161 (1981):** The Court held that Title VII wage discrimination claims are not limited to "equal work" situations, permitting claims based on intentional sex-based compensation discrimination even where the jobs compared are not substantially equal.

### Gender Pay Gap

The BLS reports that in 2023, women working full-time earned approximately 84 cents for every dollar earned by men (16 percent gap). The gap varies significantly by race: White women 83 cents, Black women 70 cents, Hispanic/Latina women 65 cents, Asian women 93 cents (compared to White male dollar).

### Paycheck Fairness Act

Repeatedly introduced since 1997 but never enacted, the PFA would strengthen the EPA by replacing the "any factor other than sex" defense with a "bona fide factor other than sex" standard requiring business necessity, prohibiting retaliation against workers who discuss wages, requiring the EEOC to collect pay data, and eliminating class action barriers. The Act passed the House in 2019 and 2021 but did not advance in the Senate.

### State Laws

| State Reform | Examples |
|-------------|---------|
| Salary history bans | California (2017), New York City (2017), Massachusetts (2016), Illinois (2019), Colorado (2019), Washington (2019) -- approximately 21 states and localities |
| Pay transparency | Colorado (2019): must include salary ranges in job postings; California (2022): SB 1162 requires pay scale disclosure; Washington (2022); New York (2023) |
| Strengthened equal pay | Massachusetts (2016): broadened "comparable work" standard; California (2015): Fair Pay Act -- "substantially similar" work |

### Lilly Ledbetter Fair Pay Act of 2009

Enacted in response to Ledbetter v. Goodyear Tire & Rubber Co., 550 U.S. 618 (2007), this Act provides that each discriminatory paycheck resets the statute of limitations for filing an equal pay claim. The Act effectively overturned the Supreme Court's restrictive holding that the limitations period began at the initial discriminatory pay-setting decision.

## Germany

### Legal Framework

Germany addresses equal pay through the Entgelttransparenzgesetz (EntgTranspG, Pay Transparency Act) of 2017, the Allgemeines Gleichbehandlungsgesetz (AGG), and the constitutional guarantee of equal rights (Article 3(2) GG).

### Entgelttransparenzgesetz (2017)

**Individual Information Right (§ 10-16):** Employees in establishments with more than 200 employees may request information about the median monthly gross pay of employees of the opposite sex performing the same or comparable work. The request may be submitted to the employer or the works council. The employer must respond within three months.

**Employer Reporting (§ 21-22):** Employers with more than 500 employees are encouraged (but not legally required) to conduct internal pay equity reviews (betriebliche Prüfverfahren). Employers with more than 500 employees subject to the CSR Directive reporting obligations must include a report on equal pay measures and the status of gender equality in their management report (Lagebericht).

### Criticism of EntgTranspG

The EntgTranspG has been widely criticized as inadequate:

- The 200-employee threshold for the information right excludes approximately 60 percent of the German workforce.
- The information provides only median figures, making it difficult to identify individual discrimination.
- There is no legal consequence if the employer's response reveals a pay gap.
- The voluntary nature of pay equity reviews for employers with 500+ employees limits effectiveness.
- Only approximately 4 percent of eligible employees have exercised the information right since 2018.

### EU Pay Transparency Directive Implementation

Germany will need to significantly strengthen its framework to transpose Directive 2023/970, including lowering the reporting threshold from 500 to 100 employees, making reporting mandatory rather than voluntary, introducing salary history bans, requiring pay range disclosure in job postings, and establishing joint pay assessments with worker representatives when a gender pay gap of 5 percent or more is identified.

### Gender Pay Gap

The unadjusted gender pay gap in Germany was approximately 18 percent in 2023 (Destatis), with significant regional variation: approximately 18 percent in former West Germany versus approximately 7 percent in former East Germany. The adjusted gap (controlling for occupation, hours, experience) was approximately 6 percent, which represents the portion attributable to discrimination rather than structural factors.

### Bundesarbeitsgericht Case Law

In a landmark 2023 decision (8 AZR 450/21), the BAG held that an employer's payment of a higher salary to a male employee performing the same work as a female employee creates a presumption of sex discrimination under Section 22 AGG, shifting the burden of proof to the employer. The employer's assertion that the male employee had negotiated a higher salary did not constitute a sufficient non-discriminatory justification.

## ECHR & EU

### EU Pay Transparency Directive 2023/970

Adopted on May 10, 2023, with transposition due by June 7, 2026, this Directive represents the most comprehensive equal pay reform in EU history:

**Pre-Employment (Article 5):** Job applicants have the right to information about the pay range for the position. Employers may not ask about salary history.

**Pay Transparency for Workers (Article 7):** Workers have the right to request and receive information about their individual pay level and the average pay levels, broken down by sex, for categories of workers doing the same work or work of equal value.

**Gender Pay Gap Reporting (Article 9):** Employers with 100+ workers must report gender pay gaps to the national authority. Initially, employers with 250+ workers report annually; employers with 150-249 report every three years; employers with 100-149 report every three years (starting June 7, 2031).

**Joint Pay Assessment (Article 10):** Where reporting reveals a gender pay gap of 5 percent or more that cannot be justified by objective gender-neutral factors, the employer must conduct a joint pay assessment with worker representatives.

**Burden of Proof (Article 18):** In equal pay disputes, when a complainant establishes facts from which discrimination may be presumed, the burden shifts to the employer to prove no violation occurred. Non-compliance with transparency obligations creates an additional presumption of discrimination.

**Remedies (Article 16):** Full compensation including back pay, related bonuses and benefits in kind, compensation for lost opportunities, moral prejudice, and interest on arrears. No prior cap on compensation.

### Article 157 TFEU

Article 157 of the Treaty on the Functioning of the European Union enshrines the principle of equal pay for equal work or work of equal value, providing: "Each Member State shall ensure that the principle of equal pay for male and female workers for equal work or work of equal value is applied." This provision has direct effect and can be invoked by individuals before national courts.

### CJEU Jurisprudence

**Defrenne v. Sabena (No. 2) (Case 43/75, 1976):** The Court held that the equal pay principle has direct effect and can be relied upon by individuals against both public and private employers.

**Enderby v. Frenchay Health Authority (Case C-127/92, 1993):** The Court established that where statistics reveal an appreciable difference in pay between two jobs of equal value, one predominantly carried out by women and the other by men, the burden shifts to the employer to justify the differential.

**K.B. v. National Health Service Pensions Agency (Case C-117/01, 2004):** The Court held that excluding transsexual persons from pension benefits constituted pay discrimination under Article 157 TFEU.

**Brunnhofer v. Bank der österreichischen Postsparkasse (Case C-381/99, 2001):** The Court established that individual pay comparisons are permissible -- a complainant is not limited to statistical evidence but may point to an individual comparator performing equal work.

### ECHR

The ECHR has addressed pay discrimination under Article 14 in conjunction with Article 1 of Protocol 1. In Andrle v. Czech Republic (Application No. 6268/08, 2011), the Court held that differential pension ages for men and women did not violate Article 14, recognizing that states may use positive measures to compensate for factual inequalities between men and women.

## Comparative Analysis

### Pay Gap Magnitude

The three jurisdictions span a wide range: South Korea's 31.2 percent gap is the largest in the OECD, Germany's 18 percent is above the EU average, and the United States' 16 percent has been slowly declining. The EU average of 12.7 percent provides a benchmark. The persistence of significant gaps despite decades of equal pay legislation in all jurisdictions demonstrates the limitations of legal prohibition alone.

### Pay Transparency

The EU Pay Transparency Directive represents a paradigm shift toward proactive, structural measures. Germany's existing EntgTranspG has proven inadequate due to high thresholds and voluntary mechanisms. South Korea lacks pay transparency requirements. The United States has seen state-level innovation (Colorado, California) but no federal action. The Directive's requirements for job posting salary ranges, salary history bans, mandatory gap reporting, and shifted burden of proof collectively represent the most comprehensive approach to closing the pay gap through transparency.

### Enforcement Approaches

The United States provides the strongest individual litigation framework through the EPA and Title VII, with substantial damages available. However, the absence of mandatory pay transparency limits the information available to potential claimants. The EU Directive addresses this by combining transparency with litigation tools, including burden-of-proof shifting. South Korea's enforcement remains weak, with the Affirmative Action program focused on representation rather than pay specifically.

### Structural Factors

All jurisdictions recognize that the pay gap is driven by structural factors -- occupational segregation, career interruptions, non-standard employment -- that are not fully addressable through equal pay legislation alone. South Korea's problem is most acute due to its seniority wage system, M-shaped participation curve, and gender-segregated labor market.
