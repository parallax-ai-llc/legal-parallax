## Summary

Food sovereignty -- the right of peoples and nations to define their own food and agriculture systems, as distinct from the narrower concept of food security focused on access to sufficient food -- represents a growing but contested area of law at the intersection of agricultural policy, trade regulation, human rights, and environmental protection. South Korea protects its domestic food system through the Framework Act on Agriculture, Rural Community and Food Industry (농업·농촌 및 식품산업 기본법), the Grain Management Act (양곡관리법), and some of the world's highest agricultural tariffs, particularly the 513% tariff on rice that was maintained until the 2015 tariff-rate quota transition, while mandating GMO labeling under the Food Sanitation Act (식품위생법). The United States, by contrast, promotes agricultural trade liberalization through the Agricultural Act (Farm Bill) system, regulates GMOs through a coordinated framework involving the FDA, USDA, and EPA with no mandatory on-package GMO labeling until the National Bioengineered Food Disclosure Standard (NBFDS) of 2018 (7 U.S.C. Section 1639), and exercises enormous influence over global food systems through commodity subsidies and trade negotiations. Germany operates within the EU's Common Agricultural Policy (CAP) framework, implements EU Regulation 1829/2003 on GM food and feed and Regulation 1169/2011 on food information to consumers, and has enacted national opt-out bans on GM crop cultivation under Directive 2015/412. The EU framework addresses food sovereignty indirectly through the CAP, the Farm to Fork Strategy, Regulation 2024/1143 on geographical indications, and trade agreements that increasingly include agricultural safeguards, while the ECHR has not directly addressed food sovereignty but has recognized related rights to food, water, and a healthy environment.

## South Korea

### Legal Framework

South Korea's food sovereignty framework is deeply rooted in historical experience with food insecurity during the colonial period and Korean War, creating strong political and cultural support for domestic agricultural protection that persists despite WTO liberalization pressures.

### Constitutional and Legislative Foundation

**Article 121 of the Constitution:** The state shall endeavor to realize the land-to-the-tiller principle with respect to agricultural land, and tenant farming shall be prohibited in principle.

**Article 123:** The state shall establish and implement a plan to develop and support the agricultural and fisheries sectors in a comprehensive manner to protect and foster agriculture and fisheries, and shall fulfill its obligation to support the balanced development of regional economies.

**Framework Act on Agriculture, Rural Community and Food Industry (농업·농촌 및 식품산업 기본법):** This 2007 act establishes the national framework for agricultural policy, declaring food self-sufficiency, agricultural sustainability, and rural community vitalization as core policy objectives. Article 3 establishes the state's obligation to ensure stable food supply and support domestic agriculture.

### Rice Protection

Rice holds an exceptional position in Korean food sovereignty policy. Korea maintained an absolute import ban on rice until 1994, when it negotiated a special waiver under the WTO Agreement on Agriculture allowing minimum market access (MMA) without tariffication.

**2015 Tariffication:** After decades of negotiations, Korea transitioned from the MMA system to a 513% tariff-rate quota system for rice imports, effective January 1, 2015. The in-quota tariff is 5%, with out-of-quota imports subject to prohibitive tariffs. Mandatory purchases of domestic rice for government stockpiles continue through the Grain Management Act.

**Public Stockholding for Food Security Purposes:** Korea has been active in WTO negotiations on public stockholding programs, supporting the G-33 coalition's position that public food security reserves should be exempt from aggregate measurement of support (AMS) commitments.

### GMO Labeling and Regulation

**Food Sanitation Act (식품위생법):** Korea requires mandatory labeling of foods containing genetically modified organisms above the 3% unintentional contamination threshold. The labeling requirement applies to 27 food categories including soybeans, corn, canola, cotton, sugar beets, and alfalfa.

**Living Modified Organisms Act (유전자변형생물체의 국가간 이동 등에 관한 법률):** Implementing the Cartagena Protocol, this act regulates the import, production, and environmental release of LMOs. Korea imports substantial quantities of GM soybeans and corn for animal feed and food processing but does not permit commercial cultivation of GM crops domestically.

**Consumer Advocacy:** Korean consumer groups, including the Citizens' Coalition for Economic Justice (경제정의실천시민연합) and the Korean Women's Association United (한국여성단체연합), have actively campaigned for stricter GMO labeling requirements, including demands to lower the threshold to 0.9% (EU level) and to require labeling of processed foods derived from GM ingredients even when no detectable GM DNA remains.

### Local Food Systems

Korea has invested heavily in local food systems. The School Meals Act (학교급식법) requires preference for locally sourced, environmentally friendly agricultural products in school meals. Local food direct sales markets (로컬푸드 직매장) have proliferated, with government support through the Local Food Act framework. The certification system for environmentally friendly agricultural products (친환경농산물) and the Good Agricultural Practices (GAP) system promote sustainable local production.

## United States

### Legal Framework

The United States approaches food systems primarily through a market-oriented framework that emphasizes agricultural productivity, trade liberalization, and consumer choice, with food sovereignty as a concept largely absent from federal legal discourse.

### Farm Bill System

The Agricultural Act (Farm Bill), reauthorized approximately every five years, is the omnibus legislation governing federal food and agriculture policy. The most recent authorization, the Agriculture Improvement Act of 2018 (P.L. 115-334), covers commodity programs, conservation, trade, nutrition assistance (SNAP), crop insurance, research, and rural development.

**Title I -- Commodities:** Price Loss Coverage (PLC) and Agriculture Risk Coverage (ARC) provide counter-cyclical support to producers of covered commodities (wheat, corn, soybeans, rice, peanuts, and other crops). These subsidies have been challenged in WTO dispute settlement, most notably in United States -- Subsidies on Upland Cotton (DS267), where Brazil successfully challenged US cotton subsidies.

**Trade Promotion:** Title III of the Farm Bill authorizes the Market Access Program (MAP) and Foreign Market Development (FMD) Program, which fund export promotion activities. The US has historically been the world's largest agricultural exporter and has used trade negotiations to open foreign markets to US agricultural products, often at the expense of food sovereignty claims by importing countries.

### GMO Regulation

**Coordinated Framework for Biotechnology (1986):** The US regulates GM organisms through existing agencies rather than dedicated GMO legislation: the USDA Animal and Plant Health Inspection Service (APHIS) regulates field testing and environmental release; the FDA evaluates food safety through voluntary consultation (no mandatory pre-market approval for most GM foods); and the EPA regulates GM plants expressing pesticidal substances (Bt crops) under the Federal Insecticide, Fungicide, and Rodenticide Act (FIFRA).

**National Bioengineered Food Disclosure Standard (NBFDS, 2018):** Following years of state-level labeling initiatives -- notably Vermont's Act 120 (2014), the first mandatory GMO labeling law -- Congress enacted the NBFDS (7 U.S.C. Section 1639), preempting state labeling laws. The USDA's implementing regulations, effective January 1, 2022, require disclosure of "bioengineered" foods but permit disclosure through text, symbol, electronic/digital link (QR code), or text message. Critics argue the standard is weaker than international norms because it permits digital-only disclosure, uses the unfamiliar term "bioengineered" instead of "GMO," and exempts highly refined products where modified DNA is no longer detectable.

### Trade Agreements and Food Sovereignty

The US has consistently opposed food sovereignty claims in trade negotiations. In USMCA (United States-Mexico-Canada Agreement, 2020), Chapter 3 on agriculture includes provisions limiting non-tariff barriers to agricultural trade. Mexico's 2023 decree restricting GM corn for human consumption was challenged by the US under USMCA dispute settlement panels, illustrating the tension between food sovereignty policies and trade obligations.

### State and Local Food Sovereignty

A growing food sovereignty movement operates at state and local levels. Maine's Constitutional Amendment -- Right to Food (Article I, Section 25, effective November 2, 2021) declares that "all individuals have a natural, inherent and unalienable right to food, including the right to save and exchange seeds and the right to grow, raise, harvest, produce and consume the food of their own choosing for their own nourishment, sustenance, bodily health and well-being." Several Maine municipalities had earlier passed local food sovereignty ordinances, though their legal effect was limited by state preemption until the constitutional amendment.

## Germany

### Legal Framework

Germany operates within the EU's Common Agricultural Policy and regulatory framework for food law, while exercising available national discretion particularly on GMO cultivation restrictions and organic agriculture promotion.

### Common Agricultural Policy Implementation

Germany receives approximately EUR 6.2 billion annually in CAP direct payments (Pillar I) and approximately EUR 2.4 billion in rural development funding (Pillar II). The CAP Strategic Plan (GAP-Strategieplan), submitted under Regulation (EU) 2021/2115, sets national priorities including support for small and medium farms, organic farming expansion (target: 30% of agricultural land by 2030), and enhanced environmental conditionality.

**Redistribution of Payments:** Germany has implemented the redistributive payment (Umverteilungsprämie), providing higher per-hectare payments for the first hectares of each farm, benefiting smaller agricultural holdings and supporting the economic viability of family farming.

### GMO Regulation

**National Opt-Out Under Directive 2015/412:** Germany has exercised the opt-out provision of Directive 2015/412, prohibiting the cultivation of GM crops on its territory. This decision, implemented through the Genetic Engineering Act (Gentechnikgesetz, GenTG), reflects strong public opposition to GM crop cultivation, with opinion polls consistently showing over 80% of German consumers opposing GMO cultivation.

**New Genomic Techniques:** The EU's proposed regulation on plants produced by new genomic techniques (NGTs), adopted in February 2024, would create a differentiated pathway for plants that could also occur naturally or through conventional breeding (Category 1 NGT plants, exempt from GMO regulations) versus those requiring full risk assessment (Category 2). Germany has been a cautious supporter of the regulation while insisting on mandatory labeling and coexistence rules.

### Geographical Indications and Regional Food

Germany benefits extensively from the EU's geographical indication system. German products with Protected Designation of Origin (PDO) or Protected Geographical Indication (PGI) include Bayerisches Bier (PGI), Nürnberger Rostbratwürste (PGI), Spreewälder Gurken (PGI), and Allgäuer Emmentaler (PDO). These designations protect regional food traditions and production methods, serving as a form of food sovereignty within the trade liberalization framework.

### Organic Agriculture

The Organic Agriculture Act (Öko-Landbaugesetz) implements EU Regulation 2018/848 on organic production. Germany is the largest organic food market in Europe, with organic retail sales exceeding EUR 15 billion. The government's target of 30% organic farmland by 2030 (from approximately 11% in 2023) represents a significant food system transformation policy.

### Agricultural Land Protection

The Grundstückverkehrsgesetz (Agricultural Land Transaction Act) restricts the sale of agricultural land to ensure it remains in productive agricultural use and to prevent speculative acquisition by non-agricultural investors. Several Länder have enacted additional restrictions, and there has been ongoing debate about strengthening protections against agricultural land concentration.

## ECHR & EU

### EU Common Agricultural Policy

The CAP, established by Articles 38-44 of the Treaty on the Functioning of the European Union (TFEU), has evolved from a production-focused policy to one increasingly addressing environmental sustainability, food quality, and rural development. The 2023-2027 CAP framework introduces enhanced conditionality (formerly cross-compliance), eco-schemes linking payments to environmental practices, and requirements for CAP Strategic Plans.

### Farm to Fork Strategy

The EU Farm to Fork Strategy, a central component of the European Green Deal (COM/2020/381), establishes targets including: 50% reduction of chemical pesticide use by 2030; 50% reduction of antimicrobial use in farming by 2030; 25% of EU agricultural land under organic farming by 2030; and mandatory origin labeling for certain products.

**Sustainable Food Systems Law:** The European Commission proposed a Framework for Sustainable Food Systems (FSFS) regulation, which would establish sustainability requirements across the food chain, including mandatory sustainability labeling, minimum criteria for sustainable food procurement, and provisions to prevent the externalization of environmental costs.

### Geographical Indications

**Regulation 2024/1143 on Geographical Indications:** This regulation consolidates and updates the EU's GI framework, covering PDO, PGI, and GI for wines and spirits. GIs protect over 3,500 European food and drink products, representing an annual sales value exceeding EUR 80 billion. The system serves food sovereignty objectives by protecting traditional production methods, ensuring fair returns for producers in designated regions, and preventing misappropriation of regional food identities.

### EU Trade Agreements and Agriculture

EU trade agreements increasingly include agricultural safeguards. The Comprehensive Economic and Trade Agreement (CETA) with Canada includes tariff-rate quotas for sensitive products (beef, pork, cheese) and recognizes over 140 EU GIs. The Mercosur agreement (politically agreed 2019, ratification pending) has been particularly contentious due to concerns about deforestation-linked beef imports, illustrating the tension between trade liberalization and food system sustainability.

### WTO Framework

**Agreement on Agriculture:** The WTO Agreement on Agriculture, concluded in the Uruguay Round (1994), disciplines agricultural subsidies through the Aggregate Measurement of Support (AMS) framework, sets tariff reduction commitments, and regulates export competition. The Agreement's "Green Box" provisions (Annex 2) exempt certain payments from reduction commitments, including environmental programs, regional assistance, and public stockholding for food security.

**SPS Agreement:** The Agreement on the Application of Sanitary and Phytosanitary Measures provides the framework within which GMO import restrictions and food safety measures must be justified. The WTO Panel in EC -- Approval and Marketing of Biotech Products (DS291/292/293, 2006) found that the EU's de facto moratorium on GMO approvals (1998-2004) violated SPS obligations, though the ruling did not directly challenge the EU's labeling and traceability requirements.

### ECHR Considerations

While the ECHR does not contain an explicit right to food or food sovereignty, several Convention provisions are relevant:

**Article 1 of Protocol No. 1 (Property):** Agricultural land use restrictions, subsidy reductions, and trade measures affecting farmers may engage property rights protections, though the Court grants states a wide margin of appreciation in economic policy.

**Article 8 (Private and Family Life):** Severe environmental contamination of food production areas may engage Article 8, as established in cases concerning environmental pollution affecting health and livelihoods.

**Article 2 (Right to Life):** In extreme cases, state failure to ensure access to food could engage positive obligations under Article 2, though this has not been directly litigated.

## Comparative Analysis

### Food Sovereignty vs. Food Security

South Korea most clearly embodies food sovereignty principles, maintaining high tariff protection, domestic production support, and restrictive GMO policies. The United States embodies the opposing food security model, emphasizing production efficiency, trade liberalization, and technology (including GMOs) to maximize food availability and affordability. Germany and the EU occupy an intermediate position, combining market orientation with significant protections for domestic agriculture, regional food traditions, and consumer choice on GMOs.

### GMO Regulation Spectrum

The jurisdictions span a wide spectrum on GMO governance. The US applies a permissive, science-based risk assessment framework with minimal labeling. South Korea permits imports but bans cultivation, with mandatory labeling. The EU and Germany require comprehensive labeling, traceability, and permit national cultivation bans, while the new genomic techniques regulation may partially relax this framework. These differences reflect fundamentally different conceptions of the precautionary principle and consumer autonomy.

### Trade Liberalization Tensions

WTO rules constrain food sovereignty policies, as demonstrated by disputes over GMO restrictions, import tariffs, and domestic subsidies. South Korea's rice tariffication illustrates how WTO membership forces compromise between domestic food sovereignty and international trade obligations. The US-Mexico USMCA dispute over GM corn imports demonstrates similar tensions. The EU's approach of embedding agricultural protections within trade agreements (GI recognition, TRQs) represents a sophisticated but imperfect mechanism for reconciling trade and food sovereignty.

### Agricultural Subsidies and Market Distortion

All four jurisdictions provide substantial agricultural support, though structures differ. US commodity subsidies primarily benefit large-scale producers of row crops. The EU CAP has progressively shifted toward environmental conditionality and redistributive payments. South Korea's support focuses on rice and small-farmer viability. Each approach distorts markets in ways that affect food sovereignty in other countries, particularly in the Global South.
