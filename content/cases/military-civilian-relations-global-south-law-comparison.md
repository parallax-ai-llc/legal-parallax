## Summary

Civil-military relations in the Global South are shaped by histories of military intervention in politics, revolutionary armed struggle, colonial military structures, and post-authoritarian transitions that have produced legal frameworks ranging from the formal subordination of the military to civilian authority to constitutionally embedded military prerogatives and persistent extra-constitutional military influence. China's People's Liberation Army (PLA, 中国人民解放军) occupies a constitutionally unique position as the armed forces not of the state but of the Communist Party of China (CPC), with Article 93 of the Constitution establishing the Central Military Commission (CMC, 中央军事委员会) as the body that "directs the armed forces of the country," while the CMC's chairman is simultaneously the CPC's CMC chairman, operationalizing the principle that "the Party commands the gun" (党指挥枪, dang zhihui qiang), a formulation attributed to Mao Zedong's 1938 speech at the Sixth Plenum of the CPC's Sixth Central Committee. India has maintained unbroken civilian control over the military since independence in 1947, institutionalized through constitutional provisions including Article 53 (executive power vested in the President, exercised through the Council of Ministers), the supremacy of the Cabinet Committee on Security (CCS) over the Chiefs of Staff Committee (COSC), and the absence of a formal role for the military in governance, though the establishment of the Chief of Defence Staff (CDS) position in 2019 (on the recommendation of the Kargil Review Committee, 1999) has restructured the military-civilian interface. Nigeria has experienced six successful military coups (1966 January, 1966 July, 1975, 1983, 1985, and 1993) and prolonged military rule (1966-1979, 1983-1999), with the 1999 Constitution (Section 217) establishing the Armed Forces under the command of the President as Commander-in-Chief and Section 218 establishing the National Defence Council, while the transition to civilian rule has been accompanied by persistent military influence in politics and security governance. Brazil's military governed the country for 21 years (1964-1985) through institutional military rule (distinct from personalist military dictatorship), and the 1988 Constitution's treatment of the armed forces reflects a negotiated transition: Article 142 defines the armed forces as "permanent and regular national institutions" whose purpose includes "the defense of the constituted branches of government" (defesa dos poderes constitucionais) -- language that some military officers and scholars interpret as authorizing military intervention to protect constitutional order, a reading contested by most civilian constitutional scholars. The Philippines endured martial law under Ferdinand Marcos (1972-1981, with authoritarian rule continuing until 1986), and the 1987 Constitution contains extensive provisions designed to prevent military intervention, including the civilian supremacy clause (Article II, Section 3), prohibition on military officers holding civil positions (Article XVI, Section 5(4)), and detailed limitations on martial law powers (Article VII, Section 18), though the Philippine military has continued to exercise significant political influence through coup attempts (1987, 1989, 2003, 2007) and counter-insurgency operations under the Internal Security Act. Indonesia's military (TNI, Tentara Nasional Indonesia) institutionalized political participation through the doctrine of dwifungsi (dual function), which assigned the armed forces both a defense role and a socio-political role including reserved seats in the national and regional legislatures, corporate economic activity, and territorial administration paralleling the civilian bureaucracy; the post-Suharto reforms, particularly Law No. 34/2004 on the Indonesian National Armed Forces, formally ended dwifungsi and required the military to withdraw from "practical politics," though military territorial command structures (Kodam, Korem, Kodim, Koramil, Babinsa) continue to parallel civilian administration at every level.

## China (中国)

### The Party-Army Relationship

The PLA's identity as a party army distinguishes China fundamentally from all other states examined here, where the armed forces are constitutionally defined as state institutions. This relationship is articulated through multiple institutional mechanisms:

**The Central Military Commission (CMC, 中央军事委员会):** The Constitution (Articles 93-94) establishes the state CMC, while the CPC Constitution establishes the Party CMC. In practice, these are the same body with identical membership. The CMC Chairman (currently and since 2012, Xi Jinping) exercises direct command authority. The 2015-2016 military reform restructured the CMC from the "four general departments" system (General Staff, General Political, General Logistics, General Armaments) to a "CMC organ, theater command, military service" (军委管总、战区主战、军种主建) structure with 15 CMC functional departments and offices, five theater commands, and the reorganized military services (Army, Navy, Air Force, Rocket Force, Strategic Support Force/Information Support Force).

**Regulations on Political Work in the PLA (中国人民解放军政治工作条例, most recently revised 2020):** These regulations operationalize the Party's leadership over the military, requiring the establishment of Party committees at every level of military organization, from the CMC down to company-level units. The political commissar system (政治委员制度) places a CPC political officer alongside each military commander, with the political commissar exercising authority over ideological education, personnel matters, and internal discipline.

**The 1998-2004 Divestiture:** In 1998, the CMC ordered the PLA to divest from commercial enterprises, following decades of military-run businesses ranging from telecommunications companies to hotels and pharmaceutical factories. The process, largely completed by 2004, aimed to professionalize the military and reduce corruption, though questions about the completeness of divestiture and the persistence of military economic interests through related state-owned enterprises continued.

### National Defence Law (国防法, 1997, revised 2020)

The National Defence Law establishes the legal framework for national defense, including:
- **Article 19 (revised):** The CMC exercises unified leadership and command of the national armed forces.
- **Article 22:** The State Council directs and administers national defence-related work including defense expenditure and budget, defense industry, defense research, and military service.
- **Article 26:** Citizens have the obligation to perform military service and participate in militia organizations under the Military Service Law (兵役法).

### The PLA and Domestic Security

The PLA's role in domestic security has diminished relative to the People's Armed Police (PAP, 中国人民武装警察部队). The 2018 reform transferred PAP command from the State Council and CMC dual-leadership structure to exclusive CMC leadership, while the PAP Law (人民武装警察法, 2020) defines PAP duties including social security, counter-terrorism, maritime security, and emergency response. This restructuring clarified the division between external defense (PLA) and internal security (PAP) while placing both under unified CPC/CMC leadership.

## India (भारत)

### Institutional Civilian Supremacy

India's record of unbroken civilian control over the military is remarkable in the postcolonial context and has been attributed to multiple factors: the Congress Party's establishment of civilian dominance before independence (particularly Nehru's assertion of authority over the first Commander-in-Chief, General K.M. Cariappa), the institutional inheritance of the British Indian Army's apolitical tradition, the bureaucratic IAS (Indian Administrative Service) dominance over the Ministry of Defence, and the deliberate structural weakening of military influence through the abolition of the Commander-in-Chief position after independence and the practice of appointing service chiefs based on seniority rather than merit (thus avoiding politicized selections).

**The Ministry of Defence (MoD):** The MoD, headed by a civilian Defence Minister and staffed overwhelmingly by IAS officers, controls defence policy, procurement, budgeting, and promotions. The service chiefs (Chief of Army Staff, Chief of Naval Staff, Chief of Air Staff) report to the Defence Minister through the Defence Secretary. This arrangement, while ensuring civilian control, has been criticized for creating a "civil-military gap" where civilian bureaucrats without military expertise make defense decisions.

**Chief of Defence Staff (CDS):** The CDS position, created by executive order in 2019, was designed to provide single-point military advice to the government and to promote jointness among the three services. General Bipin Rawat served as the first CDS until his death in December 2021. The CDS serves as permanent chairman of the Chiefs of Staff Committee and heads the Department of Military Affairs within the MoD. However, the CDS is not a commander of the three services and does not have operational command authority, preserving the existing command structure.

**The Armed Forces (Special Powers) Act (AFSPA), 1958/1990:** AFSPA, applicable to areas declared "disturbed" by the central or state government, grants military personnel extraordinary powers including authority to fire upon and use force "even to the causing of death" against persons acting in contravention of prohibitory orders, to arrest without warrant, to enter and search premises without warrant, and immunity from prosecution except with the central government's sanction. The Supreme Court in *Naga People's Movement of Human Rights v. Union of India*, (1998) 2 SCC 109, upheld AFSPA's constitutionality while imposing procedural safeguards. The Act has been the subject of persistent human rights criticism, notably the case of Irom Sharmila, who conducted a 16-year hunger strike (2000-2016) demanding its repeal.

**Extra Police Laws Investigation Team (EPLIT) v. Union of India (2016):** A landmark Supreme Court judgment holding that the armed forces cannot claim immunity from investigation for alleged extrajudicial killings in the northeast, even in areas where AFSPA applies.

### Military and Electoral Politics

Unlike many post-colonial states, India's military does not constitute a significant voting bloc or interest group in electoral politics. Military personnel vote through postal ballots under the Representation of the People Act, and retired military personnel who enter politics do so as individuals rather than as representatives of the military institution. The "One Rank One Pension" (OROP) movement (a campaign by ex-servicemen for equalized pensions) represented a rare instance of organized military-connected political mobilization, resolved through the 2015 OROP notification and subsequent litigation.

## Africa (Nigeria and South Africa)

### Nigeria: The Long Shadow of Military Rule

Nigeria's military has been the dominant political institution for most of the post-independence period, with military governments ruling for approximately 29 of the 65 years between 1960 and 2025.

**Chronology of Military Interventions:**
1. **January 15, 1966:** Major Kaduna Nzeogwu leads a coup; General J.T.U. Aguiyi-Ironsi assumes power.
2. **July 29, 1966:** Northern military officers stage a counter-coup; General Yakubu Gowon assumes power.
3. **July 29, 1975:** General Murtala Muhammed overthrows Gowon; assassinated February 1976, succeeded by General Olusegun Obasanjo.
4. **December 31, 1983:** General Muhammadu Buhari overthrows the Second Republic (President Shehu Shagari).
5. **August 27, 1985:** General Ibrahim Babangida overthrows Buhari.
6. **November 17, 1993:** General Sani Abacha assumes power after the annulment of the June 12, 1993, presidential election won by M.K.O. Abiola.

**The 1999 Transition:** The transition to civilian rule following Abacha's death in 1998 was managed by the military through General Abdulsalami Abubakar's transitional government, which adopted the 1999 Constitution (closely modeled on the 1979 Constitution) and organized elections won by Olusegun Obasanjo -- a former military head of state running as a civilian.

**Constitutional Provisions:**
- **Section 217:** There shall be an Armed Forces of the Federation consisting of an Army, Navy, and Air Force, under the command of the President as Commander-in-Chief.
- **Section 218:** The National Defence Council advises the President on matters relating to the defence of the sovereignty and territorial integrity of Nigeria.
- **Section 219:** The Armed Forces shall not interfere in the political process and shall perform only their constitutional roles.
- **Section 305:** The President may declare a state of emergency, subject to National Assembly approval within two days (if in session) or ten days (if not in session).

**The Oputa Panel (Human Rights Violations Investigation Commission, 2001):** Established by President Obasanjo to investigate human rights violations during military rule (1966-1999), the Oputa Panel heard testimony from victims and perpetrators but lacked enforcement power. The Federal Government never officially accepted its report, and the Supreme Court held in *Fawehinmi v. Babangida* [2003] 3 NWLR (Pt 808) 604 that the Panel could not compel former heads of state to testify.

### South Africa: From Apartheid Security Forces to Democratic Defence

South Africa's transition from apartheid involved the integration of seven armed forces (the South African Defence Force, Umkhonto we Sizwe/MK of the ANC, the Azanian People's Liberation Army/APLA of the PAC, and the defence forces of the four "independent" homelands: Transkei, Bophuthatswana, Venda, and Ciskei) into the South African National Defence Force (SANDF) under the Defence Act 42 of 2002.

**Constitutional Framework (Sections 200-204):**
- **Section 200:** The defence force must be structured and managed as a disciplined military force, subordinate to and functioning under the direction of the civilian authority.
- **Section 201:** Only the President, as head of the national executive, may authorize the employment of the defence force in cooperation with the police service, in defence of the Republic, or in fulfilment of an international obligation.
- **Section 199(4):** The security services must act, teach, and require their members to act in accordance with the Constitution and the law, including customary international law and international agreements binding on the Republic.

**The Truth and Reconciliation Commission (TRC):** Established by the Promotion of National Unity and Reconciliation Act 34 of 1995, the TRC (chaired by Archbishop Desmond Tutu) examined human rights violations committed between March 1, 1960, and May 10, 1994. The TRC's amnesty provisions (Section 20, allowing individual amnesty for politically motivated acts in exchange for full disclosure) were upheld by the Constitutional Court in *Azanian Peoples Organisation (AZAPO) v. President of the Republic of South Africa*, 1996 (4) SA 672 (CC), which held that the right to have justiciable disputes decided by a court (Section 34) did not preclude the legislature from establishing an amnesty process in the service of national reconciliation.

## Latin America (Brazil, Mexico, Colombia)

### Brazil: From Military Rule to Tutelary Democracy

Brazil's military government (1964-1985) was characterized by institutional military rule -- decisions were made collectively by the military high command rather than by a single strongman, and power was transferred through controlled successions among five military presidents.

**Article 142 and the "Moderating Power" Debate:** The 1988 Constitution's Article 142 states: "The Armed Forces, consisting of the Navy, the Army and the Air Force, are permanent and regular national institutions, organized on the basis of hierarchy and discipline, under the supreme authority of the President of the Republic, and are intended for the defense of the Fatherland, for the guarantee of the constitutional branches of government (garantia dos poderes constitucionais), and, on the initiative of any of these, for law and order." The phrase "guarantee of the constitutional branches of government" has been interpreted by some military and conservative actors as a constitutional mandate for the military to intervene in constitutional crises -- a modern version of the imperial-era "moderating power" (poder moderador). The STF addressed this interpretation in ADPF 519 (2020), holding that Article 142 does not authorize "moderating" military intervention and that the armed forces are subject to civilian constitutional authority.

**National Truth Commission (Comissao Nacional da Verdade, CNV, 2012-2014):** Established by Law 12.528/2011, the CNV investigated human rights violations during the period 1946-1988, focusing on the military dictatorship (1964-1985). The CNV documented 434 killings and disappearances, identified 377 perpetrators, and recommended the repeal of the 1979 Amnesty Law (Lei de Anistia, Law 6.683/1979). However, the STF had previously upheld the Amnesty Law in ADPF 153 (2010), a decision that contrasts with the Inter-American Court of Human Rights' ruling in *Gomes Lund v. Brazil* (2010), which held that Brazil's Amnesty Law violated the American Convention on Human Rights.

### Colombia: Military Justice and Transitional Justice

Colombia's extended armed conflict (approximately 1964-2016) created a complex web of civil-military legal issues, including military criminal jurisdiction (fuero penal militar), transitional justice for military personnel, and the integration of former guerrilla combatants.

**Fuero Penal Militar (Article 221):** The Constitution provides that military courts have jurisdiction over offenses committed by members of the security forces in active service and in relation to the service. The Constitutional Court has narrowly interpreted "in relation to the service" in multiple decisions (notably C-358/1997), excluding grave human rights violations (torture, extrajudicial executions, enforced disappearances, sexual violence) from military jurisdiction, requiring their trial in ordinary civilian courts.

**The Special Jurisdiction for Peace (JEP, Jurisdiccion Especial para la Paz):** Established by Legislative Act 01/2017 as part of the peace process, the JEP has jurisdiction over conflict-related offenses committed by FARC members, state agents (including military personnel), and third parties who financed or supported the conflict. Military personnel who acknowledge responsibility before the JEP receive alternative sanctions (restrictions on liberty of 5-8 years in conditions contributing to victims' reparation) rather than imprisonment.

### Mexico: Militarization of Public Security

Mexico presents a paradox: the military has not staged a coup since the 1920s (one of the longest periods of military non-intervention in Latin American history), yet military forces have been increasingly deployed in public security functions. The 2006 "War on Drugs" declared by President Felipe Calderon, the 2019 creation of the Guardia Nacional (National Guard) under military command by President Lopez Obrador, and the 2022 constitutional reform transferring the Guardia Nacional to the Defense Ministry (SEDENA) have progressively militarized public security.

The Supreme Court (SCJN) addressed the constitutionality of military participation in public security in various rulings, while the Inter-American Court of Human Rights in *Alvarado Espinoza v. Mexico* (2018) held that Mexico's military involvement in public security functions violated the American Convention where adequate civilian oversight mechanisms were lacking.

## Southeast Asia (Indonesia and Philippines)

### Indonesia: The End of Dwifungsi

Indonesia's military doctrine of dwifungsi (dual function), formalized under Suharto's New Order (1966-1998), assigned the armed forces (then called ABRI, Angkatan Bersenjata Republik Indonesia) both a defense/security role and a socio-political role. This socio-political role was operationalized through:

- **Reserved legislative seats:** Military/police held 75 of 500 DPR seats and a significant portion of MPR seats.
- **Territorial command structure:** Military commands (Kodam at provincial level, Korem at regional level, Kodim at district level, Koramil at sub-district level, and Babinsa at village level) paralleled civilian administration, creating a shadow governance structure.
- **Military business activities:** The military operated extensive commercial enterprises through foundations (yayasan) and cooperatives, ranging from timber operations to banks.

**Post-Suharto Reform:**
- **MPR Decree VII/2000:** Separated the police (POLRI) from the military (TNI) and ended the military's socio-political role.
- **Law No. 34/2004 on TNI:** Article 47 required the TNI to surrender business activities to the government by October 2009. The reality has been more complex, with some military businesses transferred to state ownership, others privatized, and questions about informal military economic activity persisting.
- **Legislative seats:** Military/police reserved legislative seats were phased out entirely by 2004.
- **The territorial command structure** has been retained as a "defense" rather than "socio-political" function, though its deployment to the village level continues to generate concern about military influence in civilian governance.

**The 1965-66 Mass Killings:** Indonesia has never conducted comprehensive transitional justice for the 1965-66 mass killings (estimated 500,000-1,000,000 deaths) that accompanied Suharto's seizure of power. A National Human Rights Commission (Komnas HAM) investigation (2012) recommended the establishment of an ad hoc human rights tribunal, but the Attorney General's office returned the case file citing insufficient evidence. The 2022 government acknowledgment of "gross human rights violations" represented the first official recognition but did not lead to prosecution.

### Philippines: Martial Law Legacy and Persistent Militarism

The Philippine military has intervened in politics repeatedly since the Marcos era, though not through successful coups:

**Marcos Martial Law (1972-1981/1986):** President Marcos declared martial law through Proclamation No. 1081 (September 21, 1972), invoking the communist and Muslim secessionist threats. The Supreme Court in *Aquino v. Enrile* (G.R. No. L-35546, 1972) initially declined to review the martial law declaration. Martial law was nominally lifted in 1981 but authoritarian rule continued through the 1973 Constitution's Amendment 6, which granted the President legislative powers.

**Post-1986 Coup Attempts:** The military staged at least nine coup attempts against the Aquino and Ramos administrations (1986-1998), the most serious being the December 1989 attempt by the Reform the Armed Forces Movement (RAM) faction, which required U.S. air support to suppress. The 2003 Oakwood Mutiny (led by Navy Lieutenant Senior Grade Antonio Trillanes) and the 2007 Manila Peninsula Incident further demonstrated the military's political inclinations.

**The 2017 Marawi Martial Law:** President Duterte declared martial law in Mindanao (Proclamation No. 216, May 23, 2017) during the siege of Marawi City by ISIS-affiliated Maute Group fighters. The Supreme Court in *Lagman v. Medialdea* (G.R. No. 231658, 2017) upheld the declaration, finding "sufficient factual basis," but asserted the Court's power of review under Article VII, Section 18. Martial law was extended twice (to December 31, 2019), raising concerns about normalization of military rule.

**Republic Act No. 10368 (Human Rights Victims Reparation and Recognition Act, 2013):** This law established the Human Rights Victims' Claims Board to receive, evaluate, and process claims of human rights violations during the Marcos regime (September 21, 1972 to February 25, 1986), funded from the ill-gotten wealth of the Marcos family recovered by the Presidential Commission on Good Government. Over 11,000 claimants received compensation.

## Comparative Analysis

### Party Armies vs. State Armies

The fundamental conceptual divide in Global South civil-military relations is between party armies (where the armed forces serve a political party or movement) and state armies (where the armed forces serve the state as an institution). China's PLA is the clearest example of a party army, but the distinction also illuminates the ANC's historical relationship with MK in South Africa, the CPC's relationship with the PLA as a party army, and the FARC's transformation from an armed insurgency to a political party in Colombia. The challenge of transitioning from party/movement armies to professional state armies has been a central concern in South Africa (SANDF integration), Indonesia (the TNI's transition from dwifungsi), and Colombia (the integration of former FARC combatants).

### Transitional Justice and Military Impunity

The Global South reveals a spectrum of approaches to military accountability for past abuses: South Africa's TRC model (truth for amnesty), Colombia's JEP (reduced sanctions for acknowledgment), Brazil's Amnesty Law (blanket impunity upheld domestically but condemned internationally), Nigeria's Oputa Panel (non-binding investigation), the Philippines' reparation fund approach, and Indonesia's near-total absence of accountability. The tension between peace/stability and justice/accountability remains unresolved in all contexts.

### Militarization of Internal Security

A common trend across the Global South is the deployment of military forces in internal security roles traditionally performed by police: China's PAP, India's AFSPA deployments, Nigeria's Joint Task Force operations in the northeast and Niger Delta, Brazil's military interventions in Rio de Janeiro favelas (federal intervention decree, February 2018), Mexico's Guardia Nacional, Colombia's military operations in conflict zones, Indonesia's territorial command structure, and the Philippine military's counter-insurgency and counter-terrorism operations. This trend blurs the line between defense and policing, raises human rights concerns, and can entrench military influence in civilian governance.
