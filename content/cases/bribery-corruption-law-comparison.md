## Summary

Bribery -- the offering, giving, receiving, or soliciting of something of value to influence the actions of a public official or other person in a position of trust -- is universally condemned across legal systems, yet the scope of criminalization, enforcement mechanisms, and penalties vary significantly by jurisdiction. South Korea addresses bribery through both its Criminal Act (형법, Articles 129-134) and the landmark Improper Solicitation and Graft Act (부정청탁 및 금품등 수수의 금지에 관한 법률), commonly known as the Kim Young-ran Act, which dramatically expanded anti-corruption enforcement beginning in 2016. The United States combats bribery through a layered statutory framework that includes domestic public corruption statutes (18 U.S.C. sections 201 and 1346), the Foreign Corrupt Practices Act (FCPA) targeting bribery of foreign officials, and the Foreign Extortion Prevention Act (FEPA) of 2023. Germany criminalizes bribery under sections 331-335a of the Strafgesetzbuch (StGB), distinguishing between corruption involving lawful official duties and corruption involving unlawful acts. At the supranational level, the OECD Anti-Bribery Convention (1997) provides the first binding international framework targeting supply-side foreign bribery, while the European Union reached agreement in December 2025 on its first comprehensive anti-corruption directive harmonizing criminal definitions and penalties across Member States.

## South Korea

### Legal Framework

South Korea's anti-bribery regime rests on two principal legislative pillars:

1. **Criminal Act (형법), Articles 129-134:** These provisions define the core bribery offenses (뇌물죄). Article 129 criminalizes the acceptance of bribes by public officials, while Article 133 addresses the offering of bribes. Article 130 covers pre-appointment bribery (accepting bribes before assuming office), and Article 131 addresses aggravated bribery where the official acts unlawfully or improperly in exchange for the bribe.

2. **Improper Solicitation and Graft Act (Kim Young-ran Act, 2015):** Proposed by Kim Young-ran, former head of the Anticorruption and Civil Rights Commission, in August 2012, this law was enacted in 2015 and took effect on September 28, 2016. It represents a paradigm shift by criminalizing the receipt of benefits by public officials, journalists, and private school staff without requiring proof of a quid pro quo -- a departure from the Criminal Act's requirement that the benefit be connected to a specific official act.

### Elements of the Offense

Under the Criminal Act, proving bribery (뇌물죄) requires:

1. **Public Official Status (공무원):** The recipient must be a public official or a person deemed equivalent under law.
2. **Acceptance of a Bribe (뇌물수수):** The official must have demanded, accepted, or agreed to accept money, goods, or other benefits.
3. **Connection to Official Duties (직무관련성):** The bribe must relate to the official's duties, though Korean courts have interpreted "duties" broadly to include general supervisory authority.
4. **Intent (고의):** The official must have knowingly accepted the benefit in connection with official duties.

Under the Kim Young-ran Act, the elements are broadened significantly:

- The Act identifies 14 categories of prohibited improper solicitations.
- It imposes strict monetary limits on permissible gifts: meals up to 30,000 won (approximately $23), gifts up to 50,000 won (approximately $38), and congratulatory or condolence money up to 100,000 won (approximately $77).
- Unlike the Criminal Act, the Kim Young-ran Act imposes criminal liability on corporations for employees' violations, unless the corporation can demonstrate that it exercised due care and supervision to prevent the violation.

### Penalties

| Offense | Penalty |
|---------|---------|
| Acceptance of bribes (Article 129) | Up to 5 years imprisonment or suspension from office |
| Aggravated bribery with unlawful act (Article 131) | 1 to 15 years imprisonment |
| Offering bribes (Article 133) | Up to 5 years imprisonment |
| Kim Young-ran Act violations (receipt of prohibited benefits) | Up to 3 years imprisonment or fine up to 5 times the value received |
| Kim Young-ran Act violations (improper solicitation) | Up to 2 years imprisonment or fine up to 20 million won |

### Landmark Cases

**Park Geun-hye Presidential Corruption Scandal (2016-2021):** President Park Geun-hye was impeached in December 2016 and removed from office in March 2017 following revelations that she and her confidante Choi Soon-sil had solicited and received approximately 77.4 billion won from major conglomerates, including Samsung, in exchange for political favors. Samsung heir Lee Jae-yong was convicted of paying bribes to organizations controlled by Choi in exchange for government support for a corporate merger. Park was convicted on charges including bribery, abuse of power, coercion, and leaking state secrets, and was initially sentenced to 24 years imprisonment, later adjusted to 20 years by the Supreme Court. She was pardoned in December 2021 by President Moon Jae-in.

**Lee Myung-bak Corruption Case (2018-2022):** Former President Lee Myung-bak was arrested in March 2018 on charges of bribery, embezzlement, and tax evasion. He was convicted of accepting approximately 5.4 million dollars in bribes from Samsung, including an alleged exchange in which Lee granted a presidential pardon to Samsung chairman Lee Kun-hee (who had been convicted of embezzlement and tax evasion) in return for payments. Lee was also found to have disguised his ownership of the auto-parts company DAS under relatives' names, embezzling 24 billion won from the firm. He was initially sentenced to 15 years imprisonment, which the Supreme Court increased to 17 years on appeal in October 2020. He was pardoned by President Yoon Suk-yeol in December 2022.

## United States

### Legal Framework

The United States employs a multi-layered statutory approach to combating bribery and corruption:

- **Federal Bribery Statute (18 U.S.C. section 201):** Prohibits bribing or offering anything of value to federal public officials to influence an official act, and prohibits public officials from demanding, seeking, receiving, or accepting anything of value in return for being influenced in the performance of an official act.
- **Foreign Corrupt Practices Act (FCPA, 15 U.S.C. sections 78dd-1 et seq.):** Enacted in 1977 following post-Watergate SEC investigations that revealed hundreds of millions of dollars in bribes paid by U.S. companies to foreign officials. The FCPA contains two main provisions: (1) anti-bribery provisions prohibiting payments to foreign officials for the purpose of obtaining or retaining business, and (2) accounting provisions requiring publicly traded companies to maintain accurate books and records and adequate internal accounting controls.
- **Honest Services Fraud (18 U.S.C. section 1346):** Defines "scheme or artifice to defraud" under the mail and wire fraud statutes to include schemes to deprive another of the intangible right of honest services. Following the Supreme Court's decision in Skilling v. United States (2010), this statute has been limited to schemes involving bribes or kickbacks.
- **Foreign Extortion Prevention Act (FEPA, 2023):** Criminalizes the demand-side of foreign bribery by making it a federal crime for foreign officials to demand or accept bribes from entities connected to the United States.

### Elements of the Offense

For a violation of the FCPA's anti-bribery provisions, the prosecution must prove:

1. **Jurisdictional Nexus:** The defendant is a domestic concern, issuer of securities, or any person acting within U.S. territory.
2. **Payment or Offer:** The defendant offered, paid, promised to pay, or authorized payment of money or anything of value.
3. **Foreign Official:** The payment was directed to a foreign official, political party, party official, or candidate for political office (or to any person while knowing it would be passed on to such individuals).
4. **Corrupt Intent:** The payment was made corruptly -- that is, with the intent to induce the foreign official to misuse his or her position.
5. **Business Purpose:** The purpose was to obtain or retain business or to direct business to any person.

For honest services fraud under section 1346:

1. A fiduciary duty is owed (e.g., by a public official to the public).
2. The defendant knowingly devised or participated in a scheme to defraud involving bribery or kickbacks.
3. The defendant acted with intent to defraud.
4. The scheme involved use of the mails or interstate wire communications.

### Penalties

| Offense | Maximum Imprisonment | Maximum Fine |
|---------|---------------------|-------------|
| Federal bribery (section 201) | 15 years | Triple the value of the bribe or $250,000 |
| FCPA anti-bribery (individuals) | 5 years | $250,000 |
| FCPA anti-bribery (corporations) | N/A | $2,000,000 per violation |
| FCPA accounting violations (individuals) | 20 years | $5,000,000 |
| Honest services fraud (section 1346) | 20 years | $250,000 |

### Landmark Cases

**Siemens AG FCPA Settlement (2008):** German industrial conglomerate Siemens AG paid a combined $1.6 billion in fines and disgorgement to U.S. and European authorities after admitting to a decades-long pattern of paying bribes totaling approximately $1.4 billion to government officials across the globe to secure public contracts. This remains one of the largest FCPA enforcement actions in history.

**Odebrecht/Petrobras Scandal (2016):** Brazilian construction firm Odebrecht S.A. and petrochemical company Braskem S.A. pleaded guilty and agreed to pay a combined $3.5 billion in penalties to authorities in the United States, Brazil, and Switzerland in what the DOJ described as the largest foreign bribery case in history. The scheme involved paying approximately $788 million in bribes to government officials in 12 countries.

**McDonnell v. United States (2016):** The Supreme Court unanimously narrowed the definition of "official act" for federal bribery purposes, overturning the conviction of former Virginia Governor Bob McDonnell. The Court held that setting up a meeting, calling another official, or hosting an event does not, standing alone, qualify as an "official act," requiring instead a formal exercise of governmental power on a specific question or matter.

## Germany

### Legal Framework

German anti-bribery law is codified in sections 331-335a of the Strafgesetzbuch (StGB), which distinguish between four core offenses:

- **Section 331 (Vorteilsannahme -- Acceptance of Benefits):** Criminalizes a public official's demand, acceptance, or agreement to accept a benefit for the discharge of official duties. Unlike classic bribery, this offense does not require an agreement to perform a specific unlawful act.
- **Section 332 (Bestechlichkeit -- Venality/Corruptibility):** Criminalizes a public official's demand, acceptance, or agreement to accept a benefit as consideration for performing an official act in violation of official duties. This is the more serious passive bribery offense.
- **Section 333 (Vorteilsgewaehrung -- Granting Benefits):** Criminalizes offering, promising, or granting a benefit to a public official for the discharge of official duties (the supply-side counterpart to section 331).
- **Section 334 (Bestechung -- Bribery):** Criminalizes offering, promising, or granting a benefit to a public official as consideration for performing an act in violation of official duties (the supply-side counterpart to section 332).

### Additional Provisions

- **Section 335 (Aggravated Cases):** Provides for enhanced penalties in serious cases, particularly those involving large-value bribes, continuous bribery, or bribery by holders of public office.
- **Section 335a (Foreign and International Officials):** Extends sections 332 and 334 to cover bribery of foreign and international public officials, implementing Germany's obligations under the OECD Anti-Bribery Convention and the EU Framework Decision on corruption in the private sector.
- **Section 299 StGB (Commercial Bribery):** Separately criminalizes bribery in commercial transactions where an employee or agent of a business demands, accepts, or is offered a benefit in connection with the procurement of goods or commercial services.

### Facilitation Payments

German law does not recognize a facilitation payment exception. Payments of even small amounts to public officials to induce them to perform their duties more quickly (such as expediting customs clearance or visa processing) constitute criminal offenses under sections 331 and 333 StGB for both the recipient and the payer.

### Penalties

| Offense | Penalty |
|---------|---------|
| Acceptance of benefits (Section 331) | Up to 3 years imprisonment or fine |
| Venality/Corruptibility (Section 332) | 6 months to 5 years imprisonment |
| Granting benefits (Section 333) | Up to 3 years imprisonment or fine |
| Bribery (Section 334) | 3 months to 5 years imprisonment |
| Aggravated cases (Section 335) | 1 to 10 years imprisonment; up to 15 years for holders of judicial office |

### Landmark Cases

**Siemens Corruption Scandal (2006-2008):** The Siemens bribery scandal remains the most significant German anti-corruption case. Investigations revealed that Siemens had maintained a systematic, multi-decade practice of bribing foreign officials to win contracts in telecommunications, power generation, transportation, and medical equipment. In Germany, Siemens paid approximately 395 million euros in fines and disgorgement imposed by the Munich public prosecutor. Multiple former executives were prosecuted, including former board member and later Siemens CEO Heinrich von Pierer, who settled with the company for 5 million euros.

**Volkswagen Works Council Bribery Case (2008):** Former Volkswagen personnel director Peter Hartz was convicted of approving approximately 2.6 million euros in improper payments to the head of VW's works council, including funding trips with prostitutes and paying inflated salaries to the works council chief's Brazilian mistress who had been placed on the VW payroll. Hartz received a two-year suspended sentence and a fine of 576,000 euros.

## European Framework

### OECD Anti-Bribery Convention

The Convention on Combating Bribery of Foreign Public Officials in International Business Transactions was signed on December 17, 1997, and entered into force on February 15, 1999. It is the first and only international anti-corruption instrument focused exclusively on the supply side of bribery. As of 2025, the Convention has 46 state parties, including all 38 OECD member countries plus Argentina, Brazil, Bulgaria, Croatia, Peru, Romania, the Russian Federation, and South Africa.

Key features include:

- **Mandatory Criminalization:** Parties must criminalize the bribery of foreign public officials in international business transactions, with penalties comparable to those for domestic bribery.
- **Corporate Liability:** Parties must establish the liability of legal persons (corporations) for foreign bribery, whether criminal, administrative, or civil.
- **Mutual Legal Assistance:** Parties must provide prompt and effective mutual legal assistance in foreign bribery investigations and proceedings.
- **Monitoring Mechanism:** The OECD Working Group on Bribery conducts a rigorous four-phase peer review process evaluating each party's legislation (Phase 1), application of that legislation (Phase 2), enforcement effectiveness (Phase 3), and continued enforcement progress (Phase 4).

### EU Anti-Corruption Framework

The European Union has progressively expanded its anti-corruption legal architecture:

- **1997 Convention on the Fight against Corruption Involving Officials:** Criminalized active and passive corruption of EU and Member State officials.
- **2003 Framework Decision on Combating Corruption in the Private Sector (2003/568/JHA):** Required Member States to criminalize active and passive bribery in the private sector.
- **OLAF (European Anti-Fraud Office):** Established in 1999 to investigate fraud, corruption, and serious misconduct within EU institutions and involving EU funds.
- **EPPO (European Public Prosecutor's Office):** Became operational in June 2021 with authority to investigate and prosecute crimes against the EU's financial interests, including corruption.
- **Proposed Anti-Corruption Directive (2023-2025):** In May 2023, the European Commission proposed the first comprehensive EU-wide anti-corruption directive. In December 2025, Parliament and Council negotiators reached a provisional agreement on the directive, which harmonizes criminal definitions for domestic and foreign bribery (both public and private sector), misappropriation, trading in influence, abuse of functions, obstruction of justice, and enrichment from corruption offenses. The directive sets minimum maximum penalties of four to six years imprisonment depending on offense severity, and Member States will have 36 months to transpose the directive into national law.

### United Nations Convention against Corruption (UNCAC)

Adopted by the UN General Assembly in October 2003 and entering into force in December 2005, UNCAC is the most comprehensive global anti-corruption treaty, with 190 state parties. It covers prevention, criminalization, international cooperation, and asset recovery, and it addresses both public and private sector corruption.

## Comparative Analysis

| Feature | South Korea | United States | Germany | EU/OECD Framework |
|---------|------------|---------------|---------|-------------------|
| **Primary Statute** | Criminal Act Articles 129-134; Kim Young-ran Act | 18 U.S.C. section 201; FCPA; section 1346 | StGB sections 331-335a | OECD Convention; EU Anti-Corruption Directive (2025) |
| **Scope of "Bribery"** | Broad: Kim Young-ran Act covers gifts without quid pro quo | Moderate: FCPA requires corrupt intent and business purpose | Graduated: Distinguishes benefit-taking from duty-violation bribery | Harmonized minimum standards for domestic and foreign bribery |
| **Foreign Bribery** | Covered under FCPA-equivalent provisions | FCPA (supply-side); FEPA 2023 (demand-side) | Section 335a extends to foreign/international officials | OECD Convention mandates criminalization; EU Directive covers foreign bribery |
| **Corporate Liability** | Kim Young-ran Act imposes corporate criminal liability (with due diligence defense) | Yes: corporations face fines up to $2M per FCPA violation | Limited: administrative fines under OWiG; no general corporate criminal liability | OECD Convention requires corporate liability; EU Directive mandates effective sanctions |
| **Facilitation Payments** | Not expressly exempted | Narrow exception under FCPA for "routine governmental action" | Prohibited; no exception | OECD Recommendation discourages; EU Directive does not exempt |
| **Maximum Imprisonment** | 15 years (aggravated bribery) | 20 years (honest services fraud) | 15 years (aggravated, judicial office holders) | Directive sets 4-6 year minimum maximums |
| **Notable Feature** | Kim Young-ran Act's strict monetary gift limits (30,000/50,000/100,000 won) | Extraterritorial reach of FCPA; demand-side criminalization via FEPA | Four-tier graduated offense structure | First binding supply-side-only convention (OECD); first EU harmonized directive (2025) |
| **Whistleblower Protection** | Act on the Protection of Public Interest Reporters (2011) | Dodd-Frank Act whistleblower rewards (10-30% of sanctions over $1M) | Whistleblower Protection Act (Hinweisgeberschutzgesetz, 2023) | EU Whistleblower Directive (2019/1937) |
