## Summary

Closed-circuit television (CCTV) regulation -- the legal framework governing the installation, operation, and use of video surveillance systems in public and private spaces -- has become an increasingly important area of law as camera technology proliferates, facial recognition capabilities advance, and the tension between public safety and privacy rights intensifies. South Korea, operating under the Personal Information Protection Act (개인정보 보호법, PIPA, Act No. 10465, 2011, comprehensively amended 2023) and the Act on the Protection of Personal Information in Public Institutions, regulates CCTV through one of the most detailed statutory frameworks globally, with PIPA Articles 25-25-8 establishing specific rules for the installation of "visual data processing devices" (영상정보처리기기) in public spaces, requiring purpose limitation, public notice, data retention limits, and access controls, while the Constitutional Court (헌법재판소) has addressed the constitutional limits of CCTV surveillance under the right to privacy (Article 17, 사생활의 비밀) and the right to personal information self-determination (개인정보자기결정권). The United States has no comprehensive federal CCTV regulation, with surveillance conducted under the general Fourth Amendment framework as interpreted in cases from Katz v. United States (1967) through Carpenter v. United States (2018), supplemented by scattered state and local ordinances regulating facial recognition technology and government surveillance cameras, with municipalities like San Francisco (2019), Boston (2020), and Portland, Oregon (2020) enacting bans or restrictions on governmental facial recognition. Germany regulates CCTV under the Federal Data Protection Act (Bundesdatenschutzgesetz, BDSG) Section 4 implementing the GDPR, with strict requirements for video surveillance of publicly accessible spaces including a documented balancing of interests, purpose limitation, and prominent signage, and the Federal Commissioner for Data Protection (BfDI) actively enforcing violations with fines reaching millions of euros. The EU framework under the GDPR (Regulation (EU) 2016/679), supplemented by the AI Act (Regulation (EU) 2024/1689) which imposes strict restrictions on real-time biometric identification in publicly accessible spaces, and the ECHR's Article 8 jurisprudence requiring proportionality in state surveillance, collectively represent the most comprehensive regulatory approach to CCTV globally, with the European Data Protection Board (EDPB) providing detailed guidance through Guidelines 3/2019 on the processing of personal data through video devices.

## South Korea

### Legal Framework

South Korea has one of the world's most detailed CCTV regulatory frameworks, embedded within the Personal Information Protection Act (개인정보 보호법, PIPA).

**PIPA Article 25 (Restrictions on Installation and Operation of Visual Data Processing Devices / 영상정보처리기기의 설치·운영 제한):**

1. Visual data processing devices may be installed and operated in publicly accessible areas only for the following purposes: crime prevention and investigation; facility safety and fire prevention; traffic information collection, analysis, and provision; and other purposes specified by Presidential Decree.
2. Installation in spaces where privacy intrusion is likely to occur (bathrooms, changing rooms, hospital rooms, accommodations) is prohibited.
3. Operators must install easily visible signs notifying the public of the presence of cameras, including the purpose, installation location, scope of filming, and contact information.
4. Operators must designate a person responsible for managing the visual data processing system.

**PIPA Article 25-2 (Installation of Visual Data Processing Devices in Public Institutions / 공공기관의 영상정보처리기기 설치):** Public institutions must conduct a prior impact assessment before installing CCTV and must consult with the Personal Information Protection Commission (개인정보보호위원회, PIPC).

**PIPA Article 25-5 (Retention and Destruction / 보관 및 파기):** Recorded data must be retained only for the minimum period necessary to achieve the stated purpose and must be destroyed upon expiration of the retention period. The standard retention period is typically set at 30 days unless longer retention is required for specific investigative purposes.

### Biometric Data and Facial Recognition

**PIPA Article 23 (Restrictions on Processing Sensitive Information / 민감정보 처리 제한):** Biometric data, including facial recognition templates, is classified as sensitive information under PIPA, requiring explicit consent or specific legal authorization for processing. The 2023 PIPA amendments strengthened protections for biometric data processing, requiring separate consent and impact assessments for biometric identification systems.

### Statistics

South Korea has one of the highest CCTV densities globally. According to the PIPC's 2023 report, approximately 1.7 million CCTV cameras are operated by public institutions, with an additional estimated 8-10 million private cameras. Seoul alone has approximately 90,000 publicly operated CCTV cameras.

### Constitutional Court

**Constitutional Court Decision 2017Hun-Ma1356 (2020):** The Court addressed a challenge to police CCTV monitoring in public protest areas, holding that while CCTV surveillance of public gatherings does not per se violate the right to assembly (Article 21) or privacy (Article 17), systematic surveillance specifically targeting protest participants without reasonable suspicion may have a chilling effect on constitutional rights and requires proportionality assessment.

## United States

### Legal Framework

The United States has no comprehensive federal CCTV regulation, relying instead on constitutional principles, scattered federal statutes, and an emerging patchwork of state and local laws.

**Fourth Amendment:** The Fourth Amendment protects against "unreasonable searches and seizures." In Katz v. United States, 389 U.S. 347 (1967), the Supreme Court established the "reasonable expectation of privacy" test. Generally, individuals have no reasonable expectation of privacy in public spaces, meaning government CCTV surveillance of public areas does not constitute a Fourth Amendment "search." However, Carpenter v. United States, 585 U.S. 296 (2018), recognized that comprehensive digital surveillance can constitute a search even in public spaces, creating uncertainty about the Fourth Amendment status of pervasive CCTV with advanced analytics.

**Federal Wiretapping Law (18 U.S.C. Section 2511):** The federal Wiretap Act primarily regulates the interception of oral and electronic communications (audio). Video-only surveillance generally falls outside the Wiretap Act's scope unless accompanied by audio recording. Several state wiretapping laws are broader, covering video surveillance in certain contexts.

### Facial Recognition Regulation

In the absence of federal legislation, state and local governments have led regulation:

| Jurisdiction | Year | Provision |
|-------------|------|-----------|
| Illinois | 2008 | Biometric Information Privacy Act (BIPA) -- requires consent for biometric data collection |
| San Francisco, CA | 2019 | Ban on city agency use of facial recognition technology |
| Oakland, CA | 2019 | Ban on city use of facial recognition |
| Boston, MA | 2020 | Ban on city use of facial recognition |
| Portland, OR | 2020 | Ban on government and private use of facial recognition in places of public accommodation |
| New York City | 2021 | Requires consent for commercial facial recognition in retail establishments |
| Massachusetts | 2021 | Statewide restrictions on government facial recognition use |
| Colorado | 2022 | AI Act includes facial recognition restrictions |

**Illinois BIPA Litigation:** The Illinois Biometric Information Privacy Act has generated extensive litigation with landmark class action settlements:
- **Rosenbach v. Six Flags, 2019 IL 123186 (2019):** Illinois Supreme Court held that a violation of BIPA's informed consent requirement constitutes a concrete injury sufficient for standing, even without additional harm.
- **Meta (Facebook) Settlement (2022):** $650 million settlement for BIPA violations related to facial recognition tagging feature.

### Department of Justice Guidelines

The DOJ has issued guidance on law enforcement use of facial recognition technology, recommending: written policies governing use, training requirements, prohibition on use as sole basis for arrest, and regular auditing of system accuracy. These guidelines are non-binding.

## Germany

### Legal Framework

Germany applies a multilayered regulatory framework combining federal data protection law, the GDPR, and Land (state) police laws.

**BDSG Section 4 (Video Surveillance of Publicly Accessible Areas / Videoüberwachung öffentlich zugänglicher Räume):**

1. Video surveillance of publicly accessible spaces is permissible only to the extent necessary for: (a) the performance of public duties; (b) the exercise of the right to use premises (Hausrecht); or (c) the protection of specifically defined legitimate interests, provided there are no indications that the data subject's overriding legitimate interests are affected.
2. Monitoring must be identified by appropriate measures (visible signage).
3. Data may be processed only for the purposes for which it was collected.
4. Data must be deleted without undue delay once it is no longer necessary for the purpose, typically within 48-72 hours unless specific grounds for longer retention exist.

**GDPR Application:** Video surveillance falls within the scope of the GDPR whenever the recorded images allow for the identification of natural persons. The legal basis for processing is typically Article 6(1)(f) GDPR (legitimate interests) for private operators, requiring a documented balancing of the operator's legitimate interests against the data subjects' rights and freedoms.

### Federal Commissioner for Data Protection (BfDI)

The BfDI and state-level data protection authorities (Landesdatenschutzbeauftragte) actively enforce CCTV regulations. Notable enforcement actions include:

- **Notebooksbilliger.de (2021):** The Lower Saxony data protection authority imposed a EUR 10.4 million fine for operating video surveillance of employees without legal basis and retaining footage for up to 60 days.
- **H&M (2020):** The Hamburg data protection authority imposed a EUR 35.3 million fine for comprehensive employee surveillance at its Nuremberg service center, including monitoring of personal activities and health data.

### Police CCTV and Facial Recognition

German police forces operate CCTV systems under Land police laws. The most controversial application has been automated facial recognition:

**BVerfG (Pending, 2025):** The Federal Constitutional Court is expected to rule on the constitutionality of real-time facial recognition in public spaces, following challenges to pilot programs in several German cities. The Hamburg Administrative Court in 2019 ruled that police use of facial recognition at public events requires a specific legal basis beyond general police powers.

## ECHR / EU

### GDPR and Video Surveillance

The GDPR applies to video surveillance that constitutes processing of personal data (identifiable images). The European Data Protection Board (EDPB) issued Guidelines 3/2019 on processing personal data through video devices, providing detailed guidance:

- **Legal Basis:** Article 6(1)(f) GDPR (legitimate interests) is the most common basis for private CCTV; public authorities typically rely on Article 6(1)(e) (public interest task) with a specific legal basis in national law.
- **Household Exemption:** Article 2(2)(c) GDPR exempts purely personal or household activities. The CJEU in Ryneš v. Úřad (Case C-212/13, 2014) held that a home surveillance camera capturing a public area falls outside the household exemption.
- **Data Protection Impact Assessment (DPIA):** Article 35 GDPR requires a DPIA for systematic monitoring of publicly accessible areas on a large scale.
- **Retention Period:** EDPB recommends that retention of CCTV footage should normally not exceed 72 hours, with longer retention requiring specific justification.

### EU AI Act (Regulation (EU) 2024/1689)

The AI Act, adopted on June 13, 2024, introduces significant restrictions on biometric identification in public spaces:

**Article 5(1)(d-h) (Prohibited AI Practices):** The Act prohibits the use of real-time remote biometric identification systems in publicly accessible spaces for law enforcement purposes, except in narrowly defined circumstances: search for specific victims of crime (including missing children); prevention of a specific, substantial, and imminent threat to life or physical safety or genuine, present, or foreseeable terrorist attack; and identification of suspects in specific serious crimes. Even permitted uses require prior authorization by a judicial authority or independent administrative authority.

**Article 5(2):** Post-hoc (retrospective) biometric identification for law enforcement is classified as a high-risk AI system requiring compliance with extensive requirements under Title III of the AI Act.

### ECHR Article 8 Jurisprudence

**Peck v. United Kingdom (Application No. 44647/98, January 28, 2003):** The Court found a violation of Article 8 where a local council released CCTV footage of the applicant's suicide attempt in a public street to the media. The Court held that while filming in a public place does not of itself constitute interference with Article 8, the disclosure and publication of the footage did constitute such interference and was not "necessary in a democratic society."

**Antović and Mirković v. Montenegro (Application No. 70838/13, November 28, 2017):** The Court found a violation of Article 8 where a university installed CCTV in lecture halls to monitor professors' teaching. The Court held that the monitoring constituted an interference with the applicants' private life, as the concept of "private life" extends to professional activities and interactions with others in a public context.

**P.G. and J.H. v. United Kingdom (Application No. 44787/98, September 25, 2001):** The Court addressed covert police surveillance involving listening devices, establishing that Article 8 requires that surveillance measures be "in accordance with the law," pursue a legitimate aim, and be "necessary in a democratic society."

## Comparative Analysis

### Regulatory Density

South Korea and the EU/Germany have the most detailed CCTV regulatory frameworks, with specific statutory provisions addressing notice, retention, access, and purpose limitation. The United States has the least comprehensive framework, relying primarily on constitutional principles that offer limited protection for surveillance in public spaces.

### Facial Recognition

The treatment of facial recognition technology reveals the sharpest regulatory divergence. The EU AI Act imposes a near-prohibition on real-time biometric identification in public spaces, with narrow law enforcement exceptions. South Korea classifies biometric data as sensitive information requiring heightened protections. Germany applies strict GDPR-based regulation. The United States has no federal regulation, with a patchwork of municipal bans creating an inconsistent landscape.

### Private vs. Public CCTV

All jurisdictions distinguish between government and private CCTV, but to different degrees. The EU/Germany apply the GDPR uniformly to both government and private CCTV. South Korea's PIPA similarly applies to all operators. The United States provides Fourth Amendment protection only against government surveillance, with private CCTV subject primarily to state wiretapping and privacy tort laws.

### Enforcement

Germany and the EU have the most effective enforcement mechanisms through data protection authorities with GDPR fine powers (up to EUR 20 million or 4% of global turnover). South Korea's PIPC has authority to impose fines and corrective orders. U.S. enforcement is fragmented, with the FTC's Section 5 authority providing limited oversight and BIPA's private right of action creating a distinct enforcement model in Illinois.
