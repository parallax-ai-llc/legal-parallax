## Summary

Business formation -- the legal process of creating a recognized business entity with defined rights, obligations, and liability protections -- is the gateway to commercial activity and varies significantly across jurisdictions in the types of entities available, formation requirements, minimum capital rules, and regulatory oversight. South Korea provides several entity types under the Commercial Code (상법), with the stock corporation (주식회사, Jusikhoesa) as the predominant form for larger businesses and the limited liability company (유한회사 and 유한책임회사) as alternatives, requiring registration with the court registry office and compliance with minimum capital requirements that were eliminated for 주식회사 in the 2009 Commercial Code amendment. The United States offers an exceptionally diverse range of entity types through state law, including corporations (C-corps and S-corps for tax purposes), limited liability companies (LLCs), partnerships (general, limited, and limited liability), and sole proprietorships, with Delaware and Wyoming being the most popular formation jurisdictions due to favorable statutory frameworks and specialized courts, and the LLC emerging as the dominant entity choice for new businesses since the IRS's "check-the-box" regulations of 1997. Germany provides entity types including the Gesellschaft mit beschränkter Haftung (GmbH, the most common limited liability form), Aktiengesellschaft (AG), Unternehmergesellschaft (UG, the mini-GmbH with EUR 1 minimum capital introduced in 2008), and various partnership forms under the Handelsgesetzbuch (HGB), with formation requiring notarization of the articles of association and registration in the Handelsregister. At the EU level, the European Commission has pursued harmonization through the Company Law Directive (2017/1132), the Digitalisation Directive (2019/1151) mandating online company formation, the proposed European Private Company (Societas Privata Europaea, SPE) which was ultimately withdrawn, and the existing Societas Europaea (SE) Regulation permitting cross-border European corporations.

## South Korea

### Legal Framework

The Commercial Code (상법) provides the legal framework for business entities in Korea, supplemented by sector-specific registration and licensing requirements.

### Entity Types

**주식회사 (Jusikhoesa -- Stock Corporation):** The predominant corporate form, equivalent to a joint-stock company. Since the 2009 amendment eliminated the minimum capital requirement (previously 50 million KRW), the 주식회사 has become accessible to small businesses. Formation requires: articles of incorporation (정관), appointment of at least one director, share subscription, and registration with the competent court registry office (등기소).

**유한회사 (Yuhan Hoesa -- Limited Liability Company):** A simpler entity with fewer governance requirements than the 주식회사. Members' liability is limited to their capital contributions. Less commonly used since the 주식회사 capital requirement was eliminated.

**유한책임회사 (Yuhan Chaegim Hoesa -- Limited Liability Company, LLC-equivalent):** Introduced in the 2012 Commercial Code amendment and modeled on the American LLC, this entity provides maximum contractual flexibility with limited liability. Relatively underutilized compared to the 주식회사 due to lack of familiarity and tax uncertainties.

**합명회사 (Hapmyeong Hoesa -- General Partnership):** All partners bear unlimited liability. Used primarily for professional services firms.

**합자회사 (Hapja Hoesa -- Limited Partnership):** Combines general partners (unlimited liability) with limited partners (liability limited to contributions).

### Formation Process

| Step | Description | Timeline |
|------|-------------|----------|
| 1. Draft articles of incorporation (정관) | Name, purpose, capital, shares, registered office | Day 1 |
| 2. Appoint directors and statutory auditor | Board of directors; statutory auditor (optional for small companies since 2009) | Day 1-2 |
| 3. Subscription and payment for shares | Founders subscribe and pay for initial shares | Day 2-3 |
| 4. Registration with court registry (등기소) | File incorporation documents; receive certificate of registration | Day 3-7 |
| 5. Business registration with tax office | Register with National Tax Service for tax identification number | Day 7-10 |
| 6. Sector-specific registrations | Industry permits, employment insurance, social insurance | Day 10-14 |

### Recent Reforms

The government's "Korean New Deal" initiative and startup support policies have streamlined business formation. The Online Business Formation System (온라인 법인설립 시스템) enables electronic submission of incorporation documents. The 2023 Commercial Code amendments further simplified requirements for small-scale 주식회사, including relaxation of mandatory annual meeting requirements and simplified financial reporting.

## United States

### Legal Framework

Business formation in the United States is governed by state law, with each state offering its own statutes for corporations, LLCs, partnerships, and other entities.

### Entity Types

**Corporation (C-Corp/S-Corp):** Formed under state corporation statutes (e.g., Delaware General Corporation Law). The "C-corp" designation refers to Subchapter C of the Internal Revenue Code (entity-level taxation). Eligible corporations may elect S-corp status under Subchapter S for pass-through taxation (limited to 100 shareholders, single class of stock, domestic shareholders).

**Limited Liability Company (LLC):** The dominant entity for new business formation since the late 1990s. The LLC combines limited liability with pass-through taxation and contractual flexibility. Wyoming enacted the first LLC statute in 1977; all 50 states now have LLC statutes. The Revised Uniform Limited Liability Company Act (RULLCA, 2006, amended 2013) provides a model framework.

**Partnership Forms:** General partnerships (governed by the Revised Uniform Partnership Act, RUPA), limited partnerships (Revised Uniform Limited Partnership Act, RULPA), and limited liability partnerships (LLPs, primarily used by professional firms).

### Delaware Dominance

Delaware remains the preferred jurisdiction for incorporation due to the sophisticated Delaware General Corporation Law, the expertise of the Delaware Court of Chancery, a separate body of case law providing predictable governance standards, and the efficient Division of Corporations (formation in 24 hours, or same-day with expedited service).

### Formation Statistics

As of 2025, the United States has approximately 33.2 million small businesses. LLCs account for over 70% of new entity filings in most states. The IRS "check-the-box" regulations (Treasury Regulation Section 301.7701-3), effective January 1, 1997, which allowed unincorporated entities to elect their tax classification, were instrumental in the LLC's rise to dominance.

### Beneficial Ownership Reporting

The Corporate Transparency Act (CTA, 2021), enacted as part of the National Defense Authorization Act, requires most domestic and foreign entities registered to do business in the United States to report beneficial ownership information to the Financial Crimes Enforcement Network (FinCEN). The requirement, effective January 1, 2024, marks a significant shift from the traditional American approach of minimal entity disclosure.

## Germany

### Legal Framework

German business entities are governed by the Bürgerliches Gesetzbuch (BGB) for civil law partnerships, the Handelsgesetzbuch (HGB) for commercial partnerships, the GmbH-Gesetz (GmbHG) for limited liability companies, and the Aktiengesetz (AktG) for stock corporations.

### Entity Types

**GmbH (Gesellschaft mit beschränkter Haftung):** The most common business entity in Germany, with approximately 1.3 million registered GmbHs. Minimum share capital of EUR 25,000, of which at least half must be paid in before registration. Formation requires notarized articles of association (Gesellschaftsvertrag) and registration in the Handelsregister.

**UG (haftungsbeschränkt) -- Unternehmergesellschaft:** Introduced by the MoMiG reform (Gesetz zur Modernisierung des GmbH-Rechts und zur Bekämpfung von Missbräuchen) in 2008, the UG is a variant of the GmbH with a minimum capital of only EUR 1. The UG must retain 25% of annual net profits until accumulated reserves reach EUR 25,000, at which point it may be converted to a full GmbH. The UG was designed to compete with the British limited company, which was increasingly used by German entrepreneurs following the CJEU's Centros (C-212/97, 1999) and Überseering (C-208/00, 2002) decisions.

**AG (Aktiengesellschaft):** The stock corporation form, requiring minimum capital of EUR 50,000, a mandatory two-tier board structure (Vorstand and Aufsichtsrat), and comprehensive governance requirements under the AktG. Used primarily by larger enterprises and publicly listed companies.

**GmbH & Co. KG:** A distinctive German hybrid form combining a limited partnership (KG) with a GmbH as the sole general partner, providing limited liability for all participants while maintaining partnership tax treatment.

### Formation Process

| Step | Description | Timeline |
|------|-------------|----------|
| 1. Draft articles of association | Must include company name, registered office, objects, capital, shares | Day 1-3 |
| 2. Notarization | Articles notarized before a German notary (Notar) | Day 3-5 |
| 3. Capital contribution | Minimum half of stated capital deposited in company bank account | Day 5-10 |
| 4. Registration application | Filed with Handelsregister at the local Amtsgericht | Day 10-14 |
| 5. Handelsregister entry | Company legally comes into existence upon registration | Day 14-28 |
| 6. Trade registration | Gewerbeanmeldung (trade registration) with local municipality | Day 28-35 |

### Online Formation (2022)

Germany enabled online GmbH formation through the DiRUG (Gesetz zur Umsetzung der Digitalisierungsrichtlinie) in 2022, implementing EU Directive 2019/1151. Notarization can now be conducted via video conference for standard GmbH formations using model articles (Musterprotokoll). This significantly reduces formation time, particularly for straightforward incorporations.

## ECHR & EU

### Company Law Directive (2017/1132)

Directive 2017/1132 consolidates earlier company law directives into a single instrument, covering formation, capital maintenance, mergers, and cross-border conversions for limited liability companies. Key provisions include minimum capital requirements for public limited liability companies (EUR 25,000), rules on creditor protection in capital reductions, and the framework for cross-border mergers and conversions.

### Digitalisation Directive (2019/1151)

Directive 2019/1151 requires member states to enable online formation of companies, online filing of company documents, and online registration of branches. Member states must ensure that the entire formation process can be completed electronically, without the need for physical presence, by August 1, 2021 (extended to August 1, 2023 for some provisions).

### Societas Europaea (SE)

Regulation 2157/2001 establishes the Societas Europaea (SE) as a European public limited liability company. The SE has a minimum capital of EUR 120,000, may adopt either a one-tier or two-tier board structure, and is subject to the employee involvement provisions of Directive 2001/86/EC. Approximately 3,000 SEs have been registered as of 2024, primarily in Germany, Czech Republic, and the Netherlands.

### Freedom of Establishment

The CJEU has established through a series of landmark decisions that companies formed in one member state have the right to operate in other member states under the freedom of establishment (Articles 49 and 54 TFEU):

**Centros Ltd v. Erhvervs- og Selskabsstyrelsen (C-212/97, 1999):** The Court held that Denmark could not refuse to register a branch of a UK-incorporated company even though the company was formed in the UK solely to avoid Danish minimum capital requirements.

**Überseering BV v. Nordic Construction Company Baumanagement GmbH (C-208/00, 2002):** The Court held that Germany must recognize the legal capacity of a company incorporated in the Netherlands, even if the company's actual center of administration is in Germany.

**VALE Építési kft. (C-378/10, 2012):** The Court held that member states must allow cross-border conversions of companies from one member state to another under the freedom of establishment.

## Comparative Analysis

### Minimum Capital Requirements

The trend across jurisdictions is toward elimination or reduction of minimum capital requirements. South Korea eliminated its minimum capital for stock corporations in 2009. The United States has never generally required minimum capital. Germany introduced the EUR 1 UG in 2008 while maintaining EUR 25,000 for the standard GmbH. The EU requires EUR 25,000 for public companies but leaves private company capital to member states.

### Formation Speed and Simplicity

The United States leads in formation speed and simplicity, with Delaware offering same-day formation and most states requiring no notarization. South Korea's electronic formation system enables 3-7 day registration. Germany's traditional notarization requirement adds time and cost, though the 2022 online formation option for GmbHs has improved efficiency. The EU Digitalisation Directive is driving convergence toward faster, simpler formation processes across Europe.

### Entity Choice and Flexibility

The American LLC represents the most flexible business entity, combining limited liability, pass-through taxation, and near-complete contractual freedom in the operating agreement. Germany's GmbH & Co. KG provides similar practical results through a more complex structural arrangement. South Korea's 유한책임회사 was designed to offer American-style LLC flexibility but has not yet achieved widespread adoption.
