## Summary

3D printing (additive manufacturing) -- the process of creating three-dimensional objects from digital files by depositing successive layers of material, encompassing technologies such as fused deposition modeling (FDM), selective laser sintering (SLS), stereolithography (SLA), and bioprinting -- poses multifaceted legal challenges across intellectual property, product liability, firearms regulation, medical device regulation, and consumer safety, as the technology enables individuals and small enterprises to manufacture physical objects that were previously produced only in controlled industrial settings, disrupting traditional assumptions about who is a "manufacturer" for liability purposes, rendering IP enforcement dramatically more difficult, and enabling the production of regulated items including functional firearms, medical implants, and aerospace components outside traditional supply chains. South Korea addresses 3D printing through the Act on Promotion of the 3D Printing Industry (삼차원프린팅산업 진흥에 관한 법률, 2015), the first dedicated 3D printing statute enacted globally, along with application of existing intellectual property laws (Patent Act, Design Protection Act, Copyright Act), firearms regulations under the Act on the Safety Management of Guns, Swords, Explosives, Etc. (총포·도검·화약류 등의 안전관리에 관한 법률), and medical device regulation under the Medical Devices Act (의료기기법). The United States lacks a comprehensive 3D printing statute, relying on existing frameworks including patent law (35 U.S.C.), copyright law (17 U.S.C.), the Undetectable Firearms Act (18 U.S.C. Section 922(p)), FDA regulation of 3D-printed medical devices (guidance issued 2017), and product liability under the Restatement (Third) of Torts: Products Liability; the litigation over 3D-printed firearms culminated in Defense Distributed v. U.S. Department of State, involving ITAR (International Traffic in Arms Regulations) and First Amendment issues. Germany regulates 3D printing within the EU framework, applying the Waffengesetz (WaffG, Weapons Act) to 3D-printed firearms, the Produkthaftungsgesetz (ProdHaftG) and revised EU Product Liability Directive (2024/2853) to 3D-printed products, and EU intellectual property directives; the EU Machinery Regulation (Regulation (EU) 2023/1230) and EU General Product Safety Regulation (Regulation (EU) 2023/988) address safety of 3D-printed goods. The ECHR and EU framework engages 3D printing through the revised Product Liability Directive's expanded definition of "product" encompassing digital manufacturing files, the EU Medical Device Regulation (MDR, Regulation (EU) 2017/745), and ongoing policy debates about whether CAD files constitute "articles" for customs and trade purposes.

## South Korea

### Legal Framework

South Korea is notable for having enacted the world's first dedicated statute on 3D printing, reflecting the government's proactive industrial policy approach to emerging technologies.

**Act on Promotion of the 3D Printing Industry (삼차원프린팅산업 진흥에 관한 법률, 2015):** Enacted in December 2015 and effective from December 2016, this Act focuses primarily on industry promotion rather than regulation. Key provisions:

- **Article 2 (Definitions):** Defines "3D printing" as technology that creates three-dimensional objects by stacking materials based on three-dimensional model data, and "3D printing service" as the provision of 3D printing outputs to others.
- **Article 6 (Comprehensive Plan):** Requires the Ministry of Science and ICT to establish a comprehensive plan for 3D printing industry promotion every three years.
- **Article 10 (Quality Certification):** Establishes a voluntary quality certification system for 3D printing service providers, with the Korean Agency for Technology and Standards (KATS) developing technical standards.
- **Article 14 (Safety Standards):** Requires the development of safety standards for 3D printing products, with emphasis on material safety and structural integrity.

**Intellectual Property:** South Korea's existing IP framework applies to 3D printing, though adaptation to the technology's specific challenges remains incomplete.

- **Patent Act (특허법) Article 127 (Indirect Infringement):** Provides that a person who manufactures, assigns, or imports articles used exclusively for the manufacture of a patented product commits indirect infringement. The question of whether distributing a CAD file constitutes indirect patent infringement -- because the file is used exclusively to manufacture the patented object -- has not been definitively resolved by the courts.
- **Design Protection Act (디자인보호법):** Protects registered industrial designs. The manufacture of a 3D-printed object embodying a registered design constitutes direct infringement. The distribution of CAD files embodying the design may constitute aiding and abetting (방조) under general tort principles.
- **Copyright Act (저작권법):** The Act protects "works of applied art" (응용미술저작물) under Article 2(15), but the threshold for copyright protection of functional objects is high. A CAD file itself may qualify as a protected work if it meets the originality threshold.

### Firearms Regulation

**Act on the Safety Management of Guns, Swords, Explosives, Etc. (총포·도검·화약류 등의 안전관리에 관한 법률):** South Korea has among the world's strictest firearms laws. Article 11 prohibits the manufacture, possession, and carrying of firearms without authorization. The manufacture of firearms using 3D printing technology is subject to the same prohibitions as conventional manufacture, and the penalties are severe: imprisonment of up to ten years (Article 70). There is no exception for "unfinished" or non-functional firearms.

### Medical Device Regulation

**Medical Devices Act (의료기기법):** 3D-printed medical devices (including implants, prosthetics, and surgical guides) are regulated under the existing medical device framework. The Ministry of Food and Drug Safety (MFDS, 식품의약품안전처) issued guidance on 3D-printed medical devices in 2019, establishing:

- **Risk-based classification:** 3D-printed devices are classified under the same risk categories (Class I-IV) as conventionally manufactured devices.
- **Material requirements:** 3D printing materials used in medical devices must meet biocompatibility standards under the MFDS guidelines.
- **Patient-specific devices:** Custom 3D-printed devices (patient-matched implants) are subject to a streamlined approval pathway under Article 26-2 (custom medical devices), though manufacturers must maintain quality management systems.

## United States

### Legal Framework

The United States has no dedicated 3D printing legislation, applying existing legal frameworks to the technology's multifaceted challenges.

**Intellectual Property:**

- **Patent Law (35 U.S.C.):** Direct infringement under Section 271(a) occurs when a person makes, uses, or sells a patented invention without authorization. Manufacturing a 3D-printed object that embodies a patented invention constitutes direct infringement. More complex questions arise regarding indirect infringement: Section 271(b) (inducement) and Section 271(c) (contributory infringement) may reach the distribution of CAD files that enable others to 3D-print patented objects. The distributor of a CAD file may be liable for inducement if they specifically intend to encourage patent infringement, or for contributory infringement if the CAD file has no substantial non-infringing use.
- **Copyright Law (17 U.S.C.):** The Supreme Court's decision in Star Athletica v. Varsity Brands, 580 U.S. 405 (2017) clarified the separability test for copyright protection of useful articles. A design feature of a useful article is copyrightable if it can be perceived as a two- or three-dimensional work of art separate from the useful article. CAD files themselves may be protected as "copies" under Section 101, and unauthorized distribution could constitute infringement. The DMCA (17 U.S.C. Section 512) safe harbor provisions apply to online platforms hosting CAD files.
- **Design Patent (35 U.S.C. Sections 171-173):** Design patents protect ornamental designs of articles of manufacture. Manufacturing a 3D-printed article embodying a patented design constitutes infringement.

### 3D-Printed Firearms

**Undetectable Firearms Act (18 U.S.C. Section 922(p)):** Prohibits the manufacture, importation, sale, shipment, delivery, possession, transfer, or receipt of any firearm that is not detectable by walk-through metal detectors or that does not generate an image distinguishable from other objects when screened by x-ray machines. The Act was enacted in 1988 and reauthorized periodically, with its application to 3D-printed firearms (many of which are plastic) being a central concern.

**Defense Distributed v. U.S. Department of State:** Defense Distributed, founded by Cody Wilson, sought to publish 3D-printable firearm CAD files online. The U.S. Department of State initially blocked publication under ITAR, arguing that the files constituted defense articles whose export required a license. In 2018, the State Department settled the case, effectively allowing publication, but multiple states (led by Washington) obtained injunctions blocking distribution. The case raised fundamental questions about the intersection of the First Amendment, arms export controls, and digital manufacturing technology.

**State Legislation:** Multiple states have enacted laws specifically addressing 3D-printed firearms, including requirements for serial numbers on self-manufactured firearms ("ghost guns"). California AB 857 (2016) requires serial numbers on all self-manufactured firearms, including 3D-printed firearms.

### Product Liability

**Restatement (Third) of Torts: Products Liability (1998):** The central challenge for product liability in the 3D printing context is identifying the "manufacturer" when the end user may have printed the object themselves. Under the Restatement:

- **CAD File Designer:** The designer of the CAD file may be treated as a "product seller" analogous to a designer or engineer whose design is incorporated into a defective product. Section 2 imposes liability for manufacturing defects, design defects, and inadequate warnings.
- **3D Printing Service Provider:** A service bureau that prints objects designed by others may be treated as a "component supplier" under Section 5 or a "seller" of the finished product.
- **Material Supplier:** The supplier of 3D printing materials (filaments, resins, powders) may bear liability for defects in the material that cause the printed object to fail.

### FDA Regulation of 3D-Printed Medical Devices

**Technical Considerations for Additive Manufactured Medical Devices (FDA Guidance, 2017):** The FDA issued comprehensive guidance on the technical considerations for 3D-printed medical devices, addressing device design and manufacturing, process validation, material characterization, and labeling. The guidance applies to devices printed using various additive manufacturing technologies and materials. The FDA has cleared over 200 3D-printed medical devices as of 2025, including spinal implants, knee replacements, and cranial plates.

**Patient-Specific Devices:** The FDA has developed a framework for patient-specific 3D-printed devices, including anatomical models for surgical planning (generally classified as Class II devices) and custom implants (regulated under the custom device exemption in 21 CFR 814.9 or standard premarket pathways).

## Germany

### Legal Framework

Germany regulates 3D printing within the EU regulatory framework, with domestic law providing additional specificity in firearms and product safety.

**Intellectual Property:**

- **Patentgesetz (PatG) Section 10 -- Indirect Patent Infringement (Mittelbare Patentverletzung):** Prohibits offering or supplying means relating to an essential element of the invention to unauthorized persons for use in practicing the invention in Germany, if the supplier knows or it is obvious that such means are suitable and intended for such use. The question of whether a CAD file constitutes such "means" is actively debated in German patent scholarship. The prevailing view is that a CAD file containing the complete design information for a patented product could constitute an essential means, but this has not been confirmed by BGH case law.
- **Urheberrechtsgesetz (UrhG) -- Copyright Act:** German copyright law protects works of applied art (angewandte Kunst) under Section 2(1) No. 4. Following the BGH's decision in Geburtstagszug (I ZR 143/12, 2013), the threshold for copyright protection of applied art is the same as for other works, requiring personal intellectual creation. CAD files may themselves be protected works.
- **Geschmacksmustergesetz/Designgesetz (DesignG):** Registered and unregistered designs are protected under the DesignG (implementing EU Design Directives). Manufacturing a 3D-printed product embodying a protected design infringes the design right.

### Firearms Regulation

**Waffengesetz (WaffG -- Weapons Act):** Germany maintains strict firearms regulation. Section 1(2) defines weapons broadly, including firearms and their essential components. Section 2(1) requires a permit (Waffenbesitzkarte) for possession and a separate license for manufacturing. Unauthorized manufacture of firearms -- including by 3D printing -- is a criminal offense under Section 51 WaffG, punishable by imprisonment of up to five years or, in serious cases, up to ten years. Section 52a specifically addresses the manufacture of firearms from non-metallic materials that are not detectable by standard security screening, implementing obligations under the EU Firearms Directive.

**EU Firearms Directive (Directive (EU) 2021/555):** Article 10a, as amended, requires member states to ensure that essential components of firearms, including those manufactured by 3D printing, are subject to registration and marking requirements. The Directive addresses the challenge of untraceable firearms produced by additive manufacturing.

### Product Liability and Safety

**Produkthaftungsgesetz (ProdHaftG):** As discussed in the context of the revised EU PLD, the ProdHaftG imposes strict liability on manufacturers for defective products. The application to 3D printing raises the question of whether the designer of a CAD file, the operator of a 3D printer, or the material supplier is the "Hersteller" (manufacturer) under Section 4.

**Produktsicherheitsgesetz (ProdSG -- Product Safety Act):** Implements the EU General Product Safety Directive (now replaced by GPSR 2023/988). Products manufactured by 3D printing that are placed on the market must comply with applicable safety requirements. Section 3 requires that products be safe; Section 6 establishes CE marking requirements for products falling under EU harmonization legislation.

**EU General Product Safety Regulation (GPSR, Regulation (EU) 2023/988):** Replacing the General Product Safety Directive from December 2024, the GPSR applies to all consumer products not covered by sector-specific legislation. The GPSR's expanded definition of "manufacturer" and its obligations regarding traceability present challenges for distributed 3D printing, where objects may be manufactured by individuals or small operators who are not traditional manufacturers.

### Medical Device Regulation

**EU Medical Device Regulation (MDR, Regulation (EU) 2017/745):** The MDR, which became fully applicable in May 2021, applies to 3D-printed medical devices. Key considerations:

- **Article 2(3) (Custom-Made Device):** A device specifically made in accordance with a written prescription of a duly qualified medical practitioner for a specific patient. 3D-printed patient-specific implants may qualify, exempting them from certain conformity assessment requirements while still requiring compliance with Annex XIII.
- **Article 5(5) (Health Institution Exception):** Health institutions that manufacture and use 3D-printed devices in-house may be exempt from certain MDR requirements if they meet conditions including the absence of equivalent devices on the market and internal quality management.
- **Notified Body Certification:** 3D-printed devices classified as Class IIa or higher require notified body certification, with the notified body assessing the manufacturing process including additive manufacturing parameters.

## ECHR & EU

### Revised Product Liability Directive (EU) 2024/2853

The revised PLD is particularly significant for 3D printing because of its expanded scope:

- **Article 4(1) -- Definition of "Product":** Includes "software, including AI systems." This has been interpreted by the Commission to encompass digital manufacturing files such as CAD files used for 3D printing. If a defective CAD file leads to a defective 3D-printed product that causes harm, the designer/distributor of the CAD file may be liable as a "manufacturer" under the Directive.
- **Article 4(5) -- Component Definition:** Components and raw materials are included. 3D printing materials (filaments, resins, metal powders) fall within the definition.
- **Article 7(3) -- Provider of a Digital Service:** Where a manufacturer of a defective product is not established in the EU, the authorized representative, importer, or distributor may be held liable. For 3D-printed products manufactured by end users from downloaded CAD files, the platform distributing the files may bear liability.

### EU Machinery Regulation (Regulation (EU) 2023/1230)

Replacing the Machinery Directive 2006/42/EC from January 2027, the new Machinery Regulation applies to 3D printers themselves as machinery and to 3D-printed products that constitute machinery or machinery components. The Regulation requires conformity assessment and CE marking, with specific attention to additive manufacturing processes that may affect the structural integrity of machinery parts.

### Intellectual Property at EU Level

**EU Trade Mark Regulation (Regulation (EU) 2017/1001):** The manufacture of 3D-printed objects bearing registered EU trade marks without authorization constitutes trade mark infringement. Online platforms hosting CAD files of trademarked products may face liability under the Digital Services Act (Regulation (EU) 2022/2065) if they fail to act expeditiously to remove or disable access to infringing files upon receiving notice.

**EU Design Protection:** Regulation (EC) No 6/2002 on Community designs protects both registered and unregistered designs. The 3D printing of a product embodying a protected Community design constitutes infringement under Article 19.

### ECHR Considerations

**Freedom of Expression (Article 10):** The distribution of CAD files, including controversial files such as 3D-printable firearm designs, engages Article 10 freedom of expression. States may restrict such distribution under Article 10(2) if the restriction is prescribed by law, pursues a legitimate aim (public safety), and is necessary in a democratic society. The balancing between information freedom and public safety in the 3D printing context mirrors broader debates about digital content regulation.

**Right to Property (Protocol No. 1, Article 1):** Intellectual property rights are protected as "possessions" under Article 1 of Protocol No. 1. States have a positive obligation to provide adequate protection for IP rights, which in the 3D printing context may require effective enforcement mechanisms against unauthorized reproduction.

## Comparative Analysis

### Legislative Approach

South Korea stands alone in having enacted dedicated 3D printing legislation, though the Act focuses on industry promotion rather than comprehensive regulation. The other jurisdictions apply existing frameworks -- IP, product liability, firearms, medical device -- to 3D printing, creating an adaptation challenge as these frameworks were designed for traditional manufacturing paradigms.

### Product Liability: The Manufacturer Problem

The most significant challenge across all jurisdictions is defining the "manufacturer" in the 3D printing ecosystem. Traditional product liability assumes a professional manufacturer who controls the production process. In 3D printing, the chain may include a CAD file designer, a material supplier, a 3D printer manufacturer, a 3D printing service provider, and an end user who prints the object. The EU's revised PLD, by including digital files within its scope, takes the most progressive approach to extending liability to CAD file designers. U.S. product liability doctrine is flexible enough to reach CAD file designers through design defect theories, but the application remains untested.

### Firearms: Universal Concern, Varied Response

All jurisdictions prohibit the unauthorized manufacture of firearms, including by 3D printing. South Korea and Germany, with their strict firearms regimes, face fewer challenges because private firearms manufacture is already heavily restricted. The United States, with its Second Amendment tradition and state-by-state variation, faces the most complex regulatory landscape, with the Defense Distributed litigation highlighting the tension between digital speech and firearms regulation.

### Medical Devices: Converging Standards

The FDA's 2017 guidance and the EU MDR provide increasingly detailed frameworks for 3D-printed medical devices, with both emphasizing process validation, material characterization, and post-market surveillance. South Korea's MFDS approach follows similar principles. The convergence reflects the technical commonality of the regulatory challenges.

### IP Enforcement: The Digital Challenge

All jurisdictions face the fundamental challenge that 3D printing enables decentralized infringement at scale: a single CAD file can be distributed globally and used to produce infringing objects in millions of homes and small workshops. Existing indirect/contributory infringement doctrines provide partial coverage, but enforcement remains practically difficult.
