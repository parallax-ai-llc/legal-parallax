## Summary

Default judgment -- the judicial determination entered against a party who has failed to appear, respond to a complaint, or otherwise defend against a claim within the time prescribed by law -- serves as a critical procedural mechanism for ensuring that litigation can proceed to resolution even when one party refuses or neglects to participate, while simultaneously raising fundamental concerns about due process, adequate notice, and the protection of absent defendants' rights. South Korea regulates default judgment through the Code of Civil Procedure (민사소송법), which provides for judgments in cases where the defendant fails to submit a responsive pleading (답변서) under Article 257 or fails to appear at oral argument under Article 150(3), with the court treating uncontested factual allegations as admitted; Korean law also provides robust mechanisms for setting aside default judgments through the retrial procedure (재심) under Articles 451-461 and through late appeals (추완항소) under Article 173. The United States governs default judgment through Federal Rule of Civil Procedure 55, which establishes a two-step process -- entry of default by the clerk (Rule 55(a)) followed by default judgment by the court (Rule 55(b)) -- with courts applying the standard articulated in Mullane v. Central Hanover Bank & Trust Co. (339 U.S. 306, 1950) requiring notice "reasonably calculated, under all the circumstances, to apprise interested parties of the pendency of the action" as a constitutional prerequisite; federal courts further apply the factors from Frow v. De La Vega (82 U.S. 552, 1872) and subsequent case law governing when default judgments should be set aside under Rule 55(c) and Rule 60(b). Germany addresses the non-participation of defendants through the Versäumnisurteil (judgment by default, ZPO §§ 330-347), which treats the factual allegations of the appearing party as admitted and permits judgment on that basis, subject to the powerful remedy of Einspruch (opposition, ZPO § 338) that fully reopens proceedings; the German system also imposes strict requirements for proper service (Zustellung, ZPO §§ 166-213) as a prerequisite for any valid default judgment. The ECHR examines default judgments under Article 6(1) of the Convention, requiring that domestic default judgment procedures respect the right of access to court, the right to be heard, and the principle of equality of arms, while the EU's Brussels I Recast Regulation (1215/2012) provides specific protections for defendants in default by requiring verification of adequate service before recognition and enforcement of default judgments across member states under Articles 28 and 45(1)(b).

## South Korea

### Legal Framework

Korean civil procedure provides several mechanisms for addressing a defendant's failure to participate in litigation, reflecting the civil law tradition's emphasis on ensuring that judicial decisions are based on a proper factual and legal foundation even when one party is absent.

**Failure to Submit Answer (답변서 미제출):** Under Article 257 of the Code of Civil Procedure, if the defendant fails to submit a responsive pleading within the prescribed period (typically 30 days from service), the court may proceed to render judgment based on the materials in the record. The 2002 amendments to the Code strengthened this mechanism by establishing mandatory answer deadlines and consequences for non-compliance.

**Deemed Admission (자백간주):** Article 150(3) provides that when a party fails to contest the opposing party's factual allegations, those allegations may be deemed admitted. This provision operates automatically when the defendant fails to appear or respond, allowing the court to treat the plaintiff's factual assertions as established for purposes of rendering judgment.

### Service Requirements

Valid default judgment in Korea requires proof of proper service (송달). The Code of Civil Procedure prescribes multiple service methods:

**Personal Service (교부송달, Articles 178-181):** The primary method, involving delivery of documents directly to the defendant or to a person at the defendant's residence or place of business authorized to receive service.

**Substituted Service (보충송달, Article 186):** When personal service fails, documents may be left with an adult family member or employee at the defendant's residence or workplace.

**Service by Publication (공시송달, Articles 194-196):** When the defendant's whereabouts are unknown, the court may order service by publication, which involves posting a notice at the court and publishing in an official gazette. Service by publication is considered complete two weeks after posting (or in international cases, two months). Korean courts have been criticized for over-reliance on service by publication, which may result in defendants being unaware of proceedings against them.

### Setting Aside Default Judgments

**Late Appeal (추완항소, Article 173):** A party who was unable to comply with a procedural deadline due to circumstances beyond their control may apply for restoration of the missed deadline, including the deadline for filing an appeal against a default judgment. The application must be filed within two weeks of the removal of the obstacle.

**Retrial (재심, Articles 451-461):** A party may seek retrial of a final judgment, including a default judgment, on specified grounds including: service defects that deprived the party of the opportunity to participate; fraud or deception by the opposing party; and newly discovered evidence that could not have been obtained through due diligence. The retrial petition must be filed within 30 days of discovering the ground for retrial.

### Supreme Court Jurisprudence

The Korean Supreme Court has developed significant case law on default judgments. In Decision 2008Da45828 (2009), the Court held that a default judgment rendered after defective service by publication, where the plaintiff knew the defendant's actual address, could be set aside on retrial as a violation of the defendant's right to be heard. In Decision 2015Da238185 (2016), the Court emphasized that even in default situations, the court must independently assess whether the plaintiff's claim is legally well-founded and may not enter judgment on legally deficient claims merely because the defendant failed to appear.

### Constitutional Protections

The Constitutional Court of Korea has held that the right to trial guaranteed by Article 27(1) of the Constitution includes the right to adequate notice and an opportunity to be heard. In Decision 2010Hun-Ma610 (2012), the Court examined whether the service by publication provisions adequately protected defendants' constitutional rights, holding that the provisions were constitutional but must be interpreted narrowly and applied only as a last resort after genuine efforts to locate the defendant.

## United States

### Legal Framework

Default judgment in the United States is governed by Federal Rule of Civil Procedure 55 in federal courts, with analogous state rules in state courts. The procedure involves two distinct steps reflecting the distinction between establishing that the defendant has defaulted and determining the consequences of that default.

### Two-Step Process

**Entry of Default (Rule 55(a)):** When a party has failed to plead or otherwise defend as required by the Rules, and that failure is shown by affidavit or otherwise, the clerk must enter the party's default. This is a ministerial act that establishes the fact of default on the record.

**Default Judgment (Rule 55(b)):** After entry of default, the plaintiff may seek default judgment. If the claim is for a sum certain, the clerk may enter judgment under Rule 55(b)(1). In all other cases, the party must apply to the court under Rule 55(b)(2), and the court may conduct hearings to determine damages, establish the truth of allegations, or investigate any other matter. The court must ensure that the plaintiff's allegations, taken as true, state a legally sufficient claim.

### Constitutional Due Process Requirements

**Mullane v. Central Hanover Bank & Trust Co., 339 U.S. 306 (1950):** The foundational Supreme Court decision establishing that due process requires notice "reasonably calculated, under all the circumstances, to apprise interested parties of the pendency of the action and afford them an opportunity to present their objections." This standard applies to all default judgments and requires actual efforts to provide notice before resorting to constructive service methods.

**Jones v. Flowers, 547 U.S. 220 (2006):** The Court held that when the government becomes aware that its attempt to provide notice has failed (e.g., certified mail is returned unclaimed), due process requires additional reasonable steps to attempt to provide actual notice before taking action affecting the property owner's rights.

### Service of Process

Federal Rule of Civil Procedure 4 governs service of process. Service may be accomplished by personal delivery, leaving copies at the defendant's dwelling with a person of suitable age and discretion, delivery to an authorized agent, or by following state law methods. For international service, the Hague Convention on the Service Abroad of Judicial and Extrajudicial Documents in Civil or Commercial Matters (1965) establishes procedures for service in signatory countries.

### Setting Aside Default and Default Judgment

**Rule 55(c):** The court may set aside an entry of default for good cause and may set aside a final default judgment under Rule 60(b).

**Rule 60(b):** A court may relieve a party from a final judgment for: (1) mistake, inadvertence, surprise, or excusable neglect; (2) newly discovered evidence; (3) fraud, misrepresentation, or other misconduct; (4) the judgment is void; (5) the judgment has been satisfied or is no longer equitable; or (6) any other reason justifying relief.

**Three-Factor Test:** Courts generally consider three factors when evaluating motions to set aside default judgment: (1) whether the default was willful; (2) whether setting aside the default would prejudice the adversary; and (3) whether the defaulting party has presented a meritorious defense. Courts apply a strong policy favoring resolution on the merits, as articulated in Frow v. De La Vega, 82 U.S. 552 (1872).

**Servicemembers Civil Relief Act (50 U.S.C. §§ 3901-4043):** Federal law provides special protections for military service members against default judgments. Under Section 3931, before entering a default judgment against any defendant who has not appeared, the court must require the plaintiff to file an affidavit stating whether the defendant is in military service and, if so, must appoint an attorney to represent the absent service member.

### Eitel Factors

In the Ninth Circuit and other jurisdictions, courts consider the factors from Eitel v. McCool, 782 F.2d 1470 (9th Cir. 1986), when deciding whether to enter default judgment: (1) the possibility of prejudice to the plaintiff; (2) the merits of the plaintiff's substantive claim; (3) the sufficiency of the complaint; (4) the sum of money at stake; (5) the possibility of dispute as to material facts; (6) whether default was due to excusable neglect; and (7) the policy favoring decisions on the merits.

## Germany

### Legal Framework

German civil procedure addresses default through the Versäumnisurteil (judgment by default), a mechanism with deep historical roots in the Germanic procedural tradition and distinct structural features that differentiate it from common law default judgment.

### Versäumnisurteil (ZPO §§ 330-347)

**Prerequisites:** A Versäumnisurteil may be entered when a party fails to appear at the scheduled oral hearing (Termin zur mündlichen Verhandlung) or appears but fails to conduct the oral proceedings (i.e., fails to make substantive submissions). The party must have been properly summoned (ordnungsgemäß geladen) with adequate notice of the hearing date.

**Against the Defendant (ZPO § 331(1)):** When the defendant defaults, the court deems the factual allegations in the plaintiff's claim to be admitted and renders judgment based on those allegations, provided the claim is legally well-founded. The court does not conduct an independent factual investigation but verifies that the alleged facts, taken as true, support the legal claim.

**Against the Plaintiff (ZPO § 330):** When the plaintiff defaults, the defendant may request dismissal of the claim. The court does not enter judgment for the defendant on any counterclaim but merely dismisses the plaintiff's action.

**Second Versäumnisurteil (ZPO § 345):** If a party defaults a second time after the first Versäumnisurteil has been set aside by Einspruch, the court may enter a second Versäumnisurteil that is not subject to further Einspruch, though it may be challenged by appeal (Berufung).

### Service Requirements (Zustellung, ZPO §§ 166-213)

German law imposes rigorous formal requirements for service, reflecting the fundamental principle that no one should be judged unheard (audiatur et altera pars).

**Formal Service (Zustellung von Amts wegen, ZPO § 166):** Service is generally effected by court officials or postal carriers pursuant to court order. The ZPO prescribes specific methods:

- **Personal delivery (ZPO § 177):** Direct delivery to the addressee.
- **Substituted delivery (Ersatzzustellung, ZPO §§ 178-181):** Delivery to an adult household member, an employee at the workplace, or deposit in the mailbox.
- **Public service (Öffentliche Zustellung, ZPO §§ 185-188):** When the defendant's whereabouts are unknown despite diligent search, the court may order service by public posting. This is treated as a last resort and requires the court to verify that the applicant has made genuine efforts to locate the defendant.

### Einspruch (Opposition, ZPO § 338)

The Einspruch is the most distinctive feature of the German default judgment system. It is not an appeal but a procedural objection that completely nullifies the Versäumnisurteil and returns the case to the original court for a hearing on the merits.

**Time Limit:** The Einspruch must be filed within two weeks of service of the Versäumnisurteil (ZPO § 339(1)).

**Effect:** A timely Einspruch returns the proceedings to the status before the default occurred (ZPO § 342). The case proceeds as if the Versäumnisurteil had never been entered, and the defaulting party has full opportunity to present their case.

**Admissibility:** The Einspruch must be filed in proper form and within the time limit. The court rejects the Einspruch as inadmissible if these requirements are not met (ZPO § 341), but does not examine the merits of the defense at this stage.

### Wiedereinsetzung in den vorigen Stand (Reinstatement, ZPO §§ 233-238)

If a party misses the Einspruch deadline due to circumstances beyond their control (ohne sein Verschulden), they may apply for reinstatement within two weeks of the removal of the obstacle (ZPO § 234). The application must set forth the facts establishing that the deadline was missed without fault and must be accompanied by the missed procedural act (i.e., the Einspruch itself).

### Practical Considerations

In practice, the German Versäumnisurteil is used relatively infrequently compared to default judgment in the United States. The availability of the Mahnverfahren (order for payment procedure, ZPO §§ 688-703d) for undisputed monetary claims means that many cases that would result in default judgment in common law systems are instead handled through the more streamlined payment order process. Additionally, German courts' active case management and the duty of judicial guidance (richterliche Hinweispflicht, ZPO § 139) often prevent situations where defendants unknowingly fail to participate.

## ECHR & EU

### ECHR Article 6(1) and Default Judgments

The European Court of Human Rights has examined default judgment procedures under the right to a fair trial, focusing on whether the absent party had adequate notice and a meaningful opportunity to participate.

**Hennings v. Germany (Application No. 12129/86, 1992):** The Court held that Article 6(1) requires that a party be given adequate notice of court proceedings and sufficient opportunity to prepare and present their case. A default judgment entered without adequate notice may violate the right to a fair trial.

**Miragall Escolano and Others v. Spain (Application No. 38366/97, 2000):** The Court held that the right of access to court under Article 6(1) is not absolute but must not be impaired in its very essence. Strict procedural time limits that result in default judgment must serve a legitimate aim and be proportionate.

**Perez de Rada Cavanilles v. Spain (Application No. 28090/95, 1998):** The Court found a violation of Article 6(1) where the applicant's appeal against a default judgment was dismissed on formal grounds without consideration of the merits, holding that excessively formalistic interpretation of procedural rules can violate the right of access to court.

### Right to Be Heard

The ECHR has consistently emphasized that the right to be heard (derived from the adversarial principle within Article 6(1)) requires that default judgments not be entered where the defendant had no actual knowledge of the proceedings and no realistic opportunity to participate. The Court distinguishes between defendants who deliberately choose not to participate (waiver of rights) and those who are unaware of the proceedings through no fault of their own.

**Beer v. Austria (Application No. 30428/96, 2001):** The Court found a violation where a default judgment was entered based on inadequate service, noting that the state has a positive obligation to ensure that service methods are effective in informing the defendant of the proceedings.

### Brussels I Recast (Regulation 1215/2012)

The Brussels I Recast contains specific protections for defendants in default in cross-border cases within the EU:

**Article 28:** Where a defendant domiciled in one member state is sued in another and does not enter an appearance, the court must stay proceedings until it is shown that the defendant was served with the document instituting proceedings in sufficient time to arrange for their defense, or that all necessary steps have been taken to that end.

**Article 45(1)(b):** Recognition of a judgment may be refused if the judgment was given in default of appearance and the defendant was not served in sufficient time and in such a way as to enable them to arrange for their defense, unless the defendant failed to commence proceedings to challenge the judgment when it was possible to do so.

These provisions ensure that default judgments rendered in one EU member state cannot be automatically enforced against defendants in other member states without verification that the defendant received adequate notice.

### Hague Service Convention

The Convention on the Service Abroad of Judicial and Extrajudicial Documents in Civil or Commercial Matters (1965) establishes uniform procedures for cross-border service. Article 15 provides safeguards against default judgments where service was effected abroad: the judge must stay proceedings until it is established that service was effected in a manner prescribed by the Convention and in sufficient time for the defendant to defend. Article 16 permits application to relieve a defendant from the effects of a judgment entered after expiration of the time for appeal if the defendant, without fault, did not have knowledge of the proceedings in time to defend.

### European Enforcement Order (Regulation 805/2004)

For uncontested claims, including those established by default, Regulation 805/2004 permits certification of judgments as European Enforcement Orders for direct enforcement in other member states. However, certification requires that the court of origin verify compliance with minimum procedural standards concerning service of the document instituting proceedings (Articles 13-14) and provision of adequate information about procedural requirements (Article 17).

## Comparative Analysis

### Notice and Service as Constitutional Prerequisites

All four systems treat adequate notice as a fundamental prerequisite for valid default judgments, though the specific standards and methods differ. The U.S. constitutional standard (Mullane) emphasizes reasonableness and actual notice. German law imposes formal, detailed service requirements. Korean law provides graduated service methods with public service as a last resort. The ECHR applies a fact-specific proportionality analysis. The universal principle is that no person should suffer an adverse judgment without having had a meaningful opportunity to learn of and respond to the proceedings.

### Remedies Against Default: Opposition vs. Appeal

The most significant structural divergence lies in the remedies available to defaulting parties. Germany's Einspruch mechanism is uniquely powerful, fully reopening proceedings without requiring any showing of good cause or meritorious defense. The U.S. Rule 60(b) framework requires a showing of excusable neglect and meritorious defense, creating a significantly higher threshold for setting aside default judgments. Korean law provides intermediate mechanisms through late appeals and retrial procedures. The ECHR requires that some effective remedy be available but does not prescribe its form.

### Treatment of Uncontested Facts

The systems differ in how they treat the factual record in default cases. The U.S. takes well-pleaded factual allegations as true but requires independent assessment of damages. Germany deems the appearing party's factual allegations admitted but requires legal sufficiency. Korea similarly treats uncontested allegations as admitted but imposes an independent duty on the court to assess legal merit. All systems reject the notion that default entitles the plaintiff to judgment on any terms, maintaining judicial oversight over the legal sufficiency of claims.

### Cross-Border Default Judgments

The recognition and enforcement of default judgments across borders presents particular challenges, as the enforcing jurisdiction must balance respect for the rendering court's judgment with protection of the absent defendant's fundamental rights. The Brussels I Recast provides the most developed framework, requiring verification of adequate service as a precondition to recognition. The Hague Service Convention provides baseline protections for international service. The practical consequence is that default judgments rendered with defective service are increasingly difficult to enforce across borders.

### Policy Tensions

Default judgment mechanisms embody a fundamental tension between two competing values: the plaintiff's right to obtain relief and the judicial system's need for finality on the one hand, and the defendant's right to be heard and the system's interest in merits-based resolution on the other. The balance struck by each system reflects its broader procedural philosophy: the U.S. favors the plaintiff's interest in efficient enforcement, Germany prioritizes the defendant's right to a hearing through the Einspruch mechanism, Korea maintains an intermediate position, and the ECHR provides supranational oversight ensuring minimum standards of fairness.
