## Summary

Same-sex marriage -- the legal recognition of marriage between two persons of the same sex -- has transformed from a virtually unrecognized concept to a legally established right in dozens of countries over the span of three decades, yet significant global disparities persist in legal recognition, with some jurisdictions granting full marriage equality, others offering civil partnerships or registered partnerships as alternatives, and many continuing to prohibit same-sex unions entirely. South Korea does not recognize same-sex marriage, and the Civil Act (민법) defines marriage implicitly as a union between a man and a woman, though the Supreme Court of Korea (대법원) issued a groundbreaking decision in July 2023 recognizing spousal health insurance benefits for a same-sex partner, and the National Human Rights Commission (국가인권위원회) has repeatedly recommended legal recognition of same-sex partnerships; LGBTQ+ advocacy organizations continue to challenge the exclusion through litigation and legislative proposals. The United States recognized same-sex marriage as a constitutional right in Obergefell v. Hodges, 576 U.S. 644 (2015), where the Supreme Court held 5-4 that the Fourteenth Amendment requires all states to license and recognize same-sex marriages, and subsequently enacted the Respect for Marriage Act in December 2022 to provide statutory protections and require interstate recognition. Germany legalized same-sex marriage through the Gesetz zur Einführung des Rechts auf Eheschließung für Personen gleichen Geschlechts (Marriage Equality Act), which entered into force on October 1, 2017, following a dramatic shift in political support when Chancellor Angela Merkel allowed a free vote in the Bundestag; Germany had previously offered registered life partnerships (Eingetragene Lebenspartnerschaft) since 2001, which the Federal Constitutional Court progressively equated with marriage in a series of decisions. The European Court of Human Rights has held that Article 12 ECHR (right to marry) does not require states to open marriage to same-sex couples (Schalk and Kopf v. Austria, 2010), but has recognized same-sex couples' right to legal recognition of their relationships under Article 8 (Oliari and Others v. Italy, 2015), while the Court of Justice of the European Union has required member states to recognize the immigration and free movement rights of same-sex spouses married in other member states (Coman, C-673/16, 2018).

## South Korea

### Legal Framework

South Korea's Civil Act (민법) does not explicitly define marriage as between a man and a woman, but uses gendered terms (남편/아내, husband/wife) throughout its marriage provisions (Articles 807-833), and administrative practice has consistently interpreted marriage as exclusively heterosexual. Same-sex marriages are not registered by local district offices, and no court has ordered the registration of a same-sex marriage.

### Supreme Court Health Insurance Decision (2023)

On July 20, 2023, the Supreme Court (대법원) issued a landmark decision in the case of So Seong-uk and Kim Yong-min, ruling that the National Health Insurance Service (국민건강보험공단) had unlawfully revoked dependent spousal health insurance coverage from So Seong-uk, who was the partner of Kim Yong-min. The Court held that the couple's relationship qualified as a "de facto marital relationship" (사실혼 관계) for the purpose of health insurance coverage, marking the first time South Korea's highest court recognized a same-sex partnership as conferring any legal rights analogous to marriage.

The decision was narrowly framed, applying only to health insurance benefits and not constituting recognition of same-sex marriage. However, it signaled a potential shift in judicial attitudes and opened the door for future litigation seeking recognition in other areas.

### National Human Rights Commission

The NHRC has issued multiple recommendations calling on the government to enact legislation recognizing same-sex partnerships. In its 2022 recommendation, the NHRC stated that the denial of legal recognition to same-sex couples constitutes discrimination based on sexual orientation in violation of Article 11 of the Constitution and international human rights standards.

### Legislative Prospects

Representative Jung Eui-yong introduced the Life Partnership Act (생활동반자 관계에 관한 법률안) in the National Assembly in 2014, which would have created a registered partnership framework for both same-sex and opposite-sex couples. The bill did not advance. Similar proposals have been introduced in subsequent National Assembly sessions without success, though polling indicates growing public support, particularly among younger demographics.

## United States

### Legal Framework

**Obergefell v. Hodges, 576 U.S. 644 (2015):** The Supreme Court held 5-4 that the Fourteenth Amendment requires all states to license same-sex marriages and to recognize same-sex marriages lawfully performed in other states. Justice Kennedy's majority opinion grounded the right in four principles: individual autonomy in intimate choices, the unique importance of marriage to the committed couple, marriage as safeguarding children and families, and marriage as a keystone of the nation's social order.

**Respect for Marriage Act (RFMA), Pub. L. 117-228 (2022):** Enacted on December 13, 2022, the RFMA repealed the Defense of Marriage Act (DOMA) and requires the federal government and all states to recognize marriages that were valid in the jurisdiction where they were performed. The Act also provides that religious organizations and their affiliated entities are not required to solemnize or celebrate marriages that conflict with their religious beliefs and cannot lose tax-exempt status for declining to do so.

### Post-Obergefell Developments

Following Obergefell, same-sex couples obtained equal rights in all areas governed by marital status under federal and state law, including immigration, taxation, Social Security benefits, adoption, and medical decision-making. The decision prompted ongoing litigation regarding religious exemptions and conscientious objections, addressed in cases such as Masterpiece Cakeshop v. Colorado Civil Rights Commission (2018) and 303 Creative LLC v. Elenis (2023).

## Germany

### Legal Framework

Same-sex marriage has been legal in Germany since October 1, 2017, when the Gesetz zur Einführung des Rechts auf Eheschließung für Personen gleichen Geschlechts entered into force. The law amended Section 1353(1) of the Civil Code (Bürgerliches Gesetzbuch, BGB) to provide: "Marriage is entered into for life by two persons of different or the same sex."

### Eingetragene Lebenspartnerschaft (2001-2017)

Prior to marriage equality, the Lebenspartnerschaftsgesetz (Life Partnership Act, LPartG), enacted in 2001, provided registered life partnerships for same-sex couples. The Federal Constitutional Court progressively expanded the rights of life partners to achieve near-parity with marriage:

**BVerfGE 105, 313 (2002):** Upheld the constitutionality of the LPartG, rejecting arguments that it violated the constitutional protection of marriage under Article 6(1) GG.

**BVerfGE 124, 199 (2009):** Held that survivor's pensions in the public sector must be extended to registered life partners.

**BVerfGE 126, 400 (2010):** Extended inheritance tax benefits to registered life partners.

**BVerfGE 133, 59 (2013):** Held that the exclusion of registered life partners from stepchild adoption violated the right to equal treatment under Article 3(1) GG.

### Legislative Process

The vote on marriage equality on June 30, 2017, was prompted by Chancellor Angela Merkel's announcement that she would permit a "conscience vote" (Gewissensentscheidung), freeing CDU/CSU members to vote according to personal conviction rather than party line. The Bundestag voted 393-226 in favor. Merkel herself voted against but did not contest the result. The Bundesrat approved the legislation on July 7, 2017.

Existing registered life partnerships were not automatically converted to marriages but could be converted through a simple declaration at the registry office. Couples who married under the new law obtained full legal equality, including joint adoption rights.

## ECHR & EU

### ECHR Jurisprudence

**Schalk and Kopf v. Austria (Application No. 30141/04, 2010):** The Court held that Article 12 ECHR does not require states to open marriage to same-sex couples, noting that the reference to "men and women" in Article 12 need not be interpreted as exclusively requiring different-sex couples, but that the question of same-sex marriage falls within the margin of appreciation. The Court also held for the first time that same-sex couples living in a stable relationship fall within the notion of "family life" under Article 8.

**Oliari and Others v. Italy (Application No. 18766/11, 2015):** The Court held that Italy's failure to provide any form of legal recognition for same-sex couples violated Article 8, recognizing a positive obligation to provide a framework for legal recognition of same-sex partnerships. This was the first time the Court found a state obligated to create legal recognition for same-sex couples.

**Fedotova and Others v. Russia (Application No. 40792/10, Grand Chamber, 2023):** The Grand Chamber held that Russia's refusal to provide any form of legal recognition for same-sex couples violated Article 8, confirming that states have a positive obligation to provide a legal framework for recognition and protection of same-sex couples.

### CJEU Jurisprudence

**Coman and Others (C-673/16, 2018):** The CJEU held that the term "spouse" in the Free Movement Directive (2004/38/EC) includes same-sex spouses married in another member state, requiring all member states to recognize such marriages for the purpose of granting derived residence rights, even if the host member state does not permit same-sex marriage under its national law.

**Asociaţia Accept (C-81/12, 2013):** The Court held that homophobic statements by a football club patron could constitute evidence of direct discrimination under the Employment Equality Directive, even absent an identifiable victim.

### Current Status in EU Member States

As of 2026, same-sex marriage is legal in 17 of 27 EU member states: the Netherlands (2001), Belgium (2003), Spain (2005), Sweden (2009), Portugal (2010), Denmark (2012), France (2013), Luxembourg (2015), Ireland (2015), Finland (2017), Germany (2017), Austria (2019), Malta (2017), Estonia (2024), Greece (2024), Andorra (2023, not EU), and Slovenia (2023). Several other member states provide registered partnerships or civil unions without full marriage equality.

## Comparative Analysis

### Pathways to Recognition

The jurisdictions illustrate three distinct pathways: judicial (United States, where the Supreme Court established marriage as a constitutional right), legislative (Germany, where parliament enacted marriage equality through statute), and evolving incremental recognition (South Korea, where judicial and administrative decisions are gradually extending partnership benefits). The ECHR framework has taken a middle path, requiring legal recognition of same-sex partnerships without mandating marriage.

### Relationship Between Intermediate Status and Full Equality

Germany's progression from registered life partnerships (2001) to full marriage equality (2017) illustrates a common pattern whereby intermediate legal recognition facilitates social acceptance and builds political support for full equality. The Netherlands, Denmark, France, and the United Kingdom followed similar trajectories. The U.S. path was more compressed, with some states moving directly from no recognition to marriage through judicial decisions.

### Religious Freedom Tensions

The tension between marriage equality and religious liberty claims is most acute in the United States, where the Respect for Marriage Act includes explicit religious exemptions. Germany's marriage equality law does not include specific religious exemptions but does not require religious communities to perform same-sex marriages. The ECHR framework accommodates both positions through the margin of appreciation.
