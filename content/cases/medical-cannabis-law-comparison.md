## Summary

Medical cannabis (의료용 대마) -- the use of cannabis and its constituent cannabinoids, principally tetrahydrocannabinol (THC) and cannabidiol (CBD), for the treatment of medical conditions under clinical supervision -- occupies one of the most rapidly evolving and jurisdictionally fragmented areas of healthcare and drug policy law. South Korea became the first East Asian country to legalize medical cannabis when the National Assembly amended the Narcotics Control Act (마약류 관리에 관한 법률) in November 2018, permitting the import and use of cannabis-based medicines (대마 성분 의약품) for patients with rare or incurable diseases who obtain approval from the Minister of Food and Drug Safety through the Korea Orphan Drug Center; however, the system remains extremely restrictive, with fewer than 200 patients receiving approval by 2024 and all domestic cultivation prohibited. The United States presents the most complex regulatory landscape, with cannabis remaining a Schedule I controlled substance under the federal Controlled Substances Act (CSA, 21 U.S.C. Sections 801-971) while 40 states, the District of Columbia, and U.S. territories have enacted medical cannabis programs through state law, creating an unprecedented federal-state legal conflict; the DEA proposed rescheduling cannabis from Schedule I to Schedule III in 2024, which would represent the most significant federal policy change since the CSA's enactment in 1970. Germany legalized medical cannabis through the Gesetz zur Änderung betäubungsmittelrechtlicher und anderer Vorschriften (Act Amending the Narcotics Act, March 2017), allowing physicians to prescribe cannabis flowers and extracts for any serious condition, with statutory health insurance reimbursement through the Betäubungsmittelgesetz (BtMG); the Cannabisgesetz (CanG, Cannabis Act, April 2024) further liberalized the framework by partially legalizing recreational use, removing cannabis from the BtMG narcotics schedules, and establishing a separate Cannabis Act. At the international level, the UN Single Convention on Narcotic Drugs (1961) and the Convention on Psychotropic Substances (1971) classify cannabis as a controlled substance, though the UN Commission on Narcotic Drugs voted in December 2020 to remove cannabis from Schedule IV (the most restrictive category) following a WHO recommendation, and the European Court of Human Rights has not recognized a right to access medical cannabis under the Convention but has indicated that states must provide adequate healthcare under Article 8.

## South Korea

### Legal Framework

South Korea's medical cannabis framework is among the most restrictive of any jurisdiction that has legalized medical use, reflecting deeply conservative drug policy traditions.

**Narcotics Control Act (마약류 관리에 관한 법률):** Cannabis (대마) is classified as a narcotic under this Act, with severe criminal penalties for unauthorized possession, use, cultivation, or distribution -- up to 5 years imprisonment for simple possession and up to life imprisonment for trafficking. The November 2018 amendment created a narrow exception for medical use.

### Medical Cannabis Access Process

The 2018 amendment established a highly centralized access system:

1. **Patient Application:** A patient diagnosed with a rare or incurable disease applies through their treating physician to the Korea Orphan Drug Center (한국희귀의약품센터).
2. **MFDS Approval:** The Minister of Food and Drug Safety (식품의약품안전처장) reviews each individual application, considering the medical necessity, the absence of alternative treatments, and the specific cannabis-based product requested.
3. **Import Authorization:** Approved products must be imported from international suppliers (no domestic production is permitted). Products include Epidiolex (cannabidiol for epilepsy), dronabinol (Marinol), and nabilone (Cesamet).
4. **Dispensing:** Approved medicines are dispensed through designated pharmacies under strict record-keeping and monitoring requirements.

### Usage Statistics

The system's restrictiveness is reflected in low utilization:

| Year | Approved Patients (Cumulative) | Primary Conditions |
|------|-------------------------------|-------------------|
| 2019 | ~20 | Epilepsy, nausea from chemotherapy |
| 2021 | ~80 | Epilepsy, chronic pain, cancer |
| 2024 | ~180 | Epilepsy, intractable pain, cancer, ALS |

### Reform Debates

Civil society groups and patient advocacy organizations have called for expanding the medical cannabis framework, including broadening eligible conditions, permitting domestic cultivation, and streamlining the approval process. The Korean Medical Association (대한의사협회) has expressed cautious support for expanded access under medical supervision, while opposing recreational legalization.

## United States

### Legal Framework

The United States presents the most extreme federal-state legal divergence on medical cannabis.

**Controlled Substances Act (CSA, 21 U.S.C. Sections 801-971):** Cannabis remains classified as a Schedule I controlled substance at the federal level, defined as having a high potential for abuse, no currently accepted medical use in treatment, and a lack of accepted safety for use under medical supervision. This classification creates tension with state medical cannabis programs.

**State Medical Cannabis Programs:** As of 2025, 40 states, the District of Columbia, Guam, Puerto Rico, and the U.S. Virgin Islands have enacted comprehensive medical cannabis programs. Key features vary by state:

| Feature | Range Across States |
|---------|-------------------|
| Qualifying conditions | 5-30+ conditions; some states permit physician discretion |
| Patient registry | Required in most states; confidential |
| Possession limits | 1-8 ounces; varies significantly |
| Home cultivation | Permitted in ~18 states with medical cards |
| Dispensary system | Licensed retail dispensaries in all medical states |

### Federal-State Conflict

**Gonzales v. Raich (545 U.S. 1, 2005):** The Supreme Court held that Congress's Commerce Clause authority extends to the regulation of locally grown cannabis consumed entirely within the state. The decision confirmed that federal enforcement authority persists even in states with legal medical cannabis, though as a practical matter, federal enforcement has been minimal.

**Cole Memorandum (2013) and Subsequent Policy:** DOJ guidance has generally deprioritized federal enforcement against state-legal cannabis operations, though this policy has fluctuated across administrations.

### DEA Rescheduling (2024)

In 2024, the DEA proposed rescheduling cannabis from Schedule I to Schedule III, following HHS's recommendation based on an FDA scientific review. Schedule III classification would:
- Acknowledge cannabis has currently accepted medical use.
- Remove federal criminal penalties for state-legal medical cannabis activities.
- Enable pharmaceutical research under standard FDA protocols.
- Apply federal tax deductions currently denied under IRC Section 280E.

The rescheduling process remained pending as of 2026, with public comment and administrative proceedings ongoing.

### FDA-Approved Cannabis Medicines

The FDA has approved several cannabis-derived or cannabis-related medications:
- **Epidiolex (cannabidiol):** Approved 2018 for treatment of seizures associated with Lennox-Gastaut syndrome, Dravet syndrome, and tuberous sclerosis complex.
- **Marinol (dronabinol):** Synthetic THC approved for chemotherapy-induced nausea and AIDS-related anorexia.
- **Syndros (dronabinol):** Liquid formulation of synthetic THC.
- **Cesamet (nabilone):** Synthetic cannabinoid for chemotherapy-induced nausea.

## Germany

### Legal Framework

Germany has undergone the most dramatic transformation in medical cannabis policy among major Western economies, culminating in partial recreational legalization in 2024.

**Medical Cannabis Legalization (March 2017):** The Gesetz zur Änderung betäubungsmittelrechtlicher und anderer Vorschriften, effective March 10, 2017, permitted physicians to prescribe cannabis flowers (Cannabisblüten), cannabis extracts, and dronabinol for patients with serious conditions where conventional therapies have been exhausted or are unsuitable. Critically, statutory health insurance (GKV) must cover the costs with prior approval.

**Cannabisgesetz (CanG, Cannabis Act, April 1, 2024):** The CanG represented a paradigm shift, partially legalizing recreational cannabis use:
- Removed cannabis from the BtMG narcotics schedules (Anlagen I-III).
- Created a separate Cannabis Act governing possession (up to 25g in public, 50g at home), personal cultivation (up to 3 plants), and non-commercial cultivation associations (Anbauvereinigungen).
- Medical cannabis prescription continues under simplified procedures -- no longer requiring BtMG narcotics prescription forms.

### Medical Prescription Framework

Under the current system:
- **Any physician** may prescribe medical cannabis; no specialized authorization is required.
- **Indications:** The physician determines the indication based on clinical judgment; there is no statutory list of qualifying conditions. Common indications include chronic pain, spasticity (particularly in multiple sclerosis), chemotherapy-induced nausea, and appetite loss.
- **Insurance Coverage:** GKV coverage requires prior approval (Genehmigung) from the health insurance fund. The application must demonstrate that standard therapies have been tried and failed or are unsuitable. Approval rates have increased from approximately 60 percent in 2017 to over 75 percent in 2024.

### Cannabis Agency (Cannabisagentur)

BfArM established a Cannabis Agency (Cannabisagentur) to oversee domestic cultivation for medical purposes. Germany awarded cultivation licenses to multiple companies, with domestic production supplementing imports from the Netherlands, Canada, and other countries. By 2024, Germany was the largest medical cannabis market in Europe, with approximately 370,000 prescriptions filled annually.

### International Treaty Compliance

Germany's partial recreational legalization under the CanG has raised questions about compliance with the UN Single Convention on Narcotic Drugs. The German government argued that the CanG is compatible with treaty obligations through a health-oriented regulatory approach, though the International Narcotics Control Board (INCB) expressed concern about the legislation.

## ECHR & EU

### UN Drug Conventions

**Single Convention on Narcotic Drugs (1961, as amended by 1972 Protocol):** Cannabis is listed in both Schedule I (substances subject to all control measures) and Schedule IV (substances also subject to special measures due to particular danger). Article 4(c) requires parties to limit use of scheduled substances to medical and scientific purposes.

**UN CND Vote (December 2020):** The Commission on Narcotic Drugs voted 27-25 (with 1 abstention) to remove cannabis from Schedule IV, following a WHO recommendation. This vote acknowledged cannabis's therapeutic potential while maintaining Schedule I classification. The decision facilitated national-level medical cannabis legislation.

### EU Regulatory Framework

The EU has no harmonized framework for medical cannabis. Key developments:

**No EU-wide Marketing Authorization:** No cannabis-based medicine has received centralized marketing authorization from the EMA (Epidiolex is nationally authorized in several member states). This reflects the regulatory complexity of botanical-derived products and the political sensitivity of cannabis classification.

**Free Movement Issues:** The CJEU addressed cannabis in the free movement context in Case C-663/18, B.S. and C.A. (2020), holding that CBD products lawfully produced in one member state cannot be prohibited by another member state if they do not pose a health risk, applying the free movement of goods principles.

**National Approaches Vary:**

| Member State | Approach |
|-------------|----------|
| Netherlands | Prescription medical cannabis through OMC (Bedrocan program) since 2003 |
| Germany | Full medical legalization (2017); partial recreational (2024) |
| Italy | Medical cannabis permitted; domestic production through military facility |
| France | Medical cannabis trial program (2021); permanent access pathway developing |
| Czech Republic | Medical cannabis permitted since 2013 |

### European Court of Human Rights

The ECHR has not directly addressed a right to access medical cannabis. However, relevant principles include:

**Wenner v. Germany (Application No. 62303/13, Judgment of September 1, 2016):** The Court found a violation of Article 3 where a prisoner with severe opioid dependence was denied access to drug substitution therapy. While not a cannabis case, the judgment established that states have an obligation to provide adequate healthcare, including drug treatment, to persons in their custody.

The Court's Article 8 jurisprudence on personal autonomy and health decisions (Pretty v. United Kingdom, 2002; Haas v. Switzerland, 2011) provides a framework within which medical cannabis access claims could be advanced, though the margin of appreciation in health policy matters remains wide.

## Comparative Analysis

### Scope of Access

Access to medical cannabis varies enormously. Germany provides the broadest access -- any physician may prescribe for any serious condition, with insurance coverage. The United States has extensive state-level programs but persistent federal illegality. South Korea maintains the most restrictive access, limited to rare diseases with individual MFDS approval. The EU lacks harmonization, with member states spanning the full spectrum from prohibition to broad medical access.

### Regulatory Model

South Korea operates a centralized, patient-by-patient approval model similar to a compassionate use/named patient program. The US state programs operate as quasi-regulatory systems with patient registries, licensed dispensaries, and qualifying condition lists. Germany integrates medical cannabis into the standard prescription medication framework. These models reflect different balances between access facilitation and risk control.

### International Treaty Compliance

All jurisdictions operate within the constraints of the UN drug conventions, but with varying interpretations. South Korea's approach is clearly compliant (narrow medical exception). Germany's recreational legalization pushes treaty boundaries. The US's state-level programs create de facto non-compliance that is tolerated internationally. The 2020 UN CND vote loosened the treaty framework but did not eliminate the medical/scientific use limitation.

### Research Barriers

Cannabis's Schedule I status in the US has historically impeded clinical research, creating a paradox where evidence is needed for rescheduling but scheduling makes evidence generation difficult. Germany and other EU member states have fewer barriers to cannabis research. South Korea's restriction to imported products limits clinical research opportunities.
