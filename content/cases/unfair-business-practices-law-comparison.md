## Summary

Unfair business practices law addresses deceptive, unconscionable, and anti-competitive commercial conduct that harms consumers, competitors, or the broader marketplace, encompassing a wide spectrum from false advertising and bait-and-switch tactics to price gouging, pyramid schemes, and predatory commercial behavior. South Korea regulates unfair business practices through the Monopoly Regulation and Fair Trade Act (독점규제 및 공정거래에 관한 법률, commonly known as the Fair Trade Act), the Act on Fair Labeling and Advertising (표시·광고의 공정화에 관한 법률), the Act on the Consumer Protection in Electronic Commerce (전자상거래 등에서의 소비자보호에 관한 법률), and the Unfair Competition Prevention and Trade Secret Protection Act (부정경쟁방지 및 영업비밀보호에 관한 법률), all enforced primarily by the Korea Fair Trade Commission (공정거래위원회, KFTC), which wields substantial administrative power including the authority to impose corrective orders, surcharges of up to 10 percent of relevant revenue, and criminal referrals. The United States combats unfair business practices through Section 5 of the Federal Trade Commission Act (15 U.S.C. Section 45), which prohibits "unfair or deceptive acts or practices in or affecting commerce," supplemented by the Lanham Act Section 43(a) (15 U.S.C. Section 1125(a)) for false advertising, state Unfair and Deceptive Acts and Practices (UDAP) statutes in all 50 states, and sector-specific federal consumer protection laws. Germany addresses unfair commercial practices through the Gesetz gegen den unlauteren Wettbewerb (UWG, Act Against Unfair Competition), comprehensively reformed in 2004 and 2008 to transpose the EU Unfair Commercial Practices Directive, and the Gesetz gegen Wettbewerbsbeschränkungen (GWB, Act Against Restraints of Competition), with enforcement by both competitors and consumer associations through private litigation and by the Bundeskartellamt (Federal Cartel Office) for competition-related unfair practices. The EU has established the most comprehensive supranational framework through the Unfair Commercial Practices Directive 2005/29/EC, which harmonizes rules on misleading and aggressive commercial practices through a combination of a general clause, a prohibition on misleading and aggressive practices, and a "black list" of 31 practices that are unfair in all circumstances, supplemented by the Misleading and Comparative Advertising Directive 2006/114/EC for business-to-business relations.

## South Korea

### Legal Framework

South Korea's unfair business practices regime is among the most comprehensive in Asia, reflecting a regulatory philosophy that prioritizes market fairness and consumer protection through active administrative enforcement.

**Fair Trade Act (독점규제 및 공정거래에 관한 법률):** The cornerstone legislation, originally enacted in 1980 and comprehensively amended in 2020 (effective December 2021), prohibits unfair business practices under Article 45, which enumerates categories including: unfair refusal to deal; discriminatory treatment; unfair exclusion of competitors; unfair solicitation of customers; unfair coercion of trading partners; unfair exploitation of trading position; and interference with business activities. The KFTC has issued detailed enforcement guidelines specifying the types of conduct that fall within each category.

### KFTC Enforcement

The Korea Fair Trade Commission has authority to investigate unfair business practices ex officio or upon complaint, issue corrective orders requiring cessation of unfair practices and publication of corrective measures, impose surcharges (과징금) of up to 10 percent of relevant revenue for violations, and refer cases for criminal prosecution. In 2024, the KFTC imposed approximately 1.2 trillion KRW (roughly $900 million) in total surcharges across all competition and fair trade enforcement actions.

### Fair Labeling and Advertising Act

The Act on Fair Labeling and Advertising (표시·광고의 공정화에 관한 법률), enacted in 1999, specifically addresses deceptive and unfair advertising practices. Article 3 prohibits false or deceptive advertising, exaggerated advertising, advertising that unfairly compares with competitors, and advertising that defames competitors. The KFTC enforces these provisions through corrective advertising orders, publication requirements, and monetary penalties.

### Unfair Competition Prevention Act

The Unfair Competition Prevention and Trade Secret Protection Act addresses a distinct category of unfair business practices focused on misappropriation of business identity and goodwill. Article 2(1) enumerates acts of unfair competition including: use of marks confusingly similar to another's well-known marks; use of marks identical or similar to famous marks; false designation of origin; misleading description of goods; and misappropriation of another's substantial investment in data or creative works (the "general clause" added in 2013 and expanded in subsequent amendments).

### Platform Economy Regulation

South Korea has been at the forefront of regulating unfair practices in the digital platform economy. The Telecommunications Business Act (전기통신사업법) amendments and the proposed Platform Fair Trade Act target unfair practices by dominant online platforms, including preferential treatment of the platform's own products, mandatory use of the platform's payment systems, restrictions on multi-homing by merchants, and exploitation of merchants' data for competitive advantage.

## United States

### Legal Framework

The United States addresses unfair business practices through overlapping federal and state frameworks.

**FTC Act Section 5 (15 U.S.C. Section 45):** The Federal Trade Commission's authority to prohibit "unfair or deceptive acts or practices" is the broadest federal consumer protection tool. The FTC applies two distinct standards:

**Deception Standard (FTC Policy Statement on Deception, 1983):** A practice is deceptive if there is a representation, omission, or practice that is likely to mislead consumers acting reasonably under the circumstances, and the representation, omission, or practice is material -- that is, likely to affect the consumer's conduct or decision with regard to a product or service.

**Unfairness Standard (15 U.S.C. Section 45(n), codified 1994):** A practice is unfair if it causes or is likely to cause substantial injury to consumers, the injury is not reasonably avoidable by consumers, and the injury is not outweighed by countervailing benefits to consumers or competition.

### State UDAP Statutes

All 50 states have enacted Unfair and Deceptive Acts and Practices statutes, providing additional layers of consumer protection:

| Feature | Variation |
|---------|-----------|
| Scope | Some states cover only deception; others also cover unfairness and unconscionability |
| Private right of action | Most states allow; some limit to attorney general enforcement |
| Damages | Many states authorize treble damages; some provide statutory minimum damages |
| Attorney's fees | Most states allow prevailing consumer to recover fees |
| Industry exemptions | Varies; some states exempt regulated industries (insurance, banking, utilities) |
| FTC Act guidance | Some states follow FTC interpretations ("FTC Act states"); others develop independent jurisprudence |

### Lanham Act Section 43(a)

The Lanham Act (15 U.S.C. Section 1125(a)) provides a federal cause of action for false advertising and unfair competition. Unlike FTC Act Section 5, which is enforced only by the FTC, Section 43(a) creates a private right of action for any person (typically a competitor) who believes they are likely to be damaged by false or misleading descriptions or representations of fact in commercial advertising or promotion.

### FTC Enforcement Trends

Recent FTC enforcement has focused heavily on digital marketplace practices, including dark patterns (user interface designs that manipulate consumers into unintended choices), negative option marketing (automatic enrollment in subscription services), data privacy deception, and influencer endorsement disclosure requirements. The FTC's proposed Trade Regulation Rule on commercial surveillance and data security, if finalized, would establish comprehensive unfair practice standards for the collection and use of consumer data.

## Germany

### Legal Framework

Germany's unfair competition law has deep historical roots, with the first Gesetz gegen den unlauteren Wettbewerb (UWG) enacted in 1909.

**UWG (2004/2008):** The current UWG, substantially reformed in 2004 and amended in 2008 to transpose the EU Unfair Commercial Practices Directive 2005/29/EC, provides a comprehensive framework addressing unfair commercial practices in both B2C and B2B contexts.

**Section 3 -- General Clause:** Prohibits unfair commercial practices. A commercial practice is unfair if it is not in conformity with the diligence of a professional and is apt to materially distort the economic behavior of the consumer or the competitor.

**Section 4 -- Examples of Unfair Practices:** Includes exerting pressure through harassment, coercion, or undue influence; exploiting the inexperience, credulity, or fear of consumers; disguising the commercial character of practices; and impeding competitors through targeted obstruction.

**Section 4a -- Aggressive Commercial Practices:** Enacted transposing Articles 8-9 of the UCPD, prohibits practices that use harassment, coercion, or undue influence to materially impair the consumer's freedom of choice, thereby causing the consumer to make a transactional decision they would not have made otherwise.

### Enforcement Mechanism

A distinctive feature of German unfair competition law is its enforcement through private litigation by competitors and qualified associations rather than through administrative enforcement:

**Competitor Standing (Section 8(3) No. 1 UWG):** Any competitor may seek injunctive relief against unfair commercial practices that affect the competitive relationship between the parties.

**Association Standing (Section 8(3) No. 2-4 UWG):** Qualified trade associations, consumer protection associations (Verbraucherzentralen), and chambers of commerce and industry (Industrie- und Handelskammern) have standing to bring injunctive actions. The Wettbewerbszentrale (Center for Protection Against Unfair Competition), a self-regulatory industry body, is the most prolific private enforcer, filing approximately 1,000 legal proceedings annually.

### UWG Annex (Black List)

The Annex to Section 3(3) UWG (Anhang zu § 3 Abs. 3) contains a list of 30 commercial practices that are considered unfair per se, without requiring a case-by-case assessment. These include bait advertising without reasonable stocks, falsely stating that a product will be available only for a limited time, pyramid promotional schemes, false claims of curative properties, and creating the impression that the consumer cannot leave the premises until a contract is concluded.

### Abmahnungssystem (Warning Letter System)

Germany's unfair competition enforcement relies heavily on the Abmahnung (cease-and-desist warning letter) system. Before filing suit, the aggrieved party typically sends a formal Abmahnung demanding that the infringer sign an Unterlassungserklärung (cease-and-desist declaration) with a contractual penalty clause. This system provides fast, efficient enforcement but has been criticized for abuse, particularly by so-called Abmahnvereine (associations that generate revenue from warning letters). The 2020 Gesetz zur Stärkung des fairen Wettbewerbs (Act for Strengthening Fair Competition) imposed restrictions on abusive Abmahnungen.

## ECHR & EU

### Unfair Commercial Practices Directive 2005/29/EC

Directive 2005/29/EC, adopted on May 11, 2005, establishes the EU-wide framework for combating unfair business-to-consumer commercial practices. The Directive achieves full harmonization, meaning member states may not adopt stricter or more lenient provisions within the Directive's scope.

**Three-Tier Structure:**

**General Clause (Article 5):** A commercial practice is unfair if it is contrary to the requirements of professional diligence and it materially distorts or is likely to materially distort the economic behavior of the average consumer with regard to the product.

**Misleading Practices (Articles 6-7):** A practice is misleading if it contains false information or deceives or is likely to deceive the average consumer regarding specified elements (existence or nature of the product, main characteristics, price, need for service or repair, nature of the trader, consumer rights), causing or being likely to cause the consumer to take a transactional decision they would not have taken otherwise. Article 7 addresses misleading omissions.

**Aggressive Practices (Articles 8-9):** A practice is aggressive if it significantly impairs or is likely to significantly impair the average consumer's freedom of choice through harassment, coercion, or undue influence, thereby causing or being likely to cause the consumer to take a transactional decision they would not have taken otherwise.

### Black List (Annex I)

Annex I contains 31 commercial practices considered unfair in all circumstances, divided into 23 misleading practices and 8 aggressive practices. Examples include falsely claiming to be a signatory to a code of conduct, displaying a trust mark without authorization, bait advertising, pyramid schemes, false claims of limited availability, directly exhorting children to buy products, and creating the impression that the consumer has already won a prize.

### CJEU Jurisprudence

**Canal Digital Danmark A/S (Case C-611/14, 2016):** The Court clarified the definition of "invitation to purchase" under the Directive and the information that must be disclosed in commercial communications presenting product characteristics and price.

**Verband Sozialer Wettbewerb eV v. TofuTown.com GmbH (Case C-422/16, 2017):** While primarily a food labeling case, the Court applied unfair commercial practices principles to product designations that mislead consumers about the nature of products.

### Omnibus Directive and Digital Fairness

Directive (EU) 2019/2161 (the "Omnibus Directive") amended the UCPD to address digital marketplace challenges. Key additions include mandatory transparency for online marketplace rankings and the parameters determining them; disclosure of whether and how the trader ensures that published reviews are from consumers who have actually used the product; prohibition of submitting or commissioning fake consumer reviews; and new black list entries for undisclosed paid search result rankings and ticket reselling using automated means.

### Consumer Protection Cooperation Network

Regulation (EU) 2017/2394 on consumer protection cooperation (CPC Regulation) establishes a network of national enforcement authorities that can take coordinated action against cross-border unfair commercial practices. The CPC Network has conducted joint enforcement actions ("sweeps") targeting specific sectors including online travel booking, in-app purchases, and social media influencer marketing.

## Comparative Analysis

### Enforcement Models

The jurisdictions represent three distinct enforcement models for unfair business practices. South Korea relies primarily on administrative enforcement by the KFTC, which combines investigation, adjudication, and penalty-imposing powers in a single agency. The United States employs a hybrid model with FTC administrative enforcement supplemented by state attorney general actions and private rights of action under state UDAP statutes and the Lanham Act. Germany relies predominantly on private enforcement by competitors and qualified associations, with administrative enforcement limited to competition law violations. The EU framework mandates minimum enforcement standards through the CPC Regulation but leaves implementation to member states.

### Consumer Standard

The "consumer" standard used to assess deception differs subtly but significantly. The EU Directive employs the "average consumer who is reasonably well-informed and reasonably observant and circumspect," derived from CJEU case law (Gut Springenheide, Case C-210/96). The U.S. FTC standard focuses on "consumers acting reasonably under the circumstances," which may encompass a broader range of consumer sophistication levels. German law applies the EU average consumer standard but also recognizes protection for particularly vulnerable consumer groups. South Korea's KFTC applies a standard comparable to the average consumer test but with particular attention to power imbalances in B2B relationships.

### Scope of Coverage

The scope of "unfair business practices" varies considerably. The EU Directive applies only to B2C commercial practices and is fully harmonizing within its scope. German law extends beyond the Directive to cover B2B unfair competition, reflecting its historical roots in competitor protection. The U.S. FTC Act covers both B2C and B2B unfair practices. South Korea's Fair Trade Act similarly covers both consumer and business relationships, with particular emphasis on the abuse of superior bargaining position in B2B contexts.
