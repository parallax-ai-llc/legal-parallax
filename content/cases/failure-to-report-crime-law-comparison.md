## Summary

Failure to report crime -- the omission to inform law enforcement authorities of criminal activity of which one has knowledge -- presents fundamental questions about the limits of civic obligation, individual liberty, and the boundaries between moral duty and legal compulsion across different legal traditions. South Korea imposes broad reporting obligations in specific statutory contexts, including mandatory reporting of child abuse under the Child Welfare Act (아동복지법, Article 25-2), domestic violence under the Domestic Violence Prevention Act (가정폭력방지 및 피해자보호 등에 관한 법률), and certain serious crimes under the Criminal Act's provisions on harboring and concealment (형법 제151조, 범인은닉죄), with penalties varying from fines to imprisonment depending on the category of unreported crime. The United States addresses this offense primarily through the federal misprision of felony statute (18 U.S.C. Section 4), which requires not merely knowledge of a felony but also an affirmative act of concealment and carries penalties of up to three years' imprisonment, alongside state mandatory reporting laws that impose specific obligations on designated professionals -- particularly for child abuse, elder abuse, and certain violent crimes -- with Texas being notable for its broad duty-to-report statute under Penal Code Section 38.171. Germany imposes a narrower general reporting obligation under Section 138 of the Strafgesetzbuch (StGB, Nichtanzeige geplanter Straftaten), which criminalizes the failure to report planned -- not past -- serious offenses including murder, terrorism, kidnapping, and certain sexual offenses, with penalties of up to five years' imprisonment, reflecting the German legal tradition's emphasis on the prevention of future harm rather than the punishment of silence about past events. The ECHR has addressed reporting obligations through the lens of Articles 3, 4, and 8, establishing positive obligations on states to maintain systems for identifying and investigating crime, while the EU has imposed specific reporting requirements through instruments such as the Anti-Money Laundering Directives and the Whistleblower Protection Directive 2019/1937.

## South Korea

### Legal Framework

South Korea does not impose a general duty on all citizens to report all crimes. Instead, the legal framework combines general criminal provisions on harboring with targeted mandatory reporting obligations for specific categories of persons and offenses.

**Article 151 (범인은닉·도피, Harboring and Aiding Escape):** "A person who harbors or enables the escape of a person who has committed a criminal offense or who has escaped from custody shall be punished by imprisonment for not more than three years or by a fine not exceeding five million won." Article 151(2) exempts family members: "The preceding paragraph shall not apply to relatives of the offender."

### Mandatory Reporting Obligations

| Statute | Reportable Offense | Mandatory Reporters | Penalty for Failure |
|---------|-------------------|---------------------|-------------------|
| Child Welfare Act (아동복지법) Art. 25-2 | Child abuse or suspected abuse | Teachers, medical professionals, childcare workers, social workers | Fine up to 10 million KRW |
| Domestic Violence Prevention Act | Domestic violence | Medical professionals, counselors, educators | Fine up to 3 million KRW |
| Act on Prevention of Child Sexual Abuse | Sexual abuse of minors | Same as child welfare mandatory reporters | Fine up to 5 million KRW |
| Protection of Youth from Sexual Violence Act | Sexual offenses against youth | Education personnel, welfare professionals | Fine up to 3 million KRW |

### Expansion of Mandatory Reporting

South Korea has significantly expanded mandatory reporting obligations in recent years, driven by high-profile child abuse cases. The 2013 revision of the Child Welfare Act broadened the categories of mandatory reporters and increased penalties for failure to report. The 2020 amendments further enhanced penalties and extended reporting obligations to additional professional categories, including apartment security guards and delivery workers who encounter signs of abuse during their work.

### Judicial Interpretation

The Supreme Court of Korea has held in Decision 2016Do17733 that the mandatory reporting obligation under the Child Welfare Act is triggered by "reasonable suspicion" rather than certainty of abuse, lowering the threshold significantly. The court emphasized that the purpose of mandatory reporting is prevention, not prosecution, and that reporters should err on the side of reporting rather than investigation.

## United States

### Legal Framework

**18 U.S.C. Section 4 (Misprision of Felony):** "Whoever, having knowledge of the actual commission of a felony cognizable by a court of the United States, conceals and does not as soon as possible make known the same to some judge or other person in civil or military authority under the United States, shall be fined under this title or imprisoned not more than three years, or both."

The federal misprision statute requires four elements: (1) a completed felony was committed, (2) the defendant had knowledge of the felony, (3) the defendant failed to notify authorities, and (4) the defendant took some affirmative step to conceal the crime. The fourth element -- affirmative concealment -- is critical: mere silence or failure to volunteer information does not constitute misprision.

### State Mandatory Reporting Laws

All 50 states and the District of Columbia have enacted mandatory reporting laws for child abuse and neglect:

| Category | States with Mandatory Reporting |
|----------|-------------------------------|
| Child abuse (designated professionals) | All 50 states + DC |
| Child abuse (universal reporting) | 18 states (all adults are mandatory reporters) |
| Elder abuse | 48 states (varies in scope) |
| Domestic violence (injury reporting) | Most states require medical professionals to report injuries from violence |
| Violent crimes (general) | Texas (PC § 38.171): broad duty to report felonies |

**Texas Penal Code Section 38.171 (Failure to Report Felony):** Texas is notable for imposing a broad duty to report. The statute provides that a person commits an offense if they observe the commission of a felony resulting in serious bodily injury or death and knowingly fail to immediately report it to a peace officer or law enforcement agency. The offense is a Class A misdemeanor (up to one year jail, $4,000 fine).

### Clergy Reporting Debates

A significant ongoing debate concerns the obligation of clergy to report child abuse disclosed during confession or spiritual counseling. While some states exempt clergy-penitent communications from mandatory reporting requirements, this exemption has faced growing criticism. In 2019, California enacted SB 360, which narrowed but did not eliminate the clergy-penitent privilege in mandatory reporting contexts.

### Self-Incrimination Protection

The Fifth Amendment privilege against self-incrimination provides a constitutional limit on reporting obligations. A person cannot be compelled to report a crime if doing so would tend to incriminate themselves. This privilege applies to both the federal misprision statute and state mandatory reporting laws.

## Germany

### Legal Framework

Germany's approach to failure to report crime is distinctive in its focus on future crimes rather than past ones.

**Section 138 StGB (Nichtanzeige geplanter Straftaten -- Failure to Report Planned Offenses):** "Whoever credibly learns of the planning or commission of [enumerated serious offenses] at a time when the commission or the result can still be averted and fails to report it in time to the authorities or to the threatened person shall be punished with imprisonment for not more than five years or a fine."

### Enumerated Offenses

| Category | Specific Offenses |
|----------|------------------|
| Crimes against the state | Preparation of aggressive war; high treason; treason |
| Crimes against life | Murder; manslaughter |
| Crimes against freedom | Kidnapping; hostage taking |
| Serious property crimes | Robbery; extortion |
| General danger crimes | Arson; explosives; nuclear weapons |
| Terrorism | Section 129a offenses (terrorist organizations) |
| Sexual offenses | Section 176 (sexual abuse of children); added in 2021 |

### 2021 Reform: Child Sexual Abuse Reporting

Following revelations of widespread institutional child sexual abuse in Germany, the Gesetz zur Bekämpfung sexualisierter Gewalt gegen Kinder (Act to Combat Sexual Violence Against Children) of June 2021 expanded Section 138 to include child sexual abuse offenses (Section 176 StGB) among the enumerated offenses that must be reported. This was a significant expansion, as previously only the most serious sexual offenses triggered the reporting obligation.

### Section 139 -- Exemptions and Privileges

Section 139 provides important limitations on the reporting obligation:

**(1)** Clergy are not punishable for failure to report if the offense was communicated during pastoral care (Seelsorge).

**(2)** A person is not punishable if they earnestly endeavored to prevent the offense or to avert the result, and the attempt was objectively suitable.

**(3)** A person is not punishable if reporting would expose themselves or a close relative to criminal prosecution.

### No General Duty to Report Past Crimes

Germany does not criminalize the mere failure to report a past crime. Section 138 applies only where the crime has been planned or where its commission is ongoing and the result can still be averted. This reflects the German legal tradition's distinction between obligations of prevention (Gefahrenabwehr) and obligations to assist prosecution of past events.

## ECHR & EU

### European Court of Human Rights

The ECHR has addressed reporting obligations primarily through the positive obligations doctrine:

**Z and Others v. United Kingdom (Application No. 29392/95, 2001):** The Court found a violation of Article 3 where British social services failed to protect children from severe abuse and neglect despite having knowledge of the situation. The Court established that states have positive obligations to take reasonable measures to prevent inhuman treatment of which they knew or ought to have known.

**O'Keeffe v. Ireland (Application No. 35810/09, 2014):** The Grand Chamber found a violation of Articles 3, 8, and 13 in relation to sexual abuse at a national school. The Court held that Ireland had failed to establish a mechanism for the effective detection and reporting of child sexual abuse in the educational system, establishing that the positive obligation under Article 3 encompasses the duty to create effective reporting mechanisms.

### EU Whistleblower Protection Directive 2019/1937

This Directive establishes EU-wide minimum standards for the protection of persons who report breaches of Union law. While focused on corporate and regulatory violations rather than traditional criminal offenses, the Directive reflects the EU's recognition that reporting obligations must be accompanied by protections against retaliation.

### EU Anti-Money Laundering Directives

The EU's AML framework (currently the Sixth Anti-Money Laundering Directive 2018/1673) imposes extensive reporting obligations on financial institutions, legal professionals, and other "obliged entities," requiring them to report suspicious transactions to Financial Intelligence Units. These reporting obligations are backed by criminal penalties in all member states.

### EU Directive on Combating Child Sexual Abuse (2011/93/EU)

Article 16 requires member states to ensure that professionals whose work involves regular contact with children are trained to identify and report child sexual abuse. The Directive does not mandate criminal penalties for failure to report but requires "effective mechanisms" for reporting.

## Comparative Analysis

### Scope of Reporting Obligation

The jurisdictions differ fundamentally in the scope of reporting obligations:

- **Germany:** Narrowest -- only planned serious offenses, with the intent of preventing future harm.
- **United States:** Federal misprision requires concealment; state mandatory reporting targets specific professions and offense categories.
- **South Korea:** Expanding mandatory reporting for vulnerable populations; general harboring offense for past crimes.
- **ECHR/EU:** Sector-specific obligations; positive state obligations to create reporting mechanisms.

### Family Exemptions

Both South Korea (Article 151(2)) and Germany (Section 139(3)) provide explicit family exemptions from reporting obligations, recognizing the tension between civic duty and family bonds. The United States does not provide a universal family exemption, though spousal privilege may limit testimonial obligations.

### Professional Obligations

All jurisdictions impose heightened reporting obligations on certain professionals, particularly those in contact with children. The categories of mandatory reporters have expanded over time in all jurisdictions, driven by revelations of institutional abuse and the recognition that professional gatekeepers are critical to child protection systems.
