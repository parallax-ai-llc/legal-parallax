## Summary

Capital gains tax -- levied on the profit realized from the sale or disposition of capital assets such as real estate, securities, and business interests -- occupies a contentious position at the intersection of investment policy, revenue generation, and wealth inequality, with jurisdictions taking fundamentally different approaches to its structure, rates, and scope. South Korea imposes capital gains tax (양도소득세) under the Income Tax Act (소득세법) and the Corporate Tax Act (법인세법), with particularly stringent provisions targeting real estate speculation including rates of up to 70 percent on short-term gains from properties in designated speculative areas, alongside a securities transaction tax and a capital gains tax on listed stock gains exceeding 1 billion KRW that was originally scheduled for full implementation in 2023 but was postponed to 2025 and ultimately further deferred amid political debate. The United States taxes capital gains under the Internal Revenue Code with a preferential long-term rate structure (0, 15, or 20 percent for assets held over one year) distinct from ordinary income rates, supplemented by a 3.8 percent Net Investment Income Tax (NIIT) under Section 1411, and a stepped-up basis at death under Section 1014 that effectively eliminates capital gains tax on appreciated assets held until death. Germany generally subjects capital gains from financial assets to a flat 25 percent withholding tax (Abgeltungsteuer) plus solidarity surcharge under Section 20 EStG since 2009, while real estate gains are tax-free after a 10-year holding period for private assets under the Spekulationsfrist provision of Section 23 EStG. At the EU level, capital gains taxation remains within member state competence, though the CJEU has constrained exit taxation provisions that impede freedom of establishment under Article 49 TFEU, most notably in the landmark National Grid Indus (C-371/10, 2011) decision establishing that member states may assess but not immediately collect tax on unrealized gains when a company transfers its seat to another member state.

## South Korea

### Legal Framework

South Korea's capital gains tax provisions are found primarily in Chapter V of the Income Tax Act (소득세법 제5장 양도소득세). Capital gains are taxed separately from global income (종합소득), with their own rate structure and deduction rules. The system places particular emphasis on real estate gains, reflecting decades of policy aimed at curbing speculative property transactions in a market characterized by extreme price volatility in major metropolitan areas.

### Real Estate Capital Gains

| Holding Period / Classification | Rate |
|-------------------------------|------|
| Less than 1 year | 70% (designated areas) or 50% |
| 1-2 years | 60% (designated areas) or 40% |
| Over 2 years | Basic progressive rates (6-45%) |
| Multi-homeowner surcharge (2+ homes) | Additional 20% (designated areas) |
| Multi-homeowner surcharge (3+ homes) | Additional 30% (designated areas) |

The designation of 투기과열지구 (speculative overheated districts) and 조정대상지역 (adjustment target areas) triggers the higher rates. Seoul and surrounding Gyeonggi Province have been consistently designated, though some areas were de-designated in 2022-2023 as the housing market cooled.

### Securities Capital Gains

The taxation of stock market gains has been politically contentious. Under the current regime, gains from listed stocks are generally exempt for small investors, but a financial investment income tax (금융투자소득세) was legislated in 2020 to impose a 20 percent tax (25 percent on gains exceeding 300 million KRW) on all securities gains above a 50 million KRW annual threshold. Originally scheduled for January 2023, implementation was postponed to 2025 and then indefinitely deferred by the Yoon government in 2024, citing concerns about capital market competitiveness and retail investor opposition. Major shareholders holding 1 percent or more of listed companies (or shares valued at 1 billion KRW or more) remain subject to capital gains tax.

### Primary Residence Exemption

A one-house-per-household exemption (1세대 1주택 비과세) provides tax-free treatment for gains on the sale of a primary residence held for at least two years, provided the household owns only one home and the sale price does not exceed 1.2 billion KRW. Gains on the portion exceeding 1.2 billion KRW are taxed at applicable rates.

## United States

### Legal Framework

Capital gains taxation in the United States is governed by multiple IRC provisions. Section 1221 defines "capital asset" broadly (all property except inventory, receivables, and certain other exclusions). Section 1222 distinguishes short-term (held one year or less) from long-term (held more than one year) capital gains. Section 1(h) provides the preferential rates for long-term gains.

### Rate Structure

| Income Level (Single, 2024) | Long-Term Capital Gains Rate |
|----------------------------|------------------------------|
| Up to $47,025 | 0% |
| $47,026 - $518,900 | 15% |
| Over $518,900 | 20% |

Additionally, the Net Investment Income Tax (NIIT) under Section 1411 imposes a 3.8 percent surtax on investment income for individuals with modified adjusted gross income exceeding $200,000 (single) or $250,000 (married filing jointly), bringing the effective maximum rate to 23.8 percent.

### Stepped-Up Basis at Death

Section 1014 provides that the basis of property acquired from a decedent is "stepped up" to its fair market value at the date of death. This effectively eliminates capital gains tax on all appreciation that occurred during the decedent's lifetime. The Joint Committee on Taxation estimated that this provision costs approximately $43 billion annually in foregone revenue. Multiple administrations have proposed eliminating or limiting the stepped-up basis, most recently in President Biden's FY 2025 budget proposal, but no legislation has been enacted.

### Section 121 Primary Residence Exclusion

Taxpayers may exclude up to $250,000 (single) or $500,000 (married filing jointly) of gain on the sale of a principal residence, provided they have owned and used the home as a primary residence for at least two of the five years preceding the sale.

### Opportunity Zones

The TCJA created Qualified Opportunity Zones (26 U.S.C. Section 1400Z-2), providing capital gains tax deferral for gains invested in designated low-income census tracts. While the deferral benefit expired at the end of 2026 for most investments, the permanent exclusion of gain on Qualified Opportunity Zone investments held for at least 10 years continues to incentivize long-term investment.

### Landmark Cases

**Cottage Savings Association v. Commissioner, 499 U.S. 554 (1991):** The Supreme Court held that an exchange of economically similar mortgage portfolios constituted a "realization" event for capital gains purposes because the properties exchanged were "materially different" in their legal entitlements, establishing the materiality standard for realization.

**Helvering v. Horst, 311 U.S. 112 (1940):** Established the assignment of income doctrine in the capital gains context, preventing taxpayers from avoiding capital gains by assigning income-producing property rights to related parties.

## Germany

### Legal Framework

Germany's capital gains tax treatment differs significantly depending on the type of asset and whether it is held privately or as a business asset.

### Financial Assets -- Abgeltungsteuer

Since January 1, 2009, gains from the disposition of financial assets (stocks, bonds, investment fund shares, derivatives) held in private portfolios are subject to the Abgeltungsteuer (flat withholding tax) at a rate of 25 percent, plus 5.5 percent solidarity surcharge on the tax amount (effective rate approximately 26.375 percent), plus church tax if applicable. This replaced the previous system under which private capital gains on securities held for more than one year (the former Spekulationsfrist) were completely tax-free.

### Real Estate -- Spekulationsfrist

For privately held real estate, gains are taxable at ordinary income tax rates if the property is sold within 10 years of acquisition (the Spekulationsfrist under Section 23 EStG). After the 10-year holding period, gains are completely tax-free. An exception exists for owner-occupied property: gains are tax-free regardless of the holding period if the property was used as the taxpayer's primary residence during the year of sale and the two preceding calendar years.

### Substantial Shareholdings -- Section 17 EStG

Gains from the sale of shares in a corporation where the taxpayer holds or has held at least 1 percent of the share capital within the five years preceding the sale are taxed under Section 17 EStG. Only 60 percent of such gains are taxable (Teileinkünfteverfahren), with corresponding 60 percent deductibility of losses. This partial exemption reflects the partial taxation of corporate profits already subject to Körperschaftsteuer.

### Business Assets

Capital gains on business assets are included in business income and taxed at ordinary rates. However, rollover relief (Section 6b EStG) allows the deferral of gains on the sale of certain business assets (land, buildings) if the proceeds are reinvested in qualifying replacement assets within specified time limits.

### Loss Limitation Rules

Germany imposes strict loss offset limitations (Verlustverrechnungsbeschränkungen). Losses from Abgeltungsteuer-covered investments can only be offset against gains of the same type. Losses from stock sales specifically can only be offset against gains from stock sales (not against bond gains or dividend income). An annual cap of EUR 20,000 applies to the offset of losses from certain derivative transactions.

## ECHR & EU

### CJEU Exit Taxation Case Law

The CJEU has developed a significant body of case law constraining member state exit taxes on unrealized capital gains:

**National Grid Indus (C-371/10, 2011):** The Court held that the Netherlands' immediate collection of tax on unrealized gains when a company transferred its seat to the United Kingdom violated the freedom of establishment. While assessment of the tax at the time of transfer was permissible, immediate collection was disproportionate. Member states must offer the option of deferred payment.

**DMC Beteiligungsgesellschaft (C-164/12, 2014):** The Court confirmed that exit taxation is permissible in principle but that immediate collection without the option of deferral is disproportionate.

**ATAD Exit Tax (Article 5, Directive 2016/1164):** Codifies the CJEU case law, requiring member states to impose exit taxation on unrealized gains when assets or tax residence are transferred to another jurisdiction, while allowing taxpayers transferring to another EU/EEA state to pay in installments over five years.

### Free Movement of Capital and Capital Gains

**Commission v. Portugal (C-493/09, 2011):** The Court held that Portugal's provision exempting capital gains on the sale of a primary residence from taxation only when the proceeds were reinvested in a Portuguese property violated the free movement of capital.

**Gielen (C-440/08, 2010):** Confirmed that member states may not apply less favorable capital gains provisions to non-residents than to residents where the situations are objectively comparable.

### ECHR Property Rights

Under Protocol 1, Article 1, the ECHR has recognized that capital gains taxes constitute an interference with property rights but has generally held such interference to be justified in the general interest:

**Hentrich v. France (Application No. 13616/88, 1994):** The Court found a violation of Protocol 1, Article 1, where the French tax authority exercised its preemption right to purchase a property at the declared price, without adequate procedural safeguards, on suspicion that the price understated the true value for capital gains tax purposes.

## Comparative Analysis

### Preferential Treatment Rationale

The United States provides the most explicit preferential treatment of capital gains through lower long-term rates, justified by arguments about encouraging investment, compensating for inflation (which erodes real gains), and reducing the "lock-in" effect that discourages asset sales. Germany's Abgeltungsteuer at 25 percent is also below the top ordinary income rate of 45 percent. South Korea's basic progressive rates apply to most capital gains but with punitive surcharges on speculative real estate, reflecting a fundamentally different policy priority: discouraging property speculation rather than encouraging capital market investment.

### Real Estate Focus

South Korea stands out for its extraordinary focus on real estate capital gains taxation as a tool of housing policy. The multi-homeowner surcharges, speculative area designations, and punitive short-term rates have no parallel in the United States or Germany. Germany takes the opposite approach, making private real estate gains completely tax-free after 10 years. The United States occupies a middle ground with the Section 121 exclusion for primary residences but standard capital gains rates on investment property.

### Treatment at Death

The jurisdictions diverge sharply on death-related treatment. The U.S. stepped-up basis eliminates unrealized gains entirely at death, creating what critics call the "angel of death" loophole. Germany uses carryover basis, meaning heirs inherit the decedent's historical cost and will owe capital gains tax when they eventually sell. South Korea revalues assets to market value at death but subjects the estate to its high inheritance tax rates.

### International Mobility and Exit Taxes

All three jurisdictions and the EU impose or permit exit taxes to prevent taxpayers from avoiding capital gains by emigrating. The U.S. Section 877A "expatriation tax" applies broadly to covered expatriates. Germany's Section 6 AStG imposes a deemed disposition on emigration. The CJEU has moderated EU exit taxes by requiring installment payment options within the EU/EEA.
