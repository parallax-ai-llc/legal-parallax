## Summary

Joint ventures -- business arrangements in which two or more parties agree to pool resources for the accomplishment of a specific task or ongoing business activity while maintaining their separate identities -- occupy a distinctive position in commercial law as a form of business collaboration that falls between arm's-length contracting and full corporate merger, and their regulation varies substantially depending on whether the venture takes contractual or equity form, the industry involved, and the competition law framework applicable. South Korea regulates joint ventures through a combination of the Korean Commercial Code (상법), the Monopoly Regulation and Fair Trade Act (독점규제 및 공정거래에 관한 법률, MRFTA), the Foreign Investment Promotion Act (외국인투자 촉진법, FIPA), and sector-specific legislation, with the Korea Fair Trade Commission (공정거래위원회, KFTC) exercising competition law oversight over joint venture formation and operation. The United States lacks a dedicated joint venture statute, instead applying general principles of partnership law (RUPA), corporate law (state corporation codes), antitrust law (Sherman Act, 15 U.S.C. §§ 1-7; Clayton Act, 15 U.S.C. §§ 12-27), and the National Cooperative Research and Production Act (15 U.S.C. §§ 4301-4306), with the Department of Justice and Federal Trade Commission jointly providing guidance through the Antitrust Guidelines for Collaborations Among Competitors (2000). Germany regulates joint ventures (Gemeinschaftsunternehmen) under the BGB, HGB, GmbH-Gesetz (GmbHG), and Aktiengesetz (AktG) depending on their legal form, with competition law review conducted under the Act against Restraints of Competition (Gesetz gegen Wettbewerbsbeschränkungen, GWB) and, for concentrations with an EU dimension, under the EU Merger Regulation (Council Regulation (EC) No 139/2004), while the ECHR protects joint venture participants' rights through Article 1 of Protocol No. 1 and Article 11 (freedom of association).

## South Korea

### Legal Framework

South Korea does not have a dedicated joint venture statute. Joint ventures are established using existing corporate and partnership forms, with the specific legal structure chosen based on the parties' commercial objectives, liability preferences, and regulatory requirements.

**Equity Joint Ventures:** The most common form is the incorporation of a Jusik Hoesa (주식회사, stock corporation) under Part 3, Chapter 4 of the Commercial Code. The joint venture partners become shareholders, and their relationship is governed by the articles of incorporation (정관), shareholders' agreement, and the Commercial Code's corporate governance provisions.

**Contractual Joint Ventures:** Parties may also establish contractual joint ventures without creating a separate legal entity. These are governed by the Civil Code's contract provisions (민법 Part 3) and, where the arrangement constitutes a partnership, by the Johap provisions of Articles 703-724 of the Civil Code.

| JV Form | Legal Basis | Key Characteristics |
|---------|------------|-------------------|
| Jusik Hoesa (주식회사) | Commercial Code, Part 3, Ch. 4 | Separate legal entity; limited liability; most common for foreign-domestic JVs |
| Yuhan Hoesa (유한회사) | Commercial Code, Part 3, Ch. 5 | Simpler governance; suitable for smaller JVs |
| Hapja Johap (합자조합) | Commercial Code, Part 3, Ch. 1-2 | Pass-through taxation; used for investment JVs |
| Contractual JV | Civil Code, contract provisions | No separate entity; governed by JV agreement |

### Foreign Investment Joint Ventures

**Foreign Investment Promotion Act (외국인투자 촉진법, FIPA):** Foreign investment in Korean joint ventures is regulated by FIPA, which defines "foreign investment" as the acquisition by a foreigner of shares or equity interest in a Korean corporation or enterprise. Key provisions include:

- **Article 2:** Foreign investment is defined as investment of 100 million KRW or more, or acquisition of 10 percent or more of shares in a Korean company
- **Article 4:** Registration with the Ministry of Trade, Industry and Energy (MOTIE) or its delegate (Korea Trade-Investment Promotion Agency, KOTRA)
- **Restricted Sectors:** Certain sectors remain closed or restricted for foreign investment under Presidential Decree, including broadcasting, telecommunications, and defense-related industries

**Sector-Specific Restrictions:**

| Sector | Foreign Ownership Limit | Governing Law |
|--------|----------------------|--------------|
| Terrestrial broadcasting | 0% (prohibited) | Broadcasting Act (방송법) |
| Newspapers | 30% maximum | Newspaper Act (신문법) |
| Telecommunications | 49% maximum (SK Telecom, KT, LG U+) | Telecommunications Business Act |
| Domestic air transport | 49% maximum | Aviation Safety Act |
| Nuclear energy | Subject to approval | Nuclear Energy Promotion Act |

### Competition Law Constraints

**Monopoly Regulation and Fair Trade Act (MRFTA):** The KFTC reviews joint venture formation under the MRFTA's merger control provisions (Articles 7 and 12).

**Merger Notification Thresholds (Article 12):** A joint venture formation constituting a "business combination" must be notified to the KFTC if:
- The combined assets or total turnover of the participating companies exceeds 300 billion KRW, and
- The assets or total turnover of the other party exceeds 30 billion KRW

**Substantive Assessment:** The KFTC assesses whether the joint venture substantially lessens competition in the relevant market (Article 7). The KFTC's Business Combination Review Guidelines (2019) provide detailed guidance on:
- Market definition
- Assessment of competitive effects (unilateral and coordinated effects)
- Efficiency claims
- Failing firm defense

**Supreme Court Decision 2013두24471 (2015):** The Court upheld the KFTC's prohibition of a joint venture between two major steel producers, finding that the venture would create a dominant position in the specialty steel market and substantially reduce competition.

### Joint Venture Governance

Korean law provides flexibility in structuring joint venture governance, but certain Commercial Code provisions are mandatory:

- **Board of Directors:** Joint ventures organized as Jusik Hoesa must have a board of directors (이사회). JV partners typically negotiate board composition (e.g., equal representation or proportional to shareholding).
- **Representative Director:** At least one representative director (대표이사) must be appointed. Joint venture agreements commonly address the rotation or joint appointment of representative directors.
- **Supermajority Requirements:** Commercial Code Article 434 requires a two-thirds supermajority of attending shareholders for fundamental matters (amendments to articles, mergers, dissolution). JV agreements may establish additional supermajority or unanimous consent requirements for reserved matters.
- **Deadlock Resolution:** Korean corporate law does not provide a specific deadlock mechanism. JV agreements typically include tiered dispute resolution (escalation to senior management, mediation, arbitration) and buy-sell provisions (Russian roulette, Texas shootout).

## United States

### Legal Framework

The United States treats joint ventures as a category of business collaboration subject to multiple overlapping legal regimes rather than a single coherent body of law.

**Definitional Challenges:** U.S. courts have struggled to define "joint venture" precisely. The Restatement (Third) of Agency (2006) does not define joint ventures separately, and courts have applied varying tests:

- **Community of interest** in the venture's subject matter
- **Right of mutual control** or management
- **Sharing of profits and losses**
- **Existence of a contract** (express or implied) establishing the venture

**Taubman v. Webfeats, 319 F.3d 770 (6th Cir. 2003):** The court identified the essential elements of a joint venture as: (1) a contract; (2) a common purpose; (3) a community of interest; (4) an equal right of control; and (5) a sharing of profits and losses.

### Legal Forms

| Form | Characteristics | Common Uses |
|------|----------------|------------|
| Corporation (C-Corp or S-Corp) | Separate entity; limited liability; double taxation (C-Corp) | Large-scale JVs; publicly traded parents |
| LLC | Separate entity; limited liability; pass-through taxation; flexible governance | Most common for domestic JVs |
| General Partnership | Entity; unlimited liability; pass-through taxation | Rarely used due to liability exposure |
| Limited Partnership | GP unlimited liability; LP limited; pass-through taxation | Real estate and investment JVs |
| Contractual JV | No separate entity; governed by contract | Project-specific collaborations |

### Antitrust Analysis

**Sherman Act § 1 (15 U.S.C. § 1):** Joint ventures among competitors are subject to scrutiny under Section 1 as agreements in restraint of trade.

**Antitrust Guidelines for Collaborations Among Competitors (DOJ/FTC, 2000):** The Guidelines establish a framework for analyzing joint ventures:

- **Per Se Rule:** Agreements among competitors that are naked restraints (price fixing, market allocation, output restriction) are per se illegal, even if structured as joint ventures
- **Rule of Reason:** Most legitimate joint ventures are analyzed under the rule of reason, which weighs procompetitive benefits against anticompetitive effects
- **Safety Zone:** Collaborations where the combined market share of the participants is 20 percent or below are generally not challenged absent extraordinary circumstances

**Texaco Inc. v. Dagher, 547 U.S. 1 (2006):** The Supreme Court held that pricing decisions by a legitimate joint venture are assessed under the rule of reason, not per se, even where the venturers are competitors. The Court emphasized that a lawfully established joint venture is a single entity for purposes of pricing its own products.

**National Cooperative Research and Production Act (NCRPA, 15 U.S.C. §§ 4301-4306):** The NCRPA provides that joint ventures for research and development are to be judged under the rule of reason, limits damages to actual (not treble) damages for registered joint ventures, and requires the FTC and DOJ to publish notices of registered joint ventures in the Federal Register.

### Hart-Scott-Rodino Review

**Hart-Scott-Rodino Antitrust Improvements Act (15 U.S.C. § 18a):** Formation of a joint venture entity may trigger HSR premerger notification requirements if:
- The contributing parties each have total assets or annual net sales of $23.9 million or more (2025 threshold, adjusted annually)
- The value of the joint venture interest exceeds $119.5 million (2025 threshold)
- The formation constitutes an "acquisition" of voting securities or assets

### CFIUS Review

**Committee on Foreign Investment in the United States (50 U.S.C. §§ 4565, as amended by FIRRMA 2018):** Joint ventures involving foreign persons may be subject to CFIUS review where the transaction involves:
- Control of a U.S. business
- Access to critical technologies, critical infrastructure, or sensitive personal data of U.S. citizens
- Proximity to sensitive government facilities

**CFIUS has increasingly scrutinized joint ventures in technology sectors**, particularly those involving Chinese, Russian, and other countries of concern. Mandatory declarations are required for transactions involving critical technologies in specified industries (27 C.F.R. § 800.401).

### Fiduciary Duties

U.S. courts generally apply partnership fiduciary duties to joint ventures:

**Meinhard v. Salmon, 249 N.Y. 458 (1928):** The duty of loyalty in joint ventures requires each venturer to disclose opportunities and refrain from self-dealing, applying the "punctilio of an honor the most sensitive" standard.

**Scope of duties:** Courts have held that joint venture fiduciary duties include:
- Duty to disclose material information to co-venturers
- Duty not to compete with the venture
- Duty to account for profits derived from venture opportunities
- Duty of good faith and fair dealing

## Germany

### Legal Framework

German law does not recognize the joint venture (Gemeinschaftsunternehmen) as a distinct legal category. Joint ventures are established using standard legal forms, with the specific structure chosen based on the parties' objectives.

**Equity Joint Venture Forms:**

| Form | Legal Basis | Common Use |
|------|------------|-----------|
| GmbH | GmbHG | Most common for domestic and cross-border JVs |
| AG | AktG | Larger JVs requiring capital market access |
| GmbH & Co. KG | HGB + GmbHG | Tax-transparent JV structure |
| GbR / OHG | BGB / HGB | Contractual-type arrangements |

**GmbH as JV Vehicle:** The GmbH (Gesellschaft mit beschränkter Haftung) is the predominant vehicle for joint ventures in Germany. Key advantages include:
- Minimum share capital of only EUR 25,000 (§ 5 GmbHG)
- Flexible internal governance structure
- Strong minority protection rights (§ 47 GmbHG, majority voting; but customizable in the Gesellschaftsvertrag)
- No mandatory supervisory board (unless employee co-determination thresholds are exceeded)

### Joint Venture Agreement (Konsortialvertrag / Joint Venture Vertrag)

German joint venture practice distinguishes between:

**Gesellschaftsvertrag (Articles of Association):** The constitutive document filed with the Handelsregister, which must comply with mandatory GmbHG provisions. For a GmbH, the articles must contain the company name, registered office, purpose, share capital, and shareholders' contributions (§ 3 GmbHG).

**Konsortialvertrag (Shareholders' Agreement / JV Agreement):** The comprehensive contract between the joint venture partners governing their relationship. Unlike the articles of association, the Konsortialvertrag is not publicly filed and may contain provisions that would be impermissible in the articles.

| Typical JV Agreement Provisions | German Legal Consideration |
|--------------------------------|--------------------------|
| Board composition and nomination rights | Permissible; may conflict with GmbHG § 46 (shareholder meeting competence) |
| Reserved matters / veto rights | Permissible; must be balanced against GmbHG mandatory provisions |
| Transfer restrictions (Vinkulierung) | § 15 GmbHG permits restrictions on share transfer |
| Non-competition obligations | Enforceable within limits of § 138 BGB (public policy) and competition law |
| Deadlock resolution | Not regulated by statute; contractual freedom |
| Put/call options | Permissible; BGH has upheld Russian roulette clauses |

### Competition Law: GWB and EU Merger Regulation

**Gesetz gegen Wettbewerbsbeschränkungen (GWB):**

The formation of a joint venture may constitute a "concentration" (Zusammenschluss) subject to merger control review under §§ 35-43 GWB if:
- The combined aggregate worldwide turnover of all undertakings concerned exceeds EUR 500 million, and
- At least one undertaking has domestic (German) turnover exceeding EUR 50 million, and
- Another undertaking has domestic turnover exceeding EUR 17.5 million (§ 35 GWB)

**Full-Function Joint Venture Doctrine:** German competition law distinguishes between:

- **Concentrative JVs (Vollfunktionsgemeinschaftsunternehmen):** JVs that perform on a lasting basis all the functions of an autonomous economic entity are assessed as concentrations under merger control rules
- **Cooperative JVs (Kooperative JVs):** JVs that primarily coordinate the competitive behavior of the parent undertakings are assessed under the cartel prohibition (§ 1 GWB / Art. 101 TFEU)

**Bundeskartellamt Practice:** The Bundeskartellamt has published extensive decisional practice on joint ventures, including:
- **B4-39/21 (Lufthansa / ITA Airways JV, 2022):** Assessment of an airline joint venture under merger control and cartel prohibition provisions
- **B2-96/14 (Edeka / Tengelmann JV):** Ministererlaubnis (ministerial authorization) granted after Bundeskartellamt prohibition

### Foreign Investment Screening

**Außenwirtschaftsgesetz (AWG) and Außenwirtschaftsverordnung (AWV):** Since the 2020 amendments, Germany has significantly strengthened its foreign investment screening regime:

- **§ 55 AWG:** The Federal Ministry for Economic Affairs and Climate Action (BMWK) may review and prohibit acquisitions of German companies by non-EU/non-EFTA investors
- **§ 55a AWG:** Sector-specific review for critical infrastructure, defense, and media companies (10 percent voting rights threshold)
- **§ 56 AWV:** Cross-sector review for all other sectors (25 percent voting rights threshold)
- Joint venture formation where a foreign investor acquires significant influence over a German entity falls within the scope of screening

### Gesellschafterstreit (Shareholder Disputes) in JVs

German law provides several mechanisms for resolving joint venture disputes:

**Deadlock at Shareholder Level:** Where the GmbH Gesellschaftsvertrag requires unanimity or supermajority and the partners cannot agree, German courts may:
- Appoint an emergency manager (Notgeschäftsführer) under § 29 BGB
- Order dissolution for important cause (Auflösung aus wichtigem Grund, § 61 GmbHG)
- Enforce contractual deadlock mechanisms (Russian roulette, mediation clauses)

**BGH, February 20, 2014 (II ZR 346/12):** The Court upheld the enforceability of a Russian roulette clause in a GmbH joint venture agreement, finding that the mechanism did not violate public policy under § 138 BGB, provided that the pricing mechanism was fair and did not create an unconscionable advantage for one party.

## ECHR / EU

### EU Merger Control

**Council Regulation (EC) No 139/2004 (EU Merger Regulation, EUMR):** The creation of a joint venture constituting a "concentration" within the meaning of the EUMR is subject to mandatory notification to the European Commission where it has an "EU dimension":

**Jurisdictional Thresholds (Article 1):**
- Combined aggregate worldwide turnover of all undertakings exceeds EUR 5 billion, **and**
- EU-wide turnover of each of at least two undertakings exceeds EUR 250 million
- Unless each undertaking achieves more than two-thirds of its EU-wide turnover within one member state

**Alternative Thresholds (Article 1(3)):**
- Combined aggregate worldwide turnover exceeds EUR 2.5 billion
- Combined turnover in each of at least three member states exceeds EUR 100 million
- Individual turnover in each of those member states exceeds EUR 25 million
- EU-wide turnover of each of at least two undertakings exceeds EUR 100 million

### Full-Function Joint Venture Test

**Commission Consolidated Jurisdictional Notice (2008/C 95/01):** The European Commission assesses whether a joint venture is "full-function" (performing on a lasting basis all the functions of an autonomous economic entity) and thus constitutes a concentration:

**Full-function criteria:**
1. Sufficient resources to operate independently (management, staff, assets, funding)
2. Activities beyond one specific function for the parent companies
3. Dealings with third parties (not solely dependent on parent companies for sales/purchases)
4. Intended to operate on a lasting basis (generally interpreted as at least three years)

**Article 2(4) EUMR -- Spillover Effects:** Where a full-function joint venture also has the effect of coordinating the competitive behavior of the parent undertakings, the Commission assesses this coordinative element under Article 101 TFEU criteria (Article 2(4) EUMR). This dual assessment is unique to EU merger control.

### Article 101 TFEU Analysis

Joint ventures that do not qualify as concentrations under the EUMR are assessed under Article 101 TFEU (prohibition of agreements restricting competition):

**Commission Guidelines on the Applicability of Article 101 TFEU to Horizontal Co-operation Agreements (2023):**

| JV Type | Assessment Framework |
|---------|---------------------|
| Research and development JVs | R&D Block Exemption Regulation (EU) 2023/1066; market share threshold 25% |
| Production JVs | Specialisation Block Exemption Regulation (EU) 2023/1067; market share threshold 20% |
| Purchasing JVs | Assessed individually under Art. 101(1) and (3) |
| Commercialisation JVs | Generally higher risk; individual assessment |
| Sustainability JVs | New guidance under 2023 Guidelines; possible Art. 101(3) exemption for verified sustainability benefits |

### EU FDI Screening

**Regulation (EU) 2019/452 (FDI Screening Regulation):** Effective October 11, 2020, this Regulation establishes a framework for member state screening of foreign direct investment on grounds of security or public order. Joint ventures involving non-EU investors may trigger screening where the investment results in effective influence over critical infrastructure, technologies, or resources.

### ECHR Jurisprudence

**Freedom of Association (Article 11 ECHR):** The right to form joint ventures is protected under Article 11 as a component of freedom of association.

**Protection of Property (Article 1, Protocol No. 1):** Joint venture interests constitute "possessions" within the meaning of the Convention. State interference -- through regulatory prohibition, forced dissolution, or confiscatory taxation -- must satisfy the proportionality requirement.

**OAO Neftyanaya Kompaniya Yukos v. Russia (Application No. 14902/04, ECtHR Grand Chamber Judgment of July 31, 2014):** While concerning corporate taxation rather than a joint venture directly, the Grand Chamber's analysis of state interference with corporate property rights through disproportionate enforcement measures has direct implications for joint venture participants.

**Bosphorus Hava Yollari Turizm ve Ticaret Anonim Sirketi v. Ireland (Application No. 45036/98, ECtHR Grand Chamber Judgment of June 30, 2005):** The Court established that EU law compliance creates a rebuttable presumption of Convention compliance ("Bosphorus presumption"), relevant where EU competition law decisions affect joint venture participants' property rights.

## Comparative Analysis

### Contractual vs. Equity Joint Ventures

| Feature | South Korea | United States | Germany | EU |
|---------|-------------|---------------|---------|-----|
| Preferred equity form | Jusik Hoesa (주식회사) | LLC | GmbH | National form of member state |
| Contractual JV recognition | Yes; Civil Code contract law | Yes; common law and UCC | Yes; BGB-Gesellschaft or Kooperationsvertrag | Assessed under Art. 101 TFEU |
| Separate legal personality | Yes (equity JV) | Yes (equity JV) | Yes (equity JV) | Depends on national form |
| Pass-through taxation option | Hapja Johap | LLC; LP; general partnership | GmbH & Co. KG | Depends on national law |

### Competition Law Treatment

The competition law analysis of joint ventures reveals the most significant divergence among jurisdictions:

- **South Korea (KFTC):** Applies a substantial lessening of competition test under MRFTA Article 7. The KFTC has been increasingly active in reviewing joint ventures, particularly in technology and telecommunications sectors.
- **United States (DOJ/FTC):** Applies a rule of reason analysis for legitimate joint ventures, with per se prohibition only for naked restraints. The 20 percent combined market share safety zone provides a clear threshold for low-risk ventures.
- **Germany (Bundeskartellamt):** Applies the SIEC test (significant impediment to effective competition) under GWB § 36, consistent with the EU framework. The Bundeskartellamt has been particularly rigorous in reviewing joint ventures in energy, digital, and automotive sectors.
- **EU (European Commission):** The most sophisticated analytical framework, distinguishing between concentrative full-function JVs (assessed under merger control) and cooperative JVs (assessed under Art. 101 TFEU), with dual assessment where a full-function JV also produces spillover effects.

### Foreign Investment Screening

All jurisdictions have significantly strengthened foreign investment screening in recent years:

- **South Korea:** FIPA provides sector-specific restrictions; National Security Council consultation for sensitive sectors
- **United States:** CFIUS review under FIRRMA (2018) with expanded jurisdiction covering non-controlling investments in critical technology, infrastructure, and data
- **Germany:** AWG/AWV amendments (2020, 2021) lowered thresholds and expanded the scope of reviewable transactions
- **EU:** FDI Screening Regulation (2019/452) created a cooperation mechanism among member states but does not establish a mandatory EU-level screening

### Deadlock Resolution

Deadlock in 50/50 joint ventures presents a universal challenge:

| Mechanism | South Korea | United States | Germany | EU |
|-----------|-------------|---------------|---------|-----|
| Russian roulette | Enforceable (contractual freedom) | Widely used and enforceable | Enforceable (BGH II ZR 346/12) | National law |
| Texas shootout | Less common | Widely used | Used in practice | National law |
| Mediation/Arbitration | Korean Commercial Arbitration Board (KCAB) | AAA; JAMS; ICC | DIS (Deutsche Institution für Schiedsgerichtsbarkeit); ICC | ICC; LCIA |
| Court-ordered dissolution | Available (Commercial Code) | Available (state law) | Available (§ 61 GmbHG) | National law |
