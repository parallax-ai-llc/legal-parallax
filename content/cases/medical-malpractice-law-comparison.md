## Summary

Medical malpractice -- the failure of a healthcare professional to meet the accepted standard of care resulting in patient harm -- sits at the intersection of tort law, criminal law, patient rights, and professional regulation across every major legal system. South Korea operates under a dual civil-criminal framework where physicians face both civil damages claims under the Civil Act (Articles 750-752) and criminal prosecution for professional negligence under Article 268 of the Criminal Act, with the Korea Medical Dispute Mediation and Arbitration Agency (KMDMAA) established by the Act on Remedies for Injuries from Medical Malpractice and Mediation of Medical Disputes (2011) offering an alternative resolution pathway. The United States governs medical malpractice through a decentralized patchwork of 50 state-level tort systems, with elements of duty, breach, causation, and damages forming the common law foundation, overlaid by decades of tort reform legislation including California's landmark MICRA (1975) and Texas HB 4 (2003) imposing damages caps, expert witness requirements, and shortened statutes of limitations. Germany codified patient rights and medical liability through the Patients' Rights Act of 2013 (Patientenrechtegesetz), which inserted Sections 630a through 630h into the Civil Code (BGB), establishing the treatment contract as a distinct legal relationship and creating a reversal of the burden of proof in cases of gross malpractice, while retaining criminal prosecution under Sections 222 and 229 of the Criminal Code (StGB) for negligent homicide and negligent bodily harm. The European Convention on Human Rights, principally through Articles 2 (right to life) and 8 (right to private life), imposes positive obligations on member states to regulate healthcare, investigate medical deaths, and provide effective remedies, as articulated in landmark Grand Chamber judgments including Calvelli and Ciglio v. Italy (2002) and Lopes de Sousa Fernandes v. Portugal (2017), while EU Directive 2011/24/EU on patients' rights in cross-border healthcare ensures compensation mechanisms for medical negligence across member states.

## South Korea

### Legal Framework

South Korean medical malpractice law operates through a combined civil and criminal liability system, with the following principal statutes:

| Provision | Description | Application |
|-----------|-------------|-------------|
| Civil Act, Article 750 | General tort: willful or negligent unlawful act causing damage | Foundation for all civil malpractice claims |
| Civil Act, Article 751 | Non-pecuniary damages (consolation money/wisaryo) | Compensation for emotional suffering of the patient |
| Civil Act, Article 752 | Independent consolation claim for bereaved family | Available to spouse, lineal ascendants/descendants, siblings in fatal cases |
| Criminal Act, Article 268 | Professional negligence resulting in death or injury | Criminal prosecution of physicians; up to 5 years imprisonment |
| Medical Dispute Mediation Act (2011) | Establishes KMDMAA for mediation and arbitration | Alternative to litigation; provides prosecution protection for participating physicians |
| Medical Service Act | Licensing, standards, and disciplinary framework | Administrative penalties including license suspension or revocation |

### Key Principles

**Dual civil-criminal liability:** South Korea is distinctive for the frequency with which medical malpractice triggers criminal prosecution. A study by the Korea Medical Association found that approximately 750 doctors per year are criminally charged with professional negligence resulting in death or injury under Article 268 of the Criminal Act -- a rate 14.7 times higher than Japan, 26.6 times higher than Germany, and 580.6 times higher than the United Kingdom. This heavy reliance on criminal law has been a central grievance of the Korean medical profession.

**Standard of care:** Korean courts evaluate medical negligence from the perspective of an ordinarily careful physician in the same field performing the same duties. The standard is determined by the level of medical science at the time of treatment, the medical environment and conditions, and the particular nature of the medical procedure. Courts consider whether the physician failed to anticipate and avoid a foreseeable consequence.

**Relaxed causation standard:** In recognition of the information asymmetry between patients and physicians, Korean courts have adopted a relaxed standard for proving causation in medical malpractice cases. Where a patient cannot be expected to prove a detailed medical mechanism, courts may infer causation from circumstantial evidence if the physician fails to provide a plausible alternative explanation.

**Prosecution protection through mediation:** Under Article 37 of the Medical Dispute Mediation Act, after successful mediation through KMDMAA or when a mediation statement is prepared, a physician who acted with professional negligence under Article 268 will not be publicly prosecuted if the victim does not express an intent to prosecute. However, this protection does not apply where the victim's life is threatened, the victim becomes disabled, or the victim suffers from a fatal or incurable disease.

### Key Cases and Developments

**Criminal prosecution reform debate (2023-2024):** The government proposed a reform package known as the "Safety Net for Medical Malpractice," which sought to exempt medical professionals from criminal punishment for malpractice occurring during essential medical procedures, provided they were enrolled in the National Health Insurance program. This proposal was part of the broader healthcare reforms that, alongside the push to increase medical school admissions quotas, contributed to the 2024 mass walkout by medical interns and residents that created a national healthcare crisis.

**2024 Medical Crisis:** Beginning in February 2024, thousands of medical interns and residents resigned en masse to protest government healthcare reforms. Among the doctors' demands was meaningful criminal prosecution reform, reflecting long-standing anger over the punitive legal environment. The crisis, which lasted over eight months, exposed the deep connection between medical malpractice law and healthcare system stability.

**Supreme Court causation jurisprudence:** Korean Supreme Court decisions have consistently held that where a patient proves a physician's negligence was a plausible cause of harm, the burden effectively shifts to the physician to demonstrate an alternative cause, representing a significant departure from strict plaintiff-bears-all-burden principles typical in civil law systems.

### Penalties

| Offense | Civil Penalty | Criminal Penalty | Administrative |
|---------|---------------|-------------------|----------------|
| Negligent injury | Full compensatory damages + consolation money | Up to 5 years imprisonment or fine under Art. 268 | License suspension |
| Negligent death | Full compensatory damages + bereaved family consolation | Up to 5 years imprisonment or fine under Art. 268 | License suspension or revocation |
| Informed consent failure | Compensatory damages | Possible criminal battery charge | Disciplinary action |
| Gross negligence | Full compensatory damages | Aggravated sentencing under Art. 268 | License revocation |

## United States

### Legal Framework

The United States has no federal medical malpractice statute of general application. Instead, malpractice law is governed by 50 separate state tort systems, each with its own statutes, case law, damages rules, and procedural requirements. A successful claim requires proving four elements:

| Element | Description | Typical Standard |
|---------|-------------|------------------|
| Duty | Physician-patient relationship establishing a duty of care | Arises when treatment relationship is established |
| Breach | Failure to meet the applicable standard of care | Degree of care, skill, and diligence used by ordinarily careful physicians in the same specialty under similar circumstances |
| Causation | The breach was the proximate cause (or substantial factor) of the injury | Requires expert testimony; "but-for" and "substantial factor" tests |
| Damages | Actual harm or injury resulted | Physical injury, economic loss, pain and suffering, wrongful death |

### Tort Reform Landscape

Beginning in the 1970s amid rising malpractice insurance premiums, states enacted waves of tort reform legislation:

**California MICRA (1975):** The Medical Injury Compensation Reform Act was the first major state malpractice reform, capping non-economic damages at $250,000, limiting contingency fees on a sliding scale, modifying the collateral source rule, permitting periodic payments, and imposing a three-year statute of limitations with a one-year discovery rule. In 2022, AB 35 amended MICRA to increase the non-economic damages cap to $350,000 for non-death cases and $500,000 for wrongful death, with annual increases of $40,000-$50,000 until reaching $750,000 and $1 million respectively, after which the cap adjusts for inflation.

**Texas HB 4 (2003):** Texas House Bill 4 capped non-economic damages at $250,000 per defendant (with a $500,000 aggregate cap for hospitals), mandated 60-day advance notice before filing suit, required detailed expert reports within 120 days, and heightened requirements for expert witness qualifications. The reforms led to a reported 50% decline in malpractice filings within the first years of implementation.

**Florida Medical Malpractice Reform Act (2003):** Limited non-economic damages to $500,000 for non-fatal injuries and $1 million for wrongful death, shortened the statute of limitations to two years, and imposed new rules for expert testimony. However, the Florida Supreme Court struck down the non-economic damages cap in Estate of McCall v. United States (2014) and North Broward Hospital District v. Kalitan (2017) as unconstitutional violations of equal protection.

**Georgia Tort Reform Act (2005):** Capped non-economic damages at $350,000 per defendant with a $1 million aggregate cap. The Georgia Supreme Court struck down this cap in Atlanta Oculoplastic Surgery, P.C. v. Nestlehutt (2010), holding it violated the constitutional right to a jury trial.

### Key Cases

**Daubert v. Merrell Dow Pharmaceuticals (1993):** The U.S. Supreme Court established the standard for admissibility of expert testimony in federal courts, requiring that scientific testimony be relevant and reliable. This ruling profoundly affected medical malpractice litigation by empowering trial judges to serve as gatekeepers for expert evidence and excluding unqualified or methodologically unsound testimony.

**Helling v. Carey (1974):** The Washington Supreme Court held an ophthalmologist liable for failing to test a young patient for glaucoma, even though the medical profession's standard did not call for such testing in patients under 40. The case established that compliance with professional custom does not automatically insulate a physician from liability if a reasonable prudent practitioner would have acted differently.

**Yanakos v. UPMC (2019):** The Pennsylvania Supreme Court struck down the state's statute of repose for medical malpractice claims, holding it unconstitutional as an unreasonable restriction on the right to access courts, illustrating the ongoing tension between tort reform legislation and constitutional protections.

**Lebron v. Gottlieb Memorial Hospital (2010):** The Illinois Supreme Court struck down the state's medical malpractice damages cap on separation-of-powers grounds, finding that the legislature had impermissibly encroached on the judiciary's authority to remit excessive damages.

### Current Status by State

As of 2025, 29 states have tort reform provisions placing caps on malpractice damages that have survived constitutional challenges. Key variations include:

| Category | States/Examples | Provisions |
|----------|----------------|------------|
| Non-economic damages caps (surviving) | California, Texas, Colorado, Indiana, Louisiana | Caps ranging from $250K to $750K+ |
| Caps struck down | Florida, Georgia, Illinois, Missouri, Oklahoma | Declared unconstitutional on various grounds |
| No damages caps | New York, Pennsylvania, New Jersey, Connecticut | No statutory limits on any category of damages |
| Pre-suit requirements | Texas, Florida, Indiana, Michigan | Mandatory notice periods, expert affidavits, or screening panels |
| Statute of limitations | All states | Typically 1-4 years; discovery rule widely adopted; special rules for minors |

### Penalties

| Consequence | Description |
|-------------|-------------|
| Civil damages | Economic (medical bills, lost wages) + non-economic (pain and suffering) + punitive (in extreme cases) |
| License discipline | State medical board action: reprimand, probation, suspension, revocation |
| Hospital credentialing | Loss of hospital privileges; reporting to National Practitioner Data Bank (NPDB) |
| Criminal charges | Rare; reserved for extreme recklessness or intentional harm (e.g., Christopher Duntsch "Dr. Death" case, convicted 2017) |
| Insurance consequences | Premium increases; non-renewal; placement in assigned risk pools |

## Germany

### Legal Framework

Germany codified medical malpractice law comprehensively through the Patients' Rights Act (Patientenrechtegesetz), enacted on February 25, 2013, which inserted Sections 630a through 630h into the Civil Code (BGB). These provisions create a distinct "treatment contract" (Behandlungsvertrag) governing the legal relationship between healthcare provider and patient:

| BGB Provision | Description | Application |
|---------------|-------------|-------------|
| Section 630a | Treatment contract: obligation to provide treatment according to current professional standards | Establishes the contractual basis of the physician-patient relationship |
| Section 630b | Applicable provisions (service contract rules apply unless otherwise specified) | Links treatment contract to general BGB service contract provisions |
| Section 630c | Duty to cooperate and inform | Physician must explain diagnosis, anticipated therapy, and relevant circumstances |
| Section 630d | Consent requirement | Treatment requires patient consent; consent must be obtained prior to treatment |
| Section 630e | Informed consent (Aufklarungspflicht) | Physician must inform patient of nature, scope, risks, alternatives, and consequences of treatment |
| Section 630f | Documentation obligation (Dokumentationspflicht) | Medical records must be complete and contemporaneous; failure creates presumption against physician |
| Section 630g | Patient access to medical records | Patients have right to inspect complete medical records |
| Section 630h | Burden of proof in malpractice and informed consent cases | Reversal of burden of proof in cases of gross malpractice; physician bears burden on informed consent |

### Criminal Liability

In parallel with civil liability, German physicians face potential criminal prosecution under the Criminal Code (Strafgesetzbuch, StGB):

| StGB Provision | Offense | Penalty |
|----------------|---------|---------|
| Section 222 | Negligent homicide (fahrlassige Totung) | Imprisonment up to 5 years or fine |
| Section 223 | Bodily harm (Korperverletzung) -- treatment without valid consent | Imprisonment up to 5 years or fine |
| Section 229 | Negligent bodily harm (fahrlassige Korperverletzung) | Imprisonment up to 3 years or fine |

Under German law, any medical treatment technically constitutes bodily harm (Korperverletzung) and is justified only by the patient's informed consent. A treatment performed without valid consent can therefore give rise to criminal liability under Section 223, even if the treatment itself was performed correctly.

### Key Principles

**Reversal of burden of proof (Beweislastumkehr):** Section 630h of the BGB is the cornerstone innovation of the Patients' Rights Act. In cases of gross malpractice (grober Behandlungsfehler), if the error is capable in principle of causing the type of injury that actually occurred, it is presumed that the malpractice caused the injury. The physician must then rebut this presumption. This reversal applies to:

- Gross treatment errors
- Failure to obtain informed consent (physician must prove consent was given)
- Failure to document treatment properly (undocumented measures are presumed not to have been taken)
- Failure of a practitioner to be fully qualified for the procedure performed

**Professional standard (Facharztstandard):** The standard of care is measured against the level of competence expected of a specialist (Facharzt) in the relevant field, based on the current state of medical science and practice at the time of treatment, not at the time of litigation.

**No punitive damages:** German law adheres strictly to the compensatory principle. There are no punitive or exemplary damages. Compensation covers treatment costs, rehabilitation, mitigation of permanent damage, long-term care, lost earnings, and pain and suffering (Schmerzensgeld). Since the 2002 reform of BGB Section 253, Schmerzensgeld is available for both contractual and tortious claims.

### Key Cases

**BGH VI ZR 204/09 (2012):** The Bundesgerichtshof (Federal Court of Justice) confirmed that in cases of gross treatment error, the burden of proof regarding causation shifts to the physician. The court held that this reversal serves the interest of justice because a gross treatment error fundamentally undermines the patient's ability to prove causation.

**BGH VI ZR 281/13 (2016):** The Federal Court of Justice ruled that a hospital's failure to maintain proper documentation creates a presumption that undocumented measures were not taken, reinforcing the documentation obligations later codified in Section 630f.

### Alternative Dispute Resolution

Germany operates medical arbitration boards (Schlichtungsstellen and Gutachterkommissionen) organized by the state medical chambers (Landesarztekammern). These boards provide expert evaluations of malpractice claims free of charge, and their opinions, while not binding, are accepted in approximately 90% of cases. This system significantly reduces the litigation burden on courts and provides faster resolution for patients.

### Penalties Summary

| Consequence | Description |
|-------------|-------------|
| Civil damages | Full compensation: treatment, rehabilitation, lost earnings, Schmerzensgeld (pain and suffering) |
| Criminal prosecution | Up to 5 years imprisonment for negligent homicide (Section 222 StGB); up to 3 years for negligent bodily harm (Section 229 StGB) |
| Professional discipline | Proceedings before the professional court (Berufsgericht); sanctions from reprimand to license revocation (Approbationsentzug) |
| Insurance reporting | Notification to professional liability insurer; potential premium adjustments |

## ECHR & EU

### European Convention on Human Rights

The ECHR does not contain an explicit right to health or a specific provision on medical malpractice. However, the European Court of Human Rights (ECtHR) has developed a substantial body of case law imposing positive obligations on member states through Articles 2 and 8:

| ECHR Provision | Obligation in Healthcare Context | Scope |
|----------------|----------------------------------|-------|
| Article 2 (Right to life) | Substantive: states must establish regulatory framework protecting patients' lives; Procedural: effective investigation of deaths in medical care | Applies to public and private healthcare; requires effective judicial system |
| Article 3 (Prohibition of torture) | Protection from medical treatment amounting to inhuman or degrading treatment | Forced procedures, inadequate conditions, treatment without consent |
| Article 8 (Right to private life) | Protection of physical integrity; right to autonomous medical decision-making; access to medical records | Informed consent, data protection, reproductive rights |
| Article 13 (Right to effective remedy) | Domestic legal system must provide effective remedies for medical negligence | Procedural requirement ensuring access to compensation |

### Landmark ECtHR Cases

**Calvelli and Ciglio v. Italy (2002):** The Grand Chamber established that Article 2 requires states to create an effective independent judicial system capable of determining the cause of death of patients in the care of the medical profession and holding accountable those responsible. The Court accepted that the obligation may be satisfied by civil proceedings (including settlement) that provide both compensation and accountability, rather than requiring criminal prosecution in every case.

**Powell v. United Kingdom (2000):** The European Commission of Human Rights defined the scope of state obligations to protect the right to life in healthcare, establishing that Article 2 requires an adequate legal framework of protection, including effective independent investigation and access to courts.

**Lopes de Sousa Fernandes v. Portugal (2017):** The Grand Chamber clarified the boundaries of state responsibility under Article 2 in healthcare. The Court held that "mere error or medical negligence" by an individual practitioner is not sufficient to engage state responsibility. However, Article 2 is engaged in two exceptional circumstances: (1) where a specific patient's life is knowingly put in danger by denial of access to life-saving emergency treatment (regardless of systemic issues), and (2) where systemic or structural dysfunction in hospital services results in a patient being deprived of access to life-saving emergency treatment with the authorities' knowledge.

**V.C. v. Slovakia (2011):** The Court found a violation of Article 3 (prohibition of inhuman treatment) and Article 8 (right to private life) where a Roma woman was sterilized during a Caesarean section without her full informed consent, establishing that medical procedures performed without proper consent may constitute inhuman or degrading treatment.

**Ternovszky v. Hungary (2010):** The Court ruled that legal uncertainty surrounding home births violated Article 8, affirming that the right to private life encompasses the right to make autonomous decisions about the circumstances of giving birth.

### EU Legal Framework

The European Union addresses medical malpractice and patient rights primarily through:

| EU Instrument | Description | Relevance to Malpractice |
|---------------|-------------|---------------------------|
| Directive 2011/24/EU | Patients' rights in cross-border healthcare | Patients treated in another EU country have right to compensation under the law of the country of treatment; requires complaint mechanisms |
| European Charter of Patients' Rights (2002) | 14 fundamental patient rights adopted in Rome | Establishes rights to information, consent, choice, privacy, complaints, and compensation |
| Directive 2005/36/EC (amended) | Recognition of professional qualifications | Ensures mutual recognition of medical qualifications; enables cross-border disciplinary information sharing |
| Product Liability Directive 85/374/EEC (replaced by Directive 2024/2853 from Dec 2026) | Strict liability for defective products | Covers medical devices and pharmaceuticals causing harm |
| General Data Protection Regulation (2016/679) | Protection of personal health data | Governs medical records, patient data, and health information sharing |

### Directive 2011/24/EU on Cross-Border Healthcare

Adopted on March 9, 2011, and transposed by member states by October 25, 2013, this Directive establishes:

- **Equal treatment:** Patients receiving healthcare in another EU member state are entitled to equal treatment with that state's own citizens
- **Reimbursement:** Patients are entitled to reimbursement from their home country up to the cost of equivalent treatment at home
- **Transparency:** Member states must provide information about quality and safety standards, complaint procedures, and available remedies
- **Compensation for malpractice:** Patients who suffer medical negligence in another member state have the right to claim compensation under the legislation of the member state of treatment
- **Continuity of care:** The home country must provide the same medical follow-up as for treatment received domestically

### Remedies and Enforcement

| Level | Mechanism | Scope |
|-------|-----------|-------|
| ECtHR | Application alleging violation of Convention rights | Just satisfaction (pecuniary and non-pecuniary damages); requires exhaustion of domestic remedies |
| EU national courts | Application of Directive 2011/24/EU and national implementing legislation | Compensation under domestic tort law of the country where treatment occurred |
| National regulatory bodies | Professional discipline and licensing | Varies by member state; cross-border information sharing under Directive 2005/36/EC |
| CJEU | Preliminary references on interpretation of EU law | Binding interpretations of EU directives applicable to medical malpractice |

## Comparative Analysis

### Criminal vs. Civil Emphasis

The most striking divergence among the four jurisdictions concerns the role of criminal law. South Korea stands out for its exceptionally high rate of criminal prosecution of physicians, with approximately 750 doctors criminally charged per year -- a rate that has been a major driver of physician dissatisfaction and contributed to the 2024 medical crisis. Germany maintains criminal liability through StGB Sections 222 and 229 but prosecutes far less frequently. The United States reserves criminal prosecution for extreme cases of recklessness or intentional harm, treating malpractice overwhelmingly as a civil tort matter. The ECHR, through Lopes de Sousa Fernandes, has clarified that mere individual negligence is generally insufficient to engage state responsibility under Article 2, reserving the Convention's reach for systemic failures or knowing denial of life-saving care.

### Burden of Proof

Germany's 2013 Patients' Rights Act introduced the most plaintiff-friendly burden of proof regime through its reversal mechanism for gross malpractice under BGB Section 630h. South Korea has adopted a relaxed standard for causation, effectively shifting the evidentiary burden in complex medical cases. The United States maintains the traditional plaintiff-bears-all-burden approach, requiring expert testimony in most states, though the discovery rule and res ipsa loquitur doctrine provide some relief. The ECHR imposes an investigative obligation on states but does not directly adjudicate individual malpractice claims.

### Damages and Compensation

The United States offers the highest potential damages awards, particularly in states without damages caps, where verdicts can reach tens of millions of dollars. However, the tort reform movement has imposed caps in 29 states. Germany follows the full compensation principle (Totalreparation) without caps but does not permit punitive damages. South Korea awards compensatory damages and consolation money but also lacks punitive damages. The ECHR's "just satisfaction" awards under Article 41 are typically modest compared to domestic awards.

### Alternative Dispute Resolution

All four systems have developed alternative pathways. South Korea's KMDMAA provides mediation with the added incentive of prosecution protection. Germany's medical arbitration boards resolve claims efficiently and without cost to the patient. Several U.S. states require pre-suit screening panels or permit binding arbitration. The EU Directive 2011/24/EU requires member states to provide transparent complaint procedures for cross-border patients.

### Informed Consent

Germany imposes the most rigorous informed consent requirements, with BGB Sections 630d-630e specifying detailed disclosure obligations and placing the burden of proving adequate consent on the physician. Under German law, a technically flawless procedure performed without valid consent constitutes bodily harm. The United States is split between the "physician standard" (what a reasonable physician would disclose) and the "patient standard" (what a reasonable patient would want to know), depending on the state. South Korea requires informed consent as an independent ground for liability. The ECHR protects autonomous medical decision-making under Article 8, as affirmed in V.C. v. Slovakia and Ternovszky v. Hungary.
