## Summary

Product liability -- the legal responsibility of manufacturers, distributors, and sellers for injuries caused by defective products -- has evolved from a negligence-based framework rooted in the privity doctrine to increasingly strict liability regimes across the world's major legal systems. South Korea enacted the Product Liability Act (제조물 책임법) in 2002, establishing a strict liability framework under which manufacturers are liable for defects in their products without proof of negligence, with a 2017 amendment shifting the burden of proof to manufacturers to demonstrate the absence of defect or causation in cases involving complex products where the defect origin is difficult for consumers to identify. The United States pioneered modern strict product liability through the landmark decisions in Greenman v. Yuba Power Products (1963) and the subsequent adoption of Section 402A of the Restatement (Second) of Torts, later refined by the Restatement (Third) of Torts: Products Liability (1998), which distinguishes among manufacturing defects, design defects, and warning defects with different liability standards for each. Germany applies the Produkthaftungsgesetz (ProdHaftG, Product Liability Act) of 1989, implementing EU Directive 85/374/EEC on liability for defective products, alongside the general tort provisions of Section 823 BGB, creating a dual-track system of strict liability under the ProdHaftG and fault-based liability with reversed burden of proof under the BGB. At the European level, the foundational Product Liability Directive 85/374/EEC established minimum harmonization of strict product liability across member states, and its successor, Directive (EU) 2024/2853 on liability for defective products adopted on October 23, 2024, modernizes the framework to address digital products, AI systems, and the circular economy, with member states required to transpose by December 9, 2026.

## South Korea

### Legal Framework

South Korea's Product Liability Act (제조물 책임법, Act No. 6109) was enacted on January 12, 2000, and entered into force on July 1, 2002. The Act establishes strict liability for manufacturers of defective products, removing the need for injured parties to prove fault on the part of the manufacturer.

**Definition of Product (Article 2(1)):** "Manufactured or processed movable property (including movable property incorporated into other movable property or immovable property)." Raw agricultural, fishery, and livestock products that have not been processed are excluded.

**Definition of Defect (Article 2(2)):** A defect exists when a product lacks the safety that may reasonably be expected, taking into account the characteristics and ordinarily foreseeable use of the product, the time when the product was supplied, and other circumstances. The Act recognizes manufacturing defects (제조상의 결함), design defects (설계상의 결함), and warning or instruction defects (표시상의 결함).

### 2017 Amendment: Reversed Burden of Proof

The most significant reform came through the 2017 amendment (effective April 19, 2018), which added Article 3-2 establishing a presumption of defect and causation. Under this provision, if a plaintiff proves that: (1) damage occurred during normal use of the product, (2) the damage was caused by the product, and (3) the damage would not ordinarily occur in the absence of a defect, then the defect and causation are presumed, shifting the burden to the manufacturer to rebut.

This amendment was driven by the Samsung Galaxy Note 7 battery explosion crisis of 2016 and the longstanding Oxy Reckitt Benckiser humidifier disinfectant scandal (가습기 살균제 사건), in which toxic chemicals in humidifier disinfectants caused over 1,500 deaths and thousands of injuries. In these cases, individual consumers faced insurmountable difficulties proving the precise nature of complex product defects.

### Defenses

| Defense | Article | Description |
|---------|---------|-------------|
| Non-supply | Article 4(1)(1) | Manufacturer did not supply the product |
| State of the art (development risk) | Article 4(1)(2) | Defect could not be discovered given the state of scientific/technical knowledge at the time of supply |
| Regulatory compliance | Article 4(1)(3) | Defect arose from compliance with mandatory government standards |
| Component manufacturer | Article 4(1)(4) | Defect attributable to design or instructions of the assembler, not the component manufacturer |

### Damages

The Act permits recovery for personal injury (including death) and property damage. Unlike the German ProdHaftG, there is no statutory aggregate cap on damages. However, South Korea does not recognize punitive damages in product liability cases -- compensation is limited to actual damages proved. The Supreme Court of Korea has permitted recovery of mental distress damages (위자료) in product liability cases.

### Landmark Case: Humidifier Disinfectant Litigation

The Oxy Reckitt Benckiser case represents the largest product liability crisis in Korean history. Beginning in 2011, the Korea Centers for Disease Control linked humidifier disinfectants containing PHMG and PGH to severe lung damage. Oxy Reckitt Benckiser's CEO Ata Safdar was convicted of negligent homicide and sentenced to 7 years in prison in 2017. Civil litigation resulted in settlements and court-ordered compensation exceeding 600 billion KRW. The crisis was a primary catalyst for the 2017 amendment strengthening consumer protections.

## United States

### Legal Framework

American product liability law is primarily state law, with no federal product liability statute. The evolution from privity-based negligence to strict liability represents one of the most significant developments in twentieth-century tort law.

**Historical Development:**
- Winterbottom v. Wright (1842): Privity doctrine bars negligence claims by non-contracting parties.
- MacPherson v. Buick Motor Co. (1916): Judge Cardozo eliminates privity requirement for negligence claims involving inherently dangerous products.
- Henningsen v. Bloomfield Motors (1960): Strict liability in warranty without privity.
- Greenman v. Yuba Power Products (1963): Justice Traynor establishes strict product liability in tort.
- Restatement (Second) of Torts Section 402A (1965): Adopts strict liability for products in "defective condition unreasonably dangerous."

**Restatement (Third) of Torts: Products Liability (1998):** The modern framework distinguishes three categories:

| Defect Type | Liability Standard | Key Feature |
|------------|-------------------|-------------|
| Manufacturing defect | Strict liability | Product departs from intended design; liability regardless of care exercised |
| Design defect | Reasonable alternative design test (risk-utility) | Plaintiff must show a feasible safer alternative design |
| Warning defect | Reasonable care (negligence-like) | Failure to provide adequate warnings or instructions |

### State Variation

Product liability law varies significantly across states:

**Liability Standards:** Some states retain the consumer expectation test for design defects (California, per Barker v. Lull Engineering, 1978, which applies both consumer expectation and risk-utility as alternative tests). Others adopt the Restatement (Third)'s risk-utility test exclusively.

**Damages Caps:** Many states have enacted tort reform legislation capping non-economic damages (e.g., Texas, $250,000 for medical malpractice; varying caps for product liability). Some states cap punitive damages at multiples of compensatory damages.

**Punitive Damages:** Available in most states for products liability involving conscious disregard of safety. The Supreme Court in BMW of North America, Inc. v. Gore (1996) and State Farm Mutual Automobile Insurance Co. v. Campbell (2003) imposed constitutional limits, suggesting that ratios exceeding single digits may violate due process.

### Federal Regulatory Framework

While product liability litigation is state law, federal agencies set product safety standards:
- Consumer Product Safety Commission (CPSC) -- general consumer products
- Food and Drug Administration (FDA) -- drugs, medical devices, food
- National Highway Traffic Safety Administration (NHTSA) -- motor vehicles
- Federal preemption of state tort claims is a recurring issue (Riegel v. Medtronic, 2008: express preemption for medical devices with FDA premarket approval)

## Germany

### Legal Framework

Germany operates a dual-track product liability system: strict liability under the Produkthaftungsgesetz (ProdHaftG) and fault-based liability with reversed burden of proof under the general tort provisions of the BGB.

**Produkthaftungsgesetz (ProdHaftG, 1989):** Enacted to implement EU Directive 85/374/EEC. Section 1(1) provides: "If a person is killed, his body or his health is injured, or a thing is damaged through a defect of a product, the producer of the product shall be obliged to compensate the injured person for the resulting damage."

**BGB Section 823(1):** "A person who, intentionally or negligently, unlawfully injures the life, body, health, freedom, property or another right of another person is liable to make compensation to the other party for the damage arising from this." In product liability cases, the BGH has reversed the burden of proof: once the plaintiff shows that the product was defective when it left the manufacturer's control, the manufacturer must prove that it was not at fault (BGH, Hühnerpest Decision, 1968).

### Dual-Track Comparison

| Feature | ProdHaftG | BGB §823 |
|---------|-----------|----------|
| Liability standard | Strict liability | Fault-based (reversed burden of proof) |
| Damages cap | EUR 85 million aggregate for personal injury | No cap |
| Property damage threshold | EUR 500 deductible | No deductible |
| Development risk defense | Available (§1(2)(5)) | Not available |
| Limitation | 3 years knowledge; 10-year long-stop | 3 years knowledge; 30-year long-stop |
| Pure economic loss | Not recoverable | Potentially recoverable under §823(2) with protective statute |

### Definition of Defect

Section 3 ProdHaftG defines a product as defective when it does not provide the safety that a person is entitled to expect, taking into account all circumstances, including the presentation of the product, the use to which it could reasonably be expected that the product would be put, and the time when the product was put into circulation.

### Development Risk Defense

Section 1(2)(5) ProdHaftG provides that liability is excluded if "the state of scientific and technical knowledge at the time when the producer put the product into circulation was not such as to enable the defect to be discovered." This defense is not available under BGB Section 823, creating a strategic advantage for plaintiffs who pursue the fault-based track for cutting-edge products.

### Product Safety Act (Produktsicherheitsgesetz, ProdSG)

The ProdSG (2011), implementing EU Directive 2001/95/EC on general product safety, establishes a comprehensive framework for product safety regulation, market surveillance, and recall procedures. The Federal Institute for Occupational Safety and Health (BAuA) coordinates the RAPEX rapid alert system for dangerous products at the national level.

## ECHR & EU

### Product Liability Directive 85/374/EEC

Council Directive 85/374/EEC of July 25, 1985, on the approximation of laws, regulations, and administrative provisions of the member states concerning liability for defective products, established the foundational EU-wide product liability framework. Key provisions:

**Strict Liability (Article 1):** "The producer shall be liable for damage caused by a defect in his product."

**Defect Definition (Article 6):** A product is defective when it does not provide the safety which a person is entitled to expect, taking all circumstances into account.

**Development Risk Defense (Article 7(e)):** Optional defense available at member state discretion. Most member states adopted it; France and Luxembourg did not for pharmaceutical products.

**Damages Cap (Article 16):** Member states may set a cap of not less than EUR 70 million for aggregate personal injury claims from identical items with the same defect.

### CJEU Jurisprudence

| Case | Year | Holding |
|------|------|---------|
| Commission v. France (C-52/00) | 2002 | France could not impose stricter liability than the Directive for non-defective products |
| Skov v. Bilka (C-402/03) | 2006 | Directive does not preclude national rules on burden of proof |
| Boston Scientific (C-503/13, C-504/13) | 2015 | Products belonging to a group with known defect rate may be classified as defective without proving individual product defect |
| Veedfald v. Århus Amtskommune (C-203/99) | 2002 | Directive applies to products used in provision of services |

### New Product Liability Directive (EU) 2024/2853

Directive (EU) 2024/2853 on liability for defective products, adopted on October 23, 2024, repeals and replaces Directive 85/374/EEC. Transposition deadline: December 9, 2026. Major changes:

**Expanded Product Definition:** Includes software, AI systems, digital manufacturing files (e.g., 3D printing files), and components integrated into other products. This addresses the long-standing gap where pure software was arguably excluded from the original Directive.

**Digital Products and AI:** Manufacturers of AI-enabled products are liable for defects, including defects arising from machine learning updates or the failure to provide necessary cybersecurity updates.

**Removal of Damages Cap:** The EUR 70 million aggregate cap for personal injury is removed.

**Disclosure of Evidence:** Courts may order the defendant to disclose relevant evidence in its possession, addressing the information asymmetry between consumers and manufacturers (Article 8).

**Presumption of Defect:** Article 9 establishes presumptions of defect and causal link in specified circumstances, including where the claimant demonstrates that the product did not comply with mandatory safety requirements, where the damage was caused by an obvious malfunction during normal use, and where the defendant fails to comply with a disclosure order.

**Limitation Period:** 3-year limitation from knowledge of damage, defect, and identity of producer. Long-stop period extended from 10 to 25 years for latent personal injury (Article 14).

### General Product Safety Regulation (EU) 2023/988

The General Product Safety Regulation, which replaced Directive 2001/95/EC and became applicable on December 13, 2024, establishes a comprehensive market surveillance and recall framework, including mandatory online marketplace obligations to remove dangerous products and enhanced Safety Gate (formerly RAPEX) rapid alert system.

## Comparative Analysis

### Strict Liability Convergence

All four jurisdictions have converged on some form of strict liability for product defects, though the scope and intensity vary. The United States was the earliest adopter (1963) and remains the most plaintiff-friendly jurisdiction due to the combination of strict liability, punitive damages, jury trials, and contingency fee arrangements. Germany's dual-track system provides both strict and fault-based avenues, with plaintiffs choosing strategically based on factors such as the development risk defense and damages caps. South Korea's 2017 reversal of the burden of proof moves it closer to practical strict liability, particularly for complex products. The EU's new Directive further strengthens the strict liability framework through presumptions and disclosure mechanisms.

### Punitive Damages and Deterrence

The United States is the only jurisdiction that permits punitive damages in product liability, serving both retributive and deterrent functions. Civil law systems rely on regulatory enforcement (fines, product recalls, criminal prosecution) for deterrence beyond compensatory damages. The absence of punitive damages in civil law jurisdictions is offset by more extensive regulatory oversight and the availability of criminal liability for manufacturers.

### Burden of Proof

The allocation of the burden of proof represents a critical practical distinction. South Korea's 2017 reform is the most explicit, creating a statutory presumption of defect and causation upon proof of basic facts. Germany achieves a similar result through the BGH's prima facie evidence doctrine (Anscheinsbeweis) and reversed burden of proof under BGB Section 823. The EU's new Directive 2024/2853 adopts statutory presumptions comparable to the Korean approach. In the United States, the burden allocation depends on the type of defect: manufacturing defects are proved through deviation from design specifications, while design defects require proof of a reasonable alternative design under the Restatement (Third).

### Digital Products and AI

The EU's new Directive 2024/2853 represents the global frontier in addressing liability for digital products and AI systems. South Korea, Germany (through ProdHaftG), and the United States have yet to adopt comparably comprehensive legislative frameworks for AI product liability, though all jurisdictions face growing pressure as AI-enabled products proliferate.
