## Summary

Franchise law governs the legal relationship between franchisors who license their business model, brand, and operational systems and franchisees who operate individual business units under that license, addressing issues of disclosure, registration, relationship conduct, termination, and dispute resolution that arise from the inherent power imbalance in franchise relationships. South Korea enacted the Fair Transactions in Franchise Business Act (가맹사업거래의 공정화에 관한 법률) in 2002, creating one of Asia's most comprehensive franchise regulatory frameworks, administered by the Korea Fair Trade Commission (KFTC); the Act was substantially amended in 2013 and 2017 to strengthen franchisee protections including mandatory cooling-off periods, restrictions on franchisor territorial encroachment, enhanced disclosure requirements, and penalties for information fraud, with the KFTC actively enforcing these provisions through administrative investigations, corrective orders, and substantial monetary penalties. The United States regulates franchising through the Federal Trade Commission's Franchise Rule (16 CFR Part 436), which requires pre-sale disclosure through a Franchise Disclosure Document (FDD), supplemented by state franchise laws in approximately 15 registration states and 20 relationship states that impose additional requirements ranging from mandatory registration and disclosure to substantive regulation of franchise relationship conduct including termination, non-renewal, and transfer restrictions. Germany lacks franchise-specific legislation, regulating franchise relationships through general contract law (BGB), commercial agency provisions of the Handelsgesetzbuch (HGB), competition law (GWB), and the judicially developed doctrine of pre-contractual disclosure obligations (vorvertragliche Aufklärungspflichten), while the Deutscher Franchise-Verband (German Franchise Association) promotes self-regulation through its Code of Ethics. The European Union has not adopted a harmonized franchise directive, though franchise relationships are affected by the Vertical Block Exemption Regulation (EU) 2022/720, the Commercial Agents Directive 86/653/EEC (by analogy), and the Platform-to-Business Regulation (EU) 2019/1150, while the European Franchise Federation promotes voluntary self-regulation through the European Code of Ethics for Franchising.

## South Korea

### Legal Framework

South Korea's franchise regulation is among the most comprehensive in Asia, centered on the Fair Transactions in Franchise Business Act (가맹사업거래의 공정화에 관한 법률), enacted on May 13, 2002, and administered by the Korea Fair Trade Commission.

**Registration and Disclosure:** All franchisors must register their franchise system with the KFTC before offering or selling franchises in South Korea. The registration requires submission of an Information Disclosure Document (정보공개서) containing detailed information about the franchisor's business history, financial condition, litigation history, franchise fees, estimated initial investment, territorial rights, training obligations, and financial performance representations. The KFTC maintains a public online database (가맹사업정보제공시스템) where prospective franchisees can access and compare disclosure documents.

### Key Regulatory Provisions

**14-Day Cooling-Off Period:** Prospective franchisees must receive the Information Disclosure Document at least 14 days before signing the franchise agreement or paying any fees. This provision, strengthened in the 2013 amendment, is intended to ensure that franchisees have adequate time to review the disclosure, seek professional advice, and make an informed decision.

**Territorial Protection (2013 Amendment):** Article 12-4, introduced in the 2013 amendment, prohibits franchisors from opening company-owned stores or granting additional franchises within an existing franchisee's designated territory without the franchisee's consent. This provision was enacted in response to widespread complaints about franchisor encroachment in densely populated Korean urban markets, particularly in the food and beverage sector.

**Restrictions on Unfair Practices:** Article 12 enumerates prohibited unfair franchise practices including requiring franchisees to purchase supplies exclusively from the franchisor or designated suppliers at above-market prices; imposing unreasonable sales targets; restricting franchisee business hours without justification; and retaliating against franchisees who form or join franchisee associations.

### Enforcement

The KFTC has pursued aggressive enforcement of franchise regulations. Notable actions include: substantial monetary penalties against major convenience store franchisors (CU, GS25, 7-Eleven Korea) for unfair pricing practices and territorial encroachment; corrective orders against coffee chain franchisors for misleading financial performance representations; and enforcement actions against restaurant franchise systems for imposing excessive supply purchase requirements.

### Franchisee Associations

South Korea is notable for its strong franchisee association movement. The Korean Franchise Association (한국프랜차이즈산업협회) represents franchisor interests, while individual franchisee groups have organized around specific franchise systems to negotiate collectively with franchisors. The 2017 amendments to the Act strengthened protections for franchisee association activities, prohibiting franchisor retaliation against franchisees who participate in collective action.

## United States

### Legal Framework

The United States employs a dual federal-state regulatory approach to franchise law.

**FTC Franchise Rule (16 CFR Part 436):** The Federal Trade Commission's Franchise Rule, originally promulgated in 1978 and comprehensively revised effective July 1, 2007, requires franchisors to provide prospective franchisees with a Franchise Disclosure Document (FDD) containing 23 specified items of information at least 14 calendar days before the prospective franchisee signs a binding agreement or pays any consideration. The 23 items include the franchisor's business background, litigation history, bankruptcy history, initial and ongoing fees, franchisee obligations, territory, financial performance representations, and audited financial statements.

**State Registration Laws:** Approximately 15 states require franchise registration or filing prior to offering or selling franchises within their borders. These states -- including California, New York, Illinois, Maryland, Minnesota, and Washington -- conduct substantive review of franchise offering documents, and state regulators may deny registration if the offering is deemed unfair, inequitable, or violative of state law.

### Arbitration in Franchise Disputes

Franchise agreements in the United States overwhelmingly contain mandatory arbitration clauses, often requiring arbitration at the franchisor's headquarters or a location convenient to the franchisor. The enforceability of these clauses under the Federal Arbitration Act (9 U.S.C. Sections 1-16) has been the subject of extensive litigation. While the Supreme Court has generally upheld arbitration clauses, some state franchise laws (e.g., California Business and Professions Code Section 20040.5) restrict the enforceability of forum selection and arbitration provisions that require franchisees to litigate or arbitrate outside their home state.

### Financial Performance Representations

Item 19 of the FDD permits but does not require franchisors to make financial performance representations (earnings claims). Franchisors who choose to include Item 19 must have a reasonable basis for their claims and must present them in a format specified by the FTC Rule. Approximately 65 percent of franchise systems now include Item 19 representations, up from roughly 30 percent prior to the 2007 rule revision.

## Germany

### Legal Framework

Germany does not have franchise-specific legislation, relying instead on general legal principles applied through several bodies of law.

**BGB Contract Law:** Franchise agreements are subject to the general contract law provisions of the BGB, including formation requirements (Sections 145-157), performance obligations (Sections 241-242), standard terms control (Sections 305-310), and termination provisions. The general clause of good faith (Treu und Glauben, Section 242 BGB) plays a particularly important role in franchise relationships, imposing duties of cooperation, information exchange, and fair dealing on both parties.

**Pre-Contractual Disclosure (Vorvertragliche Aufklärungspflichten):** German courts have developed a robust doctrine of pre-contractual disclosure obligations for franchise relationships, derived from the principle of culpa in contrahendo (Section 311(2) BGB). The Bundesgerichtshof (Federal Court of Justice) has held in a series of decisions that franchisors owe prospective franchisees a duty to disclose material information necessary for an informed investment decision, including the franchisor's financial condition, the profitability of existing franchise units, and any known risks or problems with the franchise system. Failure to make adequate disclosure can give rise to rescission of the franchise agreement and claims for damages under Sections 280, 311(2), 241(2) BGB.

### Competition Law Considerations

**GWB (Gesetz gegen Wettbewerbsbeschränkungen):** German competition law affects franchise relationships primarily through restrictions on vertical agreements. Franchise agreements that impose territorial restrictions, exclusive purchasing obligations, or resale price maintenance are assessed under both the GWB and the EU Vertical Block Exemption Regulation. Section 20 GWB prohibits abuse of relative market power, which may apply where a franchisor has a dominant position relative to its franchisees.

### Self-Regulation

The Deutscher Franchise-Verband (DFV, German Franchise Association), founded in 1978, promotes self-regulation through its Code of Ethics, which is based on the European Code of Ethics for Franchising. DFV membership requires compliance with the Code, which addresses pre-contractual disclosure, franchise agreement content, advertising standards, and dispute resolution. The DFV also operates a quality certification system (DFV-Systemcheck) that evaluates franchise systems against defined quality standards.

### Franchise Termination

Franchise termination in Germany is governed by general contract law principles. For indefinite-duration franchise agreements, ordinary termination (ordentliche Kündigung) requires reasonable notice, the length of which depends on the duration and nature of the relationship. Extraordinary termination (außerordentliche Kündigung) under Section 314 BGB is available for serious cause (wichtiger Grund), such as material breach, insolvency, or conduct that makes continued performance unreasonable.

### Commercial Agency Analogy

German courts have occasionally applied provisions of the HGB commercial agency law (Sections 84-92c) by analogy to franchise relationships, particularly regarding the franchisee's entitlement to a goodwill compensation payment (Ausgleichsanspruch) upon termination, analogous to the commercial agent's compensation under Section 89b HGB. This issue remains contested, with the Bundesgerichtshof applying the analogy selectively depending on the degree to which the franchisee is integrated into the franchisor's distribution system.

## ECHR & EU

### EU Competition Framework

The European Union's primary impact on franchise law comes through its competition rules governing vertical agreements.

**Vertical Block Exemption Regulation (EU) 2022/720:** This Regulation, which replaced Regulation (EU) 330/2010 and entered into force on June 1, 2022, provides a safe harbor for vertical agreements -- including franchise agreements -- where the market shares of both the franchisor and franchisee do not exceed 30 percent. The Regulation permits certain territorial and customer restrictions necessary for the operation of a franchise system, including obligations on the franchisee not to sell to certain categories of customers or outside an assigned territory, and obligations to use the franchisor's intellectual property only for the purposes of the franchise.

**Pronuptia de Paris v. Schillgalis (Case 161/84, 1986):** The landmark CJEU decision that established the framework for assessing franchise agreements under EU competition law. The Court held that franchise agreements are not per se restrictive of competition and that certain restrictions essential to protect the franchisor's know-how and the identity and reputation of the franchise network are permissible, including location clauses, quality standards, and purchasing obligations for goods essential to the franchise concept.

### European Code of Ethics for Franchising

The European Franchise Federation (EFF), which represents national franchise associations across Europe, has adopted a European Code of Ethics for Franchising that provides guidance on pre-contractual disclosure, franchise agreement content, advertising standards, and franchisee training. While not legally binding, the Code represents industry best practice and may be referenced by courts in assessing the reasonableness of franchise conduct.

### Platform-to-Business Regulation

Regulation (EU) 2019/1150 on promoting fairness and transparency for business users of online intermediation services, while not specifically targeting franchise relationships, affects franchise systems that operate through online platforms by imposing transparency requirements regarding ranking criteria, differential treatment, and data access.

### Harmonization Efforts

Despite periodic calls for a harmonized EU franchise law directive, the European Commission has not pursued franchise-specific legislation, relying instead on the combination of competition law, general contract law principles in member states, and self-regulatory mechanisms. The European Parliament's 2013 Resolution on franchising urged the Commission to develop minimum harmonization standards for pre-contractual disclosure, but this recommendation has not been implemented.

## Comparative Analysis

### Regulatory Intensity

The four systems represent a spectrum of regulatory intensity for franchise relationships. South Korea provides the most comprehensive regulatory framework, with mandatory registration, detailed disclosure requirements, a 14-day cooling-off period, substantive relationship protections including territorial encroachment restrictions, and active administrative enforcement by the KFTC. The United States follows with detailed federal disclosure requirements and varying state-level relationship protections, though federal law does not regulate the substantive franchise relationship. Germany relies primarily on general legal principles and self-regulation, with judicial development filling gaps through the pre-contractual disclosure doctrine and good faith principles. The EU framework focuses on competition law aspects of franchising without addressing disclosure or relationship conduct.

### Franchisee Protection Philosophy

The underlying philosophy of franchisee protection differs across jurisdictions. South Korea's approach reflects an explicit legislative determination that franchisees require protection comparable to that afforded to consumers, recognizing the information asymmetry and economic dependence inherent in franchise relationships. The United States takes a disclosure-based approach at the federal level, premised on the theory that informed franchisees can protect themselves through negotiation, while state relationship laws provide substantive protections where legislatures have determined that disclosure alone is insufficient. Germany's reliance on general contract law principles, supplemented by the good faith doctrine, reflects confidence in the judiciary's ability to address franchise-specific issues without dedicated legislation.

### Territorial Protection

Territorial protection reveals particularly sharp divergences. South Korea provides statutory territorial protection against franchisor encroachment, reflecting the extreme density of franchise outlets in Korean urban areas. The United States offers no federal territorial protection, leaving the issue to contract negotiation and limited state law provisions. Germany and the EU assess territorial exclusivity primarily through the lens of competition law, which may actually restrict the scope of territorial protections available to franchisees.
