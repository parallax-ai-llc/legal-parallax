## Summary

The age of consent -- the minimum age at which a person is legally considered capable of consenting to sexual activity -- represents one of the most culturally variable and politically contested areas of criminal law, reflecting deeply held societal views about adolescent autonomy, sexual morality, child protection, and the limits of state intervention in private life. South Korea raised its age of consent from 13 to 16 years through amendments to the Criminal Act (형법) and the Act on the Protection of Children and Youth Against Sex Offenses (아동·청소년의 성보호에 관한 법률) effective September 2020, ending decades of international criticism that Korea maintained one of the lowest ages of consent among developed nations; sexual acts with a person under 16 are now punishable by imprisonment of up to 10 years or a fine of up to KRW 50 million under Article 305 of the Criminal Act (의제강간·의제강제추행). The United States has no uniform federal age of consent, with individual states setting the age between 16 and 18 years, though federal law under 18 U.S.C. Section 2243 criminalizes sexual abuse of a minor between 12 and 16 by a person at least four years older, and the PROTECT Act of 2003 (18 U.S.C. Section 2423) criminalizes traveling in interstate or foreign commerce to engage in sexual activity with a person under 18. Germany sets the general age of consent at 14 years under Section 176 of the Strafgesetzbuch (StGB), one of the lowest in Europe, but provides heightened protections for persons aged 14-17 in situations involving exploitation of a position of trust, authority, or dependency (Section 174 StGB), and criminalizes sexual acts with persons under 18 involving compensation (Section 182(1) StGB) or exploitation of a coercive situation (Section 182(2) StGB). At the European level, the Council of Europe Convention on the Protection of Children against Sexual Exploitation and Sexual Abuse (Lanzarote Convention, 2007) requires signatory states to establish an age below which sexual activity with a child is criminalized regardless of consent, while EU Directive 2011/93/EU on combating the sexual abuse and sexual exploitation of children mandates criminal penalties for sexual activity with a child below the age of sexual consent as defined by national law, and the European Court of Human Rights has addressed age of consent through its Article 8 (private life) and Article 14 (non-discrimination) jurisprudence, notably in the landmark case of S.N. v. Sweden (Application No. 34209/96, 2002).

## South Korea

### Legal Framework

South Korea's age of consent law underwent a transformative reform in 2020, raising the threshold from 13 to 16 years -- a change that had been demanded by child protection advocates, the UN Committee on the Rights of the Child, and international human rights organizations for over two decades.

**Article 305 of the Criminal Act (형법 제305조 -- 의제강간·의제강제추행):** Prior to 2020, this provision criminalized sexual intercourse or sexual acts with a person under 13 years of age, regardless of consent. The September 2020 amendment raised this age to 16, meaning that any sexual intercourse with a person under 16 constitutes statutory rape (의제강간) punishable by imprisonment of 3 years or more, and any sexual molestation of a person under 16 constitutes statutory indecent assault (의제강제추행) punishable by imprisonment of up to 10 years or a fine of up to KRW 50 million.

**Act on the Protection of Children and Youth Against Sex Offenses (아동·청소년의 성보호에 관한 법률):** This comprehensive statute, originally enacted in 2000 and substantially amended in 2010, 2012, 2020, and 2023, provides a specialized framework for sexual offenses against persons under 19 years of age (the Korean legal definition of "child or youth," 아동·청소년). Key provisions include:

- **Article 7:** Rape of a child or youth (under 19) is punishable by imprisonment of 5 years or more. Rape of a child under 13 is punishable by life imprisonment or imprisonment of at least 10 years.
- **Article 8:** Sexual intercourse or sexual acts through the use of force, intimidation, or quasi-force with a disabled child or youth is punishable by life imprisonment or imprisonment of at least 7 years.
- **Article 10:** Sexual harassment of a child or youth through communication media is punishable by imprisonment of up to 3 years.

**Aggravated Penalties for Persons in Authority (업무상 위력 등에 의한 추행):** Under Article 303 of the Criminal Act, persons who engage in sexual acts with minors through the abuse of a business, employment, or other relationship of authority face enhanced penalties of up to 7 years imprisonment.

| Sex offender registration violation | Imprisonment up to 3 years or fine up to KRW 10 million |

### Sex Offender Registration

Under the Act on the Protection of Children and Youth Against Sex Offenses, convicted sex offenders are subject to mandatory registration and notification. The registration period ranges from 10 to 30 years depending on the severity of the offense. Personal information including name, photograph, and residential address is made available through the sex offender registry administered by the Ministry of Justice. Employment restrictions prohibit registered offenders from working in child-related facilities for up to 10 years.

### Historical Context

South Korea's age of consent of 13 had been unchanged since the enactment of the Criminal Act in 1953, reflecting the social norms of the post-Korean War era. The 2008 Jo Doo-soon case (조두순 사건), involving the brutal sexual assault of an 8-year-old girl in Ansan, Gyeonggi Province, galvanized public outrage and triggered a series of legislative reforms including chemical castration authorization (2010), enhanced sex offender monitoring (2012), and ultimately the 2020 age of consent increase. The case was dramatized in the 2013 film "Hope" (소원), further amplifying public awareness.

## United States

### Legal Framework

The United States operates a uniquely decentralized system in which the age of consent is determined by each state independently, with federal law applying only in specific jurisdictional contexts.

**State Variation:** The age of consent ranges from 16 (in 31 states and the District of Columbia) to 17 (in 8 states) to 18 (in 11 states). This variation reflects differing state legislative judgments about adolescent sexual autonomy and child protection.

**Close-in-Age Exemptions (Romeo and Juliet Laws):** Many states provide statutory exemptions or affirmative defenses for sexual activity between peers of similar age. For example, Texas Penal Code Section 22.011(e) provides a defense to statutory rape where the actor is not more than three years older than the victim and the victim is at least 14 years old. These provisions recognize that criminalizing consensual sexual activity between adolescents of similar age may be disproportionate and counterproductive.

**Federal Law -- 18 U.S.C. Section 2243 (Sexual Abuse of a Minor):** This statute criminalizes sexual acts with a person between 12 and 16 years of age where the perpetrator is at least four years older, applying in federal jurisdictions (Indian reservations, military installations, federal territories). The penalty is a fine and/or imprisonment of up to 15 years.

**18 U.S.C. Section 2241(c) (Aggravated Sexual Abuse of Children):** Sexual acts with a person under 12, or with a person under 16 using force or threat, carry penalties of imprisonment of not less than 30 years to life.

**PROTECT Act of 2003 (18 U.S.C. Section 2423):** This statute criminalizes traveling in interstate or foreign commerce with intent to engage in sexual activity with a person under 18, regardless of the age of consent in the destination state or country. The penalty is imprisonment of up to 30 years.

### Key Case Law

**Lawrence v. Texas, 539 U.S. 558 (2003):** While primarily addressing the constitutionality of sodomy laws between consenting adults, the Supreme Court explicitly noted that the case did not involve minors, preserving state authority to set and enforce age of consent laws.

**State v. Limon, 280 Kan. 275 (2005):** The Kansas Supreme Court struck down a "Romeo and Juliet" statute that provided reduced penalties for consensual heterosexual activity between teens but not for homosexual activity, finding the differential treatment violated equal protection under the Fourteenth Amendment.

**Stogner v. California, 539 U.S. 607 (2003):** The Supreme Court struck down a California law reviving expired statutes of limitations for child sex abuse, holding that such retroactive extension violated the Ex Post Facto Clause.

### Sex Offender Registration

The Sex Offender Registration and Notification Act (SORNA), Title I of the Adam Walsh Child Protection and Safety Act of 2006 (42 U.S.C. Section 16901 et seq.), establishes minimum national standards for sex offender registration. Tier III offenders (those convicted of the most serious offenses, including sexual abuse of a child under 13) must register for life. The constitutionality of sex offender registration was upheld in Smith v. Doe, 538 U.S. 84 (2003), where the Court held that registration is a civil regulatory measure rather than criminal punishment.

## Germany

### Legal Framework

Germany maintains one of the lowest ages of sexual consent among Western democracies at 14 years, though this baseline is accompanied by a nuanced system of graduated protections for persons aged 14-17.

**Section 176 StGB -- Sexual Abuse of Children (Sexueller Missbrauch von Kindern):** Sexual acts with or in front of a person under 14 years of age are punishable by imprisonment from 1 to 15 years. Following the Act to Combat Sexualized Violence against Children of June 2021 (Gesetz zur Bekämpfung sexualisierter Gewalt gegen Kinder), all sexual abuse of children under 14 was reclassified from a Vergehen (misdemeanor) to a Verbrechen (felony) with a mandatory minimum sentence of one year.

**Section 174 StGB -- Sexual Abuse of Persons Entrusted (Sexueller Missbrauch von Schutzbefohlenen):** Sexual acts with a person under 18 who is entrusted to the perpetrator for upbringing, education, training, or care are punishable by imprisonment from 3 months to 5 years. This provision extends the effective age of consent to 18 in relationships involving a position of trust.

**Section 182 StGB -- Sexual Abuse of Juveniles (Sexueller Missbrauch von Jugendlichen):** This provision addresses sexual activity with persons aged 14-17 in two specific situations: (1) sexual acts performed in exchange for compensation (Entgelt) are punishable by imprisonment up to 5 years; (2) sexual acts involving exploitation of the victim's lack of capacity for sexual self-determination (Fehlen der Fähigkeit zur sexuellen Selbstbestimmung) by a person over 21 are punishable by imprisonment up to 3 years. Notably, Section 182(3) allows the court to dispense with punishment if the wrongfulness of the act is minor having regard to the conduct of the person against whom the act was directed.

**Section 176a StGB -- Serious Sexual Abuse of Children:** Aggravated forms including penetrative acts and offenses by multiple perpetrators carry penalties of imprisonment from 2 to 15 years.

### Reform of 2021

The Act to Combat Sexualized Violence against Children (Gesetz zur Bekämpfung sexualisierter Gewalt gegen Kinder), which entered into force on July 1, 2021, introduced several significant changes:

- Reclassification of child sexual abuse from Vergehen to Verbrechen, raising the minimum sentence from 6 months to 1 year
- Reclassification of possession of child sexual abuse material from Vergehen to Verbrechen
- Extension of statute of limitations, which now begins running only when the victim turns 30
- Enhanced investigation powers including authority for undercover agents to use computer-generated images

### Debate on Raising the Age of Consent

Unlike most European countries with ages of consent of 15 or 16, Germany has maintained its age of 14 since the reunification alignment of East and West German law in 1994 (East Germany had set the age at 14 since 1968; West Germany at 14 since the 1973 reform). Periodic calls to raise the age to 16 have been resisted on the grounds that the graduated system of Sections 174 and 182 provides adequate protection without criminalizing consensual sexual exploration among teenagers.

## ECHR & EU

### Lanzarote Convention (2007)

The Council of Europe Convention on the Protection of Children against Sexual Exploitation and Sexual Abuse (CETS No. 201), opened for signature on October 25, 2007, in Lanzarote, Spain, is the most comprehensive international treaty addressing child sexual exploitation.

**Article 18 -- Sexual Abuse:** States parties must criminalize engaging in sexual activities with a child who, according to relevant national law, has not reached the legal age for sexual activities. Article 18(2) requires states to determine the age below which it is prohibited to engage in sexual activities with a child. Article 18(3) importantly provides that the criminalizing provisions "are not intended to govern consensual sexual activities between minors."

**Monitoring Mechanism:** The Lanzarote Committee monitors implementation through country-specific questionnaires and thematic reports. As of 2026, the Convention has been ratified by 49 states.

### EU Directive 2011/93/EU

Directive 2011/93/EU of the European Parliament and of the Council of December 13, 2011, on combating the sexual abuse and sexual exploitation of children, replaced the earlier Framework Decision 2004/68/JHA.

**Article 3(4):** Member states must criminalize engaging in sexual activities with a child who has not reached the age of sexual consent as defined by national law. The Directive does not mandate a uniform age of consent but requires that whatever age is set, violations must be effectively prosecuted and sanctioned.

**Article 3(5)(i):** The Directive requires maximum penalties of at least 5 years imprisonment for sexual abuse of a child below the age of consent.

**Article 8 -- Online Grooming:** The Directive criminalizes soliciting a child below the age of sexual consent for sexual purposes through information and communication technology, with a required maximum penalty of at least one year imprisonment.

### European Court of Human Rights

**M.C. v. Bulgaria (Application No. 39272/98, Judgment of December 4, 2003):** This landmark case established that states have a positive obligation under Articles 3 and 8 ECHR to penalize and effectively prosecute non-consensual sexual acts, including cases where the victim did not physically resist. The Court held that Bulgarian authorities violated their positive obligations by requiring evidence of physical resistance in a case involving a 14-year-old complainant.

**S.N. v. Sweden (Application No. 34209/96, Judgment of July 2, 2002):** The Court addressed the balance between the rights of a person accused of child sexual abuse and the need to protect child witnesses from repeated traumatization during criminal proceedings.

**Söderman v. Sweden (Application No. 5786/08, Grand Chamber Judgment of November 12, 2013):** The Court found a violation of Article 8 where Swedish law failed to provide adequate protection against covert filming of a 14-year-old stepdaughter, establishing that states must ensure effective criminal law provisions to protect children's physical and moral integrity.

**Discrimination in Age of Consent:** In S.L. v. Austria (Application No. 45330/99, Judgment of January 9, 2003), the Court found that Austria's differential age of consent for homosexual acts (18) compared to heterosexual acts (14) violated Article 14 read with Article 8. This judgment prompted Austria and several other European states to equalize their ages of consent.

## Comparative Analysis

### Age Thresholds and Cultural Context

The variation in ages of consent -- from 14 in Germany to 16-18 in the United States -- reflects different cultural and philosophical approaches to adolescent sexuality. Civil law systems in continental Europe tend to adopt lower base ages combined with graduated protections, emphasizing the distinction between exploitation and consensual peer activity. Common law systems, particularly in the United States, tend to set higher uniform thresholds, reflecting a more protective stance toward all adolescent sexual activity.

### Graduated vs. Bright-Line Approaches

Germany exemplifies the graduated approach, setting a low base age of 14 but providing escalating protections through Sections 174 (position of trust), 182 (exploitation and compensation), and 176 (under 14). This system attempts to distinguish between exploitative and non-exploitative sexual relationships involving adolescents. The United States, by contrast, primarily employs a bright-line approach with limited close-in-age exemptions, creating clearer legal certainty but potentially over-criminalizing consensual adolescent behavior.

### Position of Trust Provisions

All four jurisdictions recognize that relationships involving a power differential require heightened protection. South Korea's Article 303 addresses abuse of workplace authority, the United States raises the effective age to 18 for authority figures in most states, and Germany's Section 174 covers educators, trainers, and caregivers. The Lanzarote Convention's Article 18(2) specifically addresses abuse of a recognized position of trust.

### International Pressure and Reform

South Korea's 2020 reform illustrates the role of international pressure in age of consent legislation. The UN Committee on the Rights of the Child repeatedly recommended that South Korea raise its age of consent from 13, and the reform was partly driven by a desire to align with international norms. Germany has faced similar pressure but has resisted reform, arguing that its graduated system provides equivalent protection.
