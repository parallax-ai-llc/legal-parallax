## Summary

Critical infrastructure protection law governs the security obligations, incident reporting requirements, and regulatory oversight of essential services and systems whose disruption would have significant societal impact. South Korea regulates critical infrastructure through the Act on the Protection of Information and Communications Infrastructure (정보통신기반 보호법, enacted 2001) and the Cybersecurity Basic Act (사이버안보 기본법, enacted 2024), designating critical information and communications infrastructure under the oversight of the Ministry of Science and ICT and the National Intelligence Service (NIS), with administrative penalties for non-compliance and mandatory vulnerability assessments. The United States relies on a sector-specific framework anchored by the Cybersecurity and Infrastructure Security Agency Act of 2018 (establishing CISA), Presidential Policy Directive 21 (PPD-21, 2013) identifying 16 critical infrastructure sectors, and the Cyber Incident Reporting for Critical Infrastructure Act (CIRCIA, 2022) mandating 72-hour incident reporting. Germany implements critical infrastructure protection through the BSI Act (BSI-Gesetz, BSIG) as amended by the IT Security Act 2.0 (IT-Sicherheitsgesetz 2.0, 2021) and the KRITIS Regulation (BSI-KritisV), administered by the Federal Office for Information Security (BSI), requiring operators to implement state-of-the-art security measures and report significant incidents within 24 hours. At the EU level, the NIS2 Directive (Directive (EU) 2022/2555) -- replacing the original NIS Directive (2016/1148) -- dramatically expands the scope of regulated entities, imposes harmonized cybersecurity risk management obligations, introduces mandatory 24-hour early warning and 72-hour incident notification requirements, and establishes administrative fines of up to EUR 10 million or 2% of global turnover for essential entities.

## South Korea

### Legal Framework

South Korea's critical infrastructure protection regime has evolved significantly over two decades, culminating in the comprehensive Cybersecurity Basic Act of 2024:

**Act on the Protection of Information and Communications Infrastructure (정보통신기반 보호법, 2001):** This foundational statute establishes the framework for identifying and protecting critical information and communications infrastructure. The Act mandates that the head of each central administrative agency designate critical information and communications infrastructure facilities under their jurisdiction, subject to review by the Critical Information and Communications Infrastructure Protection Committee (정보통신기반보호위원회).

**Cybersecurity Basic Act (사이버안보 기본법, 2024):** After years of legislative debate, South Korea enacted comprehensive cybersecurity legislation establishing a National Cybersecurity Committee under the President, designating the NIS as the coordinating authority for national cybersecurity, and imposing binding cybersecurity obligations on operators of critical infrastructure across all sectors.

**Information and Communications Network Act (정보통신망 이용촉진 및 정보보호 등에 관한 법률):** Provides additional cybersecurity requirements for information and communications service providers, including data breach notification obligations.

### Designated Infrastructure

Under the Act on the Protection of Information and Communications Infrastructure, the government designates specific facilities as critical information and communications infrastructure through deliberation by the Protection Committee. Designated sectors include:

- **Information and communications:** Telecommunications networks, internet exchange points, DNS infrastructure
- **Energy:** Electric power systems (KEPCO), gas supply systems, petroleum supply infrastructure
- **Finance:** Banking systems (including the Bank of Korea's BOK-Wire+), securities trading systems, payment and settlement infrastructure
- **Transportation:** Air traffic control, railway signaling systems, maritime traffic management
- **Healthcare:** National health insurance information systems, hospital information networks
- **Government:** E-government systems, national defense information systems, public safety communications

### Obligations of Operators

Operators of designated critical infrastructure must:

1. **Develop Protection Plans:** Establish and implement comprehensive protection measures plans (보호대책) addressing physical security, access control, intrusion detection, backup and recovery, and personnel security.

2. **Conduct Vulnerability Assessments:** Perform mandatory vulnerability assessments (취약점 분석·평가) at least every two years, conducted either by the operator's own security team or by designated external assessment agencies.

3. **Report Incidents:** Report significant cybersecurity incidents to the Korea Internet & Security Agency (KISA) and the relevant supervisory ministry within prescribed timeframes. Under the Cybersecurity Basic Act, operators must provide initial notification within hours of detecting a significant incident.

4. **Participate in Exercises:** Engage in regular cybersecurity drills and exercises coordinated by the National Intelligence Service and KISA.

### Penalties

- **Failure to implement protection measures:** Administrative fines of up to 30 million KRW.
- **Failure to conduct vulnerability assessments:** Administrative fines of up to 20 million KRW.
- **Attacks on critical infrastructure:** Criminal penalties of up to 10 years imprisonment under Article 12 of the Act on the Protection of Information and Communications Infrastructure for intentional disruption, damage, or destruction of designated infrastructure.
- **Unauthorized access:** Imprisonment of up to 5 years or fines up to 50 million KRW.

### Institutional Framework

**National Intelligence Service (NIS):** Serves as the national coordinating authority for cybersecurity of public-sector critical infrastructure and exercises supervisory authority over government network security.

**Korea Internet & Security Agency (KISA):** Operates the Korea Computer Emergency Response Team Coordination Center (KrCERT/CC), provides technical support to critical infrastructure operators, and manages the national incident response framework for the private sector.

**National Cybersecurity Committee:** Established under the 2024 Cybersecurity Basic Act as a presidential-level coordinating body, responsible for setting national cybersecurity policy priorities and coordinating cross-ministerial responses to major cyber incidents.

### Landmark Incidents and Responses

**DarkSeoul Attack (2013):** A coordinated cyberattack attributed to North Korea disrupted banking and broadcasting systems across South Korea, affecting KBS, MBC, YTN, Shinhan Bank, and Nonghyup Bank. The attack accelerated the passage of strengthened cybersecurity measures and the expansion of designated critical infrastructure.

**Korea Hydro & Nuclear Power Breach (2014):** Hackers, later attributed to North Korea, breached Korea Hydro & Nuclear Power Co. (KHNP) and leaked internal documents including nuclear reactor designs. The incident prompted the designation of nuclear power systems as critical information and communications infrastructure and led to enhanced security requirements for the energy sector.

## United States

### Legal Framework

The United States employs a predominantly sector-specific approach to critical infrastructure protection, with CISA serving as the national coordinator:

**Cybersecurity and Infrastructure Security Agency Act of 2018 (6 U.S.C. §§ 651-674):** Established CISA within the Department of Homeland Security as the lead federal agency for cybersecurity and critical infrastructure protection. CISA's mandate includes coordinating vulnerability disclosures, providing technical assistance, conducting threat assessments, and serving as the federal civilian cybersecurity coordinator.

**Presidential Policy Directive 21 (PPD-21, 2013):** Identifies 16 critical infrastructure sectors and designates Sector Risk Management Agencies (SRMAs) for each: Chemical; Commercial Facilities; Communications; Critical Manufacturing; Dams; Defense Industrial Base; Emergency Services; Energy; Financial Services; Food and Agriculture; Government Facilities; Healthcare and Public Health; Information Technology; Nuclear Reactors, Materials, and Waste; Transportation Systems; Water and Wastewater Systems.

**Cyber Incident Reporting for Critical Infrastructure Act (CIRCIA, 2022):** Mandates that covered entities report substantial cyber incidents to CISA within 72 hours and ransomware payments within 24 hours. CISA published its final rulemaking in 2025, defining covered entities and reportable incidents.

**Executive Order 14028 on Improving the Nation's Cybersecurity (May 12, 2021):** Imposed sweeping cybersecurity requirements on federal agencies and their contractors, including mandatory adoption of zero-trust architecture, software supply chain security requirements (Software Bill of Materials -- SBOM), enhanced logging and detection, and deployment of endpoint detection and response (EDR) solutions.

### Sector-Specific Regulation

Unlike the EU's harmonized NIS2 approach, the United States regulates critical infrastructure cybersecurity primarily through sector-specific statutes and regulations:

**Energy Sector:** The Federal Energy Regulatory Commission (FERC) enforces mandatory Critical Infrastructure Protection (CIP) standards developed by the North American Electric Reliability Corporation (NERC). NERC CIP standards (CIP-002 through CIP-014) impose detailed cybersecurity requirements on bulk electric system operators, with penalties of up to $1 million per violation per day.

**Financial Sector:** The Gramm-Leach-Bliley Act (GLBA) and implementing regulations (FTC Safeguards Rule, OCC heightened standards) require financial institutions to implement comprehensive information security programs. The SEC adopted cybersecurity disclosure rules in 2023, requiring material incident disclosure within four business days.

**Healthcare Sector:** The Health Insurance Portability and Accountability Act (HIPAA) Security Rule (45 C.F.R. Part 164, Subpart C) requires covered entities and business associates to implement administrative, physical, and technical safeguards for electronic protected health information (ePHI). The HHS proposed enhanced cybersecurity requirements in 2024.

**Water Sector:** The EPA has used its authority under the Safe Drinking Water Act to require cybersecurity assessments for community water systems serving more than 3,300 persons, though this authority has been legally contested.

**Transportation Sector:** The Transportation Security Administration (TSA) issued emergency Security Directives in 2021-2022 following the Colonial Pipeline attack, requiring pipeline operators to implement cybersecurity measures and report incidents.

### NIST Cybersecurity Framework

The National Institute of Standards and Technology (NIST) Cybersecurity Framework (CSF), originally published in 2014 and updated to version 2.0 in February 2024, provides a voluntary, risk-based framework organized around six functions: Govern, Identify, Protect, Detect, Respond, and Recover. While not legally mandatory for most private-sector entities, the framework serves as a de facto standard referenced by sector-specific regulators and contractual requirements.

### Key Incidents Driving Policy

**Colonial Pipeline Attack (May 2021):** A ransomware attack by the DarkSide group forced Colonial Pipeline to shut down its 5,500-mile pipeline carrying 45% of the East Coast's fuel supply. The incident catalyzed the passage of CIRCIA and TSA Security Directives for the pipeline sector.

**SolarWinds Supply Chain Attack (December 2020):** Russian state-sponsored actors compromised the SolarWinds Orion software update mechanism, infiltrating nine federal agencies and approximately 100 private companies. The attack drove Executive Order 14028's supply chain security requirements and SBOM mandates.

**Change Healthcare Attack (February 2024):** A ransomware attack on UnitedHealth Group's Change Healthcare unit disrupted healthcare payment processing nationwide for weeks, affecting pharmacies, hospitals, and insurers. The incident intensified calls for mandatory cybersecurity standards in the healthcare sector.

## Germany

### Legal Framework

Germany has one of Europe's most developed critical infrastructure protection regimes, anchored in the BSI Act as progressively strengthened:

**BSI Act (BSI-Gesetz, BSIG):** Establishes the Federal Office for Information Security (Bundesamt für Sicherheit in der Informationstechnik -- BSI) as the central cybersecurity authority. The BSI Act, as amended by the IT Security Act 2.0 (2021), defines critical infrastructure operators' obligations and the BSI's supervisory powers.

**IT Security Act 1.0 (IT-Sicherheitsgesetz, 2015):** Germany's first comprehensive cybersecurity statute, requiring operators of critical infrastructure (KRITIS-Betreiber) to implement appropriate organizational and technical measures to secure their IT systems and to report significant IT security incidents to the BSI.

**IT Security Act 2.0 (IT-Sicherheitsgesetz 2.0, 2021):** Substantially expanded the scope and enforcement powers of the original act. Key provisions include:
- Extension of obligations to "companies of particular public interest" (Unternehmen im besonderen öffentlichen Interesse -- UBI), including defense contractors and companies of significant economic importance.
- Introduction of the BSI's authority to order specific cybersecurity measures.
- Mandatory use of attack detection systems (Systeme zur Angriffserkennung) by KRITIS operators, effective May 1, 2023.
- Enhanced product security requirements, including the BSI's authority to issue warnings about insecure IT products.
- Increased fines of up to EUR 20 million for non-compliance.

**KRITIS Regulation (BSI-Kritisverordnung, BSI-KritisV):** Defines sector-specific thresholds for determining which facilities qualify as critical infrastructure based on their supply capacity (e.g., a power plant serving 500,000 or more persons).

**KRITIS-Dachgesetz (2023):** Germany's transposition of the EU Critical Entities Resilience (CER) Directive, addressing the physical resilience of critical infrastructure alongside the cybersecurity requirements of the BSIG.

### Designated Sectors and Thresholds

The BSI-KritisV defines critical infrastructure across the following sectors with specific numeric thresholds:

| Sector | Example Threshold |
|--------|------------------|
| Energy | Power plants serving ≥ 500,000 persons |
| Water | Water supply serving ≥ 500,000 persons |
| Food | Facilities with ≥ 434,500 t annual production |
| IT and Telecommunications | DNS services with ≥ 250,000 domain names |
| Health | Hospitals with ≥ 30,000 inpatient cases/year |
| Finance | Payment systems processing ≥ 15 million transactions/year |
| Transport and Traffic | Airports with ≥ 20 million passengers/year |
| Waste Management | Facilities serving ≥ 500,000 persons |
| Space | Ground-based infrastructure for satellite operations |

### Obligations of KRITIS Operators

1. **Security Measures:** Implement appropriate organizational and technical measures to avoid disruptions to the availability, integrity, authenticity, and confidentiality of their IT systems, components, and processes. Measures must reflect the "state of the art" (Stand der Technik).

2. **Attack Detection Systems:** Since May 1, 2023, KRITIS operators must deploy systems for attack detection (Systeme zur Angriffserkennung) that continuously monitor for and report anomalies and threats.

3. **Incident Reporting:** Report significant IT security incidents to the BSI without undue delay. The BSI-Gesetz requires:
   - Initial notification within 24 hours of becoming aware of a significant incident.
   - Updated notification with initial assessment within 72 hours.
   - Final report within one month.

4. **Biennial Audits:** Demonstrate compliance with security requirements through audits, inspections, or certifications conducted at least every two years, with results reported to the BSI.

5. **Registration:** Register with the BSI and designate a contact point available 24/7.

### BSI Enforcement Powers

The IT Security Act 2.0 significantly strengthened the BSI's enforcement toolkit:

- **Orders:** The BSI may issue binding orders requiring operators to implement specific security measures or remediate identified vulnerabilities.
- **Inspections:** The BSI may conduct on-site inspections and request documentation.
- **Fines:** Administrative fines of up to EUR 2 million for most violations, up to EUR 20 million for critical failures.
- **Product warnings:** The BSI may publicly warn about security deficiencies in IT products and services.

### NIS2 Transposition

Germany is transposing the NIS2 Directive through the NIS2 Implementation and Cybersecurity Strengthening Act (NIS2-Umsetzungs- und Cybersicherheitsstärkungsgesetz -- NIS2UmsuCG), which will further expand the scope of regulated entities and align German requirements with the harmonized EU framework.

## ECHR/EU Framework

### NIS2 Directive (Directive (EU) 2022/2555)

The NIS2 Directive, adopted on December 14, 2022, and requiring transposition by October 17, 2024, represents the most ambitious cybersecurity legislation in EU history. Key provisions include:

**Expanded Scope:** NIS2 dramatically broadens the range of regulated entities compared to the original NIS Directive:

- **Essential Entities (Annex I -- 11 sectors):** Energy (electricity, oil, gas, hydrogen, district heating); Transport (air, rail, water, road); Banking; Financial market infrastructures; Health; Drinking water; Waste water; Digital infrastructure; ICT service management (B2B); Public administration; Space.
- **Important Entities (Annex II -- 7 sectors):** Postal and courier services; Waste management; Manufacturing of certain critical products; Food production, processing, and distribution; Chemical manufacturing; Digital providers (online marketplaces, search engines, social networking platforms); Research.

**Size-Cap Rule:** Medium-sized enterprises (50+ employees or EUR 10 million+ turnover) and large enterprises in covered sectors are automatically within scope, eliminating the previous designation-based approach.

**Risk Management Measures (Article 21):** Essential and important entities must implement appropriate and proportionate technical, operational, and organizational measures, including at minimum:
- Risk analysis and information system security policies
- Incident handling procedures
- Business continuity and crisis management (including backup management and disaster recovery)
- Supply chain security
- Security in network and information systems acquisition, development, and maintenance
- Vulnerability handling and disclosure
- Cybersecurity risk management assessment effectiveness
- Basic cyber hygiene practices and cybersecurity training
- Cryptography and encryption policies
- Human resources security, access control, and asset management
- Multi-factor authentication and secured communications

**Incident Reporting (Article 23):** A three-stage reporting regime:
1. **Early warning:** Within 24 hours of becoming aware of a significant incident, indicating whether the incident is suspected of being caused by unlawful or malicious acts or could have cross-border impact.
2. **Incident notification:** Within 72 hours, providing an initial assessment including severity, impact, and indicators of compromise.
3. **Final report:** Within one month, including a detailed description, root cause analysis, mitigation measures, and cross-border impact.

**Management Body Accountability (Article 20):** Management bodies of essential and important entities must approve cybersecurity risk management measures, supervise their implementation, and can be held personally liable for non-compliance. Members of management bodies must undergo regular cybersecurity training.

**Penalties (Article 34):**
- Essential entities: Administrative fines of up to EUR 10 million or 2% of total worldwide annual turnover, whichever is higher.
- Important entities: Administrative fines of up to EUR 7 million or 1.4% of total worldwide annual turnover, whichever is higher.

### CER Directive (Directive (EU) 2022/2557)

The Critical Entities Resilience (CER) Directive, adopted alongside NIS2, addresses the physical resilience of critical entities. It requires Member States to adopt national strategies for critical entity resilience, conduct national risk assessments, identify critical entities, and ensure that those entities implement measures to enhance their physical resilience against natural hazards, terrorist attacks, insider threats, and sabotage.

### ENISA and Cooperation Mechanisms

The European Union Agency for Cybersecurity (ENISA) plays a central coordinating role under NIS2:

- **EU-CyCLONe:** The European Cyber Crises Liaison Organisation Network supports coordinated management of large-scale cybersecurity incidents at the EU level.
- **CSIRTs Network:** National Computer Security Incident Response Teams cooperate through a structured network to share threat intelligence and coordinate incident response.
- **Cooperation Group:** Composed of Member State representatives, the Commission, and ENISA, the Cooperation Group facilitates strategic cooperation and information exchange.

### ECHR Dimensions

While the ECHR does not directly regulate cybersecurity, the European Court of Human Rights has recognized relevant obligations:

**Article 8 (Right to Private Life):** States have positive obligations to protect individuals' personal data and digital communications from interference, including by ensuring adequate cybersecurity for systems processing personal data. In Bărbulescu v. Romania, App. No. 61496/08 (2017), the Grand Chamber emphasized the importance of safeguarding the confidentiality of electronic communications.

**Article 2 (Right to Life):** Where critical infrastructure failures pose risks to life -- such as in healthcare, energy, or transportation -- states may have positive obligations under Article 2 to ensure adequate protection. The Court's jurisprudence on positive obligations in Öneryıldız v. Turkey, App. No. 48939/99 (2004), concerning dangerous industrial activities, provides an analogous framework.

**Article 1 of Protocol No. 1 (Protection of Property):** Cyberattacks causing property damage or economic loss may engage the state's positive obligation to protect property rights through adequate regulatory frameworks.

## Comparative Analysis

### Regulatory Approach: Harmonized vs. Sector-Specific

The most significant structural divergence is between the EU's harmonized, cross-sectoral approach and the U.S. sector-specific model:

| Jurisdiction | Approach | Advantages | Disadvantages |
|-------------|----------|------------|--------------|
| **EU (NIS2)** | Harmonized cross-sectoral | Consistent baseline; reduced fragmentation; clear compliance framework | May lack sector-specific nuance; implementation varies across 27 Member States |
| **United States** | Sector-specific with CISA coordination | Tailored to sector-specific risks; leverages sector expertise | Fragmented; inconsistent coverage; gaps between sectors |
| **Germany** | Hybrid (national framework implementing EU directives) | Combines EU harmonization with German engineering rigor | Complexity of dual compliance (national + EU) |
| **South Korea** | Centralized national framework | Streamlined coordination; rapid response capability | Concentration of authority in NIS raises oversight concerns |

### Incident Reporting Requirements

All four jurisdictions have converged on mandatory incident reporting, though timelines and thresholds differ:

- **EU NIS2:** 24-hour early warning + 72-hour notification + 1-month final report -- the most structured regime.
- **United States (CIRCIA):** 72-hour incident report + 24-hour ransomware payment report -- simpler but less granular.
- **Germany:** 24-hour initial + 72-hour updated + 1-month final -- aligned with NIS2 as transposition.
- **South Korea:** Timeframes prescribed by sector-specific implementing rules; generally within hours for critical incidents.

### Management Liability

NIS2's Article 20 management body accountability provision represents a paradigm shift. By requiring management bodies to personally approve and supervise cybersecurity measures -- and face personal liability for failures -- the EU has created the strongest management accountability regime in the comparative landscape. Neither the U.S., South Korean, nor pre-NIS2 German frameworks impose comparable personal liability on corporate management for cybersecurity failures.

### Supply Chain Security

The SolarWinds attack catalyzed global attention to supply chain cybersecurity:

- **EU NIS2:** Explicitly requires supply chain risk assessments (Art. 21(2)(d)) and empowers the Cooperation Group to coordinate EU-wide supply chain risk assessments.
- **United States:** Executive Order 14028 mandates SBOM requirements for federal procurement; CMMC program for defense industrial base.
- **Germany:** IT Security Act 2.0 requires critical component declarations and ministerial approval for certain infrastructure components.
- **South Korea:** Protection plans must address supply chain risks; 2024 Cybersecurity Basic Act strengthens supply chain requirements.
