## Summary

Advertising regulation -- the legal frameworks governing the content, form, targeting, and dissemination of commercial communications across traditional and digital media -- reflects each jurisdiction's balance between commercial speech protection, consumer protection, public health, and the increasingly complex challenges posed by digital advertising, influencer marketing, and algorithmic targeting. South Korea regulates advertising through the Act on Fair Labeling and Advertising (표시·광고의 공정화에 관한 법률), enforced by the Korea Fair Trade Commission (공정거래위원회, KFTC), alongside sector-specific regulations including the Pharmaceutical Affairs Act (약사법), the Food Sanitation Act (식품위생법), and the Outdoor Advertising Act (옥외광고물 등의 관리와 옥외광고산업 진흥에 관한 법률), with the Korea Communications Standards Commission (방송통신심의위원회) exercising authority over broadcast and online advertising content. The United States regulates advertising primarily through the Federal Trade Commission (FTC) under the FTC Act (15 U.S.C. Section 45), which prohibits "unfair or deceptive acts or practices in or affecting commerce," with the Supreme Court's commercial speech doctrine -- established in Virginia State Board of Pharmacy v. Virginia Citizens Consumer Council (1976) and refined through Central Hudson Gas & Electric Corp. v. Public Service Commission (1980) -- providing First Amendment protection for truthful, non-misleading commercial speech that is significantly more robust than in other jurisdictions, limiting the government's ability to restrict advertising. Germany regulates advertising through the Gesetz gegen den unlauteren Wettbewerb (UWG -- Act Against Unfair Competition), the Heilmittelwerbegesetz (HWG -- Medicines Advertising Act), the Jugendschutzgesetz (JuSchG -- Youth Protection Act), and the Medienstaatsvertrag (MStV), with a strong tradition of industry self-regulation through the Deutscher Werberat (German Advertising Council). At the EU level, the Unfair Commercial Practices Directive (2005/29/EC) provides harmonized standards prohibiting misleading and aggressive commercial practices, the Digital Services Act (Regulation 2022/2065) introduces transparency requirements for online advertising including algorithmic targeting, and the ECHR has recognized that commercial expression falls within Article 10 protection while allowing states a wider margin of appreciation in regulating it.

## South Korea

### Legal Framework

**Act on Fair Labeling and Advertising (표시·광고의 공정화에 관한 법률):** The primary consumer protection statute governing advertising, enforced by the KFTC. The Act prohibits false or exaggerated advertising, deceptive advertising, unfairly comparative advertising, and advertising that disparages competitors. The KFTC may issue corrective orders, impose surcharges (up to 2% of related revenue), and refer cases for criminal prosecution.

**Korea Fair Trade Commission Guidelines:**

| Guideline | Year | Subject |
|-----------|------|---------|
| Guidelines on Recommendation and Guarantee in Advertising | 2020 | Influencer/endorser disclosure requirements |
| Guidelines on Online Platform Advertising | 2021 | Platform advertising transparency |
| Guidelines on Environmental Claims | 2023 | Greenwashing prevention |
| Guidelines on AI-Generated Advertising | 2024 | Disclosure of AI-generated content |

### Sector-Specific Regulations

**Pharmaceutical Advertising (약사법):** Direct-to-consumer advertising of prescription drugs is prohibited. Over-the-counter drug advertising must include approved efficacy information and risk warnings. The Ministry of Food and Drug Safety (식품의약품안전처) oversees compliance.

**Food Advertising (식품위생법):** Advertising of foods to children is subject to enhanced restrictions, including limitations on using cartoon characters or celebrity endorsements for unhealthy foods. The Special Act on the Safety Management of Children's Dietary Life restricts advertising of high-calorie, low-nutrition foods during children's programming.

**Alcohol Advertising:** Television advertising of alcoholic beverages is restricted to after 10 PM (spirits) and subject to content restrictions including prohibitions on depicting minors, associating alcohol with driving, or suggesting health benefits.

### Online Advertising

The KCSC exercises authority over online advertising content. The E-Commerce Consumer Protection Act (전자상거래 등에서의 소비자보호에 관한 법률) requires clear disclosure of commercial nature in online advertising and imposes obligations on online platforms hosting advertisements.

### Penalties

| Violation | Penalty |
|-----------|---------|
| False/deceptive advertising (Fair Labeling and Advertising Act) | Corrective order; surcharge up to 2% of revenue; up to 2 years imprisonment |
| Unauthorized pharmaceutical advertising | Up to 1 year imprisonment or fine up to 10 million KRW |
| Violation of children's food advertising restrictions | Fine up to 10 million KRW |
| Influencer non-disclosure | KFTC warning; corrective order; surcharge |

## United States

### Legal Framework

**FTC Act (15 U.S.C. Section 45):** Section 5 prohibits "unfair or deceptive acts or practices in or affecting commerce." The FTC interprets advertising as deceptive if it contains a material representation or omission that is likely to mislead a reasonable consumer.

**Commercial Speech Doctrine:**

The Supreme Court's commercial speech jurisprudence significantly limits government advertising regulation:

**Virginia State Board of Pharmacy v. Virginia Citizens Consumer Council, 425 U.S. 748 (1976):** Established that commercial speech is protected by the First Amendment, striking down Virginia's prohibition on advertising prescription drug prices.

**Central Hudson Gas & Electric Corp. v. Public Service Commission, 447 U.S. 557 (1980):** Established the four-part test for government regulation of commercial speech:
1. Is the speech protected (lawful and not misleading)?
2. Is the government interest substantial?
3. Does the regulation directly advance the government interest?
4. Is the regulation no more extensive than necessary?

### FTC Endorsement Guides (Revised 2023)

The FTC's revised Guides Concerning the Use of Endorsements and Testimonials in Advertising, effective July 2023, significantly updated influencer marketing rules:

- Clear and conspicuous disclosure of material connections between endorsers and advertisers
- Advertisers liable for endorser claims even if not specifically directed
- Fake reviews and testimonials expressly prohibited
- "Clear and conspicuous" standard defined for social media (e.g., disclosure above the fold, not buried in hashtags)

### Tobacco and Alcohol

**Tobacco:** The Federal Cigarette Labeling and Advertising Act (15 U.S.C. Section 1331) banned broadcast tobacco advertising effective January 1, 1971. The Family Smoking Prevention and Tobacco Control Act (2009) gave the FDA authority over tobacco marketing, including restrictions on outdoor advertising near schools and playgrounds.

**Alcohol:** Advertising is largely self-regulated through industry codes (Distilled Spirits Council, Beer Institute, Wine Institute). The FTC exercises general authority over deceptive alcohol advertising but does not impose content restrictions comparable to European models.

### Children's Advertising

**Children's Online Privacy Protection Act (COPPA, 15 U.S.C. Section 6501):** Requires parental consent for collecting personal information from children under 13, effectively restricting targeted online advertising to children.

**Children's Advertising Review Unit (CARU):** Industry self-regulatory body within BBB National Programs that reviews advertising directed at children under 13 and applies guidelines stricter than FTC requirements.

### Penalties

| Authority | Maximum Penalty |
|-----------|----------------|
| FTC Section 5 enforcement | $50,120 per violation per day (2024) |
| FTC consumer redress | Disgorgement of ill-gotten gains |
| Lanham Act (15 U.S.C. Section 1125) | Damages, injunctive relief, attorneys' fees |
| FDA tobacco advertising violations | Civil money penalties; injunctions |

## Germany

### Legal Framework

**Gesetz gegen den unlauteren Wettbewerb (UWG -- Act Against Unfair Competition):** The primary statute governing advertising practices, implementing the EU Unfair Commercial Practices Directive. Key provisions include:

- **Section 3:** General prohibition of unfair commercial practices
- **Section 5:** Prohibition of misleading commercial practices (including advertising)
- **Section 5a:** Prohibition of misleading by omission (foundation for influencer disclosure requirements)
- **Section 6:** Rules on comparative advertising (harmonized with EU Directive 2006/114/EC)
- **Section 7:** Prohibition of unreasonable harassment (covers unsolicited commercial communications)
- **Annex to Section 3(3):** "Blacklist" of commercial practices that are always unfair (transposing UCPD Annex I)

### Sector-Specific Restrictions

**Heilmittelwerbegesetz (HWG -- Medicines Advertising Act):** Strictly regulates pharmaceutical advertising. Direct-to-consumer advertising of prescription drugs is prohibited (unlike the United States). OTC drug advertising must include mandatory warnings and may not use certain appeals (e.g., fear, endorsement by healthcare professionals in a clinical context).

**Jugendschutzgesetz (JuSchG) and Jugendmedienschutz-Staatsvertrag (JMStV):** Impose strict restrictions on advertising directed at or affecting minors, including prohibitions on advertising that exploits children's inexperience or credulity, advertising for alcohol or tobacco that targets minors, and advertising within children's programming.

### Deutscher Werberat (German Advertising Council)

The Deutscher Werberat, established in 1972, operates as the self-regulatory body of the German advertising industry. It receives and adjudicates consumer complaints about advertising content, issuing public reprimands (Öffentliche Rügen) for advertisements found to violate its behavioral rules. While its decisions are not legally binding, compliance rates exceed 90 percent, and public reprimands carry significant reputational consequences.

### Influencer Marketing

German courts have been particularly active in adjudicating influencer marketing disputes. Several landmark decisions have addressed when social media posts constitute commercial advertising requiring disclosure:

**BGH, Judgment of September 9, 2021 (I ZR 90/20 -- "Influencer I"):** The Federal Court of Justice held that influencer posts that tag brands or link to brand profiles constitute advertising requiring disclosure under UWG Section 5a only when there is a commercial relationship (payment or free product) between the influencer and the brand. Unpaid personal recommendations do not require advertising disclosure.

### Penalties

| Violation | Penalty |
|-----------|---------|
| Misleading advertising (UWG Section 5) | Injunctive relief; damages; competitors and consumer organizations may sue |
| Unauthorized pharmaceutical advertising (HWG) | Fine up to 50,000 EUR; criminal penalty for serious violations |
| Youth protection advertising violations (JuSchG) | Fine up to 50,000 EUR |
| Unfair competition (UWG Section 3) | Civil liability; injunctive relief; profit disgorgement |

## ECHR & EU

### Unfair Commercial Practices Directive (UCPD, 2005/29/EC)

The UCPD provides the harmonized framework for advertising regulation across the EU:

- **Full harmonization:** Member states may not adopt stricter or more lenient standards for the practices covered
- **General prohibition:** Commercial practices that are contrary to professional diligence and materially distort consumer behavior are unfair
- **Misleading practices (Arts. 6-7):** Practices containing false information or omitting material information
- **Aggressive practices (Arts. 8-9):** Practices using harassment, coercion, or undue influence
- **Blacklist (Annex I):** 31 commercial practices that are always considered unfair (e.g., bait advertising, fake free offers, disguised advertising)

### Digital Services Act and Online Advertising

Regulation (EU) 2022/2065 (DSA) introduces significant new requirements for online advertising:

- **Advertising transparency (Art. 26):** Platforms must ensure that recipients can identify advertising, the advertiser, and the parameters used for targeting
- **Prohibition on profiling minors (Art. 28):** Online platforms may not present advertising based on profiling using personal data of minors
- **Prohibition on sensitive data targeting (Art. 26(3)):** Advertising may not be targeted based on special categories of personal data (race, political opinions, health, sexual orientation)
- **Very large online platforms (Art. 39):** Must provide advertising repositories for public scrutiny

### ECHR Commercial Speech

**Markt Intern Verlag v. Germany (Application No. 10572/83, Judgment of November 20, 1989):** The Court held that commercial expression falls within Article 10 protection but that states enjoy a wider margin of appreciation in regulating it compared to political or artistic expression.

**Mouvement Raëlien Suisse v. Switzerland (Application No. 16354/06, Grand Chamber Judgment of July 13, 2012):** The Grand Chamber upheld Switzerland's refusal to allow poster advertising by a religious movement, affirming the wide margin of appreciation for commercial advertising restrictions.

**Sekmadienis Ltd. v. Lithuania (Application No. 69317/14, Judgment of January 30, 2018):** The Court found that Lithuania's prohibition of a clothing company's religious-themed advertising violated Article 10, as the advertising did not constitute incitement to hatred or threaten public order.

### EU Tobacco Advertising Directive

Directive 2003/33/EC prohibits tobacco advertising in the press, radio, and online services, as well as tobacco sponsorship of cross-border events. The CJEU upheld the Directive's validity in Germany v. Parliament and Council (Case C-380/03, 2006), after having annulled the predecessor directive (Case C-376/98, 2000) for exceeding EU competence.

### Greenwashing Regulation

Environmental advertising claims ("greenwashing") have become a major regulatory focus across all jurisdictions:

**South Korea:** The KFTC issued Guidelines on Environmental Claims in Advertising in 2023, requiring that environmental claims be substantiated, specific, and not misleading. Companies making vague claims such as "eco-friendly" without substantiation face corrective orders and surcharges.

**United States:** The FTC's Green Guides (16 C.F.R. Part 260, revised 2012, under further revision) provide guidance on environmental marketing claims. The FTC has brought enforcement actions against companies making unsubstantiated "carbon neutral," "biodegradable," or "sustainable" claims. Several states have enacted additional greenwashing prohibitions.

**Germany:** The UWG prohibits misleading environmental claims. German courts have been particularly active in greenwashing cases, with competitors and environmental organizations filing UWG claims. The Verbraucherzentrale Bundesverband (Federation of German Consumer Organizations) has challenged numerous environmental advertising claims.

**EU:** Directive (EU) 2024/825 on green claims (the "Green Claims Directive"), adopted in February 2024, will require companies to substantiate environmental claims with scientific evidence before making them, prohibit generic environmental claims without evidence, and restrict the use of sustainability labels not based on approved certification schemes. Transposition is required by March 2026.

### Dark Patterns in Digital Advertising

The regulation of dark patterns -- user interface designs that manipulate users into making unintended choices -- has emerged as a priority:

| Jurisdiction | Regulatory Response |
|-------------|-------------------|
| South Korea | E-Commerce Act amended (2023) to address deceptive UI practices |
| United States | FTC policy statement on dark patterns (2022); enforcement actions against subscription traps |
| Germany | UWG Section 3 (unfair practices) applied to digital manipulation; court decisions on deceptive cancellation processes |
| EU | DSA Article 25 prohibits dark patterns on online platforms; UCPD Annex I blacklist expanded |

## Comparative Analysis

### Commercial Speech Protection

The most fundamental divergence is the level of constitutional protection for commercial speech. The United States provides the strongest protection through the Central Hudson test, which requires the government to demonstrate that advertising restrictions directly advance a substantial interest and are not more extensive than necessary. Germany and the EU apply proportionality analysis but with a wider margin for restriction. South Korea provides limited constitutional protection for commercial speech. The ECHR recognizes commercial speech protection but with a notably wider margin of appreciation.

### Enforcement Models

The U.S. relies heavily on federal (FTC) and private (Lanham Act) enforcement, with significant monetary penalties. Germany combines public enforcement with a particularly effective private enforcement model, where competitors and consumer organizations have standing to sue under the UWG. South Korea's KFTC exercises broad administrative authority. The EU framework relies on national enforcement of harmonized standards.

### Digital Advertising Challenges

All jurisdictions face the challenge of regulating digital advertising, including influencer marketing, algorithmic targeting, and dark patterns. The EU's DSA represents the most comprehensive approach, with specific provisions on advertising transparency, prohibition of minor profiling, and platform responsibilities. The U.S. approach remains largely based on applying existing FTC Act standards to new media. South Korea and Germany have both updated their frameworks to address influencer marketing specifically.
