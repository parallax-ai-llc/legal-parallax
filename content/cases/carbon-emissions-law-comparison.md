## Summary

Carbon emissions regulation -- the legal framework governing the release of greenhouse gases (GHGs), particularly carbon dioxide (CO2), into the atmosphere -- has become the cornerstone of global climate change mitigation policy, with jurisdictions deploying a range of instruments including emissions trading systems (ETS), carbon taxes, regulatory standards, and border adjustment mechanisms. South Korea launched the Korea Emissions Trading Scheme (K-ETS, 한국 배출권거래제) on January 1, 2015, under the Act on the Allocation and Trading of Greenhouse Gas Emission Permits (온실가스 배출권의 할당 및 거래에 관한 법률), making it the first nationwide cap-and-trade system in East Asia and currently the third-largest ETS globally by covered emissions, covering approximately 73 percent of national greenhouse gas emissions across 684 entities in the third planning period (2021-2025). The United States lacks a federal carbon pricing mechanism, despite the failed Waxman-Markey cap-and-trade bill of 2009, but addresses carbon emissions through the Clean Air Act (42 U.S.C. Sections 7401-7671q) following the Supreme Court's landmark decision in Massachusetts v. EPA (2007) recognizing CO2 as a pollutant, supplemented by state-level programs including the Regional Greenhouse Gas Initiative (RGGI) covering power sector emissions in northeastern states and California's Cap-and-Trade Program linked with Quebec; the Inflation Reduction Act of 2022 represents the largest climate investment in U.S. history at approximately $369 billion but relies primarily on incentives rather than carbon pricing. Germany participates in the EU Emissions Trading System (EU ETS) for industrial and power sector emissions and implemented a national carbon pricing system (nationales Emissionshandelssystem, nEHS) for heating and transport fuels under the Brennstoffemissionshandelsgesetz (BEHG) in 2021, with prices rising from EUR 25/tonne CO2 in 2021 to EUR 45 in 2024 and a transition to market-based pricing planned for 2027. The EU operates the world's largest and longest-running emissions trading system (EU ETS), established by Directive 2003/87/EC, now in its fourth phase (2021-2030) with a substantially tightened cap and the introduction of the Carbon Border Adjustment Mechanism (CBAM) under Regulation (EU) 2023/956 to prevent carbon leakage.

## South Korea

### Legal Framework

South Korea's carbon emissions regulation operates through two primary statutes: the Framework Act on Carbon Neutrality and Green Growth (탄소중립·녹색성장 기본법, the "Carbon Neutrality Act"), enacted in September 2021 and effective March 2022, establishing the overarching climate policy framework and 2050 net zero target; and the Act on the Allocation and Trading of Greenhouse Gas Emission Permits (온실가스 배출권의 할당 및 거래에 관한 법률), governing the K-ETS since 2015.

### K-ETS Structure

| Planning Period | Years | Key Features |
|----------------|-------|--------------|
| First | 2015-2017 | 100% free allocation; market establishment phase |
| Second | 2018-2020 | 97% free allocation; 3% auctioned; benchmarking introduced |
| Third | 2021-2025 | 90% free allocation; 10% auctioned; expanded sector coverage |

The K-ETS covers six greenhouse gases (CO2, CH4, N2O, HFCs, PFCs, SF6) across major industrial sectors including power generation, steel, cement, petrochemicals, refining, and aviation (domestic). Entities emitting 125,000 tonnes CO2-equivalent or more annually or facilities emitting 25,000 tonnes or more are subject to the scheme.

### Carbon Neutrality Act

The Carbon Neutrality Act established legally binding targets: a 40 percent reduction in national greenhouse gas emissions by 2030 compared to 2018 levels, and carbon neutrality by 2050. The Act created the Presidential Commission on Carbon Neutrality and Green Growth (탄소중립녹색성장위원회) and mandates five-year national climate action plans.

### Challenges

The K-ETS has faced persistent challenges including low carbon prices (significantly below EU ETS levels), market illiquidity, high proportion of free allocation reducing the price signal, and difficulties in transitioning energy-intensive industries. The government has considered linking the K-ETS with other systems but no formal linkage agreements have been concluded.

## United States

### Legal Framework

The United States addresses carbon emissions primarily through regulatory action under the Clean Air Act (42 U.S.C. Sections 7401-7671q), supplemented by state-level programs and federal investment incentives.

### Massachusetts v. EPA, 549 U.S. 497 (2007)

The Supreme Court held 5-4 that greenhouse gases including CO2 are "air pollutants" under the Clean Air Act and that the EPA has authority -- and, indeed, an obligation -- to regulate them if it finds they endanger public health or welfare. The EPA subsequently issued an endangerment finding in 2009, establishing the legal foundation for GHG regulation.

### West Virginia v. EPA, 597 U.S. 697 (2022)

The Supreme Court invoked the "major questions doctrine" to strike down the EPA's Clean Power Plan, holding that the agency lacked authority under Section 111(d) of the Clean Air Act to restructure the nation's energy generation mix through a "generation shifting" approach. The decision limited but did not eliminate the EPA's authority to regulate GHG emissions from power plants, requiring that future regulations be based on measures applicable at the individual source level.

### Inflation Reduction Act of 2022

The IRA allocated approximately $369 billion in climate and clean energy investments:

| Provision | Mechanism |
|-----------|-----------|
| Clean Electricity Production Tax Credit (Section 45Y) | Technology-neutral tax credit for zero-emission electricity generation |
| Clean Vehicle Credit (Section 30D) | Up to $7,500 credit for qualifying electric vehicles |
| Methane Emissions Reduction Program | Fees on excess methane emissions from oil and gas facilities |
| Greenhouse Gas Reduction Fund | $27 billion for green bank and clean energy financing |

### State-Level Carbon Pricing

**Regional Greenhouse Gas Initiative (RGGI):** A cooperative cap-and-trade program covering CO2 emissions from the power sector in 12 northeastern and mid-Atlantic states. Allowance prices have ranged from $3 to $15 per tonne.

**California Cap-and-Trade Program:** Covers approximately 80 percent of California's GHG emissions, linked with Quebec's system since 2014. Allowance prices reached approximately $35-40 per tonne in 2024.

## Germany

### Legal Framework

Germany's carbon regulation operates at two levels: participation in the EU ETS for industrial and power sector emissions, and the national fuel emissions trading system (nationales Emissionshandelssystem, nEHS) under the Brennstoffemissionshandelsgesetz (BEHG) for heating and transport sectors not covered by the EU ETS.

### National Fuel ETS (BEHG)

Enacted on December 12, 2019, the BEHG introduced carbon pricing for fossil fuels used in heating (oil, natural gas) and transport (gasoline, diesel) starting January 1, 2021:

| Year | Price per Tonne CO2 |
|------|-------------------|
| 2021 | EUR 25 |
| 2022 | EUR 30 |
| 2023 | EUR 30 (planned EUR 35, frozen as relief measure) |
| 2024 | EUR 45 |
| 2025 | EUR 55 |
| 2026 | Price corridor EUR 55-65 |
| 2027+ | Market-based pricing (auction system) |

Revenue from the BEHG is used to reduce the EEG surcharge on electricity prices (eliminated from July 1, 2022), fund building renovation programs, and support low-income households through climate premiums.

### Bundes-Klimaschutzgesetz (Federal Climate Protection Act)

Originally enacted on December 12, 2019, the Klimaschutzgesetz (KSG) established legally binding annual emission reduction targets for individual sectors (energy, industry, buildings, transport, agriculture, waste). The BVerfG (Federal Constitutional Court) issued a landmark ruling on March 24, 2021 (1 BvR 2656/18), finding that the original KSG was partially unconstitutional because insufficient reduction targets for the period after 2030 imposed a disproportionate burden on future generations' fundamental freedoms. The amended KSG (2021) strengthened the 2030 target to 65 percent reduction (from 55 percent) below 1990 levels and set a 2045 net-zero target.

### BVerfG Climate Decision (Neubauer et al.)

The BVerfG ruling of March 24, 2021, established that Article 20a of the Basic Law (Grundgesetz) -- the state's obligation to protect natural resources -- generates an "intertemporal guarantee of freedom" (intertemporale Freiheitssicherung). Insufficient near-term climate action violates future generations' fundamental rights by necessitating more drastic restrictions later. This decision has been cited internationally as a landmark in climate litigation.

## ECHR & EU

### EU Emissions Trading System (EU ETS)

The EU ETS, established by Directive 2003/87/EC, is the world's first and largest international emissions trading system:

**Phase 4 (2021-2030):** Following the "Fit for 55" reform package, the EU ETS cap is being reduced at an annual linear reduction factor of 4.3 percent (increased from 2.2 percent), targeting a 62 percent reduction in covered emissions by 2030 compared to 2005 levels. Free allocation is being phased out for sectors covered by CBAM.

**EU ETS 2:** A separate ETS for buildings and road transport (mirroring Germany's BEHG) will launch in 2027, with a price stability mechanism (price ceiling) to protect consumers.

**Maritime Emissions:** Shipping emissions were brought into the EU ETS from January 1, 2024, covering 100 percent of emissions from intra-EU voyages and 50 percent from voyages entering or leaving the EU.

### Carbon Border Adjustment Mechanism (CBAM)

Regulation (EU) 2023/956 establishes the CBAM to prevent carbon leakage by requiring importers of carbon-intensive goods to purchase certificates reflecting the EU ETS carbon price:

| Phase | Period | Requirement |
|-------|--------|-------------|
| Transitional | October 2023 - December 2025 | Reporting obligations only; no financial adjustment |
| Definitive | January 2026 onwards | Importers must purchase CBAM certificates; price aligned with EU ETS |

Covered sectors include cement, iron and steel, aluminum, fertilizers, electricity, and hydrogen. The CBAM replaces free allocation for covered sectors, with free allocation phased out from 2026 to 2034.

### European Climate Law (Regulation 2021/1119)

The European Climate Law establishes a legally binding target of net-zero GHG emissions by 2050 and an intermediate target of at least 55 percent net reduction by 2030 compared to 1990 levels. The regulation establishes a European Scientific Advisory Board on Climate Change and requires the Commission to propose a 2040 intermediate target.

### ECHR Climate Litigation

**KlimaSeniorinnen v. Switzerland (Application No. 53600/20, Grand Chamber, 2024):** In a landmark ruling on April 9, 2024, the ECtHR Grand Chamber found that Switzerland violated Article 8 (right to private and family life) by failing to adopt adequate measures to combat climate change. The Court established that states have positive obligations under Article 8 to adopt and implement regulations and measures capable of mitigating existing and potentially irreversible future effects of climate change.

## Comparative Analysis

### Carbon Pricing Mechanisms

The jurisdictions demonstrate the full spectrum of carbon pricing approaches. The EU ETS operates a mature cap-and-trade system with substantial carbon prices (EUR 55-70). South Korea operates a similar cap-and-trade but with much lower prices ($15-19). Germany uniquely operates both an ETS (through EU participation) and a fixed-price carbon levy (BEHG) for non-ETS sectors. The United States has no federal carbon price, relying on regulation and incentives at the federal level with limited regional cap-and-trade programs.

### Legal Enforceability of Climate Targets

Germany and the EU have the most robust legally binding targets. Germany's BVerfG ruling elevated climate protection to a constitutional imperative. The EU Climate Law creates binding targets with enforcement mechanisms. South Korea's Carbon Neutrality Act establishes binding targets with institutional infrastructure. The United States lacks a binding federal climate statute, relying on executive commitments and regulatory authority.

### Border Carbon Adjustments

The EU's CBAM is the first major border carbon adjustment mechanism, addressing the competitiveness and carbon leakage concerns that have long been cited as barriers to ambitious unilateral climate action. South Korea and the United States are considering similar mechanisms but have not yet enacted them, potentially creating trade tensions as CBAM enters its definitive phase in 2026.

### Climate Litigation

Climate litigation is reshaping the legal landscape. The BVerfG's Neubauer decision and the ECtHR's KlimaSeniorinnen ruling establish that inadequate climate action can violate fundamental rights. Similar litigation is emerging in South Korea and the United States, though the U.S. Supreme Court's major questions doctrine constrains regulatory ambition.
