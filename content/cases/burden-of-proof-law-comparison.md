## Summary

The burden of proof -- the obligation placed on a party in legal proceedings to establish the truth of facts necessary to support their claim or defense -- represents one of the most fundamental structural elements of any legal system, determining who bears the risk of non-persuasion and at what level of certainty facts must be established before the state may impose criminal sanctions on an individual. South Korea allocates the burden of proof in criminal proceedings to the prosecution under the principle of "in dubio pro reo" (의심스러울 때는 피고인의 이익으로), codified through Article 307 of the Criminal Procedure Act (형사소송법) requiring proof through evidence and Article 325 requiring acquittal when charges are not proven, with the Constitutional Court in Decision 92HeonGa8 (1996) holding that statutory presumptions that effectively shift the burden of proving innocence to the defendant violate the presumption of innocence under Article 27(4) of the Constitution. The United States enshrines the reasonable doubt standard as a constitutional requirement under the Due Process Clause, established in In re Winship, 397 U.S. 358 (1970), requiring the prosecution to prove every element of the charged offense beyond a reasonable doubt, while permitting the defendant to bear the burden of proof for affirmative defenses such as insanity (established in Patterson v. New York, 432 U.S. 197 (1977) and refined in the Insanity Defense Reform Act of 1984) -- a framework that has generated ongoing controversy regarding the appropriate allocation of burdens for defenses such as self-defense, duress, and diminished capacity. Germany applies the principle of free evaluation of evidence (freie Beweiswürdigung) under Section 261 StPO, in which the court (rather than a jury) determines facts based on its own conviction formed through the entire hearing, with the in dubio pro reo principle operating as a constitutional guarantee derived from Article 20(3) of the Basic Law (Rechtsstaatsprinzip), and the prosecution bearing the burden of proving all elements of the offense and the absence of justifications and excuses -- a broader allocation than in the United States, where the defendant typically bears the burden on affirmative defenses. The European Court of Human Rights protects the presumption of innocence under Article 6(2) of the Convention, with landmark jurisprudence in Salabiaku v. France (1988) establishing that presumptions of fact or law are permissible only if confined within reasonable limits that take into account the importance of what is at stake and maintain the rights of the defense, and in Telfner v. Austria (2001) holding that the prosecution must produce evidence sufficient for conviction without relying on the defendant's silence or failure to testify.

## South Korea

### Legal Framework

South Korea's burden of proof framework rests on the constitutional presumption of innocence under Article 27(4) of the Constitution, implemented through the Criminal Procedure Act and developed through Supreme Court and Constitutional Court jurisprudence.

### Constitutional Presumption of Innocence

Article 27(4) provides: "The accused shall be presumed innocent until a judgment of guilt has been rendered." This provision establishes the foundational principle from which the prosecution's burden of proof derives.

### Criminal Procedure Act Provisions

**Article 307 -- Principle of Evidence-Based Adjudication:** "Facts shall be proved by evidence." This provision establishes that all factual findings must be based on evidence presented in proceedings.

**Article 308 -- Free Evaluation of Evidence:** "The probative value of evidence shall be left to the free judgment of the court." While similar to Germany's freie Beweiswürdigung, Korean courts have developed specific standards for different categories of evidence.

**Article 325 -- Acquittal Judgment:** "When a charged offense is not proven, a judgment of acquittal shall be rendered." This provision implements the in dubio pro reo principle as a statutory command.

### Proof Standard

The Supreme Court has consistently defined the criminal proof standard as "proof beyond a reasonable doubt" (합리적 의심 없는 증명), requiring a degree of certainty that would convince a reasonable person of the defendant's guilt. In Decision 2005Do9545 (2006), the Supreme Court clarified that "reasonable doubt" does not mean all possible doubt but doubt that is based on logical and experiential grounds.

### Constitutional Court on Presumptions

**Decision 92HeonGa8 (1996):** The Constitutional Court addressed the constitutionality of statutory presumptions in criminal law. The case involved a provision of the National Security Act that presumed knowledge of the enemy-benefiting nature of certain acts. The Court held that statutory presumptions that effectively reverse the burden of proof in criminal proceedings violate Article 27(4) unless they are narrowly tailored, supported by a rational connection between the proved and presumed facts, and rebuttable by the defendant.

### Citizen Participation Trials

Since the introduction of the citizen participation trial system (국민참여재판) in 2008, the burden of proof in jury-type trials has generated additional jurisprudence. The court instructs citizen participants that the prosecution bears the burden of proof and that they must find the defendant guilty only if the evidence proves guilt beyond a reasonable doubt.

## United States

### Legal Framework

The United States has the most extensively litigated burden of proof framework of any jurisdiction, with the reasonable doubt standard constitutionalized and the allocation of burdens between prosecution and defense generating substantial Supreme Court jurisprudence.

### In re Winship, 397 U.S. 358 (1970)

Justice Brennan's majority opinion established that the Due Process Clause of the Fourteenth Amendment "protects the accused against conviction except upon proof beyond a reasonable doubt of every fact necessary to constitute the crime with which he is charged." This holding:
- Constitutionalized the reasonable doubt standard
- Applied it to every element of the offense
- Extended the requirement to juvenile proceedings

### Elements vs. Affirmative Defenses

**Patterson v. New York, 432 U.S. 197 (1977):** The Court held that placing the burden of proving an affirmative defense (extreme emotional disturbance) on the defendant by a preponderance of the evidence does not violate due process, as long as the prosecution still bears the burden of proving all elements of the offense beyond a reasonable doubt. The decision drew a critical distinction between elements of the offense (prosecution's burden) and affirmative defenses (potentially the defendant's burden).

**Mullaney v. Wilbur, 421 U.S. 684 (1975):** The Court struck down a Maine statute that required defendants to prove provocation to reduce murder to manslaughter, holding that the prosecution must prove the absence of provocation because the state had defined the absence of provocation as an element of murder.

The tension between Winship/Mullaney and Patterson remains a central issue in American criminal law, as the distinction between "elements" and "defenses" determines whether the prosecution or defendant bears the burden.

### Self-Defense

The majority of states (approximately 49) require the prosecution to disprove self-defense beyond a reasonable doubt once the defendant raises sufficient evidence. Ohio was the sole state requiring the defendant to prove self-defense by a preponderance of evidence until its law was reformed in 2019.

### Insanity Defense

Under the Insanity Defense Reform Act of 1984 (18 U.S.C. Section 17), the defendant bears the burden of proving insanity by clear and convincing evidence in federal cases. State approaches vary:
- Approximately 30 states place the burden on the defendant (by preponderance or clear and convincing evidence)
- Approximately 16 states require the prosecution to disprove insanity beyond a reasonable doubt

### Reasonable Doubt Instructions

Despite the constitutional importance of the reasonable doubt standard, the Supreme Court has never prescribed a specific instruction and has refused to define "reasonable doubt" with mathematical precision. In Victor v. Nebraska, 511 U.S. 1 (1994), the Court upheld jury instructions that, while imperfect, did not reduce the prosecution's burden below the constitutional minimum.

## Germany

### Legal Framework

Germany's burden of proof framework operates through the principle of free evaluation of evidence (freie Beweiswürdigung), the court's duty to investigate (Amtsermittlungsgrundsatz), and the in dubio pro reo principle, all operating within a system where professional judges rather than juries serve as fact-finders.

### Section 261 StPO -- Free Evaluation of Evidence

Section 261 StPO provides: "The court shall decide on the result of the evidence taken according to its free conviction formed from the totality of the proceedings." This means:
- There are no formal rules assigning predetermined weight to categories of evidence
- The court must consider all evidence presented during the hearing
- The court must justify its findings in the written judgment
- The standard is the personal conviction (Überzeugung) of the court based on the evidence

### In Dubio Pro Reo

The in dubio pro reo principle is not explicitly codified in the StPO but is considered a constitutional requirement derived from the Rechtsstaatsprinzip (rule of law principle) under Article 20(3) GG. The principle requires that all remaining doubts after the full evaluation of evidence must be resolved in favor of the defendant.

### Prosecution's Burden

In German criminal proceedings, the prosecution bears the burden of proving:
- All objective elements (Tatbestand) of the offense
- The subjective element (Vorsatz -- intent, or Fahrlässigkeit -- negligence)
- The absence of justifications (Rechtfertigungsgründe), such as self-defense (Notwehr, Section 32 StGB) and necessity (Notstand, Section 34 StGB)
- The absence of excuses (Schuldausschließungsgründe), such as duress (entschuldigender Notstand, Section 35 StGB)

This is significantly broader than the U.S. allocation, where the defendant typically bears the burden on justifications and excuses once raised.

### Court's Duty to Investigate (Amtsermittlungsgrundsatz)

Under Section 244(2) StPO, the court has an independent duty to investigate the truth (Aufklärungspflicht), extending the investigation to all facts and evidence that are material to the judgment. This principle means the court is not limited to evidence presented by the prosecution and defense but must actively seek the truth, reducing the practical significance of burden allocation compared to adversarial systems.

### Statutory Presumptions

German criminal law contains very few statutory presumptions. The Federal Constitutional Court has held that presumptions that effectively reverse the burden of proof in criminal proceedings are incompatible with the presumption of innocence. The limited presumptions that exist (e.g., the presumption of dangerousness for preventive detention purposes) must be rebuttable and subject to strict proportionality review.

## ECHR & EU

### ECHR Article 6(2) -- Presumption of Innocence

Article 6(2) provides: "Everyone charged with a criminal offence shall be presumed innocent until proved guilty according to law."

### Key ECHR Jurisprudence

**Salabiaku v. France, Application No. 10519/83, Judgment of October 7, 1988:** The Court established that presumptions of fact or law operate in every legal system and that Article 6(2) does not prohibit them entirely. However, "Contracting States are required to confine them within reasonable limits which take into account the importance of what is at stake and maintain the rights of the defence." The prosecution must retain the ultimate burden of proof.

**Telfner v. Austria, Application No. 33501/96, Judgment of March 20, 2001:** The Court found a violation of Article 6(2) where Austrian courts convicted the applicant of causing a traffic accident based primarily on the fact that he was the principal user of the car involved, without sufficient independent evidence. The Court held that the prosecution could not rely on the defendant's failure to provide an explanation to establish guilt.

**Barberà, Messegué and Jabardo v. Spain, Application No. 10590/83, Judgment of December 6, 1988:** The Court established that the presumption of innocence requires that the burden of proof lies on the prosecution and that any doubt should benefit the accused. The presumption is not merely a procedural guarantee but a fundamental aspect of the right to a fair trial.

**Allen v. United Kingdom [GC], Application No. 25424/09, Judgment of July 12, 2013:** The Grand Chamber held that the presumption of innocence extends beyond the criminal proceedings to subsequent proceedings, including costs proceedings following acquittal. Any expression of doubt about the acquitted person's innocence violates Article 6(2).

### EU Directive 2016/343

Directive (EU) 2016/343 of March 9, 2016, on the strengthening of certain aspects of the presumption of innocence and of the right to be present at trial establishes minimum rules:

**Article 6 -- Burden of Proof:** The burden of proof for establishing the guilt of suspects and accused persons is on the prosecution, with any doubt benefiting the suspect or accused person. Member states must ensure that any presumption of guilt is rebuttable.

**Article 7 -- Right to Remain Silent and Right Not to Incriminate Oneself:** Suspects and accused persons have the right to remain silent and the right not to incriminate themselves. The exercise of these rights shall not be used against them.

## Comparative Analysis

### Standard of Proof

While all four jurisdictions require a high standard of proof for criminal convictions, they articulate it differently. The United States constitutionalizes "beyond a reasonable doubt" as a specific standard. South Korea applies the same verbal formulation. Germany requires the personal "conviction" of the court, which is functionally equivalent but operates differently in a system without jury instructions. The ECHR does not prescribe a specific standard but requires compatibility with the presumption of innocence.

### Burden Allocation for Defenses

The most significant divergence concerns who bears the burden for justifications and excuses. Germany and South Korea place the burden on the prosecution to disprove all defenses once raised, reflecting a broader conception of the presumption of innocence. The United States permits placing the burden on the defendant for affirmative defenses, with the critical distinction between elements and defenses determined by legislative definition.

### Role of the Fact-Finder

The nature of the fact-finder fundamentally affects how the burden of proof operates in practice. In the United States, juries receive formal instructions on the burden of proof and the reasonable doubt standard. In Germany, professional judges apply the standard without formal instructions to themselves. South Korea's hybrid system (professional judges with optional citizen participation since 2008) requires the court to instruct citizen participants on the burden of proof.

### Presumptions

All jurisdictions permit some factual presumptions in criminal law, but with varying constraints. The ECHR's Salabiaku framework provides the broadest permission (within "reasonable limits"). The United States permits presumptions with a rational basis. South Korea and Germany impose strict constitutional limits on presumptions that effectively shift the burden to the defendant.
