## Summary

The prohibition of slavery and forced labor -- foundational to modern human rights law -- has evolved from abolitionist movements of the eighteenth and nineteenth centuries into comprehensive constitutional, statutory, and international legal frameworks that now encompass human trafficking, debt bondage, forced marriage, and modern forms of exploitation. South Korea's Constitution (대한민국 헌법) prohibits forced labor under Article 12 (personal liberty) and Article 15 (freedom of occupation), while the Criminal Act (형법) criminalizes human trafficking under Articles 289-296, substantially reformed by the Act on the Prevention of Human Trafficking and Protection of Victims (인신매매등방지 및 피해자보호 등에 관한 법률), enacted in 2021 and effective January 1, 2023, which created Korea's first comprehensive anti-trafficking legal framework. The United States abolished slavery through the Thirteenth Amendment (1865) and addresses modern slavery primarily through the Trafficking Victims Protection Act of 2000 (TVPA, 22 U.S.C. Section 7101 et seq.), 18 U.S.C. Sections 1581-1592 (forced labor and trafficking offenses), and the Forced Labor Prevention Act (targeting Xinjiang-origin goods); the Supreme Court has interpreted the Thirteenth Amendment broadly in cases from the Civil Rights Cases, 109 U.S. 3 (1883) to United States v. Kozminski, 487 U.S. 931 (1988). Germany prohibits forced labor under Article 12(2) of the Grundgesetz and criminalizes human trafficking under Sections 232-233a of the Strafgesetzbuch (StGB), as substantially reformed in 2016 to implement EU Directive 2011/36/EU on preventing and combating trafficking in human beings. At the European and international level, Article 4 of the European Convention on Human Rights absolutely prohibits slavery and forced labor, the European Court of Human Rights has developed extensive positive obligations jurisprudence through landmark decisions including Siliadin v. France (2005), Rantsev v. Cyprus and Russia (2010), and Chowdury v. Greece (2017), while EU Directive 2011/36/EU establishes minimum standards for anti-trafficking measures across all member states and the EU Corporate Sustainability Due Diligence Directive (2024) imposes supply chain obligations to prevent forced labor.

## South Korea

### Legal Framework

South Korea's prohibition of slavery and forced labor is rooted in the Constitution of the Republic of Korea (대한민국 헌법). Article 12(1) guarantees personal liberty and provides that no citizen shall be subjected to physical restraint or punishment except as provided by law and through lawful procedures. Article 15 guarantees the freedom of occupation. Article 32(3) prohibits exploitative labor conditions. These constitutional provisions are supplemented by criminal and specialized statutes.

### Criminal Act Provisions

The Criminal Act (형법) addresses trafficking and forced labor through several provisions:

| Article | Offense | Penalty |
|---------|---------|---------|
| Art. 289 (인신매매) | Buying or selling a person | Up to 7 years imprisonment |
| Art. 289-2 | Trafficking for the purpose of sexual exploitation | Up to 15 years imprisonment |
| Art. 290 | Kidnapping for the purpose of trafficking | Up to 15 years imprisonment |
| Art. 291 | Buying a kidnapped person | Up to 7 years imprisonment |
| Art. 296 | Aiding or harboring trafficked persons | Up to 7 years imprisonment |

### Act on the Prevention of Human Trafficking (2023)

The Act on the Prevention of Human Trafficking and Protection of Victims (인신매매등방지 및 피해자보호 등에 관한 법률) was enacted on April 20, 2021, and entered into force on January 1, 2023, establishing Korea's first comprehensive anti-trafficking legal framework. Key provisions include:

**Expanded Definition:** The Act defines human trafficking broadly to include recruitment, transportation, transfer, harboring, or receipt of persons through threat, force, fraud, deception, abuse of power or vulnerability, or giving or receiving of payments for the purpose of exploitation. This aligns with the Palermo Protocol definition.

**Victim Identification and Protection:** The Act establishes a formal victim identification mechanism, requiring law enforcement to screen potential victims among vulnerable populations. Identified victims are entitled to shelter, medical services, legal assistance, and vocational training. Foreign victims are eligible for temporary residence permits.

**Institutional Framework:** The Act created an inter-ministerial committee for the prevention of human trafficking, chaired by the Prime Minister, and established regional support centers for victims.

### Migrant Worker Exploitation

South Korea's Employment Permit System (고용허가제, EPS) has faced persistent criticism from international bodies regarding forced labor conditions affecting migrant workers. The UN Special Rapporteur on Contemporary Forms of Slavery reported in 2023 that structural features of the EPS -- including employer-tied visas, debt incurred for recruitment, and inadequate labor inspection -- create conditions conducive to forced labor, particularly in agriculture, fishing, and manufacturing. The government has undertaken reforms including enhanced labor inspections and expanded workplace mobility, though advocacy groups contend that the fundamental employer-tied structure of the system remains problematic.

### Historical Context

Korea experienced forced labor under Japanese colonial rule (1910-1945), during which an estimated 780,000 Koreans were mobilized for forced labor in Japanese mines, factories, and construction projects. This historical experience profoundly shapes contemporary Korean attitudes toward labor exploitation. The Supreme Court's 2018 decision ordering Nippon Steel to compensate former forced laborers (대법원 2013다61381, October 30, 2018) remains a significant diplomatic and legal issue between South Korea and Japan.

## United States

### Legal Framework

The Thirteenth Amendment to the United States Constitution, ratified on December 6, 1865, provides: "Neither slavery nor involuntary servitude, except as a punishment for crime whereof the party shall have been duly convicted, shall exist within the United States, or any place subject to their jurisdiction." Unlike other constitutional rights, the Thirteenth Amendment applies to private actors as well as government entities and contains no state action requirement.

### Key Constitutional Jurisprudence

**The Civil Rights Cases, 109 U.S. 3 (1883):** The Supreme Court held that the Thirteenth Amendment directly prohibits slavery and involuntary servitude by private individuals, confirming Congress's power to enact legislation reaching private conduct under Section 2 of the Amendment.

**Bailey v. Alabama, 219 U.S. 219 (1911):** The Court struck down Alabama's peonage statute, which effectively criminalized breach of labor contracts, holding that the Thirteenth Amendment prohibits systems that compel labor through threat of criminal punishment.

**United States v. Kozminski, 487 U.S. 931 (1988):** The Court defined "involuntary servitude" under 18 U.S.C. Section 1584 as a condition of servitude in which the victim is forced to work by the use or threat of physical or legal coercion. This narrow interpretation prompted Congress to expand statutory definitions in the TVPA.

### Trafficking Victims Protection Act (2000)

The TVPA (22 U.S.C. Section 7101 et seq.) created a comprehensive federal framework for combating trafficking. It has been reauthorized multiple times (2003, 2005, 2008, 2013, 2018). Key provisions include:

| Offense | Statute | Maximum Penalty |
|---------|---------|-----------------|
| Forced labor | 18 U.S.C. Section 1589 | 20 years; life if death/kidnapping involved |
| Trafficking with respect to peonage, slavery, involuntary servitude, or forced labor | 18 U.S.C. Section 1590 | 20 years; life if death/kidnapping involved |
| Sex trafficking by force, fraud, or coercion | 18 U.S.C. Section 1591 | 15 years to life |
| Unlawful conduct with respect to documents | 18 U.S.C. Section 1592 | 5 years |

### Supply Chain Legislation

**Tariff Act of 1930, Section 307 (19 U.S.C. Section 1307):** Prohibits the importation of goods produced by forced labor. The Trade Facilitation and Trade Enforcement Act of 2015 closed the "consumptive demand" loophole that had previously rendered this provision largely unenforceable.

**Uyghur Forced Labor Prevention Act (2021):** Creates a rebuttable presumption that goods produced in the Xinjiang Uyghur Autonomous Region of China are produced with forced labor and are prohibited from importation under Section 307.

### Prison Labor Exception

The Thirteenth Amendment's exception for "punishment for crime" permits compulsory prison labor. Several states have moved to eliminate this exception from their state constitutions. In 2022, voters in Alabama, Oregon, Tennessee, and Vermont approved amendments removing slavery and involuntary servitude exceptions from their state constitutions. In 2024, additional states followed suit.

## Germany

### Legal Framework

Article 12(2) of the Grundgesetz provides: "No person may be compelled to perform work of a particular kind except within the framework of a traditional compulsory public service obligation that applies generally and equally to all." Article 12(3) further provides that forced labor may be imposed only on persons deprived of their liberty by court sentence. These provisions, read together with Article 1 (human dignity) and Article 2 (personal freedom), establish a comprehensive constitutional prohibition of slavery and forced labor.

### Criminal Provisions (StGB Sections 232-233a)

The criminal provisions on human trafficking were comprehensively reformed by the Act to Improve the Fight against Human Trafficking (Gesetz zur Verbesserung der Bekämpfung des Menschenhandels), effective July 15, 2016, implementing EU Directive 2011/36/EU.

| Section | Offense | Penalty |
|---------|---------|---------|
| Section 232 StGB | Human trafficking (Menschenhandel) | 6 months to 5 years imprisonment |
| Section 232a StGB | Forced prostitution (Zwangsprostitution) | 6 months to 10 years imprisonment |
| Section 232b StGB | Forced labor (Zwangsarbeit) | 6 months to 10 years imprisonment |
| Section 233 StGB | Exploitation of labor (Ausbeutung der Arbeitskraft) | Up to 3 years or fine; aggravated: 6 months to 10 years |
| Section 233a StGB | Aiding human trafficking (Beihilfe zum Menschenhandel) | 3 months to 5 years; aggravated: 1 to 10 years |

### Supply Chain Due Diligence Act (LkSG)

The Lieferkettensorgfaltspflichtengesetz (LkSG, Supply Chain Due Diligence Act) entered into force on January 1, 2023, for companies with 3,000 or more employees, and on January 1, 2024, for companies with 1,000 or more employees. The Act requires covered companies to establish a risk management system, conduct regular risk analyses for human rights violations (including forced labor) in their supply chains, implement preventive measures and remedial actions, establish a complaints mechanism, and document and report on their due diligence efforts. The Federal Office for Economic Affairs and Export Control (BAFA) is responsible for enforcement, with penalties of up to 2 percent of average annual global turnover.

### Historical Context

Germany's engagement with forced labor is profoundly shaped by the Nazi regime's systematic use of forced labor during World War II. Between 1939 and 1945, an estimated 13 million people were subjected to forced labor in Germany, including concentration camp inmates, prisoners of war, and civilian forced laborers from occupied territories. The Foundation "Remembrance, Responsibility and Future" (Stiftung Erinnerung, Verantwortung und Zukunft, EVZ), established in 2000, provided approximately 4.4 billion EUR in compensation to surviving forced laborers.

## ECHR & EU

### Article 4 ECHR

Article 4 of the European Convention on Human Rights prohibits slavery, servitude, and forced or compulsory labor. It is one of the non-derogable rights under Article 15(2), meaning it cannot be suspended even in time of war or other public emergency.

**Siliadin v. France (Application No. 73316/01, July 26, 2005):** A Togolese national, brought to France as a minor, was forced to work as an unpaid domestic servant for years. The Court found a violation of Article 4, holding that France's criminal legislation did not afford the applicant practical and effective protection against servitude. This was the first case in which the Court found a violation of Article 4.

**Rantsev v. Cyprus and Russia (Application No. 25965/04, January 7, 2010):** The Court held that trafficking in human beings falls within the scope of Article 4 and established extensive positive obligations on states, including an obligation to put in place an appropriate legislative and administrative framework to prevent trafficking, to take operational measures to protect identified or potential victims, and to investigate potential trafficking situations.

**Chowdury and Others v. Greece (Application No. 21884/15, March 30, 2017):** The Court found that the exploitation of Bangladeshi strawberry pickers in Manolada, Greece, constituted forced labor and human trafficking, and that Greece had failed to fulfill its positive obligations under Article 4 by failing to prevent the situation, protect the victims, and effectively investigate the trafficking.

**S.M. v. Croatia (Application No. 60561/14, Grand Chamber, June 25, 2020):** The Grand Chamber confirmed that Article 4 encompasses trafficking in human beings and that states have three types of positive obligations: to establish a legislative and administrative framework, to take operational protective measures, and to conduct an effective investigation.

### EU Directive 2011/36/EU

Directive 2011/36/EU on preventing and combating trafficking in human beings and protecting its victims replaced Framework Decision 2002/629/JHA. Key provisions include:

- Criminalization of trafficking for all forms of exploitation (sexual, labor, organ removal, begging, criminal activities)
- Minimum maximum penalty of 5 years imprisonment (10 years for aggravating circumstances)
- Non-punishment principle for victims
- Mandatory appointment of National Rapporteurs or equivalent mechanisms
- Specific provisions for child victims

### EU Forced Labor Regulation (2024)

Regulation (EU) 2024/3015, the Forced Labour Regulation, adopted on November 19, 2024, prohibits the placing or making available on the EU market, or exporting from the EU, of products made with forced labor. Economic operators must conduct due diligence, and national competent authorities may investigate and order the withdrawal of non-compliant products. The Regulation enters into application on December 14, 2027.

### EU Corporate Sustainability Due Diligence Directive (2024)

Directive (EU) 2024/1760 (CSDDD), adopted on July 5, 2024, requires large companies to identify, prevent, mitigate, and account for actual and potential adverse human rights impacts, including forced labor, in their operations and value chains. The Directive establishes civil liability for failure to comply with due diligence obligations.

## Comparative Analysis

### Constitutional Architecture

All four jurisdictions treat the prohibition of slavery and forced labor as a fundamental norm, but their constitutional architectures differ. The U.S. Thirteenth Amendment is unique in directly prohibiting private conduct without requiring state action. Germany's Grundgesetz Article 12(2)-(3) frames the prohibition within the broader right to occupational freedom. South Korea's Constitution addresses the issue indirectly through personal liberty and freedom of occupation provisions. The ECHR Article 4 creates a non-derogable international obligation with direct applicability.

### Definitional Evolution

The legal definition of prohibited conduct has expanded significantly over time. The United States initially defined involuntary servitude narrowly in Kozminski (1988) but expanded the statutory definition through the TVPA to include psychological coercion and abuse of the legal process. Germany's 2016 reform broadened trafficking definitions to encompass labor exploitation, organ removal, and exploitation of begging. South Korea's 2023 Act adopted the broad Palermo Protocol definition. The ECHR has progressively expanded Article 4's scope from formal slavery to servitude (Siliadin), trafficking (Rantsev), and forced labor (Chowdury).

### Supply Chain Obligations

A significant trend across all jurisdictions is the emergence of corporate supply chain due diligence obligations. Germany's LkSG (2023), the EU CSDDD (2024), the EU Forced Labor Regulation (2024), and the U.S. Uyghur Forced Labor Prevention Act (2021) all reflect the recognition that combating modern slavery requires reaching beyond direct perpetrators to the corporate entities that benefit from exploitation in global supply chains.

### Victim-Centered Approaches

All jurisdictions have moved toward victim-centered approaches, though implementation varies. The U.S. T visa program provides immigration relief for trafficking victims. Germany offers temporary residence permits under AufenthG Section 25(4a). South Korea's 2023 Act establishes comprehensive victim support services. The EU non-punishment principle protects victims from prosecution for offenses committed as a result of their trafficking.
