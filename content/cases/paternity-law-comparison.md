## Summary

Paternity law -- the body of legal rules governing the establishment, contestation, and consequences of the father-child relationship -- has been profoundly transformed by advances in DNA testing technology while continuing to navigate deep tensions between biological truth, social parenthood, and the best interests of the child. South Korea establishes paternity through the presumption of legitimacy (적출추정) under Article 844 of the Civil Act (민법), which presumes that a child born during marriage or within 300 days of its dissolution is the child of the husband; this presumption can be challenged through a legitimacy denial action (친생부인의 소, Article 846), which was subject to a strict two-year limitation period until the Constitutional Court declared the absolute time bar unconstitutional in 2015 (헌법재판소 2013헌마623), leading to legislative reform allowing challenges beyond the two-year period when the father had no knowledge of the relevant facts, while voluntary acknowledgment (인지, Article 855) establishes paternity for children born outside marriage. The United States addresses paternity through state law, with the Uniform Parentage Act (UPA, most recently revised in 2017) providing a model framework that many states have adopted in whole or in part, establishing multiple bases for paternity including genetic testing, voluntary acknowledgment, the marital presumption, and holding-out conduct; the Supreme Court has addressed paternity in several constitutional decisions, most notably Michael H. v. Gerald D., 491 U.S. 110 (1989), upholding the marital presumption against the putative biological father's due process claim. Germany governs paternity (Vaterschaft) through BGB Sections 1592-1600e, establishing three bases for paternity -- marriage, acknowledgment (Vaterschaftsanerkennung), and judicial determination (gerichtliche Feststellung) -- with the Bundesverfassungsgericht having made several interventions to protect fathers' rights, including requiring a pathway for biological fathers to establish legal paternity even against the marital presumption (BVerfG, 1 BvR 1493/96, 2003). The European framework includes the ECHR's extensive Article 8 jurisprudence balancing the right to establish biological parentage against the interest in legal certainty and the child's welfare, with landmark cases including Rasmussen v. Denmark (1984), Kroon v. Netherlands (1994), Paulik v. Slovakia (2006), and Mennesson v. France (2014) on surrogacy-established parentage, as well as CJEU decisions on the free movement implications of parentage recognition.

## South Korea

### Legal Framework

Paternity in South Korea is governed by Chapter 3 of the Civil Act (민법 제3편), addressing parent-child relationships (친자관계).

### Presumption of Legitimacy (적출추정)

**Article 844(1):** A child conceived during marriage is presumed to be the child of the husband.
**Article 844(2):** A child born within 300 days of the dissolution or annulment of a marriage is presumed to have been conceived during the marriage.

This presumption operates as a strong legal rule, not merely an evidentiary inference. Under Article 846, only the husband or wife may bring a legitimacy denial action (친생부인의 소) to rebut the presumption.

### Constitutional Court Intervention (2015)

The Constitutional Court (헌법재판소) in Decision 2013헌마623 (2015) examined the constitutionality of Article 847(1), which imposed a strict two-year limitation period for legitimacy denial actions, running from the date the husband learned of the child's birth. The Court declared the provision unconstitutional insofar as it barred challenges by persons who discovered non-paternity after the two-year period. The Court held that the absolute time bar violated the right to dignity and the pursuit of happiness (Article 10) and the right to family life (Article 36(1)) of the Constitution.

Following this decision, the Civil Act was amended to allow legitimacy denial actions beyond the two-year period when:
- The husband did not know that the child was not biologically his during the original limitation period
- The action is filed within two years of acquiring such knowledge
- Filing the action would not be contrary to the child's best interests

### Voluntary Acknowledgment (인지)

**Article 855:** A father may acknowledge an out-of-wedlock child as his own. Acknowledgment requires:
- A declaration to the family registration office
- The child's or legal representative's consent if the child is an adult
- No consent of the mother is required for acknowledgment itself

**Article 863:** A child or their legal representative may bring a forced acknowledgment action (인지청구의 소) against the putative father.

### Assisted Reproduction

Korean law does not have comprehensive legislation on paternity in the context of assisted reproduction. The Supreme Court has addressed some issues through case law, holding that the consenting husband is the legal father of a child born through artificial insemination with donor sperm (대법원 2019므11609, 2019). The lack of comprehensive legislation creates uncertainty regarding surrogacy and other assisted reproduction arrangements.

### Patrilineal Family Registration

Korea's family registration system (가족관계등록제도), which replaced the patrilineal family register (호적) system in 2008, still carries vestiges of patrilineal emphasis. The transition from the hoju (호주) system was mandated by the Constitutional Court's 2005 decision (2001헌가9-15) declaring the family headship system unconstitutional as discriminatory against women.

## United States

### Legal Framework

Paternity law in the United States is governed by state law, with the Uniform Parentage Act (UPA) providing a widely adopted model framework. The UPA was originally promulgated in 1973, significantly revised in 2000, and updated again in 2017.

### Bases for Establishing Paternity

| Basis | UPA 2017 Provision | Description |
|-------|-------------------|-------------|
| Birth | Section 201(1) | Woman who gives birth |
| Marital presumption | Section 204 | Spouse of woman who gives birth |
| Voluntary acknowledgment | Section 301 | Signed VAP filed with vital records |
| Adjudication | Section 601 | Court determination |
| Adoption | Section 201(5) | Court decree of adoption |
| Assisted reproduction | Article 7 | Consent to assisted reproduction |
| Genetic testing | Section 501 | DNA testing establishing parentage |
| De facto parentage | Article 6 (2017) | Holding out + established relationship |

### Marital Presumption and Michael H.

**Michael H. v. Gerald D., 491 U.S. 110 (1989):** The Supreme Court upheld California's marital presumption against a due process challenge by the biological father. The plurality opinion by Justice Scalia held that the putative biological father did not have a constitutionally protected liberty interest in establishing paternity where the mother was married to another man and the marital family was intact. This decision established that states may constitutionally prioritize the marital family unit over biological truth.

### Voluntary Acknowledgment of Paternity (VAP)

The Personal Responsibility and Work Opportunity Reconciliation Act of 1996 (PRWORA) requires all states to establish voluntary paternity acknowledgment programs. A signed VAP, once filed with the state vital records office, has the force of a court judgment establishing paternity. Federal law provides a 60-day rescission period (42 U.S.C. Section 666(a)(5)(D)(ii)), after which the acknowledgment may be challenged only on the basis of fraud, duress, or material mistake of fact.

### Genetic Testing

DNA testing has revolutionized paternity establishment. Modern genetic testing achieves a probability of paternity exceeding 99.99 percent. Under the UPA, a person with a probability of parentage of 99 percent or greater is presumed to be a parent (Section 505). Courts may order genetic testing in paternity proceedings, and many states provide for state-funded testing in public assistance cases.

### UPA 2017: De Facto Parentage

The 2017 revision introduced a de facto parent provision, recognizing parentage for individuals who have lived with the child and held the child out as their own in a full parental role for a significant period. This provision particularly benefits non-biological parents in diverse family structures.

## Germany

### Legal Framework

German paternity law is governed by BGB Sections 1592-1600e. Under Section 1592, a man is the father of a child if he is married to the mother at the time of birth, has acknowledged paternity, or his paternity has been judicially established.

### Paternity Acknowledgment (Vaterschaftsanerkennung)

**BGB Section 1594:** Acknowledgment of paternity requires a declaration by the man and consent by the mother (Section 1595(1)). If the mother refuses consent, the man may seek judicial establishment. If the child has a legal father (through marriage or prior acknowledgment), acknowledgment by another man is not effective while the existing paternity stands.

### Right to Know Biological Origin (Section 1598a BGB)

Introduced in 2008, BGB Section 1598a establishes a right to clarification of biological parentage (Recht auf Klärung der Abstammung). The father, the mother, and the child may each demand that the others consent to a genetic investigation and submit a genetic sample. This right exists independently of any paternity challenge and is designed to satisfy the right to know one's biological origins without necessarily altering legal parentage.

### Paternity Challenge (Vaterschaftsanfechtung)

**BGB Section 1600:** The following persons may challenge paternity:
1. The legal father
2. The mother
3. The child
4. The biological father (under limited conditions since 2004 reform)

**BGB Section 1600b:** The challenge must be filed within two years of the challenger learning of circumstances casting doubt on paternity. For the child, the period begins when the child turns 18.

### Biological Father's Rights

**BVerfG Decision (1 BvR 1493/96, April 9, 2003):** The Federal Constitutional Court held that the complete exclusion of the biological father from any possibility of establishing a legal relationship with his child violated Articles 6(2) (parental rights) and 2(1) with 1(1) (general personality right) of the Grundgesetz. This led to the 2004 amendment allowing biological fathers to challenge existing paternity, but only if no social-family relationship (sozial-familiäre Beziehung) exists between the child and the legal father.

**BVerfG Decision (1 BvR 1154/10, February 25, 2015):** The Court confirmed that the biological father has a right to establish contact with the child under Article 6(2) GG, even without legal paternity, if contact serves the child's best interests.

### Prevention of Sham Acknowledgments

Section 1597a BGB, introduced in 2017, empowers the registration authority (Standesamt) to refuse to record a paternity acknowledgment where there are concrete indications that the acknowledgment is motivated solely by obtaining a residence permit for the child, the mother, or the acknowledging man, rather than by the assumption of parental responsibility. This provision addresses the phenomenon of Scheinanerkennungen (sham acknowledgments).

## ECHR & EU

### ECHR Article 8 Jurisprudence

The ECHR has developed extensive paternity-related jurisprudence under Article 8:

**Rasmussen v. Denmark (Application No. 8777/79, November 28, 1984):** The Court found no violation of Article 14 in Denmark's differential time limits for paternity challenges by mothers and fathers, though the narrow margin (10-8) signaled evolving standards.

**Kroon v. Netherlands (Application No. 18535/91, October 27, 1994):** The Court held that where biological and social reality point to a man as the child's father, Article 8 requires that he be able to establish legal paternity. The impossibility of the biological father establishing paternity due to the marital presumption violated Article 8.

**Paulik v. Slovakia (Application No. 10699/05, October 10, 2006):** The Court found a violation of Article 8 where Slovak law provided no mechanism for challenging paternity after the expiration of the limitation period, despite DNA evidence conclusively excluding paternity. States must provide a means of challenging paternity that reflects the interests of all parties.

**Mennesson v. France (Application No. 65192/11, June 26, 2014):** The Court held that France's refusal to recognize a parent-child relationship between a child born through surrogacy abroad and the intended father (who was also the genetic father) violated Article 8's right to respect for private life. This landmark decision addressed the intersection of paternity, surrogacy, and cross-border recognition.

**Advisory Opinion on Surrogacy-Born Children (April 10, 2019):** Requested by France under Protocol No. 16, the Grand Chamber advised that Article 8 requires states to recognize the parent-child relationship between a child born through surrogacy abroad and the intended mother, though not necessarily through transcription of a foreign birth certificate -- adoption may suffice.

### CJEU: Cross-Border Parentage

**V.M.A. v. Stolichna obshtina (Case C-490/20, December 14, 2021):** The CJEU held that a member state must issue identity documents recognizing the parentage established in another member state's birth certificate (listing two mothers), at least for the purpose of exercising the child's right to free movement, even if the member state does not recognize same-sex parentage domestically.

### EU Proposed Regulation on Parenthood

In December 2022, the European Commission proposed a Regulation on the recognition of parenthood across the EU, aiming to ensure that parenthood established in one member state is recognized in all others. The proposal addresses both biological and non-biological parenthood, including parentage established through adoption, surrogacy, or assisted reproduction. As of early 2026, the proposal remains under negotiation in the Council.

## Comparative Analysis

### Strength of Marital Presumption

All jurisdictions maintain the marital presumption but differ in its rebuttability. The United States, following Michael H., allows states to make the presumption effectively conclusive against biological fathers. South Korea, following the 2015 Constitutional Court decision, has weakened the absolute time bar while maintaining the presumption. Germany, following BVerfG interventions, has created pathways for biological fathers to challenge the presumption. The ECHR requires that the presumption be challengeable through some mechanism (Paulik), though states retain significant discretion.

### Biological Truth vs. Social Parenthood

The fundamental tension in paternity law is between biological truth (established through DNA testing) and social parenthood (established through the functioning parent-child relationship). Germany's Section 1598a elegantly separates these concepts by allowing clarification of biological parentage without necessarily altering legal parentage. The United States' UPA 2017 de facto parent provisions prioritize social reality in some circumstances. South Korea's system remains more tightly linked to biological and marital ties. The ECHR balances both interests under Article 8, with increasing emphasis on the right to know biological origins.

### Assisted Reproduction and Surrogacy

Paternity in the context of assisted reproduction and surrogacy presents the greatest challenges. The United States has the most developed legal framework (UPA 2017, Article 7), though state variation is significant. Germany lacks comprehensive legislation, relying on case-by-case judicial development. South Korea similarly lacks comprehensive assisted reproduction parentage rules. The ECHR's Mennesson decision and 2019 Advisory Opinion have pushed European jurisdictions toward greater recognition of parentage established through surrogacy abroad.

### Standing to Challenge

Who may challenge paternity varies significantly. Germany and South Korea limit challengers primarily to the legal father, mother, and child, with limited biological father standing. The United States varies by state, with some permitting broader standing. The ECHR requires that all interested parties have access to some mechanism for addressing paternity questions, but does not prescribe specific standing rules.
