## Summary

Freedom of information -- the legal right of individuals to access government-held documents, records, and data, premised on the principle that democratic governance requires transparency and accountability -- has expanded from a handful of pioneering jurisdictions to a near-universal norm, with over 130 countries having enacted access-to-information laws by 2026, though the scope, exceptions, enforcement mechanisms, and practical effectiveness of these laws vary enormously. South Korea enacted the Official Information Disclosure Act (공공기관의 정보공개에 관한 법률) in 1996, effective January 1998, becoming one of the first Asian countries to adopt a comprehensive freedom of information law; the Act grants any person the right to request disclosure of information held by public institutions, with the Administrative Appeals Commission (행정심판위원회) and the courts providing review of denials, and the Act was significantly strengthened in 2013 to expand the scope of proactive disclosure and enhance enforcement mechanisms. The United States enacted the Freedom of Information Act (FOIA, 5 U.S.C. Section 552) in 1966, establishing the model that most subsequent FOI laws worldwide have followed, with nine statutory exemptions covering national security, personal privacy, law enforcement, and other interests, and the OPEN Government Act of 2007 and FOIA Improvement Act of 2016 subsequently strengthening the law by codifying the presumption of openness and requiring proactive online disclosure. Germany was a relative latecomer, enacting the Informationsfreiheitsgesetz (IFG -- Freedom of Information Act) only in 2006, supplemented by the Umweltinformationsgesetz (UIG -- Environmental Information Act, 2004) implementing EU Directive 2003/4/EC, and the Verbraucherinformationsgesetz (VIG -- Consumer Information Act, 2008); the IFG has been criticized for excessive exemptions, high fees, and the absence of an independent information commissioner at the federal level, with the Federal Commissioner for Data Protection and Freedom of Information (Bundesbeauftragte für den Datenschutz und die Informationsfreiheit, BfDI) exercising only advisory rather than binding oversight authority. At the European level, the ECHR has progressively recognized a right of access to state-held information under Article 10 (freedom of expression) through the landmark Grand Chamber judgment in Magyar Helsinki Bizottság v. Hungary (2016), the EU provides access to documents of EU institutions through Regulation (EC) No 1049/2001, and the Aarhus Convention (1998) established the model for environmental information access that has influenced broader FOI jurisprudence.

## South Korea

### Legal Framework

**Official Information Disclosure Act (공공기관의 정보공개에 관한 법률):** Enacted on December 31, 1996, and effective January 1, 1998, the Act establishes the right of any person to request disclosure of information held by public institutions, including central government agencies, local governments, public corporations, and other entities receiving public funds.

### Exemptions

The Act provides eight categories of non-disclosure under Article 9:

| Category | Description |
|----------|-------------|
| 1 | Information classified as confidential under other laws |
| 2 | National security, defense, unification, or diplomatic relations information |
| 3 | Information that would significantly harm the life, body, or property of an individual |
| 4 | Information that would impair ongoing trials, investigations, or audits |
| 5 | Information that would significantly hinder fair administrative decision-making |
| 6 | Personal information that would invade individual privacy |
| 7 | Trade secrets or proprietary business information |
| 8 | Information that would harm persons' property or daily life (e.g., real estate speculation) |

### Institutional Framework

The Information Disclosure Committee (정보공개위원회), established under the Act, advises on information disclosure policy and handles institutional coordination. Individual agencies maintain information disclosure officers. Appeals against non-disclosure decisions may be filed with the Administrative Appeals Commission (행정심판위원회) or directly with the Administrative Court.

### Proactive Disclosure

The 2013 amendment significantly expanded proactive disclosure requirements, mandating that public institutions publish categories of commonly requested information online through the Government Open Data Portal (공공데이터포털, data.go.kr). South Korea's open data initiative has been recognized internationally; the country ranked first in the OECD Open, Useful, Reusable Government Data (OURdata) Index in 2019.

### Statistics and Trends

| Year | Total Requests | Full Disclosure Rate | Partial Disclosure Rate |
|------|---------------|---------------------|----------------------|
| 2015 | 682,000 | 92.3% | 4.2% |
| 2019 | 895,000 | 93.1% | 3.8% |
| 2023 | 1,100,000 | 93.5% | 3.5% |

The high disclosure rate reflects both institutional compliance and the relatively broad exemption categories that allow agencies to withhold sensitive portions while disclosing the remainder.

## United States

### Legal Framework

**Freedom of Information Act (FOIA, 5 U.S.C. Section 552):** Signed into law by President Lyndon Johnson on July 4, 1966, FOIA established the foundational right of any person to request access to federal agency records. The Act has been amended multiple times, most significantly by the Privacy Act of 1974, the Electronic Freedom of Information Act Amendments of 1996 (E-FOIA), the OPEN Government Act of 2007, and the FOIA Improvement Act of 2016.

### Nine Exemptions

| Exemption | Subject Matter |
|-----------|---------------|
| (b)(1) | Classified national security information |
| (b)(2) | Internal agency rules and practices |
| (b)(3) | Information exempt under other federal statutes |
| (b)(4) | Trade secrets and confidential business information |
| (b)(5) | Inter-agency or intra-agency communications (deliberative process, attorney-client, attorney work product) |
| (b)(6) | Personal privacy information |
| (b)(7) | Law enforcement records (with six sub-exemptions) |
| (b)(8) | Financial institution examination information |
| (b)(9) | Geological and geophysical information about wells |

### Presumption of Openness

The FOIA Improvement Act of 2016 codified the "foreseeable harm" standard, requiring agencies to demonstrate that disclosure would cause reasonably foreseeable harm to an interest protected by an exemption before withholding information. This reversed the prior practice of applying exemptions mechanically without considering whether harm would actually result from disclosure.

### Landmark Cases

**EPA v. Mink, 410 U.S. 73 (1973):** The Supreme Court held that Exemption 1 (classified information) does not permit in camera judicial review of classified documents, prompting the 1974 FOIA amendments that authorized such review.

**Department of Justice v. Reporters Committee for Freedom of the Press, 489 U.S. 749 (1989):** The Court held that compiled FBI rap sheets are exempt from disclosure under Exemption 7(C), establishing that the central purpose of FOIA is to reveal what the government is up to, not to reveal personal information about private citizens.

**Milner v. Department of the Navy, 562 U.S. 562 (2011):** The Court narrowed Exemption 2 to cover only records related to internal personnel rules and practices, rejecting the broader "High 2" interpretation that had allowed withholding of information when disclosure would risk circumvention of law.

### Office of Government Information Services (OGIS)

OGIS, established by the OPEN Government Act of 2007 within the National Archives and Records Administration, serves as the federal FOIA ombudsman. OGIS reviews agency compliance, mediates disputes between requesters and agencies, and recommends policy improvements. However, OGIS's determinations are advisory and non-binding.

### Annual Statistics (FY2023)

- Approximately 928,000 FOIA requests received government-wide
- Approximately 60% of requests fully or partially granted
- Median processing time: 26 days (simple requests); significantly longer for complex requests
- Total FOIA litigation costs exceeded $50 million

## Germany

### Legal Framework

**Informationsfreiheitsgesetz (IFG, 2006):** Enacted on January 1, 2006, the IFG provides a right of access to official information held by federal public authorities. The Act was a relative latecomer in the European context, influenced by the EU's Aarhus Convention implementation and Regulation 1049/2001.

### Exemptions

The IFG contains broad exemption categories that have been criticized for excessive scope:

| Section | Protected Interest |
|---------|-------------------|
| Section 3(1) | International relations, military affairs, internal/external security |
| Section 3(2) | Information provided by intelligence services |
| Section 3(3) | Ongoing decision-making processes; opinions and evaluations prepared for authorities |
| Section 3(4) | Information subject to confidentiality obligations under other laws |
| Section 3(6) | Fiscal interests of the federation |
| Section 3(7) | Information that would endanger environmental protection measures |
| Section 3(8) | Intelligence service operational information |
| Section 4 | Protection of official decision-making processes |
| Section 5 | Personal data protection |
| Section 6 | Intellectual property and trade secrets |

### Fee Structure

The IFG's fee structure has been criticized as a deterrent to access. Fees of up to 500 EUR per request may be charged, with some complex requests generating fees in the thousands. Civil society organizations (e.g., FragDenStaat) have advocated for the elimination or significant reduction of fees.

### Bundesbeauftragte für den Datenschutz und die Informationsfreiheit (BfDI)

The Federal Commissioner for Data Protection and Freedom of Information exercises oversight over the IFG, but in an advisory capacity only. The BfDI may issue recommendations and opinions on information disclosure disputes but cannot issue binding orders requiring disclosure. This contrasts with stronger oversight models such as the UK Information Commissioner, who can issue legally binding decision notices.

### FragDenStaat

The online platform FragDenStaat (AskTheState), operated by the Open Knowledge Foundation Germany, has significantly increased public engagement with the IFG by enabling citizens to submit requests electronically, publishing responses, and conducting strategic litigation to expand the scope of the right of access. FragDenStaat has submitted over 300,000 requests since its launch in 2011.

### State-Level FOI Laws

Germany's federal structure means that separate Informationsfreiheitsgesetze exist at the state (Länder) level. Most states have enacted their own FOI laws, though Baden-Württemberg did not enact one until 2015 and Bavaria remains the most restrictive. Hamburg's Transparenzgesetz (Transparency Act, 2012) is considered the most progressive, requiring proactive publication of a broad range of government information in a transparency register.

## ECHR & EU

### ECHR -- Right of Access to Information

**Magyar Helsinki Bizottság v. Hungary (Application No. 18030/11, Grand Chamber Judgment of November 8, 2016):** In a landmark ruling, the Grand Chamber held that Article 10 encompasses a right of access to state-held information when the information is needed for the exercise of freedom of expression. The Court established four criteria for determining whether a right of access applies:

1. **Purpose of the request:** Whether the information is sought for the purpose of informing public debate
2. **Nature of the information:** Whether the information is of public interest
3. **Role of the applicant:** Whether the applicant acts as a social watchdog (journalist, NGO, researcher)
4. **Ready and available information:** Whether the information already exists and does not require new compilation

**Sdružení Jihočeské Matky v. Czech Republic (Application No. 19101/03, Judgment of July 10, 2006):** The Court first suggested that denial of access to information might engage Article 10, paving the way for Magyar Helsinki.

**Társaság a Szabadságjogokért v. Hungary (Application No. 37374/05, Judgment of April 14, 2009):** The Court found a violation of Article 10 where Hungary refused an NGO's request for access to a constitutional complaint filed with the Constitutional Court, holding that the state had created an obstacle to the gathering of information on a matter of public interest.

### EU Regulation 1049/2001

Regulation (EC) No 1049/2001 regarding public access to European Parliament, Council, and Commission documents provides:

- Any EU citizen or resident has the right to access documents of EU institutions
- Three categories of exceptions: public interest (public security, defense, international relations, financial policy, court proceedings, investigations), privacy and commercial interests, and protection of decision-making processes
- Partial access: where only parts of a document are covered by exceptions, the remaining parts must be released
- 15-working-day response deadline (extendable to 30)

### Aarhus Convention (1998)

The UNECE Convention on Access to Information, Public Participation in Decision-Making and Access to Justice in Environmental Matters, signed in Aarhus, Denmark on June 25, 1998, established the international standard for environmental information access. The Convention has influenced broader FOI development through three pillars: access to environmental information, public participation in environmental decision-making, and access to justice in environmental matters. The EU ratified the Convention through Decision 2005/370/EC.

### EU Open Data Directive

Directive (EU) 2019/1024 on open data and the re-use of public sector information requires member states to make government data available in open, machine-readable formats. The Directive establishes a list of "high-value datasets" (geospatial, earth observation, meteorological, statistical, company, mobility) that must be made available free of charge through APIs.

### AI and Algorithmic Transparency

The application of freedom of information principles to government use of artificial intelligence and algorithmic decision-making represents an emerging frontier:

**South Korea:** The Personal Information Protection Commission has issued guidance requiring transparency in government algorithmic decision-making. The proposed AI Basic Act (2024) includes provisions for algorithmic impact assessments by government agencies.

**United States:** FOIA requests for government algorithms and AI systems have produced mixed results. Agencies have argued that source code and training data constitute trade secrets (Exemption 4) or internal deliberative materials (Exemption 5). The AI Bill of Rights (White House, 2022) calls for algorithmic transparency but is non-binding.

**Germany:** The IFG's broad deliberative process exemptions have been invoked to withhold algorithmic decision-making information. The Transparenzgesetz of Hamburg specifically requires disclosure of algorithms used in government services. The proposed Transparenzgesetz at the federal level, advocated by civil society organizations, would mandate disclosure of government AI systems.

**EU:** The AI Act (Regulation 2024/1689) imposes transparency and documentation requirements for high-risk AI systems used by government agencies. Regulation 1049/2001 applies to AI systems used by EU institutions. The right to explanation under GDPR Article 22 provides individuals affected by automated decisions with information about the logic involved.

### Whistleblower Protection and FOI

Freedom of information and whistleblower protection frameworks increasingly intersect, as unauthorized disclosures often fill gaps left by inadequate FOI regimes:

| Jurisdiction | FOI Strength | Whistleblower Protection | Relationship |
|-------------|-------------|--------------------------|-------------|
| South Korea | Moderate (high disclosure rate; 8 exemptions) | Limited (Public Interest Whistleblower Protection Act) | Weak FOI may drive leaks |
| United States | Strong (established jurisprudence; 9 exemptions) | Moderate (Whistleblower Protection Act; sector-specific laws) | FOIA backlog drives frustration |
| Germany | Weak (broad exemptions; fees; advisory oversight) | Strengthened (EU Directive 2019/1937 transposed) | Weak FOI historically drove leaks |
| EU | Moderate (Regulation 1049/2001) | Strong (Directive 2019/1937) | Complementary frameworks |

## Comparative Analysis

### Scope and Maturity

The United States has the longest experience with FOI (since 1967), the most extensive case law, and the highest annual request volume in absolute terms. South Korea has one of the most active FOI cultures in Asia, with request volumes exceeding the United States on a per-capita basis. Germany is a relative latecomer with a weaker framework, limited independent oversight, and higher barriers (fees). The ECHR has progressively recognized FOI as a component of Article 10, but the right remains more limited than under dedicated statutory frameworks.

### Exemption Breadth

Germany's IFG has the broadest exemptions, with multiple provisions protecting decision-making processes, intelligence information, and fiscal interests. The U.S. FOIA's nine exemptions are more tightly defined, particularly after the 2016 "foreseeable harm" codification. South Korea's eight exemptions are broadly framed but practically applied with a high disclosure rate. The EU Regulation 1049/2001 has relatively narrow exceptions but the EU institutions have been criticized for over-invoking the decision-making process exception.

### Independent Oversight

South Korea and the U.S. lack fully independent oversight bodies with binding authority. Germany's BfDI has advisory powers only. The EU relies on the European Ombudsman, whose recommendations are not legally binding but carry significant institutional weight. The strongest models internationally (UK Information Commissioner, Mexico's INAI) provide binding decision-making authority, but none of the four jurisdictions compared have adopted this model at the national/federal level.
