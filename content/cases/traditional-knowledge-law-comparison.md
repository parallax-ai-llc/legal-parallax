## Summary

Traditional knowledge (TK) refers to the knowledge, innovations, and practices of indigenous peoples and local communities that have been developed, sustained, and passed on from generation to generation within a community, often forming part of its cultural or spiritual identity. The legal protection of traditional knowledge intersects intellectual property law, biodiversity law, human rights law, and cultural heritage law, and remains one of the most contested areas of international legal development. South Korea has adopted a distinctive approach focused on the documentation and valorization of traditional Korean medicine (한의학) and traditional cultural expressions through the Korean Intellectual Property Office (특허청, KIPO) traditional knowledge database and the Intangible Cultural Heritage Act (무형문화재법), with the Traditional Knowledge Digital Library pilot drawing on India's model; access and benefit-sharing for genetic resources is governed by the Act on Access to and Utilization of Genetic Resources (유전자원법, 2017) implementing the Nagoya Protocol. The United States lacks comprehensive sui generis TK legislation, relying instead on existing intellectual property frameworks (patents, trademarks, trade secrets, and copyright), with the Trademark Act's Section 2(a) (15 U.S.C. Section 1052(a)) prohibition on disparaging or falsely suggestive marks providing limited defensive protection; the Indian Arts and Crafts Act (25 U.S.C. Sections 305-305e) addresses misrepresentation of Native American cultural products, and the National Park Service administers the Native American Graves Protection and Repatriation Act (NAGPRA, 25 U.S.C. Sections 3001-3013). Germany, operating within the EU framework, addresses TK primarily through the implementation of Regulation (EU) No. 511/2014 on compliance measures for the Nagoya Protocol, administered by the Bundesamt für Naturschutz (BfN), while the Patentgesetz (PatG) Section 34(8) requires disclosure of the origin of biological material in patent applications; Germany also participates actively in WIPO Intergovernmental Committee (IGC) negotiations. The ECHR/EU framework addresses TK through the Nagoya Protocol compliance regulation, the EU Biodiversity Strategy for 2030, and the ongoing WIPO IGC negotiations toward an international legal instrument on intellectual property and genetic resources, traditional knowledge, and traditional cultural expressions, with the WIPO Diplomatic Conference on Genetic Resources convened in 2024 resulting in a new treaty on IP and genetic resources.

## South Korea

### Legal Framework

South Korea's approach to traditional knowledge protection reflects its rich heritage of traditional Korean medicine, agricultural practices, and cultural expressions, combined with its obligations under the Convention on Biological Diversity (CBD) and the Nagoya Protocol.

**Intangible Cultural Heritage Act (무형문화재법):** Enacted in 2015 (replacing the Cultural Heritage Protection Act's intangible heritage provisions), this Act establishes a comprehensive framework for the identification, documentation, safeguarding, and transmission of intangible cultural heritage, including traditional knowledge systems. The Act designates Important Intangible Cultural Heritage (중요무형문화재) holders -- often called "Living National Treasures" (인간문화재) -- who serve as custodians and transmitters of traditional knowledge and skills. As of 2025, over 150 items have been designated, ranging from traditional medicine practices to craft techniques and performing arts.

**Act on Access to and Utilization of Genetic Resources (유전자원법, Act No. 14839, 2017):** This Act implements South Korea's obligations under the Nagoya Protocol on Access and Benefit-sharing. It establishes: (1) a national access regime for genetic resources and associated traditional knowledge held within South Korea; (2) compliance measures requiring users of foreign genetic resources and associated TK to demonstrate prior informed consent (PIC) and mutually agreed terms (MAT) from the providing country; (3) the establishment of a national focal point and competent national authorities; and (4) monitoring mechanisms including checkpoint requirements.

### Traditional Korean Medicine (한의학)

Traditional Korean medicine occupies a unique position as a formally institutionalized form of traditional knowledge. Under the Medical Service Act (의료법), traditional Korean medicine practitioners (한의사) receive formal education, licensing, and regulation equivalent to Western medicine practitioners. The Korean Medicine Promotion Act (한의약 육성법, 2003) specifically promotes research, development, and industrialization of traditional Korean medicine.

**Korean Traditional Knowledge Database:** KIPO has developed a traditional knowledge database modeled on India's Traditional Knowledge Digital Library (TKDL), documenting traditional Korean medicine formulations (처방) from classical texts such as the Dongui Bogam (동의보감, 1613) and Hyangyak Jipseongbang (향약집성방, 1433). This database serves as a defensive tool to prevent the erroneous granting of patents on traditional knowledge by making TK accessible as prior art to patent examiners.

### Genetic Resources and Benefit-Sharing

The Ministry of Environment (환경부) serves as the national focal point for the Nagoya Protocol, while the Rural Development Administration (농촌진흥청) and the National Institute of Biological Resources (국립생물자원관) manage access to genetic resources and associated traditional knowledge. The 2017 Act requires benefit-sharing arrangements for the commercial utilization of genetic resources and associated TK, with monetary benefits (royalties, license fees) and non-monetary benefits (technology transfer, capacity building) negotiated through mutually agreed terms.

## United States

### Legal Framework

The United States lacks comprehensive federal legislation specifically addressing traditional knowledge protection, relying instead on a patchwork of existing IP laws, specialized statutes addressing Native American cultural property, and tribal law.

**Patent Law and Traditional Knowledge:** The America Invents Act (AIA, 2011) expanded the scope of prior art that may be used to challenge patent validity, which has implications for TK protection. Under 35 U.S.C. Section 102, prior art includes knowledge that was "publicly known" or "in public use" anywhere in the world before the effective filing date, potentially encompassing documented traditional knowledge. The USPTO has implemented enhanced prior art search procedures for patent applications that may involve traditional knowledge, including access to TK databases such as India's TKDL. Inter partes review (IPR) proceedings before the Patent Trial and Appeal Board (PTAB) provide a mechanism for challenging patents that improperly claim traditional knowledge.

**Trademark Protection:** Section 2(a) of the Lanham Act (15 U.S.C. Section 1052(a)) prohibits the registration of marks that consist of or comprise matter that may disparage or falsely suggest a connection with persons, institutions, beliefs, or national symbols. This provision has been invoked to challenge trademarks that misappropriate indigenous cultural symbols and traditional knowledge. Additionally, the certification mark and collective mark provisions (15 U.S.C. Sections 1054, 1064) provide mechanisms for indigenous communities to protect traditional products and craft designations.

**Indian Arts and Crafts Act (25 U.S.C. Sections 305-305e):** This Act makes it unlawful to offer or display for sale any art or craft product in a manner that falsely suggests it is Indian produced, an Indian product, or the product of a particular Indian tribe. The Indian Arts and Crafts Board, within the Department of the Interior, administers the Act, which provides for both criminal penalties (up to $250,000 fine and 5 years imprisonment for first offense) and civil penalties (treble damages or statutory damages of $1,000 per day of violation).

**NAGPRA (25 U.S.C. Sections 3001-3013):** The Native American Graves Protection and Repatriation Act addresses the repatriation of Native American cultural items, including sacred objects and objects of cultural patrimony that may embody traditional knowledge, from federal agencies and federally funded museums to lineal descendants and culturally affiliated tribes. The 2023 revised regulations (43 C.F.R. Part 10) strengthened consultation requirements and disposition procedures.

### Trade Secret and Misappropriation

Some forms of traditional knowledge, particularly those that communities wish to keep confidential (such as sacred practices or medicinal formulations), may qualify for protection under trade secret law. The Defend Trade Secrets Act (18 U.S.C. Section 1836) provides federal trade secret protection, though the requirement that the information holder take "reasonable measures" to maintain secrecy creates challenges for communally held knowledge.

### Tribal Sovereignty and Tribal Law

Native American tribes, as sovereign nations, have inherent authority to enact their own laws protecting traditional knowledge. Several tribes have enacted tribal ordinances governing the use and disclosure of traditional knowledge, including the Tulalip Tribes' Intellectual Property Policy and the Navajo Nation's Sovereignty in Intellectual Properties Act. Federal law recognizes tribal sovereignty over cultural resources, but enforcement across jurisdictional boundaries remains challenging.

## Germany

### Legal Framework

Germany addresses traditional knowledge protection primarily through its obligations under the Convention on Biological Diversity, the Nagoya Protocol, and EU legislation, supplemented by patent law disclosure requirements and cultural heritage protection measures.

**Nagoya Protocol Implementation:** Germany ratified the Nagoya Protocol in 2016 and implements it through Regulation (EU) No. 511/2014, with the Bundesamt für Naturschutz (BfN, Federal Agency for Nature Conservation) serving as the national competent authority. The BfN monitors compliance with due diligence requirements for users of genetic resources and associated traditional knowledge, including researchers and companies that utilize genetic resources obtained from countries that regulate access.

**Patent Disclosure Requirements:** Section 34(8) of the Patentgesetz (PatG) requires patent applicants to disclose the geographic origin of biological material if the invention is based on or uses biological material. While failure to disclose does not invalidate the patent (unlike in some other jurisdictions), it may constitute a procedural irregularity. This disclosure requirement implements EU Directive 98/44/EC (Biotechnology Directive) Article 13(1)(b), which requires disclosure of the geographical origin of biological material of plant or animal origin.

### The European Patent Office and Traditional Knowledge

Germany hosts the European Patent Office (EPO), which has integrated traditional knowledge databases into its prior art search tools. The EPO's guidelines for examination (Part G, Chapter IV) address the consideration of traditional knowledge in the assessment of novelty and inventive step, and the EPO has partnered with institutions including the World Intellectual Property Organization and the Indian government to access TK databases for examiner searches.

### Cultural Heritage and Intangible Heritage

Germany's Kulturgutschutzgesetz (KGSG, Cultural Property Protection Act, 2016) primarily addresses tangible cultural property but intersects with traditional knowledge through the protection of objects and collections that embody traditional knowledge systems. Germany is a party to the UNESCO Convention for the Safeguarding of the Intangible Cultural Heritage (2003) and has established a nationwide inventory of intangible cultural heritage, including traditional crafts, agricultural practices, and folk medicine traditions.

### Research and Development Framework

The Deutsche Forschungsgemeinschaft (DFG, German Research Foundation) has adopted guidelines on the responsible use of genetic resources and associated traditional knowledge in research, requiring compliance with the Nagoya Protocol and the CBD's access and benefit-sharing provisions. Universities and research institutions must implement due diligence procedures and maintain records of the provenance of genetic resources and TK used in their research.

### German Engagement in International Negotiations

Germany has been an active participant in WIPO IGC negotiations, generally supporting the development of an international legal instrument addressing the relationship between intellectual property and genetic resources, traditional knowledge, and traditional cultural expressions. Germany has advocated for mandatory disclosure requirements in patent applications as a key element of any international agreement, aligning with the position of the EU and many developing countries.

## ECHR & EU

### EU Framework on Genetic Resources and TK

**Regulation (EU) No. 511/2014 (Nagoya Protocol Compliance):** This regulation establishes compliance measures for users of genetic resources and traditional knowledge associated with genetic resources within the EU. Key provisions include:

**Due Diligence (Article 4):** Users of genetic resources and associated TK must exercise due diligence to ascertain that genetic resources and TK have been accessed in accordance with applicable ABS legislation and that benefits are shared on mutually agreed terms. Users must seek, keep, and transfer to subsequent users information on: (1) access permits or equivalent evidence of PIC and MAT; (2) the date and place of access; (3) the source of the genetic resources or TK; and (4) subsequent users' obligations.

**Checkpoints (Article 7):** The regulation designates research funding bodies and final product development stages as checkpoints where users must declare their exercise of due diligence, with the European Commission and national authorities monitoring compliance.

**Best Practices (Article 8):** User associations may develop best practices for compliance with due diligence, which the Commission may recognize as meeting the regulation's requirements.

### Biotechnology Directive (98/44/EC)

The EU Biotechnology Directive addresses the patentability of biotechnological inventions, with implications for traditional knowledge:

**Article 13(1)(b):** Requires disclosure of the geographical origin of biological material in patent applications where the invention involves or uses biological material.

**Recitals 26-27:** Recognize the importance of traditional knowledge in agricultural and pharmaceutical research and the need for appropriate benefit-sharing mechanisms.

### WIPO Intergovernmental Committee Negotiations

The WIPO IGC, established in 2000, has been conducting text-based negotiations toward an international legal instrument (or instruments) addressing: (1) intellectual property and genetic resources; (2) traditional knowledge; and (3) traditional cultural expressions/folklore. The EU, represented by the European Commission, has participated as a key negotiating bloc:

**2024 WIPO Diplomatic Conference on Genetic Resources:** The May 2024 Diplomatic Conference in Geneva adopted a new treaty requiring patent applicants to disclose the country of origin or source of genetic resources and traditional knowledge associated with genetic resources when the claimed invention is based on these resources. This landmark treaty, negotiated over more than two decades, represents the first international instrument directly linking the patent system to genetic resource access and benefit-sharing obligations.

### ECHR and Indigenous Cultural Rights

While the ECHR does not contain specific provisions on traditional knowledge, the European Court of Human Rights has addressed related issues under existing Convention rights:

**G. and E. v. Norway (Application No. 9278/81, 1983):** The Commission recognized that the traditional lifestyle of the Sami people could fall within the scope of Article 8 (right to respect for private and family life).

**Handolsdalen Sami Village v. Sweden (Application No. 39013/04, 2010):** The Court addressed the rights of the Sami people regarding their traditional reindeer herding practices, finding a violation of Article 6 (right to a fair trial) in proceedings concerning reindeer grazing rights that embody traditional knowledge of land management.

**Muonio Sami Village v. Sweden (Application No. 28222/18):** Recent cases have further explored the intersection of indigenous traditional practices and Convention rights, including Article 8 and Article 1 of Protocol 1 (protection of property).

### EU Biodiversity Strategy for 2030

The EU Biodiversity Strategy for 2030, adopted as part of the European Green Deal, acknowledges the role of indigenous and local communities' traditional knowledge in biodiversity conservation and sustainable use of natural resources. The strategy commits the EU to supporting the implementation of the Nagoya Protocol and strengthening benefit-sharing mechanisms for the use of genetic resources and associated traditional knowledge.

## Comparative Analysis

### Defensive versus Positive Protection

A fundamental distinction in TK protection approaches is between defensive protection (preventing others from obtaining IP rights over TK) and positive protection (granting affirmative rights to TK holders):

**Defensive Protection:** All four jurisdictions employ some form of defensive protection. South Korea's TK database and KIPO's prior art searches, the United States' expanded prior art scope under the AIA, Germany's patent disclosure requirements, and the EPO's integration of TK databases all serve to prevent the erroneous patenting of traditional knowledge. However, defensive protection alone does not address the broader appropriation of TK through means that do not involve patent claims.

**Positive Protection:** South Korea's Intangible Cultural Heritage Act and genetic resources legislation provide the most developed positive protection framework among the jurisdictions compared, though the scope remains limited. The United States offers fragmented positive protection through the Indian Arts and Crafts Act and tribal law. Germany and the EU rely primarily on the Nagoya Protocol framework for positive protection related to genetic resources and associated TK. No jurisdiction has enacted comprehensive sui generis positive TK protection legislation of the type advocated by many developing countries in the WIPO IGC negotiations.

### Disclosure Requirements as a Bridging Mechanism

Patent disclosure requirements represent a significant point of convergence. Germany (Section 34(8) PatG), the EU (Directive 98/44/EC), and the 2024 WIPO GR Treaty all mandate or promote disclosure of the origin of genetic resources and associated TK in patent applications. The United States remains the notable outlier, having resisted mandatory disclosure requirements in patent law, though the USPTO has enhanced its TK-related prior art search capabilities. South Korea has implemented disclosure requirements through its genetic resources legislation.

### The Challenge of Communal Ownership

All jurisdictions face the fundamental challenge that existing IP systems are designed around individual or corporate rights holders, while traditional knowledge is typically communally developed, held, and transmitted. South Korea's "Living National Treasures" system partially addresses this by recognizing individual custodians within a communal tradition. The United States' recognition of tribal sovereignty provides a framework for community-level governance of TK. Germany and the EU's Nagoya Protocol implementation relies on the providing country's domestic framework to define TK holders and their rights.

### Biopiracy: Prevention and Remediation

The prevention of biopiracy -- the misappropriation of genetic resources and associated TK without proper access authorization and benefit-sharing -- varies significantly across jurisdictions. South Korea's 2017 Genetic Resources Act establishes clear ABS obligations with monitoring and enforcement mechanisms. The EU's Regulation 511/2014 creates a due diligence framework with checkpoint monitoring. The United States lacks a comprehensive federal ABS framework, relying on patent challenges and limited statutory provisions. The 2024 WIPO GR Treaty's disclosure requirement represents a significant step toward a global minimum standard for biopiracy prevention through the patent system.
