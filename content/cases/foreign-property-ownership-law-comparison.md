## Summary

Foreign property ownership law governs the rights of non-citizens and non-resident entities to acquire, hold, and dispose of real property within a jurisdiction, reflecting each state's balance between attracting foreign investment and protecting national sovereignty, security interests, and domestic housing affordability. South Korea regulates foreign real property acquisition through the Foreigner's Land Acquisition Act (외국인토지법, enacted 1961, comprehensively revised 1998) and the Act on Report on Real Estate Transactions by Foreigners (외국인 부동산 등 취득·관리 및 이용에 관한 법률, enacted 2023 as a comprehensive revision of prior regulations), which require foreigners to report land acquisitions within 60 days but generally permit foreign ownership without prior approval except for land in military installations, cultural heritage areas, ecological preservation zones, and other designated restricted areas where prior permission from the relevant authority is required; the 2023 Act significantly strengthened reporting requirements and introduced new restrictions in response to concerns about foreign speculative investment in Korean real estate, particularly in Jeju Province. The United States places virtually no federal restrictions on foreign real property ownership, with the primary federal reporting requirement being the Agricultural Foreign Investment Disclosure Act (AFIDA, 7 U.S.C. Sections 3501-3508), which requires foreign persons to report holdings of U.S. agricultural land; states have varied approaches, with approximately 30 states imposing some restrictions on foreign agricultural land ownership and recent legislation in states including Florida (SB 264, 2023) restricting property purchases by citizens or entities of designated countries near military installations and critical infrastructure. Germany generally permits foreign property ownership without restriction under the principle of free movement of capital, though the Außenwirtschaftsgesetz (AWG, Foreign Trade and Payments Act) and Außenwirtschaftsverordnung (AWV) authorize the Federal Ministry of Economic Affairs to review and potentially prohibit acquisitions by non-EU/non-EFTA persons that threaten public order or security, particularly for property near critical infrastructure. The EU guarantees free movement of capital under Article 63 TFEU, which encompasses the right to acquire real property across member states, though Article 65 permits restrictions justified by public policy, public security, or tax compliance, and specific derogations have been granted to newer member states for agricultural land purchases.

## South Korea

### Legal Framework

South Korea has progressively liberalized foreign property ownership since the 1997 Asian Financial Crisis, when capital market reforms opened the real estate market to foreign investors. However, growing concerns about speculative foreign investment and national security have prompted a partial tightening of regulations.

**Act on Report on Real Estate Transactions by Foreigners (외국인 부동산 등 취득·관리 및 이용에 관한 법률):** This comprehensive Act, enacted in 2023 to replace and consolidate the Foreigner's Land Acquisition Act and related regulations, establishes the current framework. Foreigners (defined as non-Korean nationals, foreign corporations, and Korean corporations in which foreigners hold a majority stake) must report real property acquisitions to the relevant local government (시·군·구청) within 60 days of the acquisition.

### Restricted Areas

The Act designates specific categories of land where foreign acquisition requires prior permission:

| Restricted Category | Approving Authority | Basis |
|--------------------|--------------------|----|
| Military installations and protection zones | Ministry of National Defense | Military Installations Protection Act |
| Cultural heritage protection zones | Cultural Heritage Administration | Cultural Heritage Protection Act |
| Ecological and landscape conservation areas | Ministry of Environment | Natural Environment Conservation Act |
| Agricultural promotion areas | Ministry of Agriculture | Farmland Act restrictions |
| Forest protection areas | Korea Forest Service | Forest Protection Act |
| Water source protection areas | Ministry of Environment | Water Supply Act |

### Jeju Province Special Provisions

Jeju Special Self-Governing Province has been a particular focus of foreign property ownership regulation due to the Jeju Investment Immigration System (제주 투자이민제도), introduced in 2010, which offered permanent residency to foreigners investing 500 million KRW or more in Jeju real estate. This program attracted substantial Chinese investment, raising concerns about housing affordability for local residents and the proportion of foreign-owned land. The program was suspended for new applications in 2023, and the 2023 Act introduced enhanced scrutiny of foreign acquisitions in Jeju.

### Reciprocity Principle

The Act applies a reciprocity principle (상호주의, Article 3): where a foreign country restricts Korean nationals from acquiring real property in that country, the Korean government may impose equivalent restrictions on nationals of that country. This principle has been applied sparingly but provides a legal basis for country-specific restrictions.

### Statistics

As of 2024, foreign-owned land in South Korea totaled approximately 256 million square meters (256 square kilometers), representing roughly 0.26 percent of total national land area. The largest foreign landowners by nationality are citizens of the United States (primarily military-related holdings), China, Japan, and Canada.

## United States

### Legal Framework

The United States has historically maintained one of the most open foreign property ownership regimes in the world, with restrictions focused primarily on agricultural land, military-adjacent property, and sanctions compliance.

**No Federal Prohibition:** There is no general federal law prohibiting or restricting foreign ownership of real property in the United States. Non-citizens, including non-resident aliens and foreign corporations, may generally acquire, hold, and dispose of real property on the same basis as U.S. citizens.

**Agricultural Foreign Investment Disclosure Act (AFIDA, 7 U.S.C. Sections 3501-3508):** Enacted in 1978, AFIDA requires foreign persons who acquire or dispose of interests in U.S. agricultural land to report the transaction to the Secretary of Agriculture within 90 days. Failure to report is subject to civil penalties of up to 25 percent of the fair market value of the land. As of the most recent USDA report, foreign persons held approximately 43.4 million acres of U.S. agricultural land, representing approximately 3.4 percent of all privately held agricultural land.

### State Restrictions

Approximately 30 states impose some form of restriction on foreign ownership of agricultural land, with significant variation:

| State | Restriction Type |
|-------|-----------------|
| Iowa | Prohibits foreign ownership of agricultural land; existing holdings grandfathered |
| Missouri | Non-resident aliens prohibited from owning agricultural land exceeding 5 acres |
| Minnesota | Non-resident aliens limited to 80 acres |
| Wisconsin | Non-resident aliens prohibited from owning more than 640 acres |
| Oklahoma | Aliens ineligible for citizenship prohibited from acquiring property |
| North Dakota | Corporations and LLCs with alien members prohibited from farming or owning farmland |

### CFIUS and National Security

The Committee on Foreign Investment in the United States (CFIUS), operating under the Foreign Investment Risk Review Modernization Act (FIRRMA, 2018), reviews certain transactions involving foreign investment for national security implications. FIRRMA expanded CFIUS jurisdiction to cover the purchase or lease of real property near military installations and other sensitive government facilities, even where no operating business is involved.

### Florida SB 264 (2023)

Florida Senate Bill 264, signed into law on May 8, 2023, represents a significant departure from the traditionally open U.S. approach. The law prohibits citizens of China, Russia, Iran, North Korea, Cuba, Venezuela, and Syria (and entities domiciled in or controlled by persons from those countries) from purchasing real property within 10 miles of military installations, critical infrastructure facilities, and other specified areas. Chinese citizens are additionally restricted from purchasing agricultural land anywhere in Florida and residential property in certain areas. The law has faced legal challenges on constitutional equal protection grounds, with ongoing federal litigation.

## Germany

### Legal Framework

Germany generally permits foreign property ownership without restriction, consistent with EU free movement of capital principles, though recent security-focused reforms have introduced screening mechanisms for non-EU acquisitions.

**BGB Property Law:** German property law applies equally to German nationals, EU/EFTA nationals, and third-country nationals. There are no nationality-based restrictions on the acquisition, holding, or disposition of real property. Registration in the Grundbuch (land register) requires no nationality verification.

**Außenwirtschaftsgesetz (AWG) and Außenwirtschaftsverordnung (AWV):** The AWG and its implementing ordinance (AWV), significantly strengthened through amendments in 2020 and 2021, authorize the Federal Ministry for Economic Affairs and Climate Action (Bundesministerium für Wirtschaft und Klimaschutz, BMWK) to review acquisitions of German businesses and assets by non-EU/non-EFTA persons. While the primary focus is on business acquisitions, the screening mechanism can apply to real property acquisitions near critical infrastructure, military installations, or facilities relevant to public order and security.

### EU Free Movement of Capital

As an EU member state, Germany is bound by Article 63 TFEU, which prohibits restrictions on the movement of capital between member states and between member states and third countries. This provision encompasses the right to acquire real property. Restrictions are permitted only on grounds of public policy or public security (Article 65(1)(b) TFEU) and must be proportionate.

### Practical Considerations

While German law imposes no formal restrictions on foreign property ownership, several practical factors affect foreign acquisitions. The German property transfer tax (Grunderwerbsteuer, currently 3.5-6.5 percent depending on the federal state) applies equally to all purchasers. Anti-money laundering requirements under the Geldwäschegesetz (GwG) require notaries and real estate agents to conduct customer due diligence, including verification of beneficial ownership for corporate purchasers. The German Grundbuch system requires notarial authentication (Beurkundung) of all real property transfers, providing a layer of regulatory oversight.

### Post-COVID Housing Debate

The COVID-19 pandemic intensified debates about foreign investment in German residential real estate, particularly in major cities experiencing housing affordability pressures. While Germany has not adopted foreign buyer restrictions comparable to those in Canada, Australia, or New Zealand, the topic has entered political discourse, with some parties advocating for restrictions on non-EU speculative investment in residential property.

## ECHR & EU

### Free Movement of Capital (Article 63 TFEU)

Article 63 TFEU is the foundational EU provision governing foreign property ownership: "Within the framework of the provisions set out in this Chapter, all restrictions on the movement of capital between Member States and between Member States and third countries shall be prohibited."

The CJEU has consistently held that this provision encompasses the right to acquire real property in another member state:

**Konle v. Republik Österreich (Case C-302/97, 1999):** The Court held that Austrian legislation requiring prior administrative authorization for the acquisition of land by non-nationals was a restriction on the free movement of capital. While the Court accepted that restrictions could be justified by public interest objectives such as maintaining a permanent population and economic activity in rural areas, the Austrian system lacked the proportionality required by EU law.

**Ospelt v. Schlössle Weissenberg Familienstiftung (Case C-452/01, 2003):** The Court examined restrictions on the acquisition of agricultural land in Liechtenstein (applying under the EEA Agreement), holding that prior authorization requirements for agricultural land transfers could be justified by the objective of maintaining agricultural use, provided the measures were proportionate and non-discriminatory.

### Transitional Restrictions for New Member States

Several EU accession treaties included transitional periods during which new member states could maintain restrictions on the acquisition of agricultural land and forests by nationals of other member states:

| Accession | States | Transitional Period | Expiry |
|-----------|--------|--------------------|----|
| 2004 | Poland, Czech Republic, Hungary, Estonia, Latvia, Lithuania, Slovakia | 7-12 years | 2011-2016 |
| 2007 | Romania, Bulgaria | 7 years | 2014 |
| 2013 | Croatia | 7 years | 2020 |

The expiration of these transitional periods generated political controversy, particularly in Poland and Hungary, where concerns about agricultural land purchases by Western European investors led to attempts to maintain restrictions through indirect regulatory measures.

### ECHR Property and Foreign Ownership

The ECHR has addressed foreign property ownership primarily through Article 1 of Protocol No. 1. In general, restrictions on foreign property ownership are assessed under the "control of use" rule, with the Court applying a proportionality test and affording states a wide margin of appreciation. The Court has not established a right to acquire property in a foreign state but has held that where property has been acquired, its deprivation must comply with the proportionality requirements of Article 1 of Protocol No. 1.

### Anti-Money Laundering Directives

EU anti-money laundering directives, most recently the Sixth AML Directive (Directive (EU) 2024/1640), impose due diligence and reporting requirements that significantly affect foreign property acquisitions. Real estate agents, notaries, and legal professionals involved in property transactions are "obliged entities" under the AML framework, required to verify the identity and beneficial ownership of purchasers and report suspicious transactions.

## Comparative Analysis

### Openness Spectrum

The jurisdictions occupy different positions on the openness spectrum for foreign property ownership. Germany (for EU/EFTA nationals) provides the most open access, with no restrictions of any kind. The United States has historically been highly open but is experiencing a trend toward selective restrictions based on nationality and proximity to sensitive facilities. South Korea permits foreign ownership with reporting requirements and targeted restrictions for sensitive areas. All jurisdictions have moved toward greater scrutiny of foreign acquisitions in recent years, reflecting growing concerns about national security, housing affordability, and speculative investment.

### National Security Focus

National security considerations have become increasingly prominent in all jurisdictions. The United States has expanded CFIUS review to cover real property near military installations. Germany has strengthened AWG screening for non-EU acquisitions. South Korea restricts foreign acquisitions near military installations and has introduced enhanced scrutiny for acquisitions by nationals of specific countries. The EU's framework for screening foreign direct investment (Regulation (EU) 2019/452) provides a coordination mechanism for member states to address security concerns related to foreign property acquisitions.

### Agricultural Land

Agricultural land represents a specific category of concern across jurisdictions, driven by food security considerations. The United States has the most developed system of agricultural land reporting (AFIDA) and state-level restrictions. The EU granted transitional periods for new member states to restrict agricultural land sales, reflecting the sensitivity of this issue. South Korea restricts foreign agricultural land acquisition in designated agricultural promotion areas. Germany relies on general agricultural use requirements rather than nationality-based restrictions.
