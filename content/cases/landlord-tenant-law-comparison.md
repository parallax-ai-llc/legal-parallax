## Summary

Landlord-tenant law governs the relationship between property owners who lease residential or commercial space and the tenants who occupy it, addressing rent regulation, lease termination, security of tenure, habitability standards, and the procedural requirements for eviction, all reflecting fundamental choices about whether housing is treated primarily as a market commodity or as a social right requiring state intervention to protect vulnerable occupants. South Korea's landlord-tenant regime is governed by the Housing Lease Protection Act (주택임대차보호법, enacted 1981), the Commercial Building Lease Protection Act (상가건물 임대차보호법, enacted 2001), and general lease provisions of the Korean Civil Code (민법 Articles 618-654), with the landmark 2020 amendments introducing the so-called "tenant three-rights" (임차인 3법) package comprising a lease renewal request right (계약갱신청구권), a rent increase cap of 5 percent (전월세상한제), and a mandatory reporting system for lease terms (전월세신고제), profoundly reshaping the balance of power between landlords and tenants in one of the world's most expensive housing markets. The United States regulates landlord-tenant relationships primarily at the state and local level, with no comprehensive federal residential tenancy law; the Uniform Residential Landlord and Tenant Act (URLTA, 1972) has been adopted in approximately 21 states, while others maintain independent statutory frameworks; rent control exists in only a handful of jurisdictions (notably New York City, San Francisco, Los Angeles, and cities in New Jersey, Maryland, and Oregon), and eviction protections vary dramatically from state to state. Germany provides among the world's strongest tenant protections through the BGB Sections 535-580a (Mietrecht), which establish an implied-indefinite-duration tenancy model where landlords may terminate only for specific statutory grounds (Kündigungsschutz), supplemented by the Mietpreisbremse (rent brake) system enacted in 2015 and strengthened in 2019 and 2020, and local rent caps (Mietendeckel) attempted by Berlin in 2020 but struck down by the Bundesverfassungsgericht in 2021 as exceeding state legislative competence. The EU has not harmonized landlord-tenant law, though the ECHR has developed significant jurisprudence on housing rights under Article 8 (right to respect for private and family life and home), with the Court requiring states to ensure procedural fairness in eviction proceedings and recognizing the particular vulnerability of certain tenant categories.

## South Korea

### Legal Framework

South Korea's landlord-tenant law is shaped by the unique characteristics of the Korean housing market, including the distinctive jeonse (전세) deposit system, extreme housing price pressure in metropolitan Seoul, and a history of tenant displacement through rapid urban redevelopment.

**Housing Lease Protection Act (주택임대차보호법):** Enacted in 1981, this Act provides the foundational protections for residential tenants. Key provisions include a minimum lease duration of 2 years (Article 4), even if the parties agree to a shorter term; the tenant's right to oppose the new owner's eviction claim if the tenant has a confirmed date (확정일자) on the lease contract (Article 3-2); and priority of the tenant's security deposit claim in the event of the property's sale, foreclosure, or the landlord's bankruptcy.

### The 2020 "Tenant Three-Rights" Reform (임차인 3법)

The most significant reform in recent Korean tenancy law occurred through a package of three laws enacted on July 31, 2020:

**Lease Renewal Request Right (계약갱신청구권, Article 6-3):** Tenants gained the right to request renewal of their lease for one additional term of up to 2 years (for a total potential tenure of 4 years), which the landlord may refuse only on specified grounds including the landlord's or immediate family member's intention to personally occupy the property, the tenant's material breach, and the tenant's failure to pay rent for three consecutive months.

**Rent Increase Cap (전월세상한제, Article 7):** During lease renewal under the renewal request right, the landlord may not increase rent by more than 5 percent above the previous term's rent. This provision applies to both monthly rent (월세) and jeonse deposit amounts.

**Lease Reporting System (전월세신고제, Article 6-2):** Parties to residential leases above a threshold amount in designated areas must report lease terms (including rent and deposit amounts) to local government authorities, creating a transparent database of actual rental market conditions.

### Jeonse System (전세)

The jeonse system is a uniquely Korean tenancy arrangement in which the tenant provides a large lump-sum deposit (typically 50-80 percent of the property's market value) to the landlord instead of paying monthly rent. The landlord invests the deposit and earns returns, returning the full deposit at the end of the lease. This system has been in decline as a proportion of total leases but remains prevalent, particularly for apartments in Seoul. The jeonse system creates specific legal risks, including the potential for the landlord's insolvency or inability to return the deposit, which has led to the enactment of the Housing Deposit Guarantee Act and the establishment of the Housing & Urban Guarantee Corporation (주택도시보증공사, HUG) to provide deposit insurance.

### Jeonse Fraud Crisis (2022-2023)

A major crisis erupted in 2022-2023 when a surge in so-called "gap investment" (갭투자) -- the practice of purchasing rental properties by borrowing against the difference between the purchase price and jeonse deposit -- led to widespread landlord defaults as property prices fell. Thousands of tenants were unable to recover their jeonse deposits, prompting emergency legislative responses including enhanced criminal penalties for jeonse fraud, expanded deposit guarantee coverage, and a special prosecution unit dedicated to jeonse-related crimes.

## United States

### Legal Framework

Landlord-tenant law in the United States is primarily a matter of state and local regulation, creating enormous variation across jurisdictions.

**Common Law Origins:** American landlord-tenant law evolved from English feudal property law, which treated the lease as a conveyance of an estate in land rather than a contractual relationship. Modern reforms, beginning with the landmark decision in Javins v. First National Realty Corp., 428 F.2d 1071 (D.C. Cir. 1970), reconceptualized residential leases as contracts, importing the implied warranty of habitability and other contractual protections.

**Uniform Residential Landlord and Tenant Act (URLTA, 1972):** Promulgated by the National Conference of Commissioners on Uniform State Laws, the URLTA has been adopted in approximately 21 states (with variations). It establishes a comprehensive framework including the implied warranty of habitability, limitations on security deposits, procedures for lease termination and eviction, and tenant remedies for landlord noncompliance.

### Rent Control

Rent regulation in the United States is highly localized:

| Jurisdiction | Type | Key Features |
|-------------|------|--------------|
| New York City | Rent control and rent stabilization | Rent control (pre-1947 buildings, original tenants); rent stabilization (buildings with 6+ units built before 1974) |
| California (statewide) | AB 1482 (Tenant Protection Act of 2019) | Rent increases capped at 5% + local CPI (max 10%); just cause eviction after 12 months |
| Oregon (statewide) | SB 608 (2019) | First statewide rent control; caps at 7% + CPI; just cause eviction after 12 months |
| Washington, D.C. | Rental Housing Act | Rent increases limited for rent-controlled units; tenant associations; right of first refusal |
| New Jersey | Local option | Multiple municipalities with rent control ordinances |

### Eviction Process

Eviction procedures vary significantly by state but generally follow a pattern of: notice to the tenant (cure or quit, pay or quit, or unconditional quit); filing of an unlawful detainer or summary process action; court hearing; judgment and writ of possession; and execution by the sheriff or marshal. The COVID-19 pandemic led to the imposition of federal and state eviction moratoriums, most notably the Centers for Disease Control and Prevention moratorium (2020-2021), which was ultimately struck down by the Supreme Court in Alabama Association of Realtors v. Department of Health and Human Services (2021).

### Fair Housing Act

The federal Fair Housing Act (42 U.S.C. Sections 3601-3619) prohibits discrimination in housing, including rental housing, on the basis of race, color, national origin, religion, sex, familial status, and disability. Many states and localities add protected categories including sexual orientation, gender identity, source of income, and immigration status.

## Germany

### Legal Framework

Germany's tenant protection regime is among the most comprehensive in the world, reflecting a housing market in which approximately 50 percent of the population rents rather than owns (the highest rate among major developed economies).

**BGB Mietrecht (Sections 535-580a):** The BGB provisions on residential tenancy establish an indefinite-duration tenancy as the default model. Fixed-term leases are permitted only in specific circumstances enumerated in Section 575 BGB (e.g., the landlord's personal need after the fixed term, planned substantial renovation, or intended use as employee housing).

### Kündigungsschutz (Termination Protection)

The landlord may terminate a residential lease only for "legitimate interest" (berechtigtes Interesse, Section 573 BGB):

| Ground | Provision | Requirements |
|--------|-----------|-------------|
| Tenant breach (Vertragsverletzung) | Section 573(2) No. 1 | Material breach of contractual obligations; typically requires prior warning |
| Personal need (Eigenbedarf) | Section 573(2) No. 2 | Landlord needs the property for personal or family use; must be genuine and specific |
| Economic disadvantage (wirtschaftliche Verwertung) | Section 573(2) No. 3 | Continuation of lease would prevent appropriate economic use and cause substantial disadvantage to the landlord |

Notice periods increase with the duration of the tenancy: 3 months for tenancies up to 5 years; 6 months for tenancies of 5-8 years; and 9 months for tenancies over 8 years (Section 573c BGB). The tenant may object to termination and demand continuation of the lease on hardship grounds (Sozialklausel, Section 574 BGB) if the termination would impose unreasonable hardship on the tenant, their family, or members of their household.

### Mietpreisbremse (Rent Brake)

The Mietpreisbremse, introduced by the Mietrechtsnovellierungsgesetz of 2015 and strengthened by amendments in 2019 and 2020, limits rent increases for new lease agreements in "tight housing markets" (angespannte Wohnungsmärkte) designated by state governments:

**New Leases:** In designated areas, the rent for a new lease may not exceed 10 percent above the local reference rent (ortsübliche Vergleichsmiete), with exceptions for new construction (first use after October 1, 2014), comprehensive modernization, and cases where the previous tenant's rent was already above the cap.

**Existing Leases:** Rent increases during an existing tenancy are governed by Section 558 BGB, which permits increases to the local reference rent level with a cap of 20 percent over three years (reduced to 15 percent in tight housing markets).

### Berlin Mietendeckel (2020-2021)

The Berlin state legislature enacted the Gesetz zur Mietenbegrenzung im Wohnungswesen (Mietendeckel, rent cap law) on February 23, 2020, imposing a five-year rent freeze and mandatory rent reductions for rents exceeding defined thresholds. On April 15, 2021, the Bundesverfassungsgericht struck down the Mietendeckel as unconstitutional (2 BvF 1/20 and 2 BvL 5/20), holding that the federal government had exhaustively regulated rent law through the BGB, precluding state-level rent regulation under Article 72(1) GG (concurrent legislative competence).

### Mietspiegel (Rent Index)

A distinctive feature of German tenancy law is the Mietspiegel (rent index) system. Municipalities publish rent indices reflecting the "local reference rent" for comparable properties, which serve as the benchmark for rent increase limits and the Mietpreisbremse. The 2022 reform (Mietspiegelreformgesetz) made the creation of qualified rent indices mandatory for municipalities with populations exceeding 50,000.

## ECHR & EU

### ECHR Article 8 and Housing Rights

The European Court of Human Rights has developed significant housing-related jurisprudence under Article 8 ECHR (right to respect for private and family life and home).

**McCann v. United Kingdom (Application No. 19009/04, 2008):** The Court held that any person at risk of losing their home must be able to have the proportionality of the eviction determined by an independent tribunal, regardless of the legal basis for the occupancy. This decision established that even occupants without formal tenancy rights are entitled to a proportionality review before eviction.

**Connors v. United Kingdom (Application No. 66746/01, 2004):** The Court found a violation of Article 8 where a local authority evicted a Gypsy family from a local authority site without providing any reasons or opportunity to challenge the eviction. The Court held that the procedural safeguards available to the applicant were wholly insufficient to protect his Article 8 rights.

**Yordanova and Others v. Bulgaria (Application No. 25446/06, 2012):** The Court held that the forced eviction of a Roma community from informal settlements without adequate consideration of the occupants' personal circumstances and alternative housing options violated Article 8.

### European Social Charter

The European Social Charter (revised 1996) provides additional tenant protections through Article 31 (right to housing), which requires states parties to take measures designed to promote access to housing of an adequate standard, prevent and reduce homelessness, and make the price of housing accessible to those without adequate resources. The European Committee of Social Rights has found violations of Article 31 in cases involving inadequate protection against eviction and insufficient social housing provision.

### EU Housing Policy

While the EU lacks competence to legislate directly on housing law, several EU instruments affect landlord-tenant relationships: the Energy Performance of Buildings Directive 2010/31/EU (as amended by Directive (EU) 2024/1275) requires energy performance certificates for rented properties; the EU anti-discrimination directives (Racial Equality Directive 2000/43/EC and Gender Goods and Services Directive 2004/113/EC) prohibit discrimination in housing; and the European Pillar of Social Rights (Principle 19) declares that access to social housing or housing assistance of good quality shall be provided for those in need.

## Comparative Analysis

### Tenant Security Models

The four systems represent fundamentally different models of tenant security. Germany provides the strongest security of tenure through its indefinite-duration default lease and for-cause-only termination regime, treating housing stability as a fundamental social interest. South Korea's 2020 reforms moved significantly toward the German model by introducing the lease renewal request right, though with a more limited scope (one additional 2-year term rather than indefinite protection). The United States provides the weakest general tenant security, with most states permitting landlords to terminate month-to-month tenancies without cause upon notice, though specific jurisdictions provide just-cause eviction protections. The ECHR requires procedural proportionality in eviction proceedings but leaves substantive tenant protections to member states.

### Rent Regulation

Rent regulation philosophy differs dramatically. Germany's Mietpreisbremse represents a systematic approach to controlling rent increases in tight housing markets, anchored to objective local reference rents determined through the Mietspiegel system. South Korea's 5 percent renewal cap addresses the specific problem of excessive increases upon lease renewal. U.S. rent control exists only in isolated jurisdictions and faces persistent legal challenges. The ECHR permits states to regulate rents as a social policy measure but does not require rent control.

### Unique Features

Each system has distinctive features without direct parallels: South Korea's jeonse deposit system creates a unique financing and risk dynamic; the U.S. Fair Housing Act provides the most comprehensive anti-discrimination framework for housing; Germany's Mietspiegel system provides unparalleled rent transparency; and the ECHR's proportionality requirement for evictions creates a supranational floor of procedural protection.
