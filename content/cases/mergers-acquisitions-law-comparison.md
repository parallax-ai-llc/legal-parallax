## Summary

Mergers and acquisitions -- the consolidation of companies through various transaction structures including statutory mergers, tender offers, asset purchases, and share exchanges -- represent one of the most complex areas of corporate law, engaging issues of fiduciary duty, shareholder protection, competition review, and national security screening simultaneously. South Korea regulates M&A through the Commercial Code (상법) provisions on mergers (Articles 522-530) and comprehensive asset transfers, the Financial Investment Services and Capital Markets Act (자본시장법) governing tender offers and mandatory disclosures, and the Monopoly Regulation and Fair Trade Act (공정거래법) imposing merger control review by the Korea Fair Trade Commission (KFTC, 공정거래위원회), with additional scrutiny applied to chaebol-related transactions. The United States governs M&A through state corporate law (predominantly Delaware, including the landmark Revlon, Inc. v. MacAndrews & Forbes Holdings (1986) and its progeny establishing the duties of boards in change-of-control transactions), federal securities regulation (Williams Act of 1968 governing tender offers under Sections 13(d) and 14(d)-(e) of the Securities Exchange Act), and federal antitrust review under the Hart-Scott-Rodino Antitrust Improvements Act (HSR Act) with review by the FTC and DOJ. Germany addresses M&A through the Umwandlungsgesetz (Transformation Act, UmwG) governing mergers and corporate restructurings, the Wertpapiererwerbs- und Übernahmegesetz (Securities Acquisition and Takeover Act, WpÜG) regulating public takeover bids, and the Act against Restraints of Competition (Gesetz gegen Wettbewerbsbeschränkungen, GWB) providing merger control. At the EU level, the EU Merger Regulation (Regulation 139/2004) grants the European Commission exclusive jurisdiction over concentrations with a Community dimension, the Takeover Bids Directive (2004/25/EC) harmonizes minimum takeover standards across member states, and the Foreign Subsidies Regulation (2022/2560) introduces a new layer of M&A review for transactions involving foreign government subsidies.

## South Korea

### Legal Framework

South Korean M&A law must be understood in the context of the chaebol structure and the government's dual objectives of promoting market competition while maintaining industrial competitiveness.

### Merger Procedures

The Commercial Code provides two primary merger mechanisms:

**Absorption Merger (흡수합병, Art. 522):** One company absorbs another, with the absorbing company surviving and the absorbed company dissolving. Requires a 2/3 supermajority of each company's shareholders.

**Consolidation Merger (신설합병, Art. 530):** Two or more companies dissolve and form a new entity. Also requires 2/3 supermajority approval.

**Short-Form Merger:** When one company holds 90% or more of another's shares, a simplified merger procedure is available without full shareholder approval of the subsidiary (Art. 527-3).

### Tender Offers

The Financial Investment Services and Capital Markets Act requires any person acquiring 5% or more of a listed company's shares to file a large-holdings report (대량보유 보고) within five business days. Tender offers to purchase shares of listed companies from 10 or more shareholders within six months must be made through a formal public tender offer (공개매수) process with equal treatment of all shareholders.

### KFTC Merger Review

The Korea Fair Trade Commission reviews mergers where combined assets or turnover exceed 300 billion KRW and at least one party has assets or turnover exceeding 30 billion KRW. The KFTC applies a substantive test of whether the merger would substantially lessen competition (경쟁을 실질적으로 제한하는 기업결합). Review periods are 30 days (Phase I), extendable to 90 days (Phase II) for complex cases.

### Chaebol-Specific Restrictions

Large business groups designated by the KFTC face enhanced M&A scrutiny. Intra-group mergers involving chaebol affiliates are subject to heightened disclosure requirements and fairness review to protect minority shareholders from value-extractive transactions designed to benefit the controlling family.

## United States

### Legal Framework

American M&A law reflects the interplay between permissive state corporate law and rigorous federal securities and antitrust regulation.

### Delaware Fiduciary Duties in M&A

**Revlon, Inc. v. MacAndrews & Forbes Holdings, Inc., 506 A.2d 173 (Del. 1986):** When a board decides to sell the company, its fiduciary duty shifts from preservation of the corporate entity to maximization of the sale price for shareholders. The board becomes an "auctioneer" with an obligation to seek the best available price.

**Weinberger v. UOP, Inc., 457 A.2d 701 (Del. 1983):** Established the entire fairness standard for evaluating interested mergers (cash-out mergers by controlling shareholders), requiring fair dealing and fair price.

**In re MFW Shareholders Litigation, 67 A.3d 496 (Del. Ch. 2013), aff'd sub nom. Kahn v. M&F Worldwide Corp., 88 A.3d 635 (Del. 2014):** Established that a controlling shareholder cash-out merger receives business judgment review (rather than entire fairness) if from the outset the transaction is conditioned on approval by both an independent, fully empowered special committee and an informed, uncoerced majority-of-the-minority vote.

### Hart-Scott-Rodino Act

The HSR Act (15 U.S.C. Section 18a) requires parties to transactions meeting size-of-transaction and size-of-persons thresholds to file pre-merger notifications with the FTC and DOJ and observe a waiting period (30 days, extendable through a "second request" for additional information). The 2023 HSR form was substantially revised to require more detailed information about the transaction's competitive effects, labor market impacts, and prior acquisitions.

### CFIUS National Security Review

The Committee on Foreign Investment in the United States (CFIUS), strengthened by the Foreign Investment Risk Review Modernization Act (FIRRMA, 2018), reviews foreign acquisitions that could threaten national security. FIRRMA expanded CFIUS jurisdiction to cover non-controlling investments in companies involved in critical technology, critical infrastructure, or sensitive personal data.

## Germany

### Legal Framework

German M&A law provides a structured framework for corporate transformations and public takeovers.

### Umwandlungsgesetz (Transformation Act)

The UmwG provides four types of structural transformation: merger (Verschmelzung), spin-off/split (Spaltung), transfer of assets (Vermögensübertragung), and change of legal form (Formwechsel). Mergers require a 3/4 supermajority of each company's shareholders and are subject to registration in the commercial register.

### WpÜG (Securities Acquisition and Takeover Act)

The WpÜG, enacted in 2002, regulates public takeover bids for listed companies:

**Mandatory Bid (Pflichtangebot, Section 35):** Any person acquiring control (30% of voting rights) of a listed company must make a mandatory bid to all remaining shareholders at an equitable price (not less than the volume-weighted average share price during the three months preceding the announcement and not less than the highest price paid by the bidder during the six months preceding the bid).

**Board Neutrality (Vorstandneutralität, Section 33):** After publication of a bid, the Vorstand may not take actions that could frustrate the bid without Aufsichtsrat approval, subject to the exception for actions that a prudent and conscientious manager would have taken absent the bid.

### Bundeskartellamt Merger Control

The Bundeskartellamt reviews mergers where combined global turnover exceeds EUR 500 million, one party's German turnover exceeds EUR 50 million, and another party's German turnover exceeds EUR 17.5 million. The 10th Amendment to the GWB (2021) introduced a transaction value threshold of EUR 400 million for mergers involving parties with significant German operations, addressing acquisitions of high-value companies with low turnover (killer acquisitions).

### National Security Screening

The Foreign Trade and Payments Act (Außenwirtschaftsgesetz, AWG) Section 55, as amended in 2020, empowers the Federal Ministry for Economic Affairs to review and prohibit foreign acquisitions of German companies in critical sectors. The screening threshold was lowered to 10% voting rights for companies in sensitive sectors (defense, critical infrastructure, media, healthcare).

## ECHR & EU

### EU Merger Regulation (139/2004)

The EU Merger Regulation grants the European Commission exclusive jurisdiction over concentrations with a Community dimension (combined worldwide turnover > EUR 5 billion and EU-wide turnover of each of at least two parties > EUR 250 million, with exceptions). The Commission applies the "significant impediment to effective competition" (SIEC) test, which replaced the dominance test in 2004.

**Notable Decisions:** The Commission prohibited the Siemens/Alstom rail merger (2019) and the Illumina/GRAIL acquisition (2022), while conditionally approving the Microsoft/Activision Blizzard acquisition (2023) with behavioral remedies. The Illumina/GRAIL case established that the Commission can review mergers below the turnover thresholds through Article 22 referrals from member states.

### Takeover Bids Directive (2004/25/EC)

The Directive establishes minimum standards for public takeover bids across the EU:

**Mandatory Bid Rule (Article 5):** Member states must ensure that when a person acquires control of a company, they are required to make a bid to all shareholders at an equitable price.

**Board Neutrality Rule (Article 9):** Member states may require that the board of the target company not take frustrating actions without shareholder authorization during the bid period (optional provision).

**Squeeze-Out and Sell-Out (Articles 15-16):** Member states must provide squeeze-out rights for bidders acquiring 90% of voting rights and sell-out rights for remaining shareholders.

### Foreign Subsidies Regulation (2022/2560)

Regulation 2022/2560, applicable since July 12, 2023, requires notification to the European Commission of concentrations where (1) the acquired company or one of the merging parties generates EU turnover of at least EUR 500 million, and (2) the parties received financial contributions from third-country governments totaling at least EUR 50 million in the three years preceding the notification. This creates a novel layer of M&A review targeting foreign government subsidies that may distort the internal market.

### EU FDI Screening Regulation

Regulation 2019/452 establishes a framework for member state screening of foreign direct investments on grounds of security or public order. The Regulation does not mandate national screening mechanisms but creates a cooperation mechanism for information exchange among member states and the Commission.

## Comparative Analysis

### Board Duties in Change of Control

The four jurisdictions reflect fundamentally different approaches to board duties during takeovers:

**Active Value Maximization (United States):** Under Revlon, the board must actively seek the highest price for shareholders in a change-of-control transaction.

**Board Neutrality (Germany, EU):** The WpÜG and the Takeover Bids Directive adopt a neutrality approach -- the board should not frustrate bids without shareholder approval, leaving the decision to shareholders.

**General Fiduciary Duty (South Korea):** Korean law applies general duty of care and loyalty principles without a specific Revlon-type duty, giving boards significant discretion.

### Mandatory Bid Rules

Germany and most EU member states require mandatory bids at the 30% control threshold, ensuring that all shareholders benefit equally when control changes hands. The United States and South Korea do not impose mandatory bids, relying instead on market mechanisms and disclosure requirements.

### National Security Screening Convergence

All four jurisdictions have significantly expanded foreign investment screening in recent years. CFIUS (US), AWG (Germany), the EU FDI Screening Regulation, and the Korean Foreign Investment Promotion Act all reflect growing concern about strategic acquisitions by state-backed foreign entities, creating a converging layer of national security M&A review.
