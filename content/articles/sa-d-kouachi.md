## Summary

French terrorist who, with brother Chérif, attacked the Charlie Hebdo office in Paris on January 7, 2015, killing 12 people. They claimed to be acting for al-Qaeda in the Arabian Peninsula. Both killed by police.

## Additional Context

### Relationship to the 2015 Paris attacks (high-level)

- The Charlie Hebdo attack is commonly treated as a pivotal incident within a broader sequence of January 2015 events in France.
- Public accounts often describe the episode in terms of both the immediate violence and the subsequent manhunt and security operations.

### Motive claims and organizational framing

- Reporting and investigations frequently note that the attackers framed their actions in ideological terms and referenced a militant organization.
- In wider discussions of terrorism, the case is used to illustrate how perpetrators may seek to connect local action to transnational narratives.

### Public and political aftermath

- The killings triggered large public demonstrations and a global wave of solidarity and debate.
- Long-running controversies include the boundaries of satire and speech, the safety of journalists and artists, and the risks of polarization.

### Security and counterterrorism debates

- The case is often cited in debates about intelligence monitoring, radicalization pathways, and the practical limits of prevention.
- It is also referenced in discussions about protecting public venues and media organizations while maintaining civil liberties.

### Why the case remains salient

- Continues to be referenced as a defining moment in modern French political life and in global conversations about extremism and freedom of expression.
- Frequently appears in comparative discussions of how democratic societies respond to terrorism, both operationally and culturally.
