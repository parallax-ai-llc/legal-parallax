## Summary

Last king of the Median Empire whose cruelty, including feeding a nobleman his own son, led to his overthrow.

## Historical Context

- **Median Empire**: A polity centered in northwestern Iran that rose to regional prominence in the 7th–6th centuries BCE.
- **Geopolitics**: Media interacted and competed with neighboring powers including Lydia, Babylonia, and the emerging Persian state under the Achaemenids.
- **Sources**: Most surviving narratives about Astyages are Greek (notably Herodotus) and later compilations; details should be treated cautiously where uncorroborated.

## Reign (High-Level)

- Astyages is commonly presented as the **final ruler of Media** before the Persian takeover.
- Later traditions emphasize themes of **court intrigue**, **harsh punishment**, and **breakdown of elite loyalty**.
- The famous story of **Harpagus** being served his own son (as punishment) appears in classical accounts; it is often cited as a catalyst for conspiratorial opposition.

## Overthrow and Aftermath

- Around **-550**, Cyrus II ("Cyrus the Great") defeated Astyages and absorbed Media into the Achaemenid realm.
- Media did not disappear as a region; it became an important **satrapy** and supplied administrators and military personnel within the Persian Empire.
- Astyages’ fall is frequently used as a case study in how **legitimacy and elite coalition management** can be as decisive as battlefield outcomes.

## Legacy

- **Historical**: Remembered primarily through the transition from Median to Persian supremacy.
- **Literary**: His reign is often framed as a moralized narrative about tyranny and the consequences of cruelty.
- **Name/Identity**: "Astyages" is the Greek form of an Iranian royal name; spellings and reconstructions vary across traditions.

## Notes

- Dates in this article are conventionally given in **BCE** (negative years in the timeline).
