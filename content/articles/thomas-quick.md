## Summary
Sture Bergwall, known as Thomas Quick, confessed to over 30 murders in Sweden. All convictions were later overturned as his confessions were proven false, making it one of Sweden's biggest judicial scandals.

## Background
- **Legal name**: Sture Bergwall.
- **Alias**: "Thomas Quick" became widely used in media and court records during the period of confessions and prosecutions.
- Bergwall was treated in a secure psychiatric setting, where extensive interviews and therapy sessions were conducted.

## The "Quick" Confessions (Overview)
- Bergwall confessed to **dozens of killings** over many years.
- The confessions became the central pillar of multiple investigations.
- Several cases lacked typical corroboration such as physical evidence, reliable witness identification, or independent forensic links.

## Why the Case Became a Judicial Scandal
- **Confession-driven investigations**: cases were often built around admissions rather than evidence discovered independently.
- **Weak corroboration**: many details in the confessions could not be verified, or were later considered to have been influenced by investigators, records, or suggestion.
- **Reliance on expert frameworks**: therapy and interpretive models used at the time were later criticized as enabling false narratives.

## Overturned Convictions
- Starting in the late 2000s, Bergwall’s earlier statements were reassessed.
- Courts ultimately ruled that the evidence was **insufficient** once the confessions were removed or treated as unreliable.
- The result was a full reversal of the high-profile conviction set associated with "Thomas Quick".

## Impact and Legacy
- The case is frequently cited in discussions of:
  - **false confessions** and investigative contamination
  - the limits of **memory-based testimony** and therapy-derived narratives
  - prosecutorial and judicial safeguards when evidence is thin
- It prompted renewed attention to standards for corroboration, documentation, and the handling of vulnerable suspects.

## Key Takeaways
- Bergwall/Quick is commonly referenced as an example of how a system can converge on a narrative when **confessions substitute for evidence**.
- The later acquittals underline the principle that criminal convictions require proof beyond reasonable doubt, not merely consistency of statements.
