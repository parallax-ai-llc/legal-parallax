## Summary

An independent tribunal found that China has been forcibly harvesting organs from prisoners of conscience, primarily Falun Gong practitioners, on a substantial scale for years.

## Context

- "Organ harvesting" in this context refers to the removal of organs for transplantation without freely given, informed consent.
- The allegations gained international attention alongside rapid growth in China’s transplant capacity and reports of unusually short waiting times compared to voluntary donation systems.
- China has stated that it has reformed its transplant system over time, including a transition toward a citizen donation program and away from the use of organs from executed prisoners (a practice that China previously acknowledged).[^3]

## Key Claims and Findings (High-Level)

- The independent *China Tribunal* (a people’s tribunal chaired by Sir Geoffrey Nice KC) concluded in 2019 that forced organ harvesting had been committed for years “throughout China on a significant scale,” and that Falun Gong practitioners were the principal source of organs, with other groups (including Uyghurs) also discussed in public reporting.[^2]
- Public discussions typically distinguish between:
  - **Verified facts** (e.g., the Tribunal’s published findings; official Chinese policy announcements), and
  - **Contested claims** (e.g., exact victim counts, sourcing proportions, and the extent to which reforms eliminated coercive practices).

## Evidence Discussed in Public Reporting

Common categories of evidence cited in media, NGO reporting, and hearings include:

- **Capacity and volume estimates**: comparisons between officially reported transplant numbers and estimates derived from hospital bed counts, staffing, revenue, and published research output.
- **Waiting-time claims**: reports of very short waiting times for organs (which, in other jurisdictions, typically require large pools of voluntary donors).
- **Hospital marketing / pricing**: archived advertisements and materials describing transplant availability and timelines.
- **Testimony**: accounts from former detainees and witnesses describing medical testing while detained.
- **Recorded calls**: some investigations have published transcripts/audio from calls to hospitals allegedly indicating organ sourcing from prisoners of conscience.

## Official Position and Reforms

- The Chinese government has repeatedly denied allegations of systematic forced organ harvesting from prisoners of conscience.
- China announced reforms including a national donation and allocation system and stated that it would stop using organs from executed prisoners beginning in 2015.[^3]
- Critics argue that independent auditing and full transparency remain insufficient to resolve the allegations; supporters of the reforms argue that governance and donation infrastructure have materially improved.

## International Reactions

- The issue has been raised by international bodies and lawmakers, including communications by UN human rights experts and resolutions in national legislatures.[^4][^5]
- Several jurisdictions have considered or adopted policies aimed at limiting “transplant tourism” and improving reporting requirements for overseas transplants.
