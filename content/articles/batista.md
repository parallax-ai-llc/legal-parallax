## Background

- Fulgencio Batista y Zaldívar was a Cuban military officer who became a central political figure after the 1933 upheavals.
- He is commonly described as an authoritarian ruler, particularly for his post-1952 government.

## First Presidency (1940–1944)

- Won the 1940 presidential election following years of influence over the military and national politics.
- His administration operated under the 1940 Constitution, often cited as one of the more progressive constitutions of its era in Cuba.
- Left office in 1944 after an electoral defeat and did not immediately return to power.

## Coup and Second Rule (1952–1959)

- Returned to power via a military coup in 1952, suspending the normal electoral process.
- The period saw significant political repression alongside economic activity tied to tourism and urban development.
- Opposition movements included student groups, labor activists, and insurgent organizations, culminating in the rise of Fidel Castro's July 26 Movement.

## Relations with the United States

- Batista's governments maintained substantial political and economic ties with the United States.
- U.S. policy toward Cuba during this period is often discussed in relation to Cold War priorities and perceptions of regional stability.

## Fall and Exile

- By late 1958, Batista's position weakened amid military setbacks and declining domestic support.
- He departed Cuba on January 1, 1959, and lived in exile until his death in 1973.

## Legacy

- In Cuban historiography, Batista is frequently portrayed as a symbol of corruption and repression that the revolution sought to overthrow.
- Outside Cuba, assessments vary, with attention to both constitutional-era politics (1940–1944) and authoritarian governance after 1952.
