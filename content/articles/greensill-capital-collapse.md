## Background

- Greensill Capital operated in **supply chain finance (SCF)**, advancing cash to suppliers so that they could be paid early while buyers paid later.
- A key feature of its model was packaging SCF receivables into **investment funds**, creating a pipeline from corporate invoices to investor products.

## What Triggered the Collapse

- Questions escalated around the **quality and concentration** of underlying receivables, including heavy exposure to a small number of borrowers.
- **Insurance coverage** for receivables (often used to support the perceived safety of the assets) became a critical dependency; disruption to that coverage undermined confidence.
- When funding channels and counterparties pulled back, the firm faced a rapid **liquidity crisis** typical of maturity and confidence mismatches.

## Why It Mattered

- The episode highlighted how SCF can resemble **shadow banking** when short-term funding supports longer-duration or harder-to-verify claims.
- It became a prominent case study in **governance and political access risk**, as the firm’s relationships and lobbying drew public scrutiny.

## Impact

- Investors faced significant losses as fund vehicles holding Greensill-linked assets were frozen or wound down.
- Corporate clients and suppliers experienced disruption as invoice-financing facilities were paused or terminated.
- Regulators and policymakers renewed attention on transparency and risk management in SCF and fund distribution.

## Aftermath (High-Level)

- Insolvency and administration processes sought to recover value from receivables and related claims.
- Multiple investigations and legal proceedings were reported across jurisdictions, reflecting the cross-border nature of the financing structures.
