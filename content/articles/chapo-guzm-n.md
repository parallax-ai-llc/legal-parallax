## Expanded Overview

### Identity and Name

Joaquín Archivaldo Guzmán Loera is commonly known by the nickname "El Chapo" (Spanish for "Shorty"), a reference to his stature and a widely used shorthand in media and law-enforcement reporting.[^1]

### Background

- **Born:** April 4, 1957 (per commonly cited biographical records).[^1]
- **Country:** Mexico.[^1]
- **Notability:** Known internationally for leadership roles associated with the **Sinaloa Cartel**, one of Mexico's most prominent drug-trafficking organizations.[^1]

### Role in Organized Crime (High-Level)

At a high level, Guzmán is described as:

- A key figure in large-scale **drug trafficking** operations attributed to the Sinaloa Cartel.[^1]
- An organizer of transnational trafficking routes used to move narcotics toward major consumer markets, particularly the United States.[^1]
- A symbol (in public discourse) of the scale and persistence of cartel-driven violence and corruption in Mexico, even though specific allegations and culpability are addressed through legal proceedings rather than popular narrative.[^1]

### Arrests, Escapes, and Extradition (High-Level)

Public reporting and official proceedings commonly emphasize a sequence of law-enforcement actions and custody events, including:

- Arrest(s) by Mexican authorities at different points in his career.[^1]
- Notable **prison escapes** that drew international attention and became central to discussions about institutional weakness and corruption.[^1]
- **Extradition to the United States** and subsequent prosecution in U.S. federal court.[^1]

### Trial and Sentencing (United States)

Guzmán was tried in U.S. federal court on multiple charges connected to drug trafficking and organized crime activity, and was convicted; he received a lengthy prison sentence.[^1]

### Historical Significance

In many historical accounts of late-20th and early-21st century organized crime in the Americas, Guzmán is referenced as:

- A representative figure for the globalization of illicit drug supply chains.
- A case study in how cartel organizations can combine logistics, violence, and financial power.
- An example of the legal and diplomatic challenges of pursuing high-profile suspects across borders (e.g., extradition, witness protection, and international cooperation).[^1]
