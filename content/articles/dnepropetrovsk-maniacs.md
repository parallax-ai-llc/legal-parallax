## Summary
The Dnepropetrovsk maniacs were three Ukrainian teenagers — Viktor Sayenko, Igor Suprunyuk, and Alexander Hanzha — who murdered 21 people in 2007. They filmed their killings. Two received life sentences.

## Context and Modus Operandi (high-level)
- The group is commonly referred to as the **“Dnepropetrovsk maniacs”** in English-language sources.
- Crimes were committed over a concentrated period in **2007**.
- Victims were typically attacked in public or semi-public spaces; accounts emphasize opportunistic targeting.
- Some killings were **filmed**, and a portion of the material circulated online, drawing international attention.

## Investigation and Trial (overview)
- Public interest increased sharply after reports of video recordings and online dissemination.
- Court reporting frequently notes the role of:
  - digital evidence (computer/phone storage),
  - forensic links across crime scenes,
  - and testimony describing the defendants’ movements and behavior.
- The case is often cited in discussions of early internet-era “shock videos” and the challenges of content moderation.

## Impact and Legacy
- The murders are widely referenced in:
  - criminology and media studies (violence + virality),
  - online safety discourse,
  - and discussions about copycat risk and responsible reporting.
- The label became a shorthand for a specific 2007 spree case in Ukraine rather than a single individual offender.
