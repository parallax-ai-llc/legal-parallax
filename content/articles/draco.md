## Summary

Athenian lawgiver whose legal code was so harsh that minor offenses were punished by death, giving us 'draconian.'

## Historical Context

- **Place**: Athens, in the Archaic period, amid tensions between aristocratic power and demands for more predictable justice.
- **Problem Draco addressed**: customary, orally transmitted rules and ad hoc judgments that could vary by judge and circumstance.
- **Why codification mattered**: writing laws down made standards more legible and harder to manipulate, even if the content remained severe.

## The Draconian Code (High-Level)

- **Written law**: Draco is traditionally credited with producing one of Athens’ earliest written law codes (c. 621 BCE).
- **Severity**: later Athenians claimed many offenses carried death as punishment, which is the origin of the modern term **“draconian.”**
- **Scope (as remembered)**: the code is mainly associated with criminal law and penalties rather than a broad constitutional redesign.

## Homicide Law and Later Reform

- **Enduring element**: the homicide provisions were remembered as more structured than the rest and, in later tradition, were retained when other parts were repealed.
- **Solon’s role**: the lawgiver **Solon** (early 6th century BCE) is commonly said to have replaced most Draconian statutes with reforms that reduced harshness while keeping certain homicide rules.

## Legacy

- **Language**: “draconian” became a byword for extreme severity in law or policy.
- **Institutional impact**: regardless of how fully the original code survived, Draco symbolizes a key transition from custom to **publicly recorded** law in Greek civic life.

## Key Takeaways

- Draco is chiefly remembered for **codifying** Athenian law and for the **notorious harshness** attributed to that code.
- His legacy is less about detailed surviving statutes and more about the cultural memory of **severity + early legal formalization**.
