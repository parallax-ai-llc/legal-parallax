## Summary

Vandal king who sacked Rome in 455 AD in a two-week orgy of plunder that gave us the word 'vandalism.'

## Context & Background

- **Gaiseric (Geiseric)** was the most successful leader of the **Vandals**, a Germanic people who crossed into Roman territory during the late Western Empire.
- His reign is tightly linked to the **fragmentation of Roman authority** in the 5th century and the rise of mobile war-bands that could seize provinces and tax bases.

## Rise and Conquest of North Africa

- **428**: Became king of the Vandals (after the death of his half-brother Gunderic).[^1]
- Led the Vandal migration from **Hispania into North Africa**, exploiting Roman civil conflict and weakened frontier defense.
- **439**: Captured **Carthage**, a strategic and economic center that gave the Vandals a durable base, shipyards, and access to Mediterranean trade routes.[^1]

## Mediterranean Power

- After taking Carthage, Gaiseric built the Vandals into a **naval power**, able to raid coasts and islands and to pressure Roman diplomacy.
- Vandal fleets operated across the **central and western Mediterranean**, turning sea control into leverage for treaties, ransoms, and hostages.[^1]

## Relations with the Roman Empire

- Negotiated repeatedly with Western and Eastern Roman authorities, using a mix of:
  - **Treaties** (recognition in exchange for peace)
  - **Hostage-taking** as enforcement
  - **Raids** as punitive diplomacy when agreements broke down
- His actions are often described as opportunistic, but they consistently aimed to **secure land, revenue, and legitimacy** for the Vandal kingdom.[^1]

## Sack of Rome (455)

- The sack followed political crisis in the West and was carried out after the Vandals reached Rome by sea.
- Ancient and later accounts differ in tone, but the event became a **symbol of imperial vulnerability** and a shorthand for destructive plunder.[^1]

## Legacy

- Gaiseric’s rule helped create one of the most durable post-Roman kingdoms of the 5th century.
- Modern usage of the term **“vandal”** is influenced by the reputation of the Vandal sack of Rome, though the linguistic path to “vandalism” is much later and shaped by early modern writers.
