## Background

- Charles Ssenyonga is known in Ugandan press reporting for a criminal case involving intentional HIV transmission.
- Descriptions of the case emphasize both a public health dimension (HIV exposure) and a criminal law dimension (harm to victims).

## Allegations and reported conduct

- Reporting around the case states that Ssenyonga was aware of being HIV-positive.
- He was accused of pursuing sexual relationships while intentionally exposing partners to HIV.
- The widely repeated figure is that he infected "over 100" women; precise counts vary by source and are difficult to independently verify without court records.

## Criminal proceedings

- The case is commonly summarized as a murder conviction connected to deliberate HIV infection.
- Public summaries typically highlight:
  - a victim who later died after infection,
  - the prosecution argument that the infection was intentional,
  - the court treating the conduct as a serious violent offense rather than only a health-related offense.

## Significance

- The case is often cited in discussions of:
  - the criminalization of HIV exposure or transmission,
  - evidentiary challenges (intent, causation, and medical timelines),
  - balancing public health goals with punishment-oriented approaches.
- It is also referenced as an example used by media to warn about risk and prevention, though sensational coverage can distort details.

## What is known vs. what is claimed

- Known (high confidence from widely cited summaries): a Ugandan man named Charles Ssenyonga was convicted in 2007 in a case framed as deliberate HIV infection.
- Claimed (lower confidence without primary documents): exact number of people infected, the full set of victims, and specific factual findings beyond the conviction headline.
