## Summary

Thieves tunneled 78 meters from a rented property to reach the vault of Brazil's Central Bank in Fortaleza, stealing R$164 million ($71.6 million) in untraced banknotes.

## Context

- **Location**: Fortaleza, Ceará, Brazil.
- **Target**: The **vault** of the Banco Central do Brasil branch.
- **Stolen amount**: **R$164 million** in cash (often reported as ~US$70+ million at the time).
- **Notable constraint**: The notes were described as **untraceable** (no recorded serial-number tracking tied to the vault inventory), which complicated recovery efforts.

## Method (High-Level)

Accounts describe a long-term preparation phase centered on a nearby rental property.

- A property was **rented** near the bank and used as a cover site.
- A **tunnel (~78 m)** was dug from the property to the bank’s vault area.
- The tunnel was reportedly **reinforced** and equipped with basic **lighting/ventilation**, suggesting an operation planned over weeks to months.
- The theft was executed over a **weekend**, exploiting reduced on-site activity and delaying discovery until business operations resumed.

## Discovery and Immediate Aftermath

- The theft was discovered after the weekend when staff returned and found the vault compromised.
- Initial reporting emphasized the **scale of cash removed** and the professionalism implied by the tunnel construction.
- The case triggered a broad investigation focused on logistics: soil disposal, materials procurement, and participant networks.

## Investigation (Broad Themes)

Public reporting has highlighted several recurring investigative angles:

- **Supply chain signals**: purchases of wood, tools, generators, and other construction materials.
- **Operational footprint**: movement of large cash volumes, vehicles, and safe houses.
- **Participant network**: a suspected **multi-person crew** spanning planners, diggers, drivers, and fences.
- **Recovery difficulty**: cash dispersal and laundering pathways reduced the chance of full recovery.

## Impact and Legacy

- The heist became one of the most widely cited examples of **tunneling bank robberies** in modern criminal history.
- It is frequently referenced in discussions of:
  - **physical security** (subsurface monitoring, seismic sensors, perimeter hardening),
  - **insider-risk hypotheses** (how attackers obtain building layouts and security routines),
  - **cash-handling policy** (limits, auditing practices, and deterrence).

## Key Facts (Quick Reference)

- **Technique**: tunnel entry into a vault.
- **Distance**: ~78 meters (reported).
- **Amount**: R$164 million (reported).
- **Outcome**: only **partial recovery** is commonly reported; the full amount was not recovered.
