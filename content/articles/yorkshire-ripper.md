## Identity Note
- **The “Yorkshire Ripper” is the media nickname for Peter William Sutcliffe.** If another article already covers “Peter Sutcliffe,” keep this page as a cross-reference and disambiguation entry.

## Background
- Born in 1946 in England; grew up in West Yorkshire.
- Worked in a range of manual and clerical jobs before and during the period of his crimes.

## Crimes (High-Level)
- Active period commonly cited as **mid-1970s to 1980**.
- Known for murdering **13 women** and attempting to murder **several others** (often reported as **7 attempted murders**).
- Victims were largely attacked in or around **West Yorkshire and Greater Manchester**, with assaults involving blunt-force trauma and stabbing.

## Investigation (High-Level)
- The case became one of the UK’s most extensive manhunts of the era.
- Investigation was influenced by large volumes of tips and significant public fear; later reviews highlighted major investigative errors and missed opportunities.
- Sutcliffe was ultimately arrested in **early 1981** after police checks, then linked to the murders.

## Trial and Sentence
- Convicted in **1981** for multiple murders and attempted murders.
- Received a **life sentence**; later legal decisions confirmed that he would spend the remainder of his life in custody.

## Later Life and Death
- Spent decades in prison custody within England.
- Died in **2020** while incarcerated.

## Historical Significance
- The case shaped UK policing debates on major crime investigations, evidence handling, and victim stereotyping.
- Continues to be referenced in discussions of media sensationalism and public safety during late-20th-century Britain.
