## Context: 1MDB and the Audit Mandate

1Malaysia Development Berhad (1MDB) was a Malaysian state fund that became the center of a major international corruption and money‑laundering scandal. External auditors were expected to provide independent assurance that the fund’s financial statements fairly presented its position and that material misstatements would be detected or challenged.

## Deloitte’s Engagement (High-Level)

- Deloitte was appointed as 1MDB’s external auditor after earlier changes in auditors.
- The audits covered periods during which 1MDB’s reported assets and complex transactions drew scrutiny.
- Public reporting and later regulatory findings emphasized shortcomings in professional skepticism and audit procedures, rather than alleging the auditor “caused” the underlying fraud.

## Regulatory / Enforcement Outcome (Malaysia)

- Malaysian authorities took disciplinary and/or enforcement action connected to 1MDB audit work.
- The headline outcome often cited is a significant monetary penalty against Deloitte’s Malaysian affiliate for audit failures related to 1MDB.
- Such actions typically focus on whether auditing standards were followed (e.g., evidence gathering, valuation assessment, confirmation of balances, and escalation of red flags).

## Why This Case Matters

- **Audit limits vs expectations:** The 1MDB case is frequently used to illustrate the gap between what the public expects an audit to catch and what audits are designed to do.
- **Complex financial structures:** Auditing large, opaque structures increases reliance on third‑party confirmations, valuation work, and rigorous challenge of management assertions.
- **Reputation and governance:** Audit findings and penalties can affect confidence in financial reporting, governance, and the credibility of gatekeepers.

## Related Entities and Themes

- 1MDB’s controversy involved multiple jurisdictions, financial institutions, and intermediaries.
- The broader story is tied to governance failures, politically exposed persons (PEPs), and cross‑border financial controls.
