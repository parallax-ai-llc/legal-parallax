## Summary

Arthur Andersen, one of the 'Big Five' accounting firms, was convicted of obstruction of justice for shredding Enron documents. The conviction destroyed the firm and eliminated 85,000 jobs.

## Context: Enron and Auditor Independence

Arthur Andersen served as Enron's external auditor and also earned substantial fees from non-audit consulting work—an arrangement that became a symbol of perceived conflicts of interest in late-1990s/early-2000s corporate governance.

Key background points:

- Andersen was historically regarded as a top-tier accounting and professional services firm (one of the "Big Five").
- Enron's accounting relied heavily on complex structures (including special purpose entities) that obscured debt and inflated reported performance.
- The scandal unfolded alongside a broader period of high-profile reporting failures in the U.S. capital markets.

## Legal Case and the Shredding Allegations

The firm's downfall was accelerated by the U.S. Department of Justice prosecution focused on document handling during the Enron investigation.

- The core allegation was **obstruction of justice** tied to the destruction of materials related to Enron audits.
- The conviction in 2002 effectively made Andersen ineligible for many public-company audit engagements, prompting a rapid client exodus.

## Supreme Court Reversal (2005) and Practical Aftermath

In 2005, the U.S. Supreme Court unanimously overturned the obstruction conviction, holding that the jury instructions were too broad regarding "corrupt" intent.

Despite the reversal:

- Andersen's audit practice had already collapsed; most partners and staff had dispersed to other firms.
- The brand and operating footprint never recovered to its prior scale.

## Industry Impact

The collapse reshaped the accounting industry and public trust in audits.

- The "Big Five" became the "Big Four," increasing industry concentration.
- Regulators and boards placed greater emphasis on audit committee oversight and auditor independence.

## Policy and Governance Legacy

The scandal environment contributed to major reforms, most notably the **Sarbanes–Oxley Act (SOX) of 2002**, which:

- strengthened internal control reporting expectations,
- tightened rules around auditor independence and certain non-audit services,
- created the Public Company Accounting Oversight Board (PCAOB) to oversee audits of public companies.

## Why It Matters

The Arthur Andersen collapse is widely cited as a case study in how:

- reputational risk can destroy a professional services firm faster than financial losses,
- audit quality and independence are foundational to capital markets,
- legal outcomes (including later reversals) may not undo organizational collapse once trust and clients are lost.
