## Summary
Scott Peterson was convicted of murdering his pregnant wife Laci Peterson on Christmas Eve 2002. Her body washed ashore in San Francisco Bay. Originally sentenced to death, resentenced to life without parole.

## Background
- Peterson grew up in Southern California and later lived in Modesto, California.
- He married Laci Peterson (née Rocha) in 1997; the couple later expected their first child, Conner.

## Case Overview (2002–2004)
- **2002-12-24:** Laci Peterson disappeared in Modesto while eight months pregnant.
- Peterson reported fishing that day in the San Francisco Bay area; investigators focused on him as the primary suspect.
- In early 2003, the remains of Laci Peterson and the fetus were discovered along the shoreline of the San Francisco Bay.

## Investigation Notes
- The investigation drew intense national attention, with extensive press coverage and public scrutiny.
- Evidence discussed in reporting and court proceedings included:
  - Timeline and location questions surrounding Peterson’s stated fishing trip.
  - His extramarital relationship with Amber Frey, which became part of the public narrative of the case.
  - Physical and circumstantial evidence presented by the prosecution at trial.

## Trial and Sentencing
- Peterson was tried in California state court and convicted (2004) of:
  - First-degree murder for Laci Peterson.
  - Second-degree murder for the death of the fetus (Conner).
- He was originally sentenced to death; later court rulings resulted in resentencing to **life without the possibility of parole**.

## Appeals and Later Legal Status
- Peterson’s case has continued through appellate review and post-conviction litigation.
- A key outcome widely reported is the reversal of the original death sentence, followed by resentencing.
- Claims of evidentiary or procedural issues have been raised in various filings; the conviction has remained in place as of the latest update.

## Public and Media Impact
- The case became one of the most high-profile American criminal cases of the early 2000s.
- It contributed to broader discussion around:
  - Media influence on public perception during ongoing criminal investigations.
  - Venue changes and jury selection challenges in heavily publicized trials.
  - The treatment of circumstantial evidence in major homicide prosecutions.
