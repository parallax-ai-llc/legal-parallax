## Summary

Two suicide bombs exploded at a peace rally in Ankara killing 109 people, the deadliest terrorist attack in Turkish history. ISIS was blamed for the attack.

## Context

- The attack occurred amid heightened political polarization and renewed conflict between the Turkish state and the PKK in 2015.
- The rally was organized by civil-society groups and unions advocating an end to violence and a return to peace talks.
- Large public demonstrations were taking place in the run-up to Turkey’s November 2015 general election.

## What Happened

- **Date**: 10 October 2015
- **Location**: Near Ankara Central Station (Ankara Garı), Ankara, Turkey
- **Target**: A “peace rally” / “Labour, Peace, Democracy” gathering
- **Method**: Twin suicide bombings in close succession
- **Casualties**: 109 killed (widely cited), with hundreds injured

## Attribution and Investigation (High Level)

- Turkish authorities blamed the attack on ISIS/ISIL-linked militants.
- Investigations focused on suspected jihadist networks operating inside Turkey and across the Syrian border.
- The attack became a major reference point in subsequent Turkish counter-terrorism operations and policy debates.

## Immediate Aftermath

- Turkey declared national mourning and increased security measures in major cities.
- The event triggered widespread public grief, protests, and intense political controversy.
- Survivors and victim families sought accountability regarding security planning and intelligence failures.

## Why It Matters

- The Ankara bombings are often described as the deadliest terrorist attack in modern Turkish history.
- The incident shaped domestic debates about public safety, civil liberties, and the state’s approach to extremist threats.
- It remains a central case study in discussions of political violence in Turkey during the mid-2010s.
