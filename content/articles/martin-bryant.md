## Summary
Martin Bryant killed 35 people and wounded 23 at Port Arthur, Tasmania on April 28, 1996. It was the deadliest mass shooting in Australian history and led to sweeping gun law reforms.

## Background
- Bryant was an Australian man from Tasmania.
- Contemporary reporting and later accounts frequently describe him as socially isolated and having long-standing difficulties with social functioning.

## Port Arthur Massacre (1996)
- Date: 1996-04-28.
- Location: Port Arthur area, Tasmania (including the Port Arthur historic site).
- Casualties: 35 killed and 23 wounded (widely cited figures).
- The attack became a defining national trauma in Australia and is commonly referenced in debates over firearm access and public safety.

## Arrest, Legal Proceedings, and Imprisonment
- Following the attack, Bryant was pursued by police and ultimately taken into custody after a siege.
- He later entered guilty pleas and received life imprisonment sentences.
- He has remained incarcerated since 1996.

## Policy Impact
- The Port Arthur massacre was a major catalyst for Australia’s 1996 national firearms reforms.
- Reforms are widely associated with restrictions on certain semi-automatic firearms, licensing and registration changes, and a large-scale gun buyback program.

## Notes and Context
- Coverage of the case often emphasizes the scale of the attack and the speed with which national policy changed afterward.
- Details about motive and planning are discussed across sources; accounts can differ in emphasis depending on legal, journalistic, or historical framing.
