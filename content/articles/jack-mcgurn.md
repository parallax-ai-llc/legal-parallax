## Summary
Jack 'Machine Gun' McGurn was an Al Capone hitman widely believed to have planned the St. Valentine's Day Massacre. He was murdered on the anniversary of the massacre.

## Expanded Biography (High-Level)
- **Birth name:** commonly reported as *Vincenzo Antonio Gibaldi*.
- **Nickname:** “Machine Gun” (a moniker associated with the era’s gangland image and the Thompson submachine gun).
- **Base of operations:** Chicago, during the Prohibition-era gang conflicts.

### Early Life and Background
- McGurn was born in Sicily (often reported as Licata) and later grew up in the United States after immigrating as a child.
- His early years are generally described in sources as shaped by immigrant life and exposure to street-level violence in Chicago.

### Role in Capone’s Organization
- He became associated with **Al Capone’s Chicago Outfit**, which ran bootlegging, gambling, and related rackets during Prohibition.
- In popular and law-enforcement accounts, McGurn is frequently characterized as an **enforcer**—someone tasked with intimidation and targeted violence to protect the Outfit’s interests.

### St. Valentine’s Day Massacre (1929)
- McGurn is widely cited in secondary histories as a key figure suspected of planning or coordinating the attack that killed members of George “Bugs” Moran’s North Side Gang.
- The massacre became one of the most infamous episodes of the Chicago gang wars, intensifying public outrage and political pressure to pursue organized crime.
- Attribution and exact operational details remain debated; many accounts describe the case as shaped by informants, incomplete evidence, and competing narratives.

### Public Profile and Legal Pressure
- Gangland figures in this period often faced cycles of **arrest, intimidation of witnesses, and lack of convictions**, even amid strong public suspicion.
- McGurn’s name persisted in newspapers and later true-crime histories as part of the wider story of Capone-era enforcement tactics.

### Death
- McGurn was killed in 1936 in Chicago.
- The timing—on the anniversary of the 1929 massacre—has been repeatedly highlighted as symbolic, though motives and responsibility are discussed differently across sources.

## Key Facts and Context
- **Era:** Prohibition (1920–1933) and its aftermath.
- **Associated conflicts:** Chicago Outfit vs. North Side Gang.
- **Why notable:** frequent linkage to a landmark organized-crime event (1929 massacre) and enduring presence in Capone-era historiography.
