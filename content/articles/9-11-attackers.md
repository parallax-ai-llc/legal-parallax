## Overview

The term **“9/11 attackers”** refers to the **19 al-Qaeda hijackers** involved in the September 11, 2001 attacks. They are commonly described in two functional groups:

- **Pilot hijackers (4):** Individuals with flight training who were positioned to take control of each aircraft.
- **“Muscle” hijackers (15):** Individuals who helped seize control in the cabin (e.g., restraining crew/passengers and enforcing compliance).

Although many summaries focus on a few well-known figures, public investigations (U.S. government reporting, journalistic reconstructions, and academic work) identify all 19 as participants on the four flights.

## Recruitment and Preparation (high-level)

Commonly cited elements of preparation include:

- **Recruitment through extremist networks** and contact with al-Qaeda leadership and facilitators.
- **International travel** using passports and visas that enabled entry into the U.S. and movement within Europe/Middle East.
- **Operational security and cover stories,** including use of shared addresses, bank accounts, and coordinated travel itineraries.
- **Pilot training** for the four designated pilots, alongside “muscle” hijackers who did not require flight skills.

## Financing and Logistics

Public reporting on the plot’s logistics often notes:

- **Relatively small individual transaction sizes** (rent, travel, flight lessons) that nonetheless supported a major operational impact.
- Use of **bank accounts, wire transfers, and cash withdrawals** to pay for routine expenses in the U.S.
- Reliance on **facilitators and contacts** for housing, transportation, and guidance.

## Aftermath and Identification

After the attacks, investigators:

- Reconstructed identities through **passenger manifests, recovered documents, travel records, and intelligence reporting**.
- Linked the operation to **al-Qaeda’s broader structure** and its leadership, while distinguishing between direct participants (the 19 hijackers) and broader planners/facilitators.
