## Summary

Viral video campaign by Invisible Children to capture Joseph Kony received 100 million views in 6 days. Despite unprecedented awareness, Kony was never captured.

## Career Timeline

| Year | Event |
|------|-------|
| 2012 | Video went viral |

## Background

- **Invisible Children** released *Kony 2012* as part of a long-running advocacy campaign focused on the Lord's Resistance Army (LRA) and its leader **Joseph Kony**.
- The video framed Kony as a priority target for international justice and urged viewers to pressure governments to support efforts to stop him.

## Why It Went Viral

- Short, emotionally direct storytelling optimized for social platforms.
- Clear call-to-action (share the video; participate in *Cover the Night*; support specific policy goals).
- Rapid amplification across YouTube, Facebook, and celebrity influencer shares.

## Criticism and Controversy (High-Level)

- Critics argued the video **simplified** a complex regional conflict and overstated the contemporaneous scale of LRA activity.
- Questions were raised about **Invisible Children's finances**, messaging, and the ethics/effectiveness of “slacktivism” style campaigns.
- The campaign became a case study in the trade-offs between **awareness at scale** and **nuanced accuracy**.

## Aftermath

- The campaign generated massive awareness, but **Joseph Kony was not captured**.
- The planned *Cover the Night* event attracted far less participation than the initial online attention suggested.
- The organization and its founders received intense public scrutiny; public attention shifted quickly after the initial viral peak.

## Legacy

- Often cited as a defining example of **viral activism**: unprecedented reach, rapid backlash, and limited measurable policy/operational impact relative to attention.
- Used in media studies and marketing discussions to illustrate how **narrative clarity** can outperform **complexity** in the attention economy.
