## Summary

British troops under General Dyer fired on a peaceful gathering of thousands in Amritsar, killing 379-1,000 people. Dyer ordered troops to fire until ammunition was exhausted.

## Context and Causes

- **Rowlatt Act (1919):** Emergency powers extended wartime restrictions into peacetime, enabling detention without trial and curbs on civil liberties.
- **Punjab unrest:** Arrests of local leaders and protests in April 1919 heightened tension; martial law measures and public meeting restrictions were introduced in parts of Punjab.
- **Baisakhi gathering:** On 13 April 1919 (Baisakhi), a large crowd assembled at **Jallianwala Bagh**, a walled garden with limited exits, for a mix of political protest and festival-related gathering.

## What Happened (13 April 1919)

- Brigadier-General **Reginald Dyer** brought troops to the site and positioned them at the entrances.
- Without issuing a clear warning that the crowd could realistically comply with, soldiers opened fire into the densely packed people.
- Shooting continued for several minutes; the confined space and narrow exits contributed to panic and deadly bottlenecks.

## Casualties and Estimates

- **Official British figure:** commonly cited as **379 killed**.
- **Indian and other estimates:** frequently range higher (often cited in the high hundreds to over a thousand), reflecting differing records and political context.
- Many victims were wounded; some deaths occurred in the ensuing chaos (including stampede and attempted escape through the narrow exits).

## Immediate Aftermath

- The massacre triggered widespread shock and anger across India and abroad.
- Repressive measures intensified in Punjab in the short term, including strict controls and punishments under emergency regulations.

## Investigations and Accountability

- The **Hunter Commission** (Disorders Inquiry Committee, 1919) examined events and criticized Dyer's actions.
- Dyer was removed from command; however, he was also defended by sections of the British public and political establishment, revealing deep divisions over imperial policy.

## Political Impact

- The event became a major turning point in Indian public opinion about British rule.
- It helped accelerate mass political mobilization and strengthened arguments for **self-rule/independence**.
- The massacre is often discussed as a catalyst in the broader shift toward nationwide non-cooperation and anti-colonial organizing in the 1920s.

## Legacy and Commemoration

- **Jallianwala Bagh** is preserved as a memorial site in Amritsar, with commemorations marking the anniversary.
- The massacre remains a central reference point in discussions of colonial violence, state power, and the moral legitimacy of empire.

## Key Figures

- **Reginald Dyer:** commanding officer who ordered the firing.
- **Michael O'Dwyer:** Lieutenant Governor of Punjab at the time; associated with the broader policy environment in Punjab.
- **Indian protest leaders and civilians:** many victims were ordinary attendees; the mass character of the crowd shaped the national resonance of the event.
