## Summary

German colonial commander responsible for the first genocide of the 20th century against the Herero and Nama.

## Background

- **Full name**: Adrian Dietrich Lothar von Trotha
- **Rank/role**: German Imperial Army officer who served as a colonial commander in the German Empire’s overseas territories.
- **Known for**: Issuing and enforcing policies of mass expulsion and killing during Germany’s war against the **Herero** and **Nama** peoples in **German South West Africa** (today’s Namibia).

## Colonial Service (high-level)

- Served in multiple colonial contexts before 1904, including postings connected to German imperial campaigns.
- In 1904, he was appointed to take command in German South West Africa during an escalating anti-colonial conflict.

## Herero and Nama Genocide (1904–1908)

- **Battle of Waterberg (1904)**: After major fighting, Herero forces and civilians were pushed eastward toward the **Omaheke** region on the edge of the Kalahari.
- **Extermination order (Vernichtungsbefehl, Oct 1904)**: von Trotha issued a written order that effectively declared the Herero outside legal protection and demanded their removal from the territory; historians widely cite this as a central document of genocidal intent.
- **Denial of return and pursuit**: German forces sought to block routes back from the desert and pursued fleeing groups, contributing to mass death from violence, starvation, and thirst.
- **Extension of repression**: German policies of harsh punishment and forced displacement also targeted the Nama and other groups during subsequent phases of the war.
- **Policy reversal (late 1904)**: After political and public controversy, Berlin moved to moderate or rescind the explicit extermination policy, though coercive systems remained.

## Aftermath and Legacy

- The events in German South West Africa are widely recognized by scholars as one of the first genocides of the 20th century.
- von Trotha’s actions and directives are frequently discussed in studies of colonial warfare, racial ideology, and the development of coercive detention systems in imperial contexts.
- Germany has faced long-running debates and diplomatic negotiations regarding acknowledgement and reparative measures connected to the genocide.

## Key Points

- von Trotha was a senior commander whose **orders and strategy** played a decisive role in the escalation to genocidal violence.
- The **1904 extermination order** remains a focal piece of evidence for intent in historical and legal-historical analysis.
- The genocide’s memory has become central to Namibian national history and to Germany’s reckoning with colonial-era crimes.
