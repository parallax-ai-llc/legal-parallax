## Summary

German tax investigators discovered 1,400 artworks including suspected Nazi-looted art in the Munich apartment of Cornelius Gurlitt. The collection was worth over $1 billion.

## Context

- The hoard was linked to the activities of Hildebrand Gurlitt, Cornelius Gurlitt’s father, an art dealer who acquired works during the Nazi period.
- Many works required provenance research to determine whether they had been confiscated, sold under duress, or otherwise wrongfully taken from Jewish collectors and other victims.

## Why It Mattered

- The discovery became a high-profile example of the long tail of unresolved Nazi-looted art claims.
- It prompted renewed discussion about transparency, museum ethics, private collections, and state responsibilities around restitution.

## Key Issues (High Level)

- **Provenance uncertainty:** Large collections assembled over decades often contain gaps in ownership records.
- **Restitution process:** Returns typically depend on documentation and legal standards that vary by jurisdiction.
- **Public disclosure:** Initial secrecy around the discovery drew criticism and calls for more openness.

## Aftermath

- Authorities and later custodians undertook cataloging and research to identify problematic works.
- Individual restitution decisions have proceeded on a case-by-case basis as evidence is evaluated.
