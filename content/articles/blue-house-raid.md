## Summary

31 North Korean commandos infiltrated Seoul to assassinate South Korean President Park Chung-hee. They were stopped 800 meters from the Blue House; 26 commandos were killed.

## Context and Background

- The raid occurred during a period of intense inter-Korean confrontation along the Demilitarized Zone (DMZ), including frequent clashes and infiltration attempts.
- South Korea in the late 1960s was under the authoritarian government of President Park Chung-hee, with security forces heavily focused on internal stability and counter-infiltration.
- North Korea’s leadership sought high-impact operations to destabilize the South Korean government and demonstrate operational reach.

## The Operation (High-Level)

- The attackers were a 31-man unit (commonly identified in reporting as members of North Korea’s Unit 124) tasked with assassinating President Park at the Blue House (Cheong Wa Dae).
- They infiltrated across the DMZ and moved through rugged terrain toward the Seoul area, using concealment and nighttime movement to avoid detection.
- A key tactical element was the attempt to pass as South Korean soldiers by wearing similar uniforms, enabling movement closer to central Seoul.

## Key Moments

- After approaching Seoul, the group encountered South Korean civilians (woodcutters are widely cited) and attempted to prevent disclosure; the civilians later reported the incident, triggering security alerts.
- The commandos advanced toward the Blue House area but were confronted at a checkpoint near the presidential compound.
- The engagement escalated into firefights and a citywide manhunt as the group dispersed.

## Casualties and Immediate Aftermath

- The incident resulted in heavy casualties among the commandos and significant losses among South Korean security forces and civilians.
- Most of the infiltrators were killed during pursuit operations; a small number were captured or escaped.
- One commando, Kim Shin-jo, was captured and became the most publicly known participant; historical accounts commonly note that one infiltrator escaped back to North Korea.

## Security and Political Impact

- The raid led to tightened internal security measures and intensified counter-infiltration posture in South Korea.
- South Korean authorities expanded readiness and surveillance, emphasizing rapid reaction to infiltration reports and improving coordination among police, military, and intelligence services.
- The event is often cited alongside other contemporaneous incidents (including the USS Pueblo seizure in 1968) as part of a broader spike in tensions on the Korean peninsula.

## Legacy

- The Blue House Raid remains one of the most famous North Korean commando operations due to its proximity to the presidential residence and its dramatic urban pursuit.
- In South Korean public memory, it is frequently referenced in discussions of national security, inter-Korean hostility during the Cold War, and the evolution of domestic security institutions.
