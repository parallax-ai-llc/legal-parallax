## Summary

Tanzanian al-Qaeda operative convicted of conspiracy in the 1998 US embassy bombings.

## Additional Context

- **Alias/affiliation:** Frequently described in public reporting as an al-Qaeda operative linked to the East Africa cell involved in the 1998 bombings.
- **Case notoriety:** His prosecution is often cited in discussions about the U.S. approach to terrorism cases in **federal courts** versus **military commissions**.

## Key Events (high level)

- **1998:** U.S. embassies in **Nairobi (Kenya)** and **Dar es Salaam (Tanzania)** were bombed, killing hundreds and injuring thousands; multiple individuals were later charged in connection with the attacks.
- **2004:** Reported to have been captured and transferred into U.S. custody.
- **2006–2009:** Detained at the CIA “black sites” program and later held at **Guantánamo Bay** (as described in publicly available sources).
- **2009:** Transferred from Guantánamo to New York to face trial in U.S. federal court.
- **2010:** Convicted on a conspiracy count; acquitted on numerous other charges related to the bombings.

## Legal Proceedings (summary)

- **Venue:** U.S. District Court for the Southern District of New York.
- **Outcome:** Conviction for conspiracy connected to the embassy bombings; the mixed verdict is frequently referenced in debates over evidentiary rules and intelligence-derived testimony.

## Why It Matters

- Illustrates how one major terrorism case moved between **intelligence detention**, **military detention**, and **civilian criminal process**.
- Became a recurring example in policy arguments about legitimacy, transparency, and reliability of outcomes across different counterterrorism legal frameworks.
