## Summary

Boko Haram kidnapping of 276 schoolgirls from Chibok that sparked the #BringBackOurGirls campaign.

## Key Facts

- **Date:** Night of 14–15 April 2014 (reported).
- **Location:** Government Girls Secondary School, Chibok, Borno State, northeastern Nigeria.
- **Perpetrator:** Widely attributed to the Islamist militant group **Boko Haram**.
- **Scale:** **276** students were abducted; **dozens** reportedly escaped soon after.
- **Public response:** Became a global cause symbolized by the **#BringBackOurGirls** campaign.

## Background

Nigeria’s northeast experienced escalating insurgent violence in the early 2010s, including attacks on villages, security forces, and schools. Schools in particular were targeted as symbols of state authority and Western-style education, with repeated warnings and attacks contributing to insecurity across Borno State.

## What Happened (High-Level)

- Militants arrived at the school at night and gathered students.
- The students were transported away from Chibok into rural areas.
- In the immediate aftermath, information was contested and incomplete, contributing to confusion over the number abducted and the speed of the official response.

## International and Domestic Response

- Nigerian civil society groups and families organized sustained protests and advocacy.
- The **#BringBackOurGirls** slogan spread internationally, with public figures and governments amplifying attention.
- Multiple countries offered assistance (e.g., intelligence, surveillance, and advisory support), though the effectiveness and visibility of efforts varied over time.

## Aftermath and Outcomes

Over the years following the abduction, some of the girls were reported to have:

- **Escaped** during transit or in the early period after the kidnapping.
- Been **released** in groups during negotiations or prisoner-exchange efforts.
- Remained **missing** for extended periods, with uncertainty about their conditions and locations.

## Why It Mattered

The kidnapping became a defining episode of the Boko Haram insurgency and an emblem of:

- The vulnerability of civilians—especially girls and students—in conflict zones.
- Public scrutiny of state security capacity and crisis communication.
- The power and limits of global social-media-driven advocacy.

## Additional Timeline (Selected)
