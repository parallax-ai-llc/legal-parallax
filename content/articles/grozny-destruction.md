## Summary

Russian forces destroyed 85% of the Chechen capital Grozny during the Second Chechen War. The UN called it 'the most destroyed city on Earth.' Tens of thousands of civilians were killed.

## Context

Grozny is the capital of Chechnya, a federal subject of the Russian Federation in the North Caucasus. It was a major administrative and industrial center in the region.

The destruction described in most sources is associated with the Russian campaign to reassert control over Chechnya during the Second Chechen War (late 1999 into 2000), following renewed conflict between Russian forces and Chechen separatists.

## What Happened (High-Level)

Operations around Grozny included:

- Prolonged artillery and aerial bombardment of the city and surrounding areas.
- Ground assaults and urban combat as Russian forces advanced.
- Widespread damage to residential districts, infrastructure, and public buildings.

Many accounts emphasize that the intensity and duration of shelling, combined with urban fighting, produced a level of physical destruction that overwhelmed basic services and created severe humanitarian conditions.

## Destruction and Humanitarian Impact

Commonly cited points include:

- **Scale of damage:** Estimates in widely referenced summaries state that roughly **85%** of the city was destroyed.
- **Civilian casualties:** Reporting varies by source, but many summaries describe **tens of thousands** of civilians killed during the campaign and its immediate effects.
- **Displacement:** Large numbers of civilians fled Grozny and nearby towns, contributing to a broader displacement crisis within Chechnya and into neighboring regions.

The fighting also damaged or degraded critical systems such as electricity, water, housing, and medical facilities, compounding the risks faced by civilians who remained in the city.

## International Reactions

International organizations and observers repeatedly cited Grozny as an extreme case of urban destruction in a modern conflict.

- The **United Nations** was reported as calling Grozny *"the most destroyed city on Earth"* in the aftermath of the campaign.
- Human rights groups and journalists documented civilian harm and the devastation of civilian infrastructure, and these reports contributed to wider debates over proportionality, targeting, and compliance with international humanitarian law.

## Aftermath

In the years after the major combat phase, Grozny underwent substantial rebuilding. However, the reconstruction period is often discussed alongside:

- The long-term social and economic impacts of mass displacement and loss of housing.
- The legacy of trauma, missing persons, and unresolved accountability claims.
- Political consolidation and changes in governance in Chechnya under Moscow's influence.
