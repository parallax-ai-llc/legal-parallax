## Summary

US law enabling sanctions against human rights abusers and corrupt officials worldwide. Named after Russian whistleblower Sergei Magnitsky who died in custody after exposing tax fraud.

## Career Timeline

| Year | Event |
|------|-------|
| 2016 | Act signed into law |

## Key Provisions (High-Level)

- **Global scope:** Authorizes the U.S. government to sanction **foreign persons** (individuals and certain entities) implicated in **gross human rights violations** or **significant corruption**.
- **Sanctions mechanism:** Implemented through executive branch authorities and administered in practice via U.S. sanctions programs.
- **Public signaling:** Designations are typically published, creating reputational and financial consequences beyond U.S. borders.

## How It Works (Implementation)

- **Designation process:** U.S. agencies gather evidence and recommend targets; the executive branch can designate qualifying persons under Global Magnitsky authorities.
- **Administration/enforcement:** The **U.S. Department of the Treasury (OFAC)** is central for listing and enforcing restrictions; other agencies may contribute investigatory and diplomatic input.
- **Triggering conduct (typical categories):**
  - Serious abuses such as extrajudicial killing, torture, or prolonged arbitrary detention.
  - Significant corruption, including large-scale bribery, misappropriation of state assets, and related facilitation.

## Common Sanctions Tools Used

- **Asset blocking (freezing):** Property and interests in property within U.S. jurisdiction can be blocked.
- **U.S. person restrictions:** Generally prohibits U.S. persons from dealing with blocked persons.
- **Visa/travel consequences:** Often paired with immigration-related restrictions that limit entry to the United States.

## Relationship to the Original “Magnitsky Act”

- The **Magnitsky Act (2012)** focused primarily on Russia-related accountability connected to Sergei Magnitsky’s case.
- The **Global Magnitsky Act (2016)** expanded the concept into a **worldwide** framework, allowing sanctions for abuses and corruption in many countries.

## Additional Context

- The act is frequently discussed as a tool for **accountability** when domestic legal systems fail to prosecute powerful abusers or kleptocrats.
- Critics and analysts also debate:
  - **Due process and evidentiary standards** for designations.
  - **Geopolitical selectivity** (whether enforcement is consistent across regions and strategic partners).
  - **Effectiveness** (deterrence vs. symbolic impact), especially when targets have limited exposure to U.S. financial systems.
