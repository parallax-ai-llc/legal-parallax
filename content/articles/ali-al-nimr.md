## Background

- Ali al-Nimr is a Saudi national whose case drew international attention in the context of protests in Saudi Arabia's Eastern Province during the early 2010s.
- He was arrested as a teenager following allegations linked to demonstrations; reporting on the case often notes that he was a minor at the time of some alleged offenses.

## Legal Case (High Level)

- Saudi courts issued a death sentence that was widely reported as including public execution and post-execution display (described in some sources as "crucifixion").
- Human-rights organizations and news coverage raised concerns about due process, including allegations about interrogation practices and access to legal counsel.
- Over time, public reporting indicated shifts in the legal and political environment, including debate about the application of capital punishment for offenses committed by minors.

## International Response

- Multiple international human-rights groups advocated for clemency and highlighted the case in campaigns about juvenile sentencing and capital punishment.
- The case was frequently cited in broader discussions of Saudi Arabia's criminal justice system and protest-related prosecutions.

## Key Points

- **Known for**: Becoming a high-profile example in discussions about juvenile offenders and the death penalty.
- **Status**: Public reporting has varied over time; some accounts indicate he remained imprisoned for years after the original sentence.
