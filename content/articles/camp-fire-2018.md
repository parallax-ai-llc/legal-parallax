## Summary

PG&E utility's poorly maintained equipment sparked the Camp Fire that destroyed Paradise, California, killing 85 people. The deadliest and most destructive wildfire in California history.

## Career Timeline

| Year | Event |
|------|-------|

| 2020 | PG&E pleads guilty to 84 counts of involuntary manslaughter (Camp Fire victims) and one count of unlawfully causing a fire. |

## Background

- The Camp Fire started on **November 8, 2018** in **Butte County, California**, and rapidly spread under dry fuels and strong winds.
- The town of **Paradise** and nearby communities were heavily impacted, with large-scale evacuations and long-term displacement.

## Cause (High-Level)

- Investigations identified **electrical equipment associated with Pacific Gas & Electric (PG&E)** as a key ignition source.
- The event became a widely cited case study in how **utility infrastructure risk**, vegetation management, and extreme weather can combine into catastrophic wildfire outcomes.

## Impact

- **Human toll:** 85 people were killed; many others were injured or displaced.
- **Structures:** Thousands of buildings were destroyed in Paradise and surrounding areas.
- **Environmental & health:** Dense smoke produced severe air-quality impacts across Northern California and beyond.
- **Economic:** Cleanup, rebuilding, and litigation generated multi-billion-dollar costs and long-running financial consequences.

## Legal / Regulatory Aftermath

- PG&E faced extensive civil litigation related to loss of life and property.
- Criminal proceedings resulted in PG&E’s guilty plea related to the deaths and the fire’s ignition.
- The disaster influenced subsequent public policy discussions on:
  - utility wildfire prevention and grid hardening
  - power shutoffs during extreme fire weather (public safety power shutoffs)
  - emergency alerting and evacuation planning
