## Summary

Following the 1923 Great Kanto Earthquake, Japanese mobs massacred an estimated 6,000 ethnic Koreans and Chinese based on false rumors they were poisoning wells and looting.

## Context and Background

- The **Great Kantō earthquake** struck the Tokyo–Yokohama region on **1923-09-01**, causing widespread collapse, fires, and disruption to communications and public order.[^1]
- In the immediate aftermath, **rumors spread rapidly** (via word of mouth, some printed notices, and ad hoc patrols) that **Koreans** were committing arson, looting, or **poisoning wells**—claims that were largely false and fueled ethnic prejudice.[^1]

## What Happened (High-Level)

- **Vigilante groups and local mobs** formed checkpoints, stopped travelers, and used language tests or other crude “screening” methods to identify suspected Koreans.
- Large numbers of **ethnic Koreans**—and in some cases **Chinese residents and Japanese mistaken for Koreans**—were attacked and killed across multiple locations in the region.[^1]
- The violence is commonly described as a **massacre / mass killing** triggered by misinformation and social panic during a natural-disaster emergency.[^1]

## Victims and Estimates

- Estimates vary; a commonly cited figure is **around 6,000 Koreans killed**, with additional Chinese victims and Japanese victims of mistaken identity.[^1]
- Exact totals are difficult to confirm due to chaos, uneven record-keeping, and the politicization of later accounts.

## Role of Authorities and Aftermath

- Contemporary reporting and later scholarship discuss **mixed responses** by authorities: some actions aimed at restoring order and protecting civilians, alongside failures to prevent vigilante violence and the broader climate of rumor-driven targeting.[^1]
- The event remains a major case study in how **disinformation and ethnic scapegoating** can escalate during crises.

## Legacy and Memory

- The massacre is remembered in debates over **historical responsibility, public commemoration, and minority rights** in Japan.
- It is frequently referenced when discussing **emergency governance** and the risks of unofficial “security” groups operating without accountability.

- The episode is sometimes contrasted with later disaster responses to highlight the importance of **credible information channels** and protections for vulnerable groups.
