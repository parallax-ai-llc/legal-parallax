## Context

- The Democratic Republic of the Congo (DRC) experienced multiple armed conflicts in the late 1990s and 2000s, involving state forces and numerous non-state armed groups.
- In several of these conflicts, the use and recruitment of children by armed forces or groups was widely documented by international organizations and human-rights monitors.

## Why the Conviction Matters (High-Level)

- Domestic prosecutions for child-soldier recruitment can serve as a complement to international justice mechanisms by showing that national courts can address serious international crimes.
- Cases involving child recruitment also help clarify that commanders and recruiters can be held criminally responsible for using children in hostilities.

## Child Soldier Recruitment — Overview

- "Child soldier" commonly refers to a person under 18 who is recruited or used by an armed force or armed group in any capacity (fighters, porters, cooks, spies, or for sexual purposes).
- Recruitment can be forced (abduction/coercion) or nominally "voluntary" under conditions of severe pressure, insecurity, or lack of alternatives.
- Harms include interrupted education, physical injury, long-term trauma, and difficulty reintegrating into civilian life.
