## Summary

Eric Harris and Dylan Klebold murdered 13 people at Columbine High School in Colorado. The massacre transformed school security and influenced subsequent mass shootings.

## Key Facts (High-Level)

- **Date / location:** April 20, 1999, Columbine High School (Littleton, Colorado, United States).
- **Perpetrators:** Two students, **Eric Harris** and **Dylan Klebold**.
- **Victims:** **13 people were killed** and **24 were injured**; the perpetrators died by suicide.
- **Weapons / tactics (broad):** Firearms and improvised explosive devices were involved; many devices failed to detonate as intended.
- **Why it mattered:** The event became a major inflection point in U.S. discussions about school safety, youth violence, media effects, and gun policy.

## What Happened (Overview)

The attack unfolded during the school day and included shootings inside and outside the building. Public understanding initially relied on fast-moving news reports; later investigations clarified a more complex sequence of events and planning.

## Expanded Timeline (Selected)

- **Before 1999:** Planning and weapons acquisition occurred over time rather than as a single-day impulse.
- **1999-04-20:** Attack at Columbine High School; 13 victims killed and 24 injured; both perpetrators die by suicide.
- **1999 (following months):** Multiple investigations and official reports are produced at local and state levels.
- **2000s:** U.S. schools increasingly adopt security measures (e.g., controlled access, school resource officers) and threat-assessment practices.

## Aftermath and Impact

### School Safety and Policing

- Accelerated adoption of **school resource officers**, building access controls, and emergency drills.
- Contributed to the growth of **behavioral threat assessment** approaches used by schools and law enforcement.

### Media, Culture, and Public Debate

- Became a widely cited reference point in debates on **violent media**, **bullying**, **youth mental health**, and **firearms**.
- Inspired extensive coverage and later works (books, documentaries), shaping how later incidents were framed in the public imagination.

### Copycat Concerns and Later Events

Researchers and policymakers have discussed whether intense publicity around mass violence can contribute to imitation behavior, leading some outlets and experts to advocate for more restrained reporting practices.

## Investigation Notes (High-Level)

- Subsequent reporting emphasized that early narratives contained inaccuracies, which is common in major breaking-news events.
- Official reviews examined law-enforcement response, information sharing, and emergency tactics; lessons learned influenced later active-shooter training and response doctrine.
