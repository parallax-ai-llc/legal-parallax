## Summary

Lebanese militia commander who led the Sabra and Shatila massacre of Palestinian refugees in 1982. Assassinated before he could testify about Ariel Sharon's role.

## Additional Background

Elie Hobeika was a Lebanese militia figure associated with the Lebanese Civil War era. In many open-source summaries, his name is closely linked to the **Sabra and Shatila massacre** in 1982, an event widely described as a major atrocity committed during the conflict’s most violent phases.[^1]

## Lebanese Civil War Context (High-Level)

Public overviews typically stress that the Lebanese Civil War involved:

- Multiple competing militias and political factions.
- Shifting alliances, including the involvement of external state actors.
- Cycles of reprisal violence, displacement, and sectarian polarization.

Within this environment, militia commanders could exercise substantial de facto power, including control over armed units and local security.[^1]

## Sabra and Shatila (High-Level)

Wikipedia and other high-level summaries commonly note:

- The massacre occurred in **1982** in Beirut-area refugee camps.
- It resulted in the killing of a large number of civilians (figures vary by source).
- The event remains a focal point of international legal, political, and historical debate regarding responsibility and command structures.[^1]

## Later Political Role and Controversy (Context)

Open sources describe Hobeika as later participating in Lebanese political life after the war, which is frequently cited as an example of how post-conflict political systems can incorporate former militia leaders. Summaries often emphasize that accountability for wartime atrocities was contested and uneven in the postwar period.[^1]

## Assassination (Context)

Hobeika was assassinated in Beirut in 2002 by a car bomb. Public reporting and summaries frequently mention that his death occurred amid ongoing discussion of possible testimony or disclosures related to the 1982 events, though details and interpretations differ across sources.[^1]

He is often referenced in discussions of:

- **Transitional justice** and the long-term consequences of amnesty or limited accountability.
- **Command responsibility** in irregular warfare settings.
- **Memory and narrative conflict**: how civil-war histories remain politically active decades later.
