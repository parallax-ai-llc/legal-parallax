## Summary

Al-Shabaab suicide bombers attacked two venues in Kampala showing the World Cup final, killing 74 people. The first al-Shabaab attack outside Somalia.

## Career Timeline

| Year | Event |
|------|-------|
| 2010 | 74 killed in twin bombings |

## Background

- The attacks occurred on **11 July 2010**, during public screenings of the **2010 FIFA World Cup Final**.
- Kampala, Uganda’s capital, hosted large crowds in bars and clubs showing the match.
- The bombings were widely reported as a **milestone in al-Shabaab’s external operations**, marking an attack outside Somalia.

## What Happened (High Level)

- **Two near-simultaneous explosions** struck separate venues where people gathered to watch the match.
- The devices were reported as **suicide bombs**.
- The attack targeted civilians in crowded public settings, maximizing casualties.

## Perpetrators (Attribution)

- The attack was attributed to **al-Shabaab**, the Somalia-based Islamist militant group.
- Reporting commonly framed the bombings as retaliation for Uganda’s role in **AMISOM** (the African Union mission in Somalia).

## Targets and Locations

- The bombings hit **two venues** in Kampala that were screening the World Cup final.
- These locations were chosen for:
  - high foot traffic,
  - predictable timing (match schedule),
  - dense crowds concentrated in confined spaces.

## Casualties and Immediate Impact

- **74 people were killed**.
- Many more were injured, and the incident led to heightened security concerns around public events and mass gatherings.

## Aftermath

- Uganda increased security measures and intensified investigations after the attacks.
- The event influenced regional counterterrorism posture, especially regarding the security implications of deployments in Somalia.

## Significance

- Often cited as **the first major al-Shabaab attack outside Somalia**.
- Demonstrated the group’s intent and ability to project violence regionally.
- Became a reference point in analyses of militant group strategy: deterrence messaging, retaliation framing, and civilian-targeted mass-casualty tactics.
