## Historical Context

Vanuatu (formerly the New Hebrides) moved toward independence in the late 1970s, with competing visions for political authority across the archipelago. On Espiritu Santo—Vanuatu’s largest island—local political, economic, and land issues became entwined with debates over the structure of the new state.

The 1980 Santo secession attempt is often referred to as the **Coconut War**, a name that reflects the limited scale of conventional combat even as the crisis posed a serious challenge to the sovereignty of the newly independent country.

## Role in the Santo Rebellion ("Coconut War")

High-level points commonly associated with Stevens’ leadership during the crisis include:

- **Leadership of a secessionist movement** centered on Espiritu Santo.
- **Declaration of a breakaway entity** (often described in sources as the “Republic of Vemarana”).
- **Opposition to central authority** during the period immediately surrounding independence (1980).
- **Eventual defeat and arrest**, after which the secession project ended.

## Aftermath and Legacy

In mainstream historical accounts of Vanuatu’s early independence period, Stevens is remembered primarily for:

- Testing the capacity of the new state to maintain territorial integrity.
- Embodying tensions over land, local governance, and external influence in late-colonial politics.
- Becoming a reference point in later discussions about regional autonomy and post-independence nation-building.
