## Summary

Dubai's real estate market is a major destination for laundered money from corruption, crime, and sanctions evasion. An estimated $34 billion in suspicious property purchases were documented.

## How real-estate laundering works (high-level)

Money laundering through property generally involves converting proceeds of crime (or funds linked to corruption and sanctions evasion) into an asset that can be held, rented, mortgaged, or resold. Real estate is attractive because it can absorb large sums, transactions can be complex, and value is often subjective.

## Common techniques reported in Dubai property cases

- **Use of shell companies and nominees**: Purchases are made through corporate vehicles or stand-ins to obscure the beneficial owner.
- **Cross-border layering**: Funds pass through multiple jurisdictions (banks, intermediaries, trade flows) before reaching the purchase.
- **All-cash or opaque-source purchases**: Paying without a clear paper trail, or using sources of funds that are difficult to verify.
- **Fragmentation**: Splitting a large sum into smaller transfers and entities to avoid scrutiny.
- **Rapid flipping and refinancing**: Selling quickly or borrowing against property to “legitimize” funds.

## Why Dubai is frequently cited as a destination

- **Global investor demand and high transaction volume**: Large markets can make suspicious deals harder to detect.
- **International connectivity**: Dubai’s role as a regional business hub can facilitate cross-border movement of people and capital.
- **Beneficial ownership challenges**: When the true owner is not easily discoverable, enforcement becomes harder.
- **Regulatory arbitrage**: Criminal networks may shift activity to jurisdictions perceived as less stringent or slower to enforce.

## Impacts

- **Distorts housing and land prices** by injecting illicit demand.
- **Enables continued criminal activity** by preserving and growing proceeds.
- **Creates reputational and diplomatic risk** for the jurisdiction and financial sector.

## Policy and enforcement context (brief)

Dubai property laundering is often discussed alongside broader anti-money-laundering (AML) and counter–terrorist financing (CTF) enforcement in the UAE. International bodies and investigative reporting have highlighted risks in real estate, which has led to stronger compliance expectations for developers, brokers, and other “designated non-financial businesses and professions” (DNFBPs).

- **FATF grey listing (2022)**: The Financial Action Task Force (FATF) placed the UAE under increased monitoring in 2022, citing AML/CTF effectiveness gaps.
- **Subsequent reforms**: The UAE has reported regulatory and supervisory improvements intended to strengthen beneficial ownership transparency and enforcement.
