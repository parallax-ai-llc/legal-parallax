## Summary

Cocaine flows from South America to Europe through West Africa, with Guinea-Bissau, Ghana, and Senegal as major transit points. Annual European cocaine market worth $10 billion.

## How the Route Works (High Level)

- **Origin**: Cocaine production is concentrated in parts of the Andean region of South America.
- **Primary flow**: Shipments move by air and sea to staging points, then onward to European consumer markets.
- **Why West Africa**: Geography between South America and Europe, coastline access, and governance/capacity gaps have made parts of the region attractive for transshipment.

## Common Logistics Patterns

- **Maritime**: Containerized cargo, fishing vessels, and smaller craft used to break up loads.
- **Aviation**: Private aircraft and light planes used for high-value, smaller consignments.
- **“Fragmentation”**: Traffickers often split shipments across multiple routes to reduce the impact of any single seizure.
- **Repackaging/warehousing**: Temporary storage and repackaging may occur before onward movement.

## Noted Transit and Staging Areas

This varies over time with enforcement pressure and political conditions, but reporting frequently highlights:

- **Guinea-Bissau** (often cited as a transit hub)
- **Guinea and Sierra Leone** (periodically referenced in regional trafficking reporting)
- **Ghana, Senegal, and Cape Verde** (as nodes in broader Atlantic/West African routes)

## Regional and Market Impact

- **Governance risk**: Trafficking revenue can amplify corruption and weaken institutions.
- **Violence and organized crime**: Competition over routes and protection markets can increase local insecurity.
- **Public health spillovers**: While the primary destination may be Europe, domestic consumption and related harms can rise in transit countries.
- **Economic distortion**: Illicit finance can distort local economies and undermine legitimate commerce.

## Countermeasures and Adaptation

- **Interdiction**: Maritime patrols, port controls, container screening, and intelligence-led seizures.
- **International cooperation**: Joint operations and information sharing between West African states, European agencies, and international bodies.
- **Financial disruption**: Anti-money-laundering efforts targeting facilitators, shell companies, and trade-based money laundering.
- **Displacement effect**: As enforcement increases in one corridor, routes often shift rather than disappear.

## Notes on Sources

Route descriptions and “major transit points” are typically compiled from seizure data, investigative reporting, and assessments by international and regional organizations. Specific countries and methods may change year-to-year as traffickers adapt.
