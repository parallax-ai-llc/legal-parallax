## Context

Artaxerxes III (also known by the name **Ochus**) was a ruler of the late **Achaemenid Empire**, governing in a period of recurring satrapal revolts and pressure from the eastern Mediterranean. His reign is often summarized by ancient and later sources as a drive to **restore central authority** through a combination of administrative control, military campaigns, and harsh repression.

## Reign (high-level)

- **Court politics and succession:** Sources describe a violent consolidation of power early in his reign; modern summaries often treat details cautiously because accounts are shaped by hostile traditions and later moralizing histories.
- **Imperial control:** He is associated with efforts to curb the autonomy of satraps and to reassert the king’s authority across the empire.
- **Military focus:** Major attention went to the western provinces, especially the long-running goal of bringing **Egypt** back under Persian rule.

## Reconquest of Egypt

- **Strategic importance:** Egypt had broken away from Persian control and represented both a major revenue base and a prestige issue for the empire.
- **Outcome:** Artaxerxes III ultimately succeeded in retaking Egypt (commonly dated to **343 BCE**), after which Greek and Egyptian traditions report severe reprisals against resistance.
- **Administration:** Following reconquest, the Persian court installed new officials and garrisons to hold the province, seeking to prevent renewed secession.

## Reputation and legacy

Artaxerxes III’s legacy is strongly influenced by reports of **purges and mass violence**, which contribute to the “tyrant” image in popular retellings. Historically, he is also remembered as one of the last Persian kings to achieve a major reconquest and as a ruler whose death amid palace intrigue preceded the rapid sequence of successions before the rise of **Alexander the Great**.

## Notes on sources

Much of what is popularly repeated about Artaxerxes III comes through Greek authors and later compilations; these can emphasize sensational court violence. Where possible, treat specific casualty numbers and dramatic anecdotes as **indicative** rather than precise.
