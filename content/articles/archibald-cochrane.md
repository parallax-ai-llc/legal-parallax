## Context and Identity

The name **“Archibald Cochrane”** appears on Wikipedia as a *disambiguation* entry covering several people with the same name. The biographical dates in this file (1748–1831) correspond to **Archibald Cochrane, 9th Earl of Dundonald**—a British aristocrat, military officer, and inventor best known for work on the **utilisation of coal byproducts**.[^2]

## Inventions and Industrial Experiments

Cochrane’s most notable practical work centered on extracting value from coal on an industrial scale:

- **Coal tar process (patented 1781):** Developed a method to manufacture coal tar commercially, with additional byproducts (e.g., coke and coal gas) emerging from the process.[^2]
- **Naval applications:** He attempted to market coal tar as a protective coating/sealant for ship hulls (as an alternative to copper sheathing). Contemporary tests reportedly compared coated vs. uncoated surfaces for marine damage and fouling.[^2]
- **Other experiments:** He explored manufacturing processes such as alum production, paint, and food substitutes (including potato-based bread), with mixed commercial results.[^2]

## Institutions and Recognition

- Elected **FRSE** in 1784; proposers listed include **James Hutton** and **Adam Smith**.[^2]
- Elected to the **American Philosophical Society** (International Member) in 1795.[^2]

## Family

He married three times and had four sons. His best-known son was **Thomas Cochrane**, a prominent Royal Navy officer and later an influential naval leader in several newly independent countries.[^2]
