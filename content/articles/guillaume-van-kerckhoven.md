## Summary

Belgian colonial officer who paid his soldiers per severed head during Congo Free State operations.

## Context

- Served under the **International Association of the Congo** and later the **Congo Free State**, the personal colonial regime of King Leopold II (1885–1908).[^1]
- Operated in an era of armed expeditions, forced labor, and violent coercion used to impose and maintain colonial control in the interior of the Congo basin.[^1]

## Notable Activities

- **Bangalas station / recruitment:** Wikipedia describes him as gaining support from local chiefs around the Bangala post and later sending Bangala soldiers to Boma; some became among the earliest African recruits for the **Force Publique** (the Congo Free State’s military force).[^1]
- **Uele–Nile expedition:** He is noted for an extended expedition through the **Uele River** basin toward the **Nile**, described as causing **over 1,800 deaths** and establishing a Belgian presence in the region.[^1]
- **Bounty system:** Contemporary reporting and later historiography have highlighted his use of **payments tied to severed heads** as a “proof” of kills—an extreme example of the incentive structures that could intensify violence in Congo Free State campaigns.[^1]

## Death

- Wikipedia reports he died in 1892 near **Jebel Watti** (Congo Free State), with the immediate cause described as a **gunshot wound**, before completing the Nile objective of his expedition.[^1]
