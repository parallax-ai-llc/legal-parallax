## Summary

German colonial governor during the Maji Maji Rebellion who used scorched earth tactics killing 250,000-300,000.

## Context: German East Africa

- German East Africa roughly covered much of present-day mainland Tanzania (plus parts of modern Rwanda and Burundi).
- In the early 1900s, German colonial authorities relied on coercive labor, taxation, and forced production systems that intensified local grievances.
- The Maji Maji Rebellion (1905–1907) became one of the largest anti-colonial uprisings in the region.

## Role in the Maji Maji Rebellion

- Gustav von Götzen served as governor of German East Africa during the initial outbreak of the rebellion.
- German forces responded with “scorched earth” tactics, including:
  - destruction of villages,
  - seizure or burning of stored food,
  - destruction of crops.
- These measures contributed to widespread famine and mass civilian deaths; historical estimates commonly cite a death toll in the hundreds of thousands (often 250,000–300,000).

## Consequences and historical assessment

- The famine that followed the campaigns is widely regarded as a central driver of the rebellion’s catastrophic human cost.
- The suppression is frequently discussed in scholarship as an example of how colonial counterinsurgency tactics can shift from military aims to broad civilian devastation.
- In biographical summaries, von Götzen is often linked directly to the colonial administration’s policies during this period, even as the violence involved multiple commanders and units operating across a wide area.
