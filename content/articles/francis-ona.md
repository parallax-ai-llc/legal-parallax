## Summary

Led the Bougainville Revolutionary Army in a secessionist conflict that killed up to 20,000 people.

## Background

- Bougainville (an island region of Papua New Guinea) experienced escalating tensions in the late 1980s linked to environmental damage, land rights, and revenue disputes tied to the Panguna copper mine.
- The Bougainville conflict is widely described as one of the deadliest in the South Pacific after World War II; estimates of deaths vary in sources.

## Role in the Bougainville Conflict

- Ona was widely reported as a leading figure of the BRA, a key armed group advocating Bougainvillean self-determination and, in some accounts, independence.
- His leadership became closely associated with the anti-Panguna mine movement and the broader secessionist cause.
- The conflict involved multiple actors over time (Papua New Guinea security forces, BRA factions, local militias, and community leadership), and loyalties were not always uniform across Bougainville.

## Politics and Negotiations (High-Level)

- Peace efforts progressed through several agreements and international mediation, particularly in the late 1990s and early 2000s.
- Ona was often described as operating outside, or at the margins of, the main Bougainville peace process, which ultimately produced the 2001 Bougainville Peace Agreement.
- The post-conflict political framework created an Autonomous Bougainville Government and committed to a future referendum on Bougainville’s political status.

## Legacy

- To supporters, he is remembered as a symbol of resistance and Bougainvillean self-determination.
- To critics, he is associated with the violent period of the conflict and its humanitarian costs.
- Regardless of interpretation, he remains a significant figure in accounts of Bougainville’s modern political history.
