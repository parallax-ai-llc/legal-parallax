## Background

Public reporting describes al-Nashiri as a Saudi national associated with al-Qaeda’s external operations network in the late 1990s and early 2000s.[^1]
He has been alleged to have operated across multiple countries in the Middle East and South Asia, using travel and logistics networks that supported al-Qaeda attacks.[^1]

## Notable Allegations and Charges (High Level)

Open sources and U.S. government reporting commonly associate him with planning, directing, or supporting several maritime-related attacks, including:

- The **USS Cole bombing (2000)** in Aden, Yemen, which killed 17 U.S. sailors.[^1]
- The attempted attack on **USS The Sullivans (2000)**, which failed when the small boat sank.[^1]
- The bombing of the French tanker **MV Limburg (2002)** off Yemen, which killed one crew member and caused an oil spill.[^1]

These allegations form a central part of the case against him in U.S. military commission proceedings.[^1]

## Capture, Detention, and Transfer

According to widely cited accounts, al-Nashiri was captured in 2002 and then held in U.S. custody, including periods in the CIA’s former “black sites” program, before transfer to **Guantánamo Bay**.[^1]
His detention history is frequently discussed in relation to debates over interrogation practices, evidentiary rules, and due process in terrorism cases.[^1]

## Military Commission Proceedings

Al-Nashiri has been charged under the U.S. military commissions system, with litigation spanning many years due to contested legal issues such as:

- Admissibility of evidence connected to earlier detention and interrogation
- Jurisdiction and the scope of commission authority over alleged conduct
- Handling of classified information and security constraints
- Appeals and pre-trial rulings affecting timelines

Because the proceedings are ongoing and shaped by court decisions, summaries in open sources can change over time.[^1]

## Significance

In high-level historical terms, his case is often cited as:

- A major example of **maritime terrorism** targeting naval and commercial shipping
- A landmark test of the **post-9/11 detention and trial framework** used by the United States
- A reference point in public and legal debates about the long-term consequences of secret detention programs
