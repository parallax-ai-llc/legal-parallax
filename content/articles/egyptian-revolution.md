## Summary

Massive protests in Tahrir Square overthrew Hosni Mubarak after 30 years. Security forces killed 846 protesters during the 18-day revolution.

## Context and Causes

- Part of the broader wave of 2010–2011 Arab Spring protests across the Middle East and North Africa.
- Long-running grievances included emergency law and police abuses, corruption perceptions, unemployment and underemployment (especially among youth), and rising food and living costs.
- Political competition was constrained under President Hosni Mubarak’s rule, which began in 1981.

## Key Events (2011)

- **Jan 25:** Large demonstrations begin, drawing tens of thousands in Cairo and other cities.
- **Jan 28 ("Friday of Anger")**: Major nationwide protests and clashes; authorities restrict communications and deploy security forces.
- **Early Feb:** Labor strikes and professional-group mobilization add economic pressure.
- **Feb 1:** Mubarak announces he will not seek another term.
- **Feb 11:** Mubarak resigns; power is transferred to the Supreme Council of the Armed Forces (SCAF).

## Outcomes and Aftermath

- The end of Mubarak’s presidency marked a major political rupture, but the transition period was contested and unstable.
- Post-revolution governance featured a sequence of interim military and civilian authorities and repeated constitutional and electoral changes.
- Human rights and accountability debates persisted, including scrutiny of security forces’ conduct during and after the uprising.

## Actors and Institutions (high-level)

- **Protesters and civil society:** broad coalition including youth movements, labor groups, and opposition networks.
- **Security forces:** police and state security were central to street-level confrontation and public controversy.
- **Military (SCAF):** became the transitional authority after Feb 11, shaping the initial post-Mubarak roadmap.
