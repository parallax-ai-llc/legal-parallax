## Summary

167-year-old Swiss bank collapsed and was absorbed by UBS after years of scandals and criminal clients.

## What Happened (High-Level)

- Credit Suisse entered 2023 after multiple years of reputational damage, risk incidents, and client trust erosion.
- In early-to-mid March 2023, confidence deteriorated rapidly and large outflows accelerated.
- Swiss authorities facilitated an emergency combination with UBS to stabilize the Swiss and global financial system.

## Key Drivers and Precedents

- Risk management and governance failures that accumulated over time.
- Major loss events that weakened capital and credibility (for example: Archegos Capital and the Greensill funds episode).
- A series of legal, compliance, and client-screening controversies that increased scrutiny and costs.
- Market conditions in 2022–2023 (rate rises, tighter liquidity) that reduced tolerance for weak balance sheets.

## The UBS Rescue Deal (March 2023)

- UBS agreed to acquire Credit Suisse in a Swiss government-brokered transaction announced on 2023-03-19.
- The Swiss National Bank (SNB) and Swiss authorities announced liquidity backstops intended to reduce immediate funding pressure.
- Swiss regulator FINMA ordered a write-down of Credit Suisse Additional Tier 1 (AT1) instruments to absorb losses, an outcome that drew international attention and litigation risk.

## Immediate Aftermath

- The event became a global case study in how quickly modern bank confidence can collapse once outflows become self-reinforcing.
- It prompted renewed focus on bank liquidity profiles, concentration of deposits/clients, and transparency of funding sources.
- It also increased attention on the creditor hierarchy and contractual terms of bank capital instruments (especially AT1).

## Why It Matters

- Credit Suisse had been a long-standing pillar of Swiss banking and a globally significant investment bank.
- Its disappearance as an independent institution marked one of the most consequential European bank failures since the 2008 crisis era.
- The takeover consolidated Swiss banking power in a single dominant institution, raising long-run competition and "too-big-to-fail" questions.
