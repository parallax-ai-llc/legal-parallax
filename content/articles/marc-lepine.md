## Summary
Marc Lépine murdered 14 women at École Polytechnique in Montreal on December 6, 1989. He targeted female engineering students. The massacre led to stricter gun control in Canada.

## Context
- **Location**: École Polytechnique (engineering school), Montreal, Quebec, Canada.
- **Date**: December 6, 1989.
- **Common name**: The attack is widely referred to as the **École Polytechnique massacre** (or the **Montreal Massacre**).

## Attack (high-level)
- Lépine entered the school armed and moved through areas where students and staff were present.
- He separated men and women in at least one classroom and **shot women after stating anti-feminist motives** (as commonly reported in contemporary accounts).
- The event ended with Lépine’s death by suicide.

## Victims and impact
- **Fatalities**: 14 women were killed.
- **Injuries**: Additional people were wounded during the attack.
- The targeting of women—particularly women studying engineering—made the massacre a defining event in Canadian discussions about **misogyny**, **gendered violence**, and **public safety**.

## Aftermath and legacy
- The massacre contributed to sustained public pressure for Canadian **firearms regulation** and improved controls over access to certain weapons.
- **December 6** is commemorated in Canada as the **National Day of Remembrance and Action on Violence Against Women**.
- The event is frequently cited in policy, academic, and advocacy work on preventing mass violence and violence against women.
