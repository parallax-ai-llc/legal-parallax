## Summary

Fire captain convicted of being a serial arsonist who set fires he would then investigate, killing 4.

## Background

- Orr was a U.S. fire captain and arson investigator who gained trust and access by working within the fire service.
- Sources describe a “firefighter-arsonist” pattern in which the offender helps respond to, investigate, or document fires they caused.

## Modus Operandi (High-Level)

- Allegations and convictions describe fires being set and then reported or “discovered,” allowing Orr to be present as a responder or investigator.
- The case is often discussed as an example of occupational access increasing both opportunity (to set fires) and credibility (to deflect suspicion).

## Investigation and Prosecution

- The public record (summarized by Wikipedia) emphasizes behavioral and pattern-based evidence: repeated incidents, similarities across fire scenes, and investigative linkage across jurisdictions.
- The case drew attention because it involved an individual trained in fire science and investigation, complicating early attribution.

## Writings and Notoriety

- Wikipedia notes that Orr wrote a novel titled *Points of Origin*, which investigators cited as relevant to understanding motive and method.
- In criminology and fire investigation training, his case is frequently referenced as a cautionary example about insider threats within emergency services.

## Impact / Lessons (Non-Speculative)

- Reinforced the need for independent oversight and cross-agency review when a responder or investigator could be connected to suspicious incidents.
- Highlighted the importance of data sharing and pattern detection across multiple fire jurisdictions.
