## Context: Non-Consensual Deepfake Imagery

Non-consensual intimate imagery (NCII) refers to the creation or distribution of sexual images of a person without that person’s consent.
When generative AI is used to synthesize a person’s face or body into sexual content, it is often described as a “deepfake” even when the technique is closer to face-swapping or image synthesis.

### Why this category matters

- **Low barrier to entry:** consumer-grade tools can produce convincing results without specialized expertise.
- **High harm:** victims commonly report reputational damage, harassment, professional consequences, and severe psychological distress.
- **Rapid spread:** once posted, content can be mirrored across multiple platforms, making removal difficult.

## Typical Modus Operandi (high-level)

This section describes patterns frequently observed in NCII deepfake cases; it does not claim specifics beyond the public summary above.

- **Source collection:** scraping social media photos and videos to gather target face angles and expressions.
- **Synthesis/editing:** using face-swap, diffusion-based image generation, or compositing workflows.
- **Distribution:** posting to adult sites, forums, messaging groups, or sending directly to acquaintances/employers.
- **Monetization (sometimes):** paywalled channels, extortion (“sextortion”), or ad-driven traffic.

## Indicators & Investigation (overview)

Common investigative and evidentiary elements in deepfake exploitation matters can include:

- Platform account records (registration emails, IP logs, device identifiers)
- Upload timestamps and file hashes
- Cloud-storage or local device artifacts (models, checkpoints, prompts, exported media)
- Communication records showing intent, coercion, or knowledge of lack of consent
- Victim reports and corroborating witnesses

## Legal & Policy Landscape (United States)

Laws vary by state and are evolving; deepfake-specific statutes often focus on sexual content, elections, or fraud.
Key recurring legal themes include:

- **Consent:** whether the depicted person consented to creation and/or distribution.
- **Intent and knowledge:** whether the actor knew the material was non-consensual and harmful.
- **Distribution vs. creation:** some regimes penalize dissemination more heavily than private creation.
- **Identity and privacy:** use of a person’s likeness can trigger privacy and right-of-publicity considerations.

## Impact & Risk

- **Victim targeting:** women, minors, and public-facing individuals are disproportionately targeted in many reported datasets.
- **Secondary victimization:** repeated requests to “prove” authenticity/in-authenticity can itself be harmful.
- **Operational risk for organizations:** workplaces, schools, and platforms may face harassment, false reports, and trust breakdowns.

## Mitigations (practical, non-exhaustive)

- **Platform controls:** rapid takedown workflows for NCII, hash-sharing for known abusive content, and dedicated reporting channels.
- **Safety by design:** watermarking/provenance for generated media where feasible, plus friction on high-risk generation requests.
- **Victim support:** clear guidance on preservation of evidence, reporting, and mental-health resources.
- **Education:** public awareness that “photorealistic” does not imply “real,” and that sharing can be criminal.
