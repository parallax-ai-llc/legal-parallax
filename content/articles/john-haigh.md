## Identity Note
This entry refers to **John George Haigh** (1909–1949), a British serial killer sometimes nicknamed the **"Acid Bath Murderer"** in later media.

Because historical datasets and name variants occasionally split him into multiple records, you may also see him indexed as:
- John Haigh
- John G. Haigh
- John George Haigh

## High-Level Overview
- **Active period (commonly cited):** mid-to-late 1940s
- **Jurisdiction:** England (United Kingdom)
- **Known for:** financial-motive murders and attempts to conceal evidence using corrosive chemicals (a key detail behind the nickname)

## Early Life and Background (Brief)
- Born in 1909 in England.
- Worked in various roles across his life, including clerical and sales-related work.
- Developed a pattern of deception and fraud that intersected with later violent offenses.

## Modus Operandi (General)
- Targeted individuals he believed he could exploit financially.
- Used **confidence schemes** and forged documents to access victims’ money and property.
- Made calculated efforts to reduce discoverable evidence after the crimes.

## Investigation and Trial (Summary)
- The case gained significant public attention in post-war Britain.
- Haigh was arrested, tried, and **convicted of murder**.
- He was **executed in 1949**.

## Legacy and Cultural Impact
- Frequently referenced in British true-crime histories as an example of post-war sensational murder coverage.
- The "Acid Bath" label is widely repeated, though the underlying case facts are best understood via court reporting and archival sources.
