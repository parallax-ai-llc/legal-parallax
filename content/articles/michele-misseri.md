## Summary
The case of Sarah Scazzi — murdered by relatives in Avetrana, Italy in 2010 — became one of Italy's most followed murder cases. Michele Misseri confessed but his niece Sabrina Misseri and sister-in-law were convicted.

## Case Overview
- **Location**: Avetrana, province of Taranto (Apulia), southern Italy.
- **Victim**: Sarah Scazzi, a 15-year-old girl who disappeared in August 2010.
- **Why it mattered**: The investigation and trials became a major Italian media phenomenon, with intense coverage, televised debates, and prolonged public scrutiny.

## Role in the Case
Michele Misseri (Sarah's uncle by marriage) initially confessed to the killing and to hiding the body. Over time, he provided **multiple, conflicting accounts**, and prosecutors argued that responsibility lay with other relatives. His confessions, retractions, and allegations were central to the case narrative and to public controversy about reliability of testimony under pressure.

## Key People
- **Sabrina Misseri**: Sarah's cousin; ultimately convicted for murder.
- **Cosima Serrano**: Sabrina's mother and Michele Misseri's wife; convicted as an accomplice.
- **Investigators/Prosecution**: Focused on reconstructing the events and motivations, including alleged family conflict and concealment.

## Investigation & Trial Notes (High Level)
- **Discovery**: Misseri led investigators to the location of Sarah's remains after confessing.
- **Evidentiary focus**: Phone records, timelines, witness statements, and inconsistencies across statements were widely discussed.
- **Public debate**: The case raised questions in Italy about media influence on investigations, the treatment of suspects, and how courts weigh confession evidence when accounts shift.

## Legal Outcomes (Summary)
- **Misseri**: While he admitted to the killing at points, courts ultimately convicted him primarily for **related offenses** (e.g., concealment and false accusations), rather than as the sole murderer.
- **Sabrina Misseri & Cosima Serrano**: Convictions for murder/accomplice liability were upheld through appeals.

## Media and Cultural Impact
- One of the most talked-about Italian criminal cases of the 2010s.
- Became a reference point in discussions of **"trial by media"**, televised true-crime formats, and the psychological dynamics of interrogations and family-centered crimes.
