## Summary

Supporters of President Trump stormed the US Capitol building to prevent certification of the 2020 election. 7 people died; over 1,000 have been charged in the worst attack on the Capitol since 1814.

## Key Facts

- **Date**: January 6, 2021 (during the joint session of the U.S. Congress to certify Electoral College results).
- **Location**: United States Capitol, Washington, D.C.
- **Primary target**: The electoral vote count and certification process for the 2020 U.S. presidential election.
- **Immediate impact**: Evacuation and lockdown of congressional chambers; interruption of proceedings; extensive damage and injuries.

## Background and Context

- The attack occurred after weeks of false claims that the 2020 election had been “stolen.”
- A large rally and demonstrations in Washington, D.C., preceded the breach of the Capitol complex.
- Law enforcement and security preparations, intelligence warnings, and interagency coordination became major topics of subsequent review.

## Aftermath

- **Security**: Significant changes to Capitol security posture and coordination, including reviews of planning, perimeter control, and rapid-response capabilities.
- **Legal response**: A large federal investigation led to hundreds of arrests and prosecutions across a range of alleged offenses, from unlawful entry to assault and conspiracy.
- **Political response**: Congressional inquiries and public hearings examined causes, planning, and the roles of key actors, and compiled extensive documentary and testimonial records.

## Public and Historical Significance

- The event is widely described as one of the most serious assaults on U.S. democratic institutions in modern history.
- It influenced debates over political violence, misinformation, domestic extremism, and the resilience of constitutional processes.
- Ongoing court cases and public reporting continue to add detail to the historical record.
