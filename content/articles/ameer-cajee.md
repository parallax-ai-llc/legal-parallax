## AfriCrypt (context)

AfriCrypt was described in media reports as a South Africa–based cryptocurrency investment platform. It drew attention for marketing high-return crypto investing to retail clients during the 2020–2021 crypto bull market.

High-level characteristics commonly reported:

- **Asset class**: primarily Bitcoin (and potentially other cryptocurrencies).
- **Client base**: retail investors seeking exposure to crypto returns.
- **Risk profile**: custody and operational risk typical of lightly regulated or opaque crypto investment schemes.

## Allegations and 2021 disappearance

Public reporting and subsequent legal commentary generally describe the following sequence (details vary by source):

1. Clients were told AfriCrypt had suffered a security incident or hack.
2. Withdrawals and communication reportedly became difficult or stopped.
3. The founders were reported to be unreachable, and significant client funds were alleged to be missing.

Because many claims were disputed or unfolded through legal processes and investigative reporting, descriptions are often framed as **allegations** rather than adjudicated fact.

## Reported scale of losses (caution)

The figure often cited in coverage is **up to $3.6 billion** in Bitcoin, typically presented as an estimate based on Bitcoin prices at the time of reporting. Such numbers can be volatile because:

- Bitcoin’s price can swing significantly.
- Reported balances may mix on-chain estimates with client statements.
- Not all claimed client deposits are independently verifiable.

## Why the case is notable

- It became one of the most widely referenced crypto-investment collapse stories from South Africa.
- It is frequently used as a cautionary example for **custody risk** (handing control of private keys to a third party).
- It highlights how cross-border crypto flows and pseudonymous transfers can complicate recovery efforts.

## Key terms (plain language)

- **Custody**: who controls the private keys or access to the assets.
- **Investment platform vs. exchange**: marketing can blur the difference; platforms may not provide the same transparency as regulated exchanges.
- **On-chain tracing**: analyzing blockchain transactions to track movements of funds, which still may not identify the real-world controller.

## Practical takeaways for readers

- Prefer arrangements where you can verify custody (or retain control) rather than relying on opaque promises.
- Treat unusually consistent or high returns as a red flag, especially without audited reporting.
- Diversify counterparty exposure: avoid concentrating assets with a single unregulated operator.
