## Summary

The chewing stimulant khat is legal in some countries but banned in others. A massive trade from East Africa and Yemen generates billions while causing social and health problems.

## Context and Drivers

- **Plant and use**: Khat (Catha edulis) leaves are typically chewed for mild stimulant effects; freshness matters because the active compounds degrade after harvest.
- **Geography**: Major cultivation and export have been associated with parts of **Ethiopia, Kenya, Somalia**, and **Yemen**, with regional diaspora demand sustaining distribution networks.
- **Time-sensitive logistics**: The market often depends on rapid transport (road/air) to reach consumers while leaves are still potent.

## Trade Structure (High-Level)

- **Production**: Smallholder farms and commercial plots supply local brokers.
- **Aggregation**: Brokers consolidate, sort, and bundle leaves for shipment.
- **Transport**: Routes vary by country; in some markets, air cargo has been used to move perishable bundles quickly.
- **Wholesale/retail**: Community-based retail points and informal networks commonly handle last-mile distribution.

## Legal Status and Policy Variance

Khat sits in a legal gray zone internationally:

- **Legal/regulated in some jurisdictions** (often treated as a traditional stimulant).
- **Controlled/banned in others**, typically due to concerns about public health, dependency, or associated social harms.
- Policy changes (like the **UK ban in 2014**) can rapidly reshape routes, prices, and enforcement priorities.

## Economic and Social Impacts (Summary)

- **Income and employment**: Provides cash income for growers, traders, transporters, and retailers.
- **Household spending**: Regular use can divert disposable income from savings, food, or education in vulnerable households.
- **Productivity and health**: Reported concerns include sleep disruption, appetite suppression, and broader impacts from heavy use.
- **Community effects**: In some settings, khat sessions function as social gatherings; in others, they are criticized for reinforcing unemployment or isolation.

## Enforcement and Risk

- **Customs interdiction**: Because the product is bulky and time-sensitive, seizures can impose steep losses.
- **Substitution effects**: When routes are blocked, trafficking networks may shift to new corridors or methods.
- **Criminalization externalities**: Where banned, enforcement can increase the role of organized intermediaries and raise prices.

## Notes

This article describes the **trade ecosystem** around khat rather than a single individual or organization; it is often discussed as a transnational commodity flow shaped by law, logistics, and diaspora consumption.
