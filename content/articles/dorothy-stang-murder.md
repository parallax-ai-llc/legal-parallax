## Summary

American-born nun and environmental activist murdered by gunmen hired by ranchers in the Brazilian Amazon. She had fought for decades to protect the rainforest and landless farmers.

## Background

Dorothy Mae Stang (1931–2005) was an American-born Catholic religious sister (Sisters of Notre Dame de Namur) who spent decades working in Brazil and later became a naturalized Brazilian citizen. From the 1960s onward, she worked with rural communities in the Amazon region and became known for advocating for landless farmers and for rainforest conservation.

## Context

Her activism took place amid long-running land disputes and violence tied to illegal logging, cattle ranching expansion, and contested property claims in Pará state. Stang supported models of small-scale agriculture and forest-based livelihoods intended to reduce deforestation while protecting local families from displacement.

## The killing

Stang was shot and killed on 2005-02-12 near Anapu, Pará, while traveling to a community meeting. Reporting on the case commonly describes her as having received prior threats from landowners and loggers in the region.

## Investigation and legal proceedings (high level)

- The case drew international attention and pressure for accountability in Amazon-region land-conflict violence.
- Two gunmen were identified as having carried out the shooting; prosecutions also focused on alleged intermediaries and alleged financiers connected to local ranching interests.
- Court proceedings extended for years, including retrials and appeals reported in connection with some defendants.

## Why it matters

- Stang’s death became a widely cited example of the risks faced by environmental defenders and rural-rights advocates.
- The case is frequently referenced in discussions of governance, rule of law, and environmental protection in the Brazilian Amazon.
