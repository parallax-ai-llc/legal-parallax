## Summary

Former Stutthof concentration camp guard convicted at age 93 of accessory to 5,232 murders.

## Key Facts

- **Role**: Guard at the Stutthof concentration camp during World War II.[^1]
- **Legal characterization**: Convicted as an **accessory** (rather than as a direct perpetrator) to mass murder, reflecting the case theory that camp guards contributed to the camp’s killing system.[^1]
- **Scale of the conviction**: Accessory to **5,232 murders** (as stated in the conviction coverage summarized by Wikipedia).[^1]
- **Age at conviction**: Tried and convicted in his 90s, a prominent example of late-era Holocaust-related prosecutions in Germany.[^1]

## Historical Context: Stutthof Concentration Camp

- Stutthof was a Nazi concentration camp located near what is now Gdańsk (then in German-occupied territory).[^1]
- In many postwar prosecutions, the question is not only individual acts but also whether participation in the camp’s functioning constitutes criminal assistance to systematic killings.[^1]

## Trial and Conviction (High Level)

- **Jurisdiction/venue**: The case was heard in Hamburg (as summarized by Wikipedia).[^1]
- **Charge framing**: Prosecutors pursued liability for facilitating the camp’s lethal operation, not necessarily alleging personal commission of each killing.[^1]
- **Outcome**: Convicted in 2020.[^1]

## Why This Case Is Notable

- Illustrates Germany’s continued efforts to prosecute Nazi-era crimes decades after the events, especially where documentary and institutional evidence supports “accessory” theories for camp personnel.[^1]
- Highlights the historical and legal shift toward recognizing that participation in a concentration camp’s guard system can amount to criminal assistance in mass murder, even absent proof of specific individual killings by the accused.[^1]
