## Summary

Although celebrated, his African hospital maintained paternalistic colonial attitudes toward Africans.

Schweitzer was also known as a theologian, musician (notably an organist and Bach scholar), and physician whose public reputation was shaped by his humanitarian work in Gabon and his ethical philosophy of "reverence for life".

## Context and Contributions

- **Medicine and public health:** Schweitzer trained as a physician and is most widely associated with founding and operating a hospital at **Lambaréné**. The project became a major part of his public identity and fundraising efforts.
- **Theology and philosophy:** His writings include work in New Testament scholarship and the ethical idea often summarized as **"reverence for life"**.
- **Music scholarship:** He wrote about Johann Sebastian Bach and was active as an organist, helping shape early-20th-century Bach interpretation.

## Criticism and Controversy (High-Level)

- **Colonial paternalism:** Scholars and critics have argued that some of Schweitzer's practices and attitudes reflected the paternalistic norms of European colonialism, including views about African patients and local autonomy.
- **Institutional conditions:** Accounts of the hospital's organization, living arrangements, and decision-making have been debated, with later observers reassessing how humanitarian narratives can obscure unequal power dynamics.

## Legacy

- Celebrated internationally in the mid-20th century as a humanitarian and moral voice, including recognition through the **Nobel Peace Prize**.
- In contemporary discussions, often used as a case study in how **humanitarian work** can coexist with (or be shaped by) **colonial structures**.
