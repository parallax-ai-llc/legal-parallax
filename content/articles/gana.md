## Background

Terwase Akwaza—commonly known by the nickname “Gana”—was associated with armed violence in Nigeria’s Benue State, a region that has experienced recurring communal conflict, criminal banditry, and competition over land and security. Reporting and later summaries often describe his network as blending local grievances with opportunistic crime.

## Role and Allegations

Accounts generally characterize Gana as a militia leader linked to:

- armed attacks on communities in Benue State,
- kidnappings and extortion,
- targeted killings and intimidation.

Because local conflicts involve multiple armed groups and shifting alliances, public descriptions of responsibility and motive have frequently been contested.

## Death and Controversy

Gana’s killing in 2020 became a major political and security controversy. Public narratives emphasized that he was traveling under some form of military escort connected to a surrender process when he was killed, prompting questions about:

- whether surrender terms were credible or honored,
- chain-of-command responsibility for the killing,
- the impact on future demobilization or amnesty efforts.

## Significance

The episode is often used to illustrate the challenges of negotiating “surrenders” with armed actors in environments with weak trust between communities, local authorities, and security forces. It also highlights how ad hoc security arrangements can become politically explosive when outcomes are disputed.

## Context: Benue and the Middle Belt

Benue State sits within Nigeria’s “Middle Belt,” where clashes have often been reported around rural insecurity, cattle grazing routes, farming land, and local political competition. In this environment, labels such as “militia,” “bandit,” or “vigilante” can overlap in public discourse.

## Why the Case Drew Attention

- The reported surrender/escort circumstances made the death a test case for whether negotiated exits are credible.
- The episode fed public debate about extrajudicial force versus formal arrest and prosecution.
- It highlighted the risks of informal security deals when multiple agencies and political actors are involved.

## Terminology Note

Some sources treat “Gana” as a single commander; others describe a broader network operating under that name. Reporting can vary based on local politics and security narratives.
