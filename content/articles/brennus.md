## Summary

Gallic chieftain who sacked Rome and demanded ransom in gold, declaring 'Woe to the vanquished.'

## Historical Context

- **Who the Gauls were**: A collection of Celtic-speaking peoples in Western/Central Europe; in Roman sources, “Gauls” often refers to groups north of the Alps and in northern Italy (Cisalpine Gaul).
- **Northern Italy in the 5th–4th century BCE**: Celtic migrations and settlement in the Po Valley brought Gallic groups into repeated contact and conflict with Etruscans and the growing Roman Republic.
- **Ancient evidence caveat**: Details of Brennus’s life and the sack are largely mediated through later Roman narratives; modern historians treat some elements as legendary or stylized.

## The Sack of Rome (-390)

- **Trigger in Roman tradition**: Roman envoys allegedly violated diplomatic norms during a dispute involving the Etruscan city of Clusium, after which Gallic forces marched south.
- **Battle of the Allia**: Roman forces suffered a major defeat on the River Allia (traditionally dated to -390), opening the road to Rome.
- **Occupation and ransom**:
  - Romans held the Capitoline Hill while much of the city was overrun.
  - A payment in gold was negotiated to secure the Gauls’ withdrawal.
- **“Vae victis” (“Woe to the vanquished”)**: A famous anecdote claims Brennus threw his sword onto the scales to increase the ransom weight; it is widely cited as emblematic of victor’s power, though the scene itself is often treated as rhetorical.

## Significance and Legacy

- **Psychological impact on Rome**: The sack became a foundational trauma in Roman collective memory, shaping later rhetoric about vulnerability and resilience.
- **Military and civic reforms (ascribed)**: Roman tradition links the experience to subsequent changes in fortifications and military organization; specific reforms are debated, but the sack is consistently used as a narrative turning point.
- **Long-term Roman–Gallic antagonism**:
  - Later Roman campaigns in Cisalpine Gaul and beyond were sometimes justified by reference to past humiliation.
  - The episode remained a convenient moral lesson in Roman historiography about preparedness and discipline.

## Historiography Notes

- **Dating**: The year of the sack is commonly given as -390; alternative chronologies exist in scholarship due to uncertainties in early Roman dating.
- **Name and identity**: “Brennus” may function as a title or a name used across different Celtic leaders in ancient accounts; Roman writers also mention other figures named Brennus in later periods.
- **Source layering**: Surviving accounts are preserved in later historians (e.g., Livy and Plutarch) who wrote centuries after the events and relied on earlier annalists.

## Additional Reading

- Livy, *Ab Urbe Condita* (Book V) — traditional Roman narrative of the sack and its aftermath.
- Plutarch, *Life of Camillus* — later biographical framing that emphasizes Roman recovery.
