## Summary

Danske Bank's Estonian branch laundered $230 billion of suspicious money, mostly from Russia. The largest money laundering scandal in European history.

| Year | Event |

## What Happened (High Level)

- **Where**: Primarily **Danske Bank’s Estonian branch**.
- **When**: Large-scale suspicious flows are commonly reported as occurring **roughly 2007–2015**.
- **How**: Use of **non-resident customer accounts** and weak controls enabled movement of funds through the banking system.
- **Scale**: Investigations and internal reviews cited **~€200+ billion / ~$230 billion** in potentially suspicious transactions.

## Key People & Institutions

- **Danske Bank**: Denmark’s largest bank; parent institution responsible for group governance.
- **Estonian branch**: The operational center of the non-resident portfolio implicated in the case.
- **Regulators / investigators** (varied by jurisdiction): Danish and Estonian financial supervisors, police authorities, and later U.S. enforcement attention.
- **Whistleblower reporting**: Public coverage frequently references internal warnings that preceded the scandal’s full public exposure.

## Why It Mattered

- **European AML benchmark case**: Became a reference point for how cross-border banks can fail on **anti–money laundering (AML)** controls.
- **Correspondent-banking risk**: Highlighted the difficulty of monitoring flows that transit multiple banks and jurisdictions.
- **Governance lessons**: Showed the gap that can occur between **local branch operations** and **group-level oversight**.

## Consequences (Selected)

- **Leadership and reputational impact**: The scandal triggered high-profile resignations and sustained public scrutiny.
- **Regulatory actions**: Authorities pursued investigations and enforcement, pushing European AML standards and supervisory coordination.
- **Financial penalties / settlements**: The bank later faced large penalties and settlements in connection with AML control failures (details vary by forum and year).

## Signals & Red Flags (Generalizable)

- Large volumes from **opaque shell companies**.
- Heavy use of **non-resident accounts** without strong beneficial-ownership verification.
- **Repeated compliance warnings** not leading to effective remediation.
- Revenue incentives tied to high-volume customer portfolios with poor transparency.
