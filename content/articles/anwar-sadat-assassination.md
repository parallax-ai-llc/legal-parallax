## Summary

Egyptian President Anwar Sadat was assassinated during a military parade by Islamic extremist soldiers who opened fire from a passing truck. Killed for making peace with Israel.

## Background

- **Camp David Accords (1978)** and the **Egypt–Israel peace treaty (1979)** made Sadat a target for militant Islamist factions and some hardline nationalist opponents.
- Sadat pursued a state crackdown on opposition networks in the late 1970s and early 1980s, increasing tensions with Islamist groups.
- The attack occurred amid broader regional conflict dynamics following the 1973 Yom Kippur War and shifting Cold War alliances in the Middle East.

## The Assassination (6 October 1981)

- The assassination took place during an annual **Armed Forces Day** parade in Cairo commemorating the 1973 war.
- Assailants approached in a military vehicle, dismounted, and **opened fire** and threw grenades toward the reviewing stand.
- Sadat was mortally wounded; several others were killed or injured during the attack.

## Perpetrators and Motives

- The attackers were linked to **Egyptian Islamist extremist organizations**, including networks associated with **Egyptian Islamic Jihad**.
- A core stated motive was retaliation for Sadat’s normalization with Israel and opposition to his domestic policies.
- The operation exploited parade security assumptions that participating units and vehicles were vetted and controlled.

## Immediate Aftermath

- **Vice President Hosni Mubarak** survived and was later confirmed as Sadat’s successor.
- Egyptian authorities launched a large security response, including arrests and prosecutions of suspected conspirators.
- The assassination contributed to sustained emergency-style governance and intensified internal security measures.

## Historical Significance

- The event marked a turning point in modern Egyptian politics, reinforcing the central role of the security state.
- It underscored the volatility surrounding the Arab–Israeli peace process and the risks leaders faced for diplomatic realignment.
- Sadat’s killing became a frequently cited case study in political violence, insider threats, and ceremonial-event security.
