## Career Timeline

| Year | Event |
|------|-------|
| 1956 | Won the U.S. Junior Chess Championship at age 13; the "Game of the Century" vs. Donald Byrne drew international attention. |
| 1957–1958 | Won the U.S. Championship (first of eight U.S. titles). |
| 1958 | Qualified via the Interzonal and received the grandmaster title (then a record-young age for a GM). |
| 1962 | Won the Stockholm Interzonal; later reduced tournament participation for several years. |
| 1970–1971 | Returned to top play; won the Interzonal and then the Candidates matches, including two 6–0 sweeps (vs. Mark Taimanov and Bent Larsen) before defeating Tigran Petrosian. |
| 1972 | Defeated Boris Spassky in Reykjavík to become World Chess Champion. |
| 1975 | Did not defend the title against Anatoly Karpov after disputes over match conditions; title was forfeited. |
| 1992 | Played an unofficial rematch with Spassky in Yugoslavia, violating U.S. sanctions and triggering legal consequences. |
| 2004 | Detained in Japan on passport issues; later granted Icelandic citizenship. |
| 2008 | Died in Reykjavík, Iceland. |

## Playing Style and Contributions

- Known for deep opening preparation and a preference for sharp, unbalanced positions when they suited the objective position.
- Helped professionalize training expectations for elite players in the United States, with heavy emphasis on analysis and endgame technique.
- Associated in popular discussion with systems like the King's Indian Attack as White and fighting Sicilian defenses as Black, though his repertoire was broad.

## Public Controversies (High Level)

- Frequently clashed with organizers and chess authorities over conditions, scheduling, and prize funds.
- Later-life public statements drew widespread criticism and remain a major part of his public legacy apart from his chess results.

## Legacy

- Commonly cited as one of the strongest players of the 20th century; his run through the 1971 Candidates matches is often highlighted for its dominance.
- The 1972 championship match triggered a major surge of chess interest in the United States and beyond.
- His games remain widely studied for tactical calculation, practical endgame play, and conversion of small advantages.
