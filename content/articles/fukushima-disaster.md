## Summary

Earthquake and tsunami caused meltdowns at three Fukushima Daiichi reactors. TEPCO had ignored warnings about tsunami risks. 154,000 evacuated; cleanup will take decades.

## Context and Contributing Factors

- **Plant design basis:** Fukushima Daiichi was designed for a smaller tsunami than the one generated by the 2011 Tōhoku earthquake.
- **Station blackout vulnerability:** Flooding disabled offsite power and inundated backup diesel generators and electrical switchgear.
- **Hydrogen management:** Loss of cooling led to hydrogen production; accumulated hydrogen contributed to explosions in several reactor buildings.

## Immediate Response

- **Emergency declaration and evacuations:** Authorities expanded evacuation zones over the first days as conditions evolved.
- **Cooling improvisation:** Operators used seawater injection and ad‑hoc pumping to reduce core temperatures after normal systems failed.
- **Containment venting:** Venting was used to manage pressure, with releases of radioactive material as a consequence.

## Human and Environmental Impact (High-Level)

- **Displacement:** Large-scale, long-duration evacuation and relocation disrupted communities and livelihoods.
- **Contamination management:** Decontamination efforts focused on populated areas, waste handling, and controlling runoff.
- **Health outcomes:** Major public-health effects were driven by displacement stress and disruption, alongside long-term radiation monitoring.

## Accountability and Governance

- **Regulatory scrutiny:** Post-accident reviews criticized risk assessment, hazard communication, and preparedness.
- **Corporate responsibility:** The disaster became a case study in governance failures and the cost of underweighting low-probability, high-impact hazards.

## Cleanup and Decommissioning

- **Long horizon:** Decommissioning and fuel-debris retrieval are expected to take decades.
- **Water management:** Ongoing handling of contaminated water and storage capacity became a sustained operational challenge.
- **Site stabilization:** Priorities include reducing radiation exposure, maintaining cooling/stability, and safely dismantling structures.

## Key Takeaways

- **Defense-in-depth matters:** Diverse, protected power and cooling pathways reduce common-cause failure risk.
- **External hazards are not static:** Seismic/tsunami assumptions must be periodically revisited with updated science.
- **Crisis communication:** Clear, timely information is essential for public trust and effective protective actions.
