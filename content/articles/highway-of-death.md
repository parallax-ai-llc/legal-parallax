## Summary

US forces bombed retreating Iraqi military and civilian vehicles on Highway 80 from Kuwait, creating a miles-long scene of destruction. The proportionality of the attack was debated.

## Background and Context

- Occurred during the final phase of the 1991 Gulf War, after Iraq’s forces had occupied Kuwait and a US-led coalition launched air and ground operations to expel them.
- The term commonly refers to attacks on vehicles along Highway 80 (the main road between Kuwait City and Basra) and related routes used during the Iraqi withdrawal.
- Coalition forces had achieved air superiority, and attacks on moving columns were a feature of the broader interdiction campaign against Iraqi logistics and retreat routes.

## What Happened (High-Level)

- Retreating vehicles formed long convoys moving north from Kuwait toward Iraq.
- Coalition aircraft and other forces struck the columns, destroying or disabling many vehicles.
- Images of burned-out vehicles and casualties became widely circulated and were later used as shorthand for the scale of destruction.

## Who Was Involved

- **Coalition forces:** Primarily US-led air assets involved in attacks on the retreating columns.
- **Iraqi forces:** Elements of the Iraqi military withdrawing from Kuwait.
- **Civilians and non-combatants:** Reporting and later debate noted that some civilian vehicles or non-combatants may have been present among the traffic.

## Why It Became Controversial

- **Proportionality:** Critics questioned whether the level of force used against retreating units was proportionate, particularly if the convoy was not actively engaging.
- **Distinction:** Debate included whether all vehicles were legitimate military targets and how reliably combatants could be distinguished from civilians within the column.
- **Narrative impact:** The event became part of post-war discussions about rules of engagement, airpower, and the ethics of striking retreating forces.

## Aftermath and Legacy

- The phrase “Highway of Death” entered popular and media usage as a symbol of the Gulf War’s destructive imagery.
- The incident has been referenced in later discussions of air campaigns, retreat interdiction, and the interpretation of international humanitarian law principles.

## Notes on Terminology

- “Highway of Death” is a journalistic/popular label rather than an official operation name.
- Some accounts distinguish between Highway 80 and adjacent routes; usage varies across sources.
