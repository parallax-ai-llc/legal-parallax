## Summary

Leaked documents revealed major banks moved $2 trillion in suspicious transactions despite knowing the money was potentially dirty. Banks including HSBC and Deutsche Bank were implicated.

## What Was Leaked

The FinCEN Files are based on a cache of U.S. Treasury Department Financial Crimes Enforcement Network (FinCEN) records, centered on **Suspicious Activity Reports (SARs)** filed by banks and other financial institutions.

- **SARs** are regulatory reports submitted when an institution detects potentially illicit activity.
- A SAR is an alert for compliance and law-enforcement follow-up; it is not, by itself, a criminal conviction.

## Key Themes Reported by Investigations

High-level themes described by the reporting consortium included:

- Large transaction volumes flagged as suspicious moving through the financial system over multiple years.
- Repeated patterns where institutions identified risk indicators but transactions still continued.
- Cross-border flows involving shell companies and opaque beneficial ownership structures.

## Why It Mattered

The reporting drew attention to the gap between:

- **Detection** (banks’ internal monitoring and SAR filing), and
- **Prevention/Disruption** (stopping transactions, freezing assets, or enabling timely enforcement action).

It also renewed scrutiny of anti-money-laundering (AML) enforcement capacity and information-sharing constraints.

## Actors Commonly Discussed

While the underlying documents were FinCEN-related, the public story focused on how global finance routes payments and clears transactions.

Commonly referenced actors and concepts include:

- Major international banks (as intermediaries, correspondents, or clearing institutions)
- Corporate registries and beneficial ownership records
- Professional enablers (e.g., company formation services) in some typologies

## Aftermath and Policy Discussion

Public debate following publication commonly referenced:

- Strengthening beneficial ownership transparency requirements
- Improving AML supervision and penalties for repeat failures
- Better resourcing for financial intelligence and cross-border cooperation
