## Background

- Born JoAnne Deborah Byron (later Assata Olugbala Shakur) in New York City; raised in part in the U.S. South.
- Became politically active amid the civil-rights and Black Power movements of the 1960s–1970s.

## Political Activity

- Associated with organizations including the Black Panther Party and later the Black Liberation Army (BLA), according to widely reported accounts.
- Her public image has remained contested: supporters frame her as a political prisoner and activist; U.S. authorities describe her as a convicted murderer and fugitive.

## Key Legal Case (High Level)

- In 1973, Shakur was involved in a New Jersey Turnpike incident that resulted in the deaths of a New Jersey state trooper and a BLA member.
- She was tried on multiple charges across several cases; in 1977 she was convicted in New Jersey for the trooper’s murder.
- The case and trial conditions have been debated for decades in journalism and scholarship.

## Escape and Exile

- Shakur escaped from prison in 1979 and went into hiding.
- She later received political asylum in Cuba, where she has lived as a fugitive from the United States.

## Public and Government Actions

- In 2013, the FBI placed Shakur on its Most Wanted Terrorists list and announced a reward for information leading to her capture.
- U.S.–Cuba relations and diplomatic shifts have periodically renewed attention to her status.

## Legacy

- Became an enduring symbol within some activist circles, especially around issues of policing, incarceration, and racial justice.
- Also remains a polarizing figure in U.S. public discourse, cited by critics as emblematic of domestic political violence in the 1970s.

## Names and Terminology

- Birth name: **JoAnne Deborah Byron**; later used **Assata Olugbala Shakur**.
- In many sources, she is described as a former Black Panther and an alleged or confirmed member/associate of the **Black Liberation Army (BLA)**; descriptions vary by author and the evidentiary standard applied.
