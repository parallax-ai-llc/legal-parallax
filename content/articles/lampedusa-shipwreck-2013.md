## Summary

A boat carrying 500 Eritrean and Somali migrants sank near Lampedusa, Italy, killing 368 people. One of the deadliest Mediterranean shipwrecks; smugglers had overloaded the vessel.

## Context

- The Central Mediterranean route has been one of the most dangerous migration corridors into Europe, involving long sea crossings on overcrowded and often unseaworthy boats.
- Lampedusa, a small Italian island closer to North Africa than mainland Italy, has long been a frontline destination for sea rescues and disembarkations.

## What Happened (High-Level)

- On 3 October 2013, a migrant boat carrying hundreds of people sank close to Lampedusa after a fire was lit on board (reportedly as a distress signal) and the vessel capsized.
- The disaster resulted in hundreds of deaths and became a defining tragedy in public awareness of Mediterranean migration risks.

## Immediate Response

- Local residents, fishermen, and Italian authorities participated in rescue efforts; survivors were taken to Lampedusa for emergency assistance.
- The scale of loss triggered official mourning and a major public funeral and memorial response in Italy.

## Aftermath and Significance

- The shipwreck intensified scrutiny of human smuggling networks that profit from unsafe crossings.
- It also fueled political and policy debates in Italy and across the European Union about search-and-rescue capacity, border management, and responsibility-sharing.
- The event is frequently cited as a turning point that contributed to later expansions of maritime rescue operations and wider debate over Europe’s migration governance.
