## Summary

Greek traitor who showed the Persians the secret mountain path at Thermopylae, dooming the Spartan defenders.

## Historical Context

- **Greco-Persian Wars**: Ephialtes is associated with the Persian invasion of Greece under Xerxes I (commonly dated to 480 BCE), when a coalition of Greek city-states attempted to block the Persian advance.
- **Thermopylae as a bottleneck**: The pass of Thermopylae was strategically valuable because its narrow terrain limited the effectiveness of a much larger army.
- **Anopaea path**: Ancient accounts describe a mountain track that could outflank the main pass; Ephialtes is remembered for revealing this route to Persian forces.

## Role at Thermopylae

- **Nature of the betrayal**: The core claim is that Ephialtes provided intelligence—guidance to the path—rather than commanding troops or fighting directly.
- **Operational effect**: By enabling a flanking movement, the defenders’ position became untenable, contributing to the last stand associated with Leonidas and a small rearguard.
- **Attribution in sources**: Ephialtes is often singled out by name in later retellings, making him a symbolic figure for treachery in the Thermopylae narrative.

## Aftermath and Reputation

- **Greek response**: Greek sources report that Ephialtes was condemned for his actions; later tradition treats his name as synonymous with betrayal.
- **Death**: His reported death is sometimes described as occurring later and not necessarily as a direct result of Thermopylae itself; details vary by account.
- **Cultural legacy**: Modern popular culture frequently reuses the Thermopylae story, and Ephialtes is regularly cast as the archetypal traitor who “sold out” the defenders.

## Notes

- Dating for individuals from antiquity is often approximate; the commonly cited event anchoring Ephialtes in history is the **Battle of Thermopylae (480 BCE)**.
- The mountain route is sometimes referred to as a “secret path,” though in practical terms it is best understood as a lesser-known track that local knowledge could exploit.
- In Herodotus’ *Histories*, Ephialtes is named as the informant who guided the Persians via the path—one of the key ancient narrative sources for the episode.
