## Summary

Multiple buildings constructed by Hyundai and other Korean construction companies have suffered structural failures due to cutting corners on materials. Several building collapses caused deaths.

## Background

- The term is commonly used as a catch-all label for a series of high-profile construction and demolition safety incidents in South Korea involving Hyundai-branded construction/development firms and subcontractors.
- Reporting around the incidents frequently emphasizes systemic risk factors: layered subcontracting, weak on-site supervision, schedule pressure, and inadequate safety controls.

## What the “scandal” refers to

- Multiple incidents where buildings or building elements collapsed (during demolition or construction), resulting in fatalities and injuries.
- Public controversy focused on whether corners were cut in safety procedures, materials handling, structural support, or site management.

## Notable incidents (high level)

- **Gwangju demolition collapse (2021):** A building being demolished collapsed, causing multiple deaths and injuries.
- **Gwangju apartment construction collapse (2022):** A collapse at an apartment construction site caused deaths, triggered investigations, and intensified scrutiny of safety governance.

## Typical allegations and compliance issues discussed in coverage

- Insufficient temporary supports and safety barriers.
- Inadequate monitoring of subcontractor work and site-wide risk controls.
- Documentation/inspection shortcomings (e.g., whether required checks were performed and recorded appropriately).

## Impact

- Criminal and administrative investigations (including arrests/indictments in some related cases, depending on jurisdiction and facts).
- Reputational damage to major developers/contractors and increased public distrust of construction safety.
- Regulatory and industry pressure to strengthen enforcement, supervision, and accountability across contracting chains.

## Additional context

- South Korea has seen recurring public debates about construction safety following major collapses, with calls for stronger penalties and clearer responsibility for executives and prime contractors.
- The incidents are often cited in discussions of how large-scale development projects manage risk, quality assurance, and worker/public safety.
