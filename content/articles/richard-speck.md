## Summary
Richard Speck murdered eight student nurses in a Chicago townhouse on July 14, 1966. He was sentenced to death, later commuted to 400-1200 years. He died in prison.

## Context and Background
- Speck was an American drifter with a history of unstable employment and repeated arrests prior to 1966.
- In mid-1966 he was living in Chicago and moving through low-wage work and transient lodging.

## The 1966 Chicago Murders
- On **July 14, 1966**, Speck entered a townhouse used as a residence for student nurses.
- He held multiple victims at gunpoint and carried out the killings over the course of the night.
- One nurse, **Corazon Amurao**, survived by hiding and later provided key identifying information to police.

## Investigation and Arrest
- The case drew intense public attention and a large-scale police response.
- Speck was arrested shortly after the murders; physical evidence and witness identification linked him to the scene.

## Trial and Sentencing
- Speck was convicted in 1967 and initially sentenced to death.
- After U.S. Supreme Court rulings temporarily invalidated existing death-penalty statutes, his sentence was **commuted**.
- Courts replaced the death sentence with an extremely long term commonly reported as **400–1200 years**.

## Incarceration and Death
- Speck spent the remainder of his life in prison.
- He died in custody in **1991**.

## Historical Significance
- The murders became one of the most notorious mass killings in modern Chicago history.
- The case is frequently cited in discussions of:
  - mass-casualty violence in urban settings,
  - criminal investigation under public pressure,
  - and sentencing outcomes affected by broader constitutional changes.
