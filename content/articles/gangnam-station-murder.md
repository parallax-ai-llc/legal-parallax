## Summary

A 34-year-old man stabbed a 23-year-old woman to death in a public bathroom near Gangnam Station. The random killing sparked a national conversation about misogyny in South Korea.

## Context

- The killing occurred in the area around Gangnam Station, one of Seoul’s busiest transit hubs.
- It was widely framed in public discourse as a gendered violence case, and it accelerated an already-growing debate about misogyny and everyday safety for women in South Korea.

## What Happened (High-Level)

- A woman was attacked and killed in a public restroom near the station.
- The incident was described by many observers as a “random” attack because the victim and perpetrator were not known to each other.
- The case rapidly spread through social media and news coverage, leading to large public gatherings and memorials.

## Public Response and Social Impact

- Vigils and memorials formed quickly, including notes and flowers placed in public spaces near the station.
- The case became a reference point in Korean discussions about:
  - misogyny and online/offline harassment
  - fear of public spaces and “everyday” violence
  - how law enforcement and courts should interpret motive in gendered violence
- Counter-reactions also emerged, reflecting polarization around gender politics and the language used to describe the crime.

## Why It Matters

- The incident is often cited as a moment when the topic of misogyny moved from online debate into sustained, mainstream public conversation.
- It influenced how many people—especially young women—talked about personal safety, public facilities, and urban life.
