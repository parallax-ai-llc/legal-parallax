## Summary

J&J knew for decades that its baby powder contained asbestos but concealed the information. Thousands of women developed ovarian cancer; the company faces $8.9 billion in settlements.

## What the scandal is about (high level)

- **Central claim:** Some talc-based products (most prominently Johnson’s Baby Powder) were alleged to be contaminated with **asbestos**, a known carcinogen.
- **Why asbestos matters:** Asbestos exposure has long been linked to diseases such as **mesothelioma**; plaintiffs also alleged links to **ovarian cancer**.
- **Core dispute:** Whether J&J’s talc contained asbestos, whether any contamination was detectable/known, and whether product warnings were adequate.

## Background: talc and contamination risk

- **Talc** is a mineral used for moisture absorption and friction reduction; cosmetic-grade talc is used in powders.
- Some talc deposits occur near **asbestos-forming minerals**, creating a contamination risk unless mining and processing controls prevent it.
- Litigation often focused on **historical testing**, internal records, and how different test methods detect (or miss) trace asbestos.

## Major litigation themes

- **Internal documents and knowledge:** Reporting has highlighted allegations that company records showed awareness of contamination concerns at certain points in time.
- **Scientific evidence:** Cases commonly featured dueling experts on exposure pathways, dose, and the reliability of various analytical methods.
- **Labeling and consumer warnings:** Plaintiffs argued that consumers were not warned about potential risks; J&J disputed the need for such warnings.

## Timeline (additional context)

- **Pre-2018:** Talc safety questions and product-liability lawsuits accumulated over time, with increasing media and legal attention.
- **2018:** A major investigative report helped elevate the issue in public view and intensified scrutiny.
- **2020s:** The dispute expanded into broader settlement talks, corporate restructuring strategies in litigation, and continuing appellate activity in some jurisdictions.

## Settlements and business response (overview)

- J&J has publicly stated that its talc products were safe and that testing showed no asbestos; plaintiffs and some juries reached different conclusions in individual cases.
- The company ultimately moved away from talc-based baby powder in several markets and later announced broader transitions to **cornstarch-based** formulations.
- Large settlement figures have been reported in connection with resolving categories of claims; terms and coverage can vary by claimant group and jurisdiction.

## Why this case is studied

- **Product safety governance:** How companies monitor upstream material risks (mining, suppliers, testing standards) and communicate uncertainty.
- **Litigation strategy:** Mass-tort dynamics, bellwether trials, and how settlement frameworks are negotiated at scale.
- **Public trust:** A widely used household product becoming associated with severe health outcomes created lasting reputational consequences.

## Key terms

- **Asbestos:** A group of fibrous silicate minerals; inhalation exposure is strongly linked to mesothelioma and other diseases.
- **Mesothelioma:** Cancer often associated with asbestos exposure; frequently cited in asbestos-related product cases.
- **Mass tort:** Litigation involving many plaintiffs with similar claims against one or more defendants.
- **Cosmetic talc:** Talc used in consumer products; regulated differently than industrial asbestos materials.
