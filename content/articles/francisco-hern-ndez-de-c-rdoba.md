## Additional Context

### Identity and Disambiguation

- This article refers to **Francisco Hernández de Córdoba (c. 1475–1526)**, the conquistador associated with the Spanish conquest of Nicaragua.
- He is **not** the same person as the earlier **Francisco Hernández de Córdoba (died 1517)** linked to the 1517 expedition to the Yucatán Peninsula.

### Role in the Conquest of Nicaragua

- In the mid-1520s, Hernández de Córdoba participated in Spanish campaigns in Central America and became a leading figure in the early Spanish presence in what is now Nicaragua.
- He is commonly credited with founding Spanish colonial towns in the region, including **León** and **Granada** (both founded in **1524**, per standard historical accounts).
- Contemporary and later sources describe violence, coercion, and severe exploitation of Indigenous communities as part of the conquest and early colonial administration.

### Conflict with Spanish Authorities and Death

- Power struggles among Spanish leaders in the region were frequent, including disputes over authority, tribute, and control of settlements.
- Hernández de Córdoba came into conflict with **Pedrarias Dávila** (governor of Castilla del Oro / Panama), who asserted authority over the Nicaraguan settlements.
- He was arrested and **executed in 1526** (often described as beheading), a result of these internal colonial rivalries.

### Historical Assessment

- Modern historical writing typically treats Hernández de Córdoba as an early organizer of Spanish colonial control in Nicaragua while also emphasizing the destructive effects of conquest on Indigenous societies.
- In Nicaraguan national historiography, he is often mentioned in connection with early urban foundations (León and Granada), alongside critical attention to colonial violence.
