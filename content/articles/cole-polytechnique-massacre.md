## Summary

Marc Lépine killed 14 women at Montreal's École Polytechnique, specifically targeting female engineering students. Led to Canada's gun control legislation and National Day of Remembrance.

## Background and Context

- The attack occurred in Montreal, Quebec, on 6 December 1989 at the École Polytechnique (now Polytechnique Montréal), an engineering school affiliated with Université de Montréal.
- It is widely discussed in Canada in connection with misogyny and violence against women, and it had a lasting impact on public debate about firearms and public safety.

## Key Facts (High-Level)

- **Location:** Montreal, Quebec, Canada.
- **Date:** 1989-12-06.
- **Victims:** 14 women were killed; others were injured. The perpetrator died at the scene.
- **Targeting:** Women (including female engineering students) were explicitly targeted.

## Public Impact and Aftermath

- The massacre became a reference point in Canadian discussions on gun control, including subsequent legislative and regulatory changes in the decades that followed.
- It also influenced how institutions and the broader public in Canada talk about gender-based violence, campus safety, and prevention.

## Memorialization

- **National Day of Remembrance and Action on Violence Against Women:** Observed annually in Canada on December 6.
- Polytechnique Montréal and other institutions hold commemorations and educational initiatives tied to the event.
