## Summary

Duplicate consolidated.

Muammar Muhammad Abu Minyar al-Gaddafi (often rendered in English as Muammar Gaddafi) ruled Libya from 1969 until 2011.
He came to power after a military coup that overthrew King Idris I, and he governed through a mix of revolutionary committees, security services, and patronage networks rather than conventional party politics.
Gaddafi promoted a political ideology he called the “Third International Theory,” outlined in *The Green Book*, and presented Libya as a “Jamahiriya” (state of the masses).
His rule was marked by authoritarian repression, a highly personalized state, and periods of confrontation and later partial accommodation with Western governments.
In 2011, amid the Arab Spring, an uprising escalated into civil war and a NATO-backed intervention; Gaddafi was captured and killed after the fall of Sirte.

## Ideology and Governing Model

- Gaddafi’s “Third International Theory” rejected both Western liberal democracy and Soviet-style communism, proposing a form of direct popular rule.
- The ideas were presented in *The Green Book*, which argued for governance through people’s congresses and committees.
- In practice, decision-making power concentrated around Gaddafi, loyalists, and security institutions, with limited avenues for genuine opposition.

## Domestic Rule (High-Level)

- Libya’s oil revenues funded major state spending, including infrastructure and social programs, while also reinforcing centralized control.
- Political dissent was severely constrained, with repression of opponents at home and, according to multiple accounts, intimidation of dissidents abroad.
- Governance structures shifted repeatedly, but the state remained highly personalized, with key levers of power tied to Gaddafi’s circle.

## Foreign Policy (High-Level)

- Libya pursued pan-Arab and later pan-African initiatives, including attempts at regional influence and union projects.
- The country’s relations with Western states fluctuated sharply: periods of confrontation in the 1970s–1980s were followed by partial normalization in the 2000s.
- Libya faced isolation due to allegations of supporting militant groups and terrorism, then later re-engaged diplomatically after policy reversals on WMD and compensation-related settlements.

## 2011 Uprising and Death

- Protests in early 2011 escalated into armed conflict as the government responded with force and opposition groups organized militarily.
- The UN Security Council authorized measures related to civilian protection; NATO conducted an air campaign in support of that mandate.
- After the fall of Tripoli and continued fighting, Gaddafi was captured near Sirte and killed on 2011-10-20.

## Legacy and Debates

- Supporters have cited state capacity, anti-colonial rhetoric, and public spending during parts of his rule.
- Critics emphasize authoritarianism, human-rights abuses, corruption, and the long-term institutional fragility created by personalized governance.
- Assessments of his impact often hinge on comparisons between pre-2011 state stability and the prolonged instability that followed his removal.
