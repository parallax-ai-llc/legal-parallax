## Summary

French aristocrat, diplomat, and writer best known for arguing that human history is driven by "racial" hierarchy and degeneration. His work helped popularize pseudo-scientific racial thinking in 19th-century Europe and was later cited by racist and antisemitic movements, including strands of Nazi ideology, though his own views were not identical to later Nazi doctrine.

## Background

- Born Joseph Arthur, Comte de Gobineau, into a French aristocratic milieu.
- Worked as a diplomat as well as a prolific author (essays, novels, travel writing).
- His intellectual project mixed romantic historiography with claims of biological determinism.

## Major Works

- *Essai sur l'inégalité des races humaines* (1853–1855): central text asserting a hierarchy of "races" and portraying "racial mixing" as historical decline.
- Later literary works (novels and short fiction) that reflected his broader cultural pessimism and fascination with "origins" and civilizational decay.

## Core Ideas (High-Level)

- "Race" as destiny: claimed that inherited traits explain cultural achievement and political outcomes.
- Degeneration narrative: argued that societies decline through "mixing" and loss of an imagined "pure" elite.
- Selective history: drew on sweeping, speculative interpretations of ancient and modern history rather than empirical anthropology.
- Pseudo-science: relied on the 19th-century fashion for classificatory "science" that later scholarship rejected.

## Influence and Reception

- Read by some European elites during a period when colonial empires sought ideological justification.
- His ideas fed into later racialist and eugenic currents (often via reinterpretation and cherry-picking).
- In the late 19th and early 20th centuries, parts of his work were taken up by nationalist and racist ideologues.
- Modern historians treat his arguments as a key example of the intellectual history of scientific racism.

## Criticism and Legacy

- Rejected by contemporary anthropology and genetics: "race" categories in his sense are not scientifically valid.
- Notable for how literary style and grand historical storytelling made harmful claims seem plausible to readers.
- Studied today mainly to understand how racism was rationalized in elite European thought.
