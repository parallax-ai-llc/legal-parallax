## Summary

Sikh extremists bombed Air India Flight 182 over the Atlantic, killing all 329 people aboard. Deadliest aviation terrorism before 9/11 and Canada's worst mass murder.

## Background

- The bombing is widely linked to Sikh-Canadian extremist militancy that emerged in the 1980s, including elements advocating for an independent Sikh state (Khalistan).
- In the same time period, India experienced significant political violence, including the 1984 assassination of Prime Minister Indira Gandhi and subsequent unrest.

## What Happened

- Air India Flight 182 (a Boeing 747) was flying from Canada to India, with a stop in Europe, when an explosive device detonated in flight.
- The aircraft broke apart and crashed into the Atlantic Ocean off the coast of Ireland.
- All 329 passengers and crew were killed.

## Related Incident (Same Day)

- A separate explosive device connected to the plot detonated at Narita International Airport in Japan.
- Two baggage handlers were killed in that blast.

## Investigation and Prosecution (High Level)

- The case involved a long, complex investigation spanning Canada and multiple international jurisdictions.
- Prosecutions in Canada unfolded over many years; convictions were limited compared to the scale of the attack.
- The event is often cited in discussions of investigative failures and the challenges of counterterrorism coordination.

## Impact and Legacy

- Long regarded as the deadliest act of aviation terrorism prior to the September 11 attacks.
- Often described as Canada’s worst mass murder because most victims had strong ties to Canada.
- Led to major public inquiries and reforms touching aviation security, intelligence sharing, and policing practices.

## Key Facts

- **Date:** 1985-06-23
- **Fatalities:** 329 on Flight 182 (plus 2 at Narita from a related device)
- **Location:** Atlantic Ocean (off Ireland)
- **Type:** Aircraft bombing / mass-casualty terrorist attack

## Why It Matters (Context)

This bombing remains a reference point for Canada’s modern approach to terrorism and public safety—both for the scale of loss and for the long-running debates about prevention, surveillance, and accountability.
