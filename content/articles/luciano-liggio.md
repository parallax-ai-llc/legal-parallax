## Summary
Luciano Liggio was the boss of the Corleonesi clan in Sicily. He was the mentor of Totò Riina and Bernardo Provenzano. Despite multiple arrests, he continued to wield power from prison.

## Expanded Context
- **Organization**: Liggio led the Corleonesi faction within the Sicilian Mafia (Cosa Nostra), originating from Corleone in the province of Palermo.
- **Role in succession**: He is widely described as a key mentor and predecessor to later Corleonesi leaders **Salvatore (Totò) Riina** and **Bernardo Provenzano**.
- **Operational style (high level)**: Accounts emphasize a shift toward more disciplined, violent enforcement and a stronger internal command structure compared to earlier rural power brokers.

## Key Events (High-Level Timeline)
- **1925** — Born in Corleone, Sicily.
- **Post–World War II** — Emerges in local criminal networks tied to rural protection and the postwar black market economy.
- **Late 1950s–early 1960s** — Consolidates influence in the Corleonesi; linked in open sources to lethal internal disputes and power struggles.
- **1960s** — Expands reach from a local base toward Palermo-centered Mafia politics, increasingly intersecting with broader Cosa Nostra leadership.
- **1970s** — Arrests and prosecutions intensify; nevertheless, the Corleonesi line of succession continues to grow.
- **Prison period** — Reported to retain influence through intermediaries, with trusted associates acting externally.
- **1993** — Dies while incarcerated.

## Influence and Legacy
- **Corleonesi ascendancy**: Liggio is often treated as an early architect of the Corleonesi rise that later culminated in their dominance over Sicilian Mafia structures.
- **Mentorship narrative**: Biographical treatments frequently frame him as a formative influence on Riina and Provenzano’s later methods.
- **Historical significance**: His career is used as a case study for how Mafia leadership can persist despite imprisonment, via delegation and clandestine communications.
