## Summary

Over 500,000 migrants crossed the treacherous Darién Gap jungle between Colombia and Panama in 2023 alone. Many are robbed, raped, or die in the crossing controlled by criminal groups.

## Context and Geography

- The **Darién Gap** is a roadless, swamp-and-jungle region between **northern Colombia** and **eastern Panama**, forming a break in the Pan-American Highway.
- Most northbound migrants enter Panama near border communities and then move toward official reception points where Panamanian authorities and aid groups provide initial processing and humanitarian support.

## Why the Route Became Prominent

- The route is used by people fleeing **violence, political repression, and economic collapse**, as well as those seeking family reunification or asylum pathways in North America.
- Migrant nationalities have included people from **Venezuela** (a major share in many recent years) as well as people from Haiti, Ecuador, Colombia, and extra-hemispheric countries.

## Risks and Humanitarian Concerns

- Key dangers include:
  - **Drowning** during river crossings and flash floods.
  - **Injuries** (falls, fractures, infections) compounded by limited medical access.
  - **Robbery and extortion**, often involving armed criminal actors.
  - **Sexual violence** and exploitation, particularly affecting women and children.
  - **Family separation** and missing persons due to terrain, illness, or criminal threats.
- The journey can take multiple days and is especially hazardous during the rainy season.

## Actors and Governance

- The area has seen activity by **criminal networks** and armed groups on both sides of the border, contributing to high risk and limited state control.
- Panama has periodically adjusted its reception-and-transit policies (registration, transport, and movement restrictions) in response to changing flows.
