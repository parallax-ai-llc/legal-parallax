## Summary
Ian Brady with Myra Hindley committed the Moors murders. They kidnapped, sexually assaulted, and murdered five children, burying some on Saddleworth Moor. He died in Ashworth Hospital.

## Background
- Born in Glasgow, Scotland; spent periods in foster care and institutions in childhood.[^2]
- Moved to England as a young adult and worked in clerical/office roles.[^2]
- Met Myra Hindley in the early 1960s; they began a relationship that became central to the later crimes.[^2]

## Moors Murders (high-level)
- The "Moors murders" refers to a series of killings of children and teenagers in and around Manchester in the early-to-mid 1960s.[^2]
- Victims were abducted and killed; several bodies were concealed on Saddleworth Moor, leading to the enduring name of the case.[^2]
- The case became one of the most notorious criminal investigations in modern British history due to the victims' ages and the prolonged search for remains.[^2]

## Investigation and Trial
- Brady and Hindley were arrested in 1965 after evidence and witness accounts linked them to the crimes.[^2]
- They were tried and convicted in 1966; both received life sentences.[^2]
- Over time, further admissions and evidence clarified that there were five victims attributed to the pair, with ongoing efforts to locate remaining burial sites.[^2]

## Incarceration and Later Years
- Brady spent decades in prison custody and later in a high-security psychiatric hospital setting (including Ashworth Hospital).[^2]
- He made periodic statements and admissions that affected searches and public understanding of the case, while also generating controversy about reliability and motives.[^2]
- Died in 2017 while in institutional custody.[^2]

## Public and Historical Impact
- The Moors murders influenced UK public debate on child protection, policing, sentencing, and media ethics around reporting on crimes involving minors.[^2]
- The case remains a reference point in discussions of how societies remember victims, handle long-term investigations, and balance public interest with retraumatization risks.[^2]
