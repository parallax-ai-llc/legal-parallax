## Summary

A coal tip collapsed onto a school in Aberfan, Wales, killing 116 children and 28 adults. The National Coal Board had ignored warnings about the tip's instability for years.

## Background

- **Location**: Aberfan, a village near Merthyr Tydfil in South Wales.
- **Industry context**: Coal mining dominated local employment; waste material from mines was routinely piled into large spoil tips on hillsides above communities.
- **Tip location**: The spoil tip that failed was positioned on a slope above the village, in an area with known springs and wet ground.

## What Happened (1966-10-21)

- After days of heavy rain, a section of the spoil tip liquefied and **slid downhill as a fast-moving flow of coal slurry**.
- The slide struck the village, **engulfing Pantglas Junior School** and nearby houses.
- **Casualties**: 116 children and 28 adults were killed.

## Contributing Factors

- **Siting and design failures**: Tips were placed on unstable, waterlogged ground without adequate engineering assessment.
- **Ignored warnings**: Local concerns about tip safety had been raised prior to the disaster.
- **Water management**: Inadequate drainage and monitoring increased the risk of saturation and instability.

## Response and Investigation

- Rescue efforts began immediately, with local residents, miners, and emergency services attempting to dig through slurry and debris.
- A formal inquiry later concluded that the disaster was **avoidable** and resulted from **systemic negligence** in tip management.
- The National Coal Board (NCB) faced severe criticism for governance failures, safety culture, and accountability gaps.

## Aftermath and Legacy

- The disaster became a major turning point in the UK’s approach to industrial safety and the management of mining waste.
- Aberfan is remembered through memorials, anniversaries, and ongoing community commemoration.
- The event remains a widely cited example of how **risk normalization** and **institutional complacency** can lead to catastrophic harm.

## Key Takeaways

- Safety-critical infrastructure requires clear ownership, routine inspection, and transparent reporting.
- Community warnings and frontline observations should be treated as valuable safety signals, not noise.
- Engineering decisions (like waste-tip placement and drainage) can become public-safety decisions when communities lie downstream.
