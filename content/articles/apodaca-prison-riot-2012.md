## Summary

Los Zetas cartel members slaughtered 44 rival Gulf Cartel inmates at Apodaca Prison using hammers, rocks, and shivs. Guards colluded in the massacre and allowed attackers to escape.

## Background

- Apodaca Prison (Centro de Readaptación Social de Apodaca) is a state facility in Nuevo León, near Monterrey.
- In the early 2010s, Mexican prisons in several states were widely reported to be vulnerable to corruption, contraband, and de facto control by organized-crime groups.
- Los Zetas and the Gulf Cartel were in open conflict during this period, and their rivalry often extended into detention facilities.

## What Happened (High-Level)

- On 19 February 2012, violence broke out inside the prison and escalated into a targeted attack on rival inmates.
- Reporting described attackers using improvised weapons (including hammers, stones, and shanks).
- Accounts indicated that certain prison staff enabled the assault and that security failures allowed the incident to unfold.
- The incident was accompanied by an escape; a number of prisoners left the facility during or immediately after the violence.

## Immediate Consequences

- Authorities reported dozens of deaths (commonly cited as 44).
- The prison leadership and guards came under scrutiny for alleged collusion and negligence.
- The episode became part of a broader public debate over prison governance, cartel influence, and the integrity of local security institutions.

## Significance

- Illustrates how cartel rivalries can manifest inside state custody and produce mass-casualty events.
- Frequently cited as an example of the risks created when prisons become porous to organized-crime control.
- Contributed to calls for reforms in oversight, staffing, and anti-corruption mechanisms within Mexican penitentiary systems.
