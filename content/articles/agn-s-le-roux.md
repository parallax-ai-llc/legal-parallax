## Summary

French casino heiress who disappeared and was allegedly murdered by her lover Maurice Agnelet.

## Background

- Agnès Le Roux was the daughter of a prominent family involved in the casino business in Nice, on France’s Mediterranean coast.
- She became associated with the Palais de la Méditerranée, a major casino and hotel property whose control and management were contested in the 1970s.

## Disappearance (1977)

- In late 1977, she disappeared from Nice.
- The case was treated as a suspected homicide after she was reported missing and evidence suggested she did not leave voluntarily.
- Her body was never recovered, which complicated the investigation and later prosecutions.

## Key People

- **Maurice Agnelet**: a lawyer and Le Roux’s lover; he became the principal suspect and was later convicted.
- **Renée Le Roux**: Agnès’s mother, who pursued the case for decades and remained a central figure in public attention to the disappearance.

## Investigation and Allegations

- The case intersected with broader disputes over casino ownership and influence in Nice.
- Investigators and later court proceedings examined allegations of financial motives and pressure related to control of the casino.
- Because there was no body, prosecutors relied heavily on witness testimony, circumstantial evidence, and reconstruction of events.

## Trials and Legal Proceedings

- The case went through multiple stages over many years, including acquittals and retrials.
- A major development occurred in **2014**, when Maurice Agnelet was convicted of murder.
- The prolonged legal history is often cited as an example of how “no-body” homicide cases can hinge on evolving testimony and long-running judicial review.

## Cultural and Public Impact

- The disappearance became one of France’s well-known unresolved (and later adjudicated) criminal cases.
- Media coverage and public interest persisted for decades, in part due to the mix of wealth, politics, and the Riviera setting.
