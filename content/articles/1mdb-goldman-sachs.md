## Background

1Malaysia Development Berhad (1MDB) was a Malaysian state investment fund created in 2009. It became the center of an international corruption and money-laundering scandal after billions of dollars were allegedly diverted from the fund through a network of shell companies and intermediaries.

Goldman Sachs became associated with 1MDB primarily through its role in arranging bond offerings for the fund, which later drew scrutiny from regulators and prosecutors in multiple jurisdictions.

## What Goldman Sachs Was Accused Of (High Level)

- Helping 1MDB raise money through large bond deals while failing to prevent or detect red flags associated with the transactions.
- Inadequate controls and compliance processes around the deals, including due diligence and oversight.
- Enabling, facilitating, or failing to stop the misuse of proceeds that were later alleged to be diverted.

## Major Outcomes

- Goldman Sachs agreed to pay multibillion-dollar sums to resolve U.S. and Malaysian investigations related to 1MDB, including penalties, disgorgement, and other settlement components.
- The scandal contributed to major political fallout in Malaysia and prompted broader attention to governance, anti-corruption enforcement, and bank compliance expectations.

## Why It Matters

The 1MDB–Goldman Sachs episode is frequently cited as an example of how large financial institutions can face severe legal and reputational consequences when controls fail around high-risk sovereign or quasi-sovereign transactions.

It is also used as a case study for:

- Anti-money-laundering (AML) program design and enforcement
- Third-party risk and politically exposed person (PEP) exposure
- Cross-border jurisdictional coordination in financial-crime investigations
