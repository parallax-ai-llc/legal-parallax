## Background

AfriCrypt was a South Africa–based cryptocurrency platform associated with two brothers (Ameer and Raees Cajee). It came to wider attention in 2021 after reports that the operators could not be reached and that client funds were missing.

## What Happened

- Customers reported being unable to withdraw or access balances.
- The operators reportedly informed clients of a “hack” and advised them not to involve lawyers or authorities (a detail repeatedly cited in press summaries).
- Large Bitcoin outflows were alleged, leading to public claims that billions of dollars’ worth of BTC had been taken.

## Amount and Uncertainty

The frequently quoted figure (around **$3.6B**) reflects an estimate based on BTC prices and assumptions about the missing balances. Public reporting has also emphasized that:

- The total exposure is uncertain because the platform’s internal records were not publicly verifiable.
- The USD value depends heavily on the time window used for pricing BTC.
- On-chain tracing can indicate movements of known addresses, but it cannot prove the full scope of liabilities without matching customer ledgers.

## Aftermath and Investigation (High Level)

- A number of clients sought legal assistance and forensic investigation to trace potential fund movements.
- Authorities and courts in South Africa were reported to have become involved in related proceedings as complaints accumulated.
- Over time, AfriCrypt became a commonly cited example in discussions of exchange risk, custody, and the dangers of trusting opaque platforms.

## Why It Matters

- **Custody risk:** If users do not control private keys, they rely on the operator’s solvency and honesty.
- **Operational opacity:** Limited audits and unclear governance can make loss events hard to verify.
- **Regulatory pressure:** High-profile incidents tend to accelerate calls for licensing, disclosures, and consumer protections.

## Open Questions

- The definitive size of the loss and the exact wallets involved.
- Whether the event was primarily fraud, mismanagement, or a genuine external compromise.
- The ultimate recoverability of any missing assets.
