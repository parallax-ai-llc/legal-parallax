## Career Timeline

| Year | Event |
|------|-------|
| 1946–1949 | Joined the King's African Rifles (British colonial regiment), beginning a military career. |
| 1962 | Uganda gained independence; Amin was among the prominent Ugandan soldiers rising through the ranks. |
| 1966 | Took part in operations that strengthened Prime Minister Milton Obote’s position during a constitutional crisis. |
| 1971 | Seized power in a military coup while Obote was abroad; declared himself President. |
| 1972 | Ordered the expulsion of most Asians from Uganda, redistributing businesses and property; economy and institutions deteriorated. |
| 1976 | Entebbe hijacking crisis: a plane held at Entebbe Airport; Israeli commandos carried out a rescue operation. |
| 1978–1979 | Invaded Tanzania; Tanzanian forces and Ugandan exiles counterattacked, toppling Amin’s regime. |
| 1979–2003 | Lived in exile (including Saudi Arabia) until his death. |

## Key Themes

- **Military rule and repression:** Amin’s government is widely documented as responsible for severe human-rights abuses, including mass killings and arbitrary detention.
- **Economic disruption:** The 1972 expulsion of Asians removed a key commercial community; combined with mismanagement, it contributed to shortages, inflation, and institutional collapse.
- **Cold War-era diplomacy:** Uganda’s external alignments shifted over time, with Amin seeking support from multiple foreign partners as relations with former backers soured.

## Why He Matters

Idi Amin is commonly studied as a case of how rapid militarization of politics, weak institutions, and personalized rule can produce state violence and long-term economic damage.

## Notes (high level)

- Estimates of deaths under Amin vary widely across sources; the key point is that the scale of repression is regarded as substantial by historians and human-rights reporting.
- Many accounts emphasize propaganda, patronage networks, and the use of security forces to maintain control.
