## Summary

Hungarian gendarme officer acquitted in Canada of deporting Jews during the Holocaust.

## Background

- Imre Finta (1912–2003) was a Hungarian gendarmerie officer associated with actions taken during the 1944 deportations of Hungarian Jews under Nazi occupation and the Hungarian authorities’ collaboration.
- After World War II, he left Europe and later settled in Canada, where decades later he became the subject of one of Canada’s best-known early modern war-crimes prosecutions.

## Allegations (high level)

- Canadian prosecutors alleged that Finta participated in, or was responsible for, unlawfully confining, robbing, and deporting Jews as part of wartime measures in Hungary.
- The case was treated as a Holocaust-related prosecution under Canadian criminal law provisions targeting war crimes and crimes against humanity.

## Canadian trial and outcome

- Finta was tried in Canada and ultimately acquitted (1990).
- The acquittal was sustained through the appellate process, and the matter reached the Supreme Court of Canada (often cited as **R v Finta**), which left the acquittal in place.

## Why this case is cited

- The prosecution is frequently referenced in discussions of:
  - how Canada implemented and interpreted criminal liability for war crimes/crimes against humanity;
  - evidentiary and intent standards for events occurring decades earlier;
  - the practical challenges of Holocaust-era prosecutions in domestic courts.
