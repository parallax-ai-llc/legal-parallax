## Summary

Ongoing conflict in eastern Congo involving dozens of armed groups fighting for control of mineral-rich territory. Over 6 million have died since 1998 in related conflicts.

## Background

- The term "Kivu Conflict" is commonly used for a set of overlapping insurgencies and communal conflicts in the North Kivu and South Kivu provinces of the eastern Democratic Republic of the Congo (DRC).
- The conflict is often discussed as part of the broader cycle of violence that followed the First (1996–1997) and Second Congo Wars (1998–2003), with many armed actors persisting after the formal end of the interstate war.

## Main Actors (High-Level)

- **Congolese state forces:** Armed Forces of the Democratic Republic of the Congo (FARDC) and associated security services.
- **Local and foreign armed groups:** A shifting landscape of militias and rebel movements operating in eastern DRC (including groups with roots in Rwanda, Uganda, and Burundi-related dynamics).
- **Civilian communities:** Numerous ethnic and local communities are affected, sometimes forming self-defense groups; cycles of retaliation can sustain localized violence.

## Conflict Drivers

- **Control of territory and resources:** Parts of North and South Kivu contain valuable mineral deposits; armed groups have historically used taxation, forced labor, smuggling, and protection rackets to finance operations.
- **Land and identity disputes:** Competition over land, customary authority, and political representation can intersect with ethnic tensions.
- **Regional security dynamics:** Cross-border interests and the presence of foreign-origin armed groups complicate demobilization and stabilization.
- **Weak governance and impunity:** Limited state capacity, fragmented command structures, and inconsistent accountability can allow abuses to persist.

## Humanitarian Impact

- **Mass displacement:** Eastern DRC has repeatedly experienced large waves of internal displacement as fighting shifts between territories.
- **Civilian harm:** Reported patterns include killings, sexual violence, abductions, and recruitment of children by armed groups.
- **Economic disruption:** Insecurity affects farming, mining, trade routes, and access to health and education services.

## International and Regional Response

- **UN peacekeeping:** The UN mission in DRC (MONUC, later MONUSCO) has maintained a long-term presence, with mandates that have evolved to include civilian protection and support to stabilization efforts.
- **Peace processes and agreements:** Multiple rounds of negotiations, integration attempts, and disarmament/demobilization initiatives have been pursued, with uneven results.
- **Sanctions and monitoring:** International actors have used targeted sanctions and reporting mechanisms to address financing networks and armed group leadership.

## Notable Patterns and Developments

- **Fragmentation of armed groups:** Groups frequently split, rebrand, or form alliances of convenience, making "end states" and ceasefires fragile.
- **Urban and rural insecurity:** Violence can be concentrated in rural areas but often affects major towns through sporadic attacks, crime, and political pressure.
- **Minerals and supply chains:** Discussions of conflict minerals often reference eastern DRC; policy and corporate due diligence efforts have aimed to reduce armed group revenue from specific commodities.
