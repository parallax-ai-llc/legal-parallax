## Summary

Mississippi police fired 150 rounds into a women's dormitory at Jackson State College, killing 2 Black students. Coming 11 days after Kent State, it received far less media attention.

## Background and Context

- In May 1970, the United States saw widespread campus unrest tied to the Vietnam War, including protests after the U.S. announced the Cambodia campaign.
- The Jackson State shootings occurred shortly after the Kent State shootings, but involved a historically Black college and received markedly less sustained national attention.

## What Happened (High-Level)

- Late on May 14 and into the early hours of May 15, 1970, law enforcement officers responded to unrest near Jackson State College in Jackson, Mississippi.
- Officers fired a large number of rounds toward campus buildings, including a women’s dormitory.
- Two students were killed: **Phillip Lafayette Gibbs** and **James Earl Green**.
- Multiple other people were wounded (commonly reported as around a dozen).

## Immediate Aftermath

- The campus was placed under heightened police presence in the immediate aftermath.
- Investigations followed at local, state, and federal levels; however, accountability outcomes were limited compared to the scale of the shooting.

## Media Coverage and Public Attention

- Contemporary reporting and later scholarship often emphasize the disparity in coverage between Jackson State and Kent State.
- Factors frequently cited include race, regional politics, and the visibility of predominantly white institutions in national media.

## Why It Matters

- The Jackson State killings are widely referenced in discussions of:
  - unequal media attention to tragedies affecting Black communities,
  - the use of force against student protesters,
  - the broader pattern of 1970-era political violence and state response.
