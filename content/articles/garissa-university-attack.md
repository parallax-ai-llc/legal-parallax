## Summary

Al-Shabaab attack on Garissa University College in Kenya on April 2, 2015 that killed 148 people, mostly students. It was Kenyas deadliest terrorist attack.

## Background

Garissa County lies in northeastern Kenya near the Somalia border, a region affected by cross-border militant activity and long-running security operations tied to the conflict in Somalia. Al-Shabaab has carried out attacks in Kenya as part of a broader campaign that includes retaliation narratives and attempts to undermine Kenyan security presence.

## Attack Overview

High-level descriptions of the incident emphasize:

- a coordinated assault on a university campus,
- the targeting of students and staff,
- a siege-like sequence with security forces responding and eventually killing the attackers.

The casualty figure (148 killed) made it one of the deadliest attacks in Kenya’s modern history.

## Response and Aftermath

Following the attack, Kenyan authorities pursued multiple lines of response:

- security operations aimed at disrupting al-Shabaab networks,
- reviews of campus and public-institution security,
- public debate over intelligence failures, policing, and discrimination concerns in counterterrorism.

## Social and Political Impact

The attack had a lasting impact on Kenyan public life, including national mourning, heightened fear of terrorism, and intensified scrutiny of state capacity to protect soft targets such as schools and universities.

## Security Lessons Often Discussed

Post-attack commentary commonly focuses on “soft target” protection challenges, including:

- campus access control and emergency communications,
- intelligence sharing and rapid-response capacity,
- balancing counterterrorism with community relations to avoid marginalization.

## Victims and Commemoration

The attack is widely remembered for the scale of student casualties and for subsequent commemorations and public debate about how universities and other civilian institutions should plan for mass-casualty threats.
