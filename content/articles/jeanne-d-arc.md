## Summary

Already in the database as Joan of Arc.

Jeanne d'Arc (English: Joan of Arc) was a French peasant-born visionary and military leader whose claims of divine guidance helped rally French resistance during the later phase of the Hundred Years' War. She is closely associated with the lifting of the Siege of Orléans (1429) and the campaign that enabled the coronation of Charles VII at Reims.

Key points (high-level):
- **Context:** The Hundred Years' War and internal French political divisions created a legitimacy crisis for the French crown.
- **Role:** She served as a symbolic and practical catalyst for French morale and organization, accompanying armies and insisting on the coronation of Charles VII.
- **Trial and death:** Captured by Burgundian allies of England, tried for heresy, and executed by burning in Rouen (1431).
- **Posthumous reversal:** A later retrial (1455–1456) nullified the verdict.
- **Canonization:** Venerated as a saint in the Catholic Church (canonized 1920).

## Why She Matters

- **National identity:** Became an enduring emblem of French unity and resistance.
- **Religious history:** Her visions and the ecclesiastical trial record provide unusually detailed documentation for a medieval figure.
- **Political symbolism:** Appropriated across centuries by competing movements, showing how historical memory can be repurposed.

## Names and Variants

- French: *Jeanne d'Arc*
- Common English rendering: *Joan of Arc*
- In structured databases, the English form is often used as the primary label; this article keeps the French name while acknowledging the equivalence.

## Legacy and Interpretation

- **Military impact vs. symbolic impact:** Historians often distinguish between direct tactical control (limited) and outsized influence on morale, legitimacy, and strategic momentum.
- **Sources:** Her condemnation trial transcripts are a central primary source; they record testimony from clerics, officials, and Jeanne herself.
- **Cultural afterlife:** The figure of Jeanne d'Arc appears in literature, art, film, and political rhetoric, frequently reflecting the priorities of the era interpreting her.

## Quick Facts

- **Associated places:** Domrémy (birthplace), Orléans (siege), Reims (coronation), Rouen (trial and execution)
- **Cause of death:** Execution by burning after conviction for heresy (later annulled)
- **Sainthood:** Canonized 1920; feast day observed in various calendars
