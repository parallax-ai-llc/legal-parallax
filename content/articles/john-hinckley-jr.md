## Additional Context

### Background

- John Warnock Hinckley Jr. is an American man whose 1981 attack on President Ronald Reagan became a major turning point in U.S. public debate about the insanity defense and psychiatric commitment.
- He was born in 1955 and was in his mid-20s at the time of the attempted assassination.

### 1981 Attempted Assassination (high-level)

- The shooting occurred in Washington, D.C., outside the Washington Hilton Hotel on March 30, 1981.
- Reagan was wounded; other people, including members of the president’s security detail and press, were also shot.
- Hinckley was arrested immediately at the scene.

### Trial and Verdict

- In 1982, Hinckley was found **not guilty by reason of insanity**.
- The case contributed to subsequent changes in how some U.S. jurisdictions frame and apply insanity defenses, and how the burden of proof may be allocated.

### Confinement and Release Process

- Following the verdict, he was committed to a psychiatric hospital under court supervision.
- Over time, courts granted increasing privileges and conditional release arrangements, with restrictions designed to reduce risk and provide monitoring.
- He was ultimately released from inpatient custody subject to conditions (per court orders reported in public records).

### Why It Matters

- The case is frequently cited in legal and forensic-psychiatry discussions as an example of how a single, widely publicized trial can reshape standards, statutes, and public perceptions.
- It is also referenced in media-history contexts because of the high-profile nature of the victim and the immediate national coverage.
