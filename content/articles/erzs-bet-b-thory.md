Hungarian countess known as the Blood Countess who allegedly tortured and killed hundreds of young women.

## Background

- Erzsébet Báthory (Elizabeth Báthory) was a Hungarian noblewoman from the influential Báthory family in the Kingdom of Hungary.
- She married Ferenc Nádasdy, a prominent noble and military commander, and lived primarily at family estates in what is now Slovakia and Hungary.

## Allegations and Investigation

- Báthory became the subject of accusations that she abused and killed young women and servants within her household.
- In late 1610, Palatine György Thurzó led an inquiry associated with the Habsburg administration, gathering testimony and arresting household members.
- Contemporary records include depositions and statements from witnesses; the reliability and motivations of these testimonies remain debated by historians.

## Detention and Legal Outcome

- Báthory was detained in 1610 and confined at Čachtice Castle.
- Unlike several of her servants—who were tried and executed—she was not publicly tried, reflecting her status and the political sensitivity of prosecuting a high-ranking aristocrat.
- She died in confinement in 1614.

## Historical Debate

- Some accounts portray her as a prolific serial killer (the later “Blood Countess” legend), while other interpretations argue the allegations were exaggerated or weaponized amid property, debt, and factional politics.
- Because much of the evidence comes from collected testimonies rather than a public trial record, modern reconstructions vary in certainty and detail.

## Legacy

- Báthory’s story became a durable part of Central European folklore and later popular culture, frequently linked (sometimes inaccurately) to vampire and gothic traditions.
- She is often referenced as a case study in how gender, aristocratic power, and state politics can shape criminal narratives in early modern Europe.
