## Summary

Founder of the GSPC, the Algerian terrorist group that later became AQIM.

In open-source accounts, Hattab is typically described as a veteran of Algeria’s 1990s Islamist insurgency who broke away from the Armed Islamic Group (GIA) amid internal disputes and allegations of indiscriminate violence. His breakaway faction, the Salafist Group for Preaching and Combat (GSPC), later rebranded and formally aligned with al-Qaeda, ultimately becoming known as al-Qaeda in the Islamic Maghreb (AQIM).

## Key Organizations

- **GIA (Armed Islamic Group)**: Frequently cited in biographies as an early insurgent context from which splinters emerged.
- **GSPC (Salafist Group for Preaching and Combat)**: Breakaway group founded in 1998; later served as the organizational base that transitioned into AQIM.
- **AQIM (al-Qaeda in the Islamic Maghreb)**: Name adopted after the GSPC’s alignment with al-Qaeda; commonly described as the GSPC’s successor organization.

## Notes

## Legacy and Significance

- Hattab is primarily remembered for founding the GSPC, a key organizational node in the evolution of Algerian jihadist militancy from the 1990s insurgency into the AQIM era.
- Later AQIM branding and strategy are generally attributed to successors and shifting regional dynamics rather than to Hattab personally.
- Open-source biographies frequently frame his trajectory as illustrative of factional splits, leadership turnover, and re-alignment pressures within armed groups.
