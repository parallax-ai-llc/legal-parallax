## Summary

Poaching for ivory has decimated African elephant populations. Armed groups including the LRA and al-Shabaab use ivory sales to fund operations; 100,000 elephants killed in a decade.

## Background

- **Demand drivers**: Ivory has historically been used for carvings, jewelry, piano keys, and status goods; modern demand concentrates in certain consumer markets.
- **Supply context**: African elephants are targeted because tusks are high-value, portable, and can be moved through established smuggling networks.
- **Shift in threat**: In parts of Central and East Africa, poaching evolved from opportunistic killing to **organized, heavily armed** operations.

## How the trade works (high level)

1. **Poaching**: Elephants are killed for tusks, often in or near protected areas.
2. **Aggregation**: Tusks are consolidated by intermediaries; shipments are prepared with concealment methods.
3. **Transport & laundering**: Ivory is moved across borders and may be laundered via falsified paperwork, corruption, or mixed legal/illegal stock.
4. **Processing & retail**: Ivory is carved/processed and sold through formal and informal markets, including online channels.

## Links to armed groups (often-cited allegations)

- Some reporting and enforcement assessments have linked ivory trafficking profits to financing for armed groups in certain regions (including groups cited in overviews such as LRA and al‑Shabaab).
- The **degree and consistency** of such links can vary by place and time; in many cases the larger enabling factors include corruption, weak enforcement, and transnational criminal logistics.

## Impact

- **Biodiversity**: Large-scale elephant loss can destabilize ecosystems where elephants act as key seed dispersers and habitat engineers.
- **Security & governance**: Poaching and trafficking can undermine park security and border control, and can incentivize corruption.
- **Local communities**: Communities may face violence, lost tourism revenue, and retaliatory human–wildlife conflict dynamics.

## Policy and enforcement responses

- **International framework**: CITES restrictions and national bans aim to reduce demand and constrain legal cover for laundering.
- **Protected-area enforcement**: Ranger training, intelligence-led patrols, aerial surveillance, and community-based conservation programs are common approaches.
- **Demand reduction**: Public campaigns and market closures in some countries target consumer behavior and commercial availability.

## Notes (scope)

This article uses “Blood Ivory Trade” as a shorthand label for the intersection of **illegal ivory trafficking** and **conflict/crime financing** narratives.
