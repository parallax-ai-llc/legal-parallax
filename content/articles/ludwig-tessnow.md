## Summary
Ludwig Tessnow was a German serial killer who murdered four children in 1898-1901. His case was landmark: the first time a biological forensic test (precipitin) was used to distinguish human from animal blood.

## Crimes
- **Victims**: Four children (1898–1901).[^2]
- **Pattern**: Contemporary reporting emphasized the brutality of the attacks and the difficulty investigators faced in conclusively tying physical evidence to a suspect at the time.[^2]

## Investigation and Trial
- The investigation became notable because it relied not only on witness accounts and circumstantial evidence, but also on early laboratory analysis of bloodstains.[^2]
- Tessnow was tried and convicted for the child murders; the proceedings are frequently cited in histories of German criminology because of the forensic methods introduced during the case.[^2][^3]

## Forensic Significance (Precipitin Test)
- The **precipitin test** is an early immunological method used to determine whether a blood sample is of **human** origin versus **animal** origin.[^4]
- In the Tessnow case, the test was applied to blood evidence to address a central defense question: whether stains could have come from animal slaughtering/hunting rather than from a human victim.[^2][^4]
- The case is widely referenced as an early public demonstration of how **serology** could support criminal investigations, laying groundwork for later advances (blood typing and, much later, DNA profiling).[^4][^5]

## Legacy
- Tessnow’s name persists primarily in forensic and criminology literature as a marker of the shift toward **science-informed** evidentiary standards in courtrooms.[^4]
- The precipitin test is now historically important rather than cutting-edge, but it illustrates how incremental laboratory methods changed investigative practice long before modern genetics.[^4][^5]
