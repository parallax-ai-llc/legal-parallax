## Summary

South Korean special forces brutally suppressed the Gwangju pro-democracy uprising, killing between 165-2,000 citizens. The massacre became a rallying point for Korean democracy.

## Background and Causes

- In late 1979, South Korea entered a period of political turmoil after President Park Chung-hee was assassinated.
- Military leaders consolidated power (including the December 12, 1979 coup) and expanded martial law nationwide on May 17, 1980.
- Universities were closed, political activity was restricted, and opposition leaders (notably Kim Dae-jung) were arrested—actions that intensified public anger.

## What Happened (May 18–27, 1980)

- **May 18:** Demonstrations began in Gwangju as students and citizens protested martial law; paratroopers were deployed and clashes escalated.
- **May 21:** After days of violence, large crowds gathered; citizens began obtaining weapons, and the city effectively came under civilian control for several days.
- **May 22–26:** A “liberated” period followed with local committees, attempts at negotiation, and intermittent standoffs.
- **May 27:** The military re-entered and retook key sites (including the provincial government building), ending the uprising.

## Casualties and Contested Numbers

- Reported death tolls vary widely across sources.
- Official counts historically cited **hundreds** of deaths, while other estimates and testimonies have argued the true number may have reached **into the thousands**.
- Beyond fatalities, many more people were injured, detained, and subjected to harsh treatment.

## Aftermath and Legacy

- The event became a central symbol of South Korea’s pro-democracy movement and later political liberalization.
- The uprising is commonly referred to in South Korea as the **May 18 Democratic Uprising/Movement** (5·18 민주화운동).
- In the 1990s, South Korea pursued legal accountability related to the 1979–1980 military takeover; former leaders including Chun Doo-hwan and Roh Tae-woo were convicted (later pardoned).
- The **May 18 Democratization Movement Archives** were inscribed in UNESCO’s *Memory of the World* Register (2011), reflecting its historical significance.
