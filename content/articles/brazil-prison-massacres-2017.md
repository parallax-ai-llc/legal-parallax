## Summary

Gang wars inside Brazilian prisons killed over 130 inmates in January 2017 alone. Victims were beheaded and dismembered as PCC and CV fought for drug route control.

## Background and Context

- Brazil’s prison system has long been characterized by overcrowding, weak state control inside facilities, and the presence of powerful inmate-run criminal factions.
- Major factions frequently cited in reporting include **Primeiro Comando da Capital (PCC)** and **Comando Vermelho (CV)**, along with regionally strong groups such as **Família do Norte (FDN)**.
- Analysts and journalists commonly frame the 2017 massacres as part of a broader dispute over drug-trafficking corridors and influence inside prisons.

## What Happened (High-Level)

- In early January 2017, multiple prisons experienced large-scale violence during riots and takeover attempts.
- Accounts from the period describe extreme brutality, including beheadings and mutilation, which contributed to national and international attention.
- The incidents were not a single event but a **cluster of massacres** occurring in different states over a short period.

## Reported Major Sites

- **Manaus, Amazonas (COMPAJ and related facilities)** — reported as one of the earliest and deadliest incidents.
- **Roraima (Boa Vista, Monte Cristo complex)** — reported days later with dozens killed.
- **Rio Grande do Norte (Alcaçuz complex)** — subsequent violence with additional fatalities reported.

## Responses and Aftermath

- Brazilian federal and state authorities announced emergency measures in response to the wave of killings, including transfers of inmates and heightened security operations.
- The massacres intensified public debate about prison governance, organized crime, and conditions of confinement.
- Reporting in the aftermath often emphasized that sustained reforms would require both infrastructure/conditions changes and disruption of factional control mechanisms.

## Notes on Sources

- Casualty totals and attribution to specific factions can vary across sources and over time; summaries often rely on contemporaneous reporting and later compendiums.
