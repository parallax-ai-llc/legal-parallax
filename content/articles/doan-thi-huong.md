## Summary

Vietnamese woman convicted of applying VX nerve agent to Kim Jong-nam as part of North Korean plot.

## Career Timeline

| Year | Event |
|------|-------|
| 2017 | Arrested in Malaysia after the killing of Kim Jong-nam at Kuala Lumpur International Airport (KLIA2).[^1] |
| 2017 | Charged with murder under Malaysian law and stood trial alongside Indonesian defendant Siti Aisyah.[^1] |
| 2019 | Murder charge reduced; pleaded guilty to a lesser charge related to causing hurt by a dangerous weapon/substance.[^1] |
| 2019 | Sentenced to 3 years and 4 months; released after time served and good behavior credits, returning to Vietnam.[^1] |

## Background

- Nationality: Vietnamese.
- Public reports describe her as a young adult from Vietnam who traveled to Malaysia and became involved in activities that culminated in the 2017 airport attack.[^1]
- She maintained that she believed she was participating in a prank or reality-TV style stunt rather than an assassination.[^1]

## Role in the Kim Jong-nam Assassination (2017)

- Victim: Kim Jong-nam, the half-brother of North Korean leader Kim Jong-un, was attacked at Kuala Lumpur International Airport in February 2017.[^1]
- Method (as reported): The attack involved the application of VX, a highly toxic nerve agent, to the victim’s face.[^1]
- Alleged operational pattern in public summaries: the women were described as the visible attackers, while other individuals (often characterized as handlers) allegedly planned and facilitated the operation.[^1]

## Legal Proceedings (Malaysia)

- Initial posture: Prosecutors initially pursued a murder charge, which carries severe penalties under Malaysian law.[^1]
- Case changes: Her charge was later reduced; she entered a guilty plea to a lesser offense and received a fixed prison sentence.[^1]
- Outcome: She was released in 2019 after accounting for time already served and sentencing credits, and then returned to Vietnam.[^1]

## Aftermath and Significance

- The case drew international attention because VX is classified as a chemical weapon and is associated with state-level capabilities and constraints.[^1]
- Reporting frequently framed the incident as a suspected North Korean operation, with the defendants portrayed as being used as instruments rather than core planners.[^1]
