## Summary

The 'Monster with 21 Faces' kidnapped Glico president and poisoned candy on store shelves across Japan. The extortionists were never caught in one of Japan's most famous unsolved cases.

## Background

- **Targeted companies:** The incident began with **Ezaki Glico** and later expanded to other Japanese food manufacturers, including **Morinaga**.
- **Period:** The main wave of threats and attacks occurred from **1984 to 1985**, during Japan's late-Showa era.
- **Primary goal:** The perpetrators repeatedly sought **extortion payments**, using public threats to force compliance.

## Key Events (High-Level)

- **Kidnapping and escape:** Glico's president was abducted and later **escaped**, escalating the confrontation.
- **Threat letters:** The group sent letters to companies and police, adopting the persona/name **"The Monster with 21 Faces"**.
- **Product tampering scare:** Threats included **poisoning or contaminating** consumer products, creating widespread fear and prompting recalls and warnings.
- **Widening campaign:** After pressure on Glico increased, the group shifted attention to other firms (notably Morinaga), keeping the extortion campaign alive.

## Modus Operandi

- **Psychological pressure:** The case is remembered for combining intimidation with theatrics—letters, deadlines, and highly public warnings.
- **Supply-chain vulnerability:** The threats highlighted how retail shelves and distribution networks could be exploited to create panic.
- **Anonymity:** The group avoided capture despite extensive public attention, leaving identities and organization unknown.

## Impact and Legacy

- **Corporate crisis response:** The incident influenced how Japanese companies approach **product safety**, **recalls**, and **public communication** during threats.
- **Public memory:** It remains one of Japan's most famous **unsolved** criminal cases, frequently referenced in discussions of modern Japanese policing and corporate security.
- **Cultural echo:** The "Monster with 21 Faces" name entered broader popular culture as shorthand for elusive, media-savvy offenders.

## Investigation Notes (Publicly Reported)

- **Massive manpower:** The case involved major police mobilization across multiple jurisdictions.
- **Unresolved outcome:** No definitive arrests or convictions are widely recognized as having closed the case.

## Open Questions

- **Identity and membership:** Who composed the group and how many people were involved remains unclear.
- **Motivation beyond money:** Whether the campaign was purely financial or also intended as notoriety/retaliation is still debated in retrospective commentary.
