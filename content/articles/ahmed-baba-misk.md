## Summary

Mauritanian intellectual and politician who documented the systematic slavery and racial discrimination in Mauritania, one of the last countries to abolish slavery.

## Background and Context

- **Mauritania and slavery**: Mauritania has long had entrenched systems of hereditary slavery and caste-like stratification, including the continued exploitation of Haratin (often described as Black Mauritanians) despite formal abolition.
- **Legal vs. lived reality**: Formal legal abolition (including the 1981 decree noted above) did not automatically end coercive labor relationships or discrimination; enforcement and social change lagged substantially.

## Advocacy and Public Work

- Documented and publicized allegations of **systematic slavery**, racial discrimination, and social exclusion in Mauritania.
- Contributed to international awareness by framing these issues as **human-rights violations** rather than isolated local disputes.
- Is frequently cited in discussions of Mauritania’s late and uneven transition away from slavery, including the gap between **policy announcements** and **effective protections** for victims.

## Why He Matters

- Helped establish a clearer historical record on slavery and race relations in modern Mauritania.
- His work is used as a reference point in broader debates on:
  - how post-colonial states address entrenched social hierarchies
  - the limits of abolition without enforcement mechanisms
  - the role of intellectuals and politicians in human-rights documentation
