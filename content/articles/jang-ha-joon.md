## Background

- South Korean economist known for work in development economics and political economy.
- Educated in economics and associated with the University of Cambridge, where he has held academic roles.
- Frequently writes for general audiences as well as academic and policy audiences, focusing on how states shape industrial development.

## Key Ideas (High-Level)

- **Infant-industry protection**: argues that temporary protection and industrial policy can help late-industrializing countries build capabilities.
- **Critique of "free-market" orthodoxy**: emphasizes that successful industrializers historically used tariffs, subsidies, and state coordination more than standard narratives admit.
- **Institutions and development**: highlights the interaction between markets, firms, and public institutions, cautioning against one-size-fits-all policy prescriptions.
- **Political economy lens**: stresses that development outcomes depend on power, interests, and the practical constraints of implementation, not just abstract efficiency.

## Selected Books

- *Kicking Away the Ladder* (2002) — historical argument that today’s rich countries used protectionist policies during their own development.
- *Bad Samaritans* (2007) — popular critique of prevailing development advice and international economic policy pressures.
- *23 Things They Don’t Tell You About Capitalism* (2010) — essays challenging common assumptions about markets, firms, and globalization.

## Public Impact

- Widely cited in debates on industrial policy, globalization, and development strategy.
- Often referenced in discussions about the role of the state in technological upgrading and manufacturing competitiveness.
- Known for framing economic history as evidence in contemporary policy debates (e.g., tariffs, trade rules, and development space).
