## Summary

Persian king who murdered his brother Bardiya, conquered Egypt with great cruelty, and went mad.

## Context and Rule

- **Dynasty:** Achaemenid Empire (Persian imperial dynasty founded by Cyrus the Great).
- **Reign:** Commonly dated to **530–522 BCE** (dates vary slightly by source).
- **Family politics:** Ancient accounts emphasize a violent court struggle involving his brother **Bardiya/Smerdis**; modern reconstructions treat parts of the story cautiously because our narratives are late and shaped by propaganda.

## Conquest of Egypt (High-Level)

- Egypt was one of the major, wealthy targets available after Cyrus’s expansion; controlling it also meant influence over eastern Mediterranean trade routes.
- Greek literary tradition (especially Herodotus) depicts Cambyses as committing religious and political outrages in Egypt; some elements may reflect hostile or moralizing storytelling.
- Regardless of tone in the sources, Cambyses is historically associated with **bringing Egypt under Achaemenid control**, initiating Persian rule there.

## Reputation and Historiography

- **“Madness” motif:** Many later narratives frame Cambyses as irrational or insane; this is a common ancient trope used to explain shocking actions and legitimize later rulers.
- **Source mix:** Our picture comes from Greek historians, later Persian traditions, and Egyptian/near-eastern evidence; these sources can disagree on motives and details.
- **Legacy:** Remembered both as a successful conqueror (strategic expansion) and as a cautionary example of tyranny in classical storytelling.

## Quick Facts

- Known for the **Egyptian campaign** and for a **succession crisis** at the end of his reign.
- Closely linked to the controversy over **Bardiya/Smerdis** and the transition to **Darius I**.
