## Summary

Mexican serial killer known as 'El Chalequero' who murdered 20 sex workers in the 19th century.

## Historical Context

Guerrero's murders took place in late-19th-century Mexico City, a period shaped by rapid urban growth, deep inequality, and the long presidency of Porfirio Díaz (the *Porfiriato*). Contemporary reporting and later retellings often framed the crimes around fears about "vice" districts and the perceived vulnerability of women living or working at the margins.

## Known Victimology and Modus Operandi (High Level)

- **Victims:** Most accounts describe victims as sex workers or other working-class women.
- **Approach:** Sources commonly state that he approached women in public spaces and lured them to more secluded areas.
- **Violence:** He is associated with repeated killings using physical violence; specific details vary across accounts.
- **Nickname:** He became widely known as **"El Chalequero"** (often translated as "the vest-wearer"), a moniker tied to how he was described or portrayed in popular narratives.

## Investigation and Prosecution (Overview)

Public attention to the killings rose as patterns were noticed and bodies were found across multiple incidents. He was arrested and prosecuted, and later histories commonly present him as one of Mexico's earliest widely discussed serial killers.

### Notes on the 1908 Conviction

The 1908 conviction is frequently cited as the definitive legal outcome in popular summaries. Earlier arrests and releases are mentioned in secondary accounts; when building a detailed chronology, it is important to verify dates against primary records or well-cited historical studies.

## Legacy and Interpretation

Guerrero is often referred to in media as the **"Mexican Jack the Ripper"**, reflecting the tendency to compare serial crimes across countries rather than a direct historical link. His case appears in discussions of:

- Early criminology and policing narratives in Mexico
- Sensationalist crime reporting and moral panic around urban "vice"
