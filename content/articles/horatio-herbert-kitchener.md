## Summary

British military commander who used concentration camps in the Boer War, killing 26,000 women and children.

## Historical Context

- Kitchener rose to prominence during the high period of British imperial expansion in the late 19th century.
- His career combined battlefield command with colonial administration, and he became a widely recognized public symbol of British military power.

## Major Posts and Campaigns (High-Level)

### Sudan and the Mahdist War

- Served in campaigns connected to the reconquest of Sudan.
- Associated with the 1898 Battle of Omdurman, after which British-Egyptian control was consolidated in the region.

### Egypt and Colonial Administration

- Held senior military positions in Egypt and later administered parts of Britain’s imperial security posture in the region.
- Built a reputation as an organizer focused on logistics, rail, and supply systems that supported mobile military operations.

### Second Boer War (South Africa)

- Took over British forces in South Africa during the later phase of the war.
- Implemented a strategy often described as “scorched earth” alongside a system of blockhouses and sweeps intended to deny Boer commandos supplies and shelter.
- Oversaw the use of civilian concentration camps.
  - Mortality in the camps was driven largely by disease, inadequate sanitation, overcrowding, and shortages.
  - Commonly cited figures include roughly 26,000 deaths among Boer women and children; historians also note substantial deaths among Black African internees, with estimates varying by source.

### First World War

- Appointed Secretary of State for War in 1914.
- Became closely associated with Britain’s early-war mobilization, including the recruitment of mass volunteer forces (often referred to as “Kitchener’s Army”).
- Died in 1916 when HMS *Hampshire* struck a mine and sank while he was traveling on a wartime mission.

## Controversies and Legacy

- Kitchener’s name remains linked to policies that inflicted severe civilian suffering, particularly in South Africa.
- At the same time, he is remembered in British public history as an emblem of wartime recruitment and national mobilization.
- Modern evaluations often separate his administrative effectiveness from the humanitarian and ethical consequences of his counterinsurgency methods.
