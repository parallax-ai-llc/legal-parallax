## Summary

The 7.8 magnitude earthquake killed over 59,000 people. Massive corruption in Turkish construction — ignored building codes, fake inspections — magnified the death toll by thousands.

## What Happened (High-Level)

- **Date / time**: The main shock struck on **6 February 2023** (local time), followed by a second major quake later the same day.
- **Magnitude**: The first event was **Mw 7.8**, with widespread strong shaking across southeastern Turkey and northern Syria.
- **Aftershocks**: Thousands of aftershocks followed, including at least one **major** aftershock that caused additional collapses and hindered rescue work.

## Geographic Impact

- **Turkey**: Heavy damage in multiple provinces in the southeast, with dense urban areas experiencing severe building failures.
- **Syria**: Significant destruction in the northwest, including areas already strained by conflict and displacement.
- **Winter conditions**: Cold weather complicated rescue and survival, increasing the risk of hypothermia among survivors.

## Why the Death Toll Was So High

- **Building vulnerability**: Many structures were not designed or constructed to withstand strong seismic forces.
- **Enforcement gaps**: Investigations and reporting highlighted **weak code enforcement**, irregular permitting, and inspection failures.
- **Construction amnesties**: Past policies that legalized non-compliant buildings were widely cited as a factor that increased exposure.

## Humanitarian & Government Response

- **Search and rescue**: Local responders were rapidly overwhelmed; international teams and equipment were deployed in the following days.
- **Medical surge**: Hospitals and field clinics faced shortages and damage, while patient loads spiked.
- **Shelter needs**: Millions required temporary housing, with large-scale use of tents, containers, and public facilities.

## Aftermath & Consequences

- **Displacement**: The quake triggered mass internal displacement and intensified existing refugee pressures.
- **Economic damage**: Infrastructure loss (roads, utilities, public buildings) created long recovery timelines and high reconstruction costs.
- **Legal accountability**: Arrests and prosecutions were reported related to contractors and building practices, reflecting public anger over preventable collapses.

## Lessons (Systems, Not Just Nature)

- **Preparedness**: Earthquakes become mass-casualty events when emergency logistics, communications, and shelter capacity are not pre-positioned.
- **Governance**: Transparent permitting, independent inspections, and anti-corruption enforcement can directly translate into lives saved.
- **Retrofitting**: Strengthening existing housing stock often yields larger safety gains than focusing only on new construction.
