## Summary
Myra Hindley and Ian Brady committed the 'Moors murders,' killing five children in Manchester between 1963-65. She died in prison in 2002, never released despite repeated appeals.

## Background
- **Early life**: Hindley was raised in Manchester. Contemporary accounts describe a turbulent home environment and a working-class upbringing.
- **Employment**: Before her convictions, she held clerical and office roles in Manchester.

## The Moors murders (high-level overview)
- The crimes became known as the **Moors murders** because some victims were buried on **Saddleworth Moor**.
- The case involved **five victims** (children/teenagers) and occurred primarily between **1963 and 1965**.
- Hindley and Brady were convicted for **three murders** initially; subsequent investigations and statements clarified responsibility for additional killings.

## Investigation and trial
- **Arrest and evidence**: Police inquiries into missing children and links to Brady and Hindley led to arrests in 1965.
- **Trial (1966)**: The trial drew national attention in the UK; both defendants received **life sentences**.
- The case is often cited in discussions of how criminal investigations used **forensic evidence, witness testimony, and material exhibits** in high-profile murder trials.

## Imprisonment and legal history
- Hindley remained in custody for life and became one of the most widely recognized prisoners in modern British history.
- **Tariff and appeals**: Over time, her minimum-term/tariff and parole eligibility were subjects of repeated legal disputes and political scrutiny.
- **Public reaction**: The possibility of release generated strong and sustained public opposition.

## Legacy and impact
- The Moors murders had long-term effects on UK public life, including:
  - heightened concern about child safety and missing-person investigations,
  - enduring debates about life sentences, tariffs, and executive influence over punishment,
  - extensive media ethics discussions about notoriety and victims’ families.
- The case remains a reference point in criminology and criminal justice history in the UK.
