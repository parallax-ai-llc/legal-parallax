## Summary

Persian eunuch and kingmaker who poisoned Artaxerxes III and his son to control the throne.

## Career Timeline

| Year | Event |
|------|-------|
| -338 | Poisoned Artaxerxes III to control succession |

| -336 | Removed Arses and several of his children when Arses resisted Bagoas’ control |
| -336 | Elevated Darius III to the throne, expecting him to be pliable |
| -336 | According to later accounts, was forced to drink his own poison after plotting against Darius III |

## Historical Context

- The late Achaemenid court was shaped by intense succession politics, factional rivalry, and the influence of powerful courtiers.
- Eunuchs could hold significant authority in Near Eastern royal courts, serving in trusted household and administrative roles.
- Artaxerxes III (r. 358–338 BCE) is often portrayed as a forceful restorer of central power; his death created a sudden vacuum at the top of the empire.

## Role in the Achaemenid Court

- **Court power-broker:** Bagoas is remembered as a senior figure close to the king with access to the inner household, a position that could translate into political leverage.
- **Regicide and succession management:** Ancient and later narratives describe him as using poison to remove rulers and shape the line of succession.
- **Kingmaker dynamics:** His rise illustrates a recurring imperial pattern: when legitimacy is contested, control over the palace and succession mechanisms can outweigh formal titles.

## Downfall and Consequences

- Sources commonly describe Darius III as ultimately refusing to remain a figurehead.
- The story that Bagoas was compelled to drink poison is often used as a moralizing end to a “poisoner” narrative, emphasizing reversal of fortune.
- In broader histories, the turmoil surrounding these successions is treated as one factor that weakened imperial cohesion on the eve of Alexander’s invasion.

## Legacy and Sources

- Bagoas appears primarily through **Greco-Roman historiography**, which can be fragmentary, hostile, and shaped by court-intrigue tropes.
- Many modern summaries rely on later compilations of these traditions; details (especially motives and exact sequences) should be treated cautiously.
- Despite uncertainty on specifics, Bagoas remains a widely cited example of how non-royal actors could dominate late-imperial politics.
