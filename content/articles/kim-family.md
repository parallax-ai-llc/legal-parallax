## Overview

The "Kim family" commonly refers to the hereditary leadership lineage that has dominated the politics of the Democratic People's Republic of Korea (DPRK) since its founding. The family’s authority is embedded in the ruling Workers' Party of Korea (WPK), the state, and the military, and is reinforced through a state-sponsored personality cult.

## Key Figures (Commonly Cited)

- **Kim Il-sung (1912–1994)**: Founder of the DPRK and the first Supreme Leader; served as Premier and later President.
- **Kim Jong-il (1941/1942–2011)**: Succeeded Kim Il-sung; expanded the leadership cult and is often associated with "Songun" (military-first) politics.
- **Kim Jong-un (b. 1983/1984)**: Current leader; holds top party, state, and military positions and has overseen continued nuclear and missile development.

## Succession and Governance (High-Level)

- Succession is typically described as occurring through a combination of party appointments, control of security institutions, and elite consensus.
- The family’s legitimacy is tied to official narratives around anti-Japanese resistance, state founding myths, and the "Mount Paektu" bloodline concept.
- Key institutions linked to leadership consolidation include the **WPK** (party control), the **Korean People's Army** (military), and internal security bodies.

## Notes

- The term can be used broadly to include extended relatives and in-laws who have held influential positions, though visibility and power levels have varied over time.
- Many biographical details (including some birth years and internal roles) are contested or reported differently across sources.
