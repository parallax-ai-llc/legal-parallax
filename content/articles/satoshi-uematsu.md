## Summary
Satoshi Uematsu killed 19 disabled people and wounded 26 at the Tsukui Yamayuri En care home in Sagamihara, Japan on July 26, 2016. It was the deadliest mass killing in Japan since WWII.

## Background
- Uematsu previously worked at a care facility and had direct exposure to people with severe disabilities.
- In the months before the attack, he expressed extremist views advocating the killing of people with disabilities, framing it as a form of "euthanasia" (a view widely condemned as ableist and violent).
- Japanese media and court reporting describe a period of deterioration in his behavior, including alarming statements that prompted concern by people around him.

## 2016 Sagamihara Stabbings (Tsukui Yamayuri En)
- **Date**: 2016-07-26 (early morning)
- **Location**: Tsukui Yamayuri En, a residential care home in Sagamihara, Kanagawa Prefecture, Japan.
- **Method**: Knife attack inside the facility.
- **Victims**: 19 people killed; 26 injured (many with severe disabilities).
- **Significance**: Often cited as the deadliest mass killing in Japan since World War II.

## Motive and Ideology (high-level)
- Prosecutors and major reporting describe the crime as driven by hatred toward people with disabilities and a belief that their lives were less valuable.
- The attack is frequently discussed in the context of **ableism**, stigma, and extremist thinking about "purity" or "social burden".

## Arrest and Legal Proceedings
- Uematsu was arrested shortly after the attack.
- He was prosecuted in Japan and later sentenced to death.
- Legal reporting also covered questions about his mental state and responsibility, which are common considerations in serious violent-crime trials.

## Public Reaction and Impact
- The killings provoked national mourning and international attention.
- Disability-rights advocates emphasized the need to confront stigma and improve protections and inclusion for disabled people.
- The case continues to be referenced in discussions about hate-motivated violence, institutional safety, and social attitudes toward disability.
