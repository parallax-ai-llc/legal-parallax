## Summary

Deputy PM who orchestrated the coup and execution of PM Maurice Bishop in Grenada.

## Context and Significance

- **Position in government**: Coard served as Deputy Prime Minister and held senior responsibilities within Grenada's revolutionary government during the New Jewel Movement (NJM) period.[^1]
- **1983 crisis**: In October 1983, internal NJM conflict culminated in the overthrow of Prime Minister Maurice Bishop, followed by Bishop's detention and subsequent execution at Fort Rupert (later Fort George). Coard is widely described as a central figure in the faction that removed Bishop.[^1]
- **International consequences**: The collapse of the revolutionary government and the killings at Fort Rupert were pivotal events preceding the U.S.-led invasion of Grenada (Operation Urgent Fury) in late October 1983.[^1]

## Role in the 1983 Coup (High-Level)

- Coard was associated with the hardline faction that favored collective leadership and took steps to neutralize Bishop's influence when tensions escalated.[^1]
- The coup period is characterized in historical summaries as a decisive break within the NJM leadership, with Coard frequently identified as an organizer or leading participant in the events that produced the new military-backed authority.[^1]

## Trial, Conviction, and Sentence

- After the 1983 upheaval, Coard and other defendants were prosecuted in Grenada for offenses connected to the deaths of Maurice Bishop and others (often discussed publicly under the label "Grenada 17").[^1]
- Coard was convicted and sentenced to imprisonment; later reporting and biographical summaries note that sentences were ultimately reduced, and he was released after serving a lengthy term.[^1]

## Legacy and Public Assessment

- Coard remains a controversial figure in Grenadian political history due to his association with the overthrow and killing of Bishop, who is widely regarded as a charismatic leader of the revolution.[^1]
- In many accounts, the 1983 episode is treated as a cautionary case about revolutionary movements fracturing under internal power struggles, and Coard's role is cited in that broader narrative.[^1]
