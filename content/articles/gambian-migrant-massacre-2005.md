## Summary

Massacre of 56 West African migrants by Gambian security forces known as the 'Junglers' on orders of President Jammeh. The migrants were shot and buried in mass graves.

## Background

The victims were migrants traveling through The Gambia while attempting to reach destinations in West Africa and, in some cases, onward routes. Accounts summarized by later investigations described the group as being detained after crossing into Gambian territory and then transported to different locations under guard.

## Alleged Perpetrators and Command

The killings have been widely attributed to members of an elite security unit popularly known as the “Junglers,” which multiple witnesses have described as operating under the direction of then-President Yahya Jammeh. In this framing, the incident is treated as a state crime carried out by security personnel rather than an attack by non-state actors.

## Investigation and Accountability

- The incident later became one of the cases examined in The Gambia’s post-Jammeh truth-seeking process, including testimony about detention, extrajudicial executions, and disposal of bodies.
- The Truth, Reconciliation and Reparations Commission (TRRC) hearings contributed to public documentation of alleged command responsibility and the role of security services.
- As with many transitional-justice processes, the extent of criminal accountability has depended on domestic legal follow-through and international cooperation.

## Historical Significance

The massacre is often cited in discussions of Jammeh-era human-rights abuses, including enforced disappearances and extrajudicial killings. It also illustrates the vulnerability of migrants transiting through small states where security services can exercise broad discretion.

## Key Terms

- **Junglers**: A term used in Gambian public discourse and testimony to describe a covert or elite unit linked to politically motivated violence.
- **TRRC**: The Gambia’s Truth, Reconciliation and Reparations Commission, established after the political transition away from Jammeh.

## Key Points (High-Level)

- The victims were largely migrants from across West Africa; the incident is often discussed as an abuse of state power directed at a vulnerable, mobile population.
- Public accounts emphasize clandestine detention, transport, execution, and concealment, consistent with patterns described in other Jammeh-era allegations.
- Post-2017 transitional-justice mechanisms brought renewed attention, but long-term accountability depends on prosecutions, reparations, and institutional reform.

## Documentation Notes

Most detailed narratives in public circulation come from later testimony and investigative reporting rather than contemporaneous official records, which is common in cases involving alleged covert security units.
