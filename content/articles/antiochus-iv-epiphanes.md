## Additional Context

### Position in the Seleucid Dynasty

- Antiochus IV ruled the Seleucid Empire (a major Hellenistic successor state founded after Alexander the Great).
- He was a son of Antiochus III “the Great” and came to power after the death of his brother Seleucus IV.
- His epithet **Epiphanes** (“God Manifest”) reflected a style of kingship that emphasized public display and divine favor.

### Accession and Consolidation of Power

- After Seleucus IV’s death, Antiochus maneuvered into the kingship amid a contested succession.
- Like many Hellenistic monarchs, he used court alliances, legitimacy claims, and public patronage to stabilize rule.
- He promoted civic projects and royal festivals in various cities, reinforcing loyalty through visible benefaction.

### Policy Toward Judea and the Temple

- His reign is closely associated with the **suppression of traditional Jewish religious practices**.
- Measures attributed to his government included restrictions on circumcision, Sabbath observance, and Temple rites.
- The **desecration of the Jerusalem Temple** and installation of non-Jewish cult practices became a central grievance.
- These actions helped catalyze armed resistance, remembered as the **Maccabean Revolt**.

### Regional Geopolitics

- Antiochus IV governed during a period of intense competition among Hellenistic kingdoms and Rome’s growing influence.
- Seleucid rulers faced fiscal and military pressures following earlier defeats and treaty obligations.
- Roman diplomacy increasingly constrained Seleucid actions in the eastern Mediterranean.

### Historical Significance

- In Jewish historical memory, Antiochus IV became a symbol of religious persecution and forced cultural assimilation.
- The revolt against his policies contributed to the rise of the Hasmonean state and reshaped Judea’s political trajectory.
- In broader Hellenistic history, his reign illustrates how imperial policy and local religious identity could collide.

### Key Facts (High-Level)

- **Empire:** Seleucid Empire (Near East, Levant, and parts of Asia Minor at various points).
- **Era:** Hellenistic period.
- **Notability:** Triggering events leading to the Maccabean Revolt; remembered for actions against Jerusalem’s Temple cult.
