## Summary

Armed groups in eastern Congo fund their operations through control of tin, tantalum, tungsten, and gold mines. These 'conflict minerals' end up in electronics worldwide.

## Background

The term is most commonly associated with minerals mined in the Democratic Republic of the Congo (DRC) and adjoining countries, where long-running conflict, weak governance, and corruption have enabled armed groups and some military units to profit from extraction and trade.

## “3TG” and Related Materials

Many compliance programs focus on **3TG** (tin, tantalum, tungsten, and gold), because they are widely used in consumer and industrial products and have been central to major disclosure rules.

- **Tin**: often used for solder on circuit boards.
- **Tantalum**: commonly used in capacitors.
- **Tungsten**: used for vibration motors, tooling, and high-density applications.
- **Gold**: used for corrosion-resistant electrical contacts.

## How Conflict Financing Occurs (High-Level)

Common risk patterns include:

- Armed actors taxing mine sites, transport routes, or trading hubs.
- Forced labor, extortion, and violent control over mining communities.
- Smuggling and laundering of minerals through complex regional trading networks.

## Regulation and Corporate Due Diligence

- **United States (Dodd-Frank Act, Section 1502)**: required certain public companies to assess and report whether 3TG in their products may have originated in the DRC or adjoining countries, and to describe due-diligence steps.
- **OECD Due Diligence Guidance**: a widely referenced framework encouraging companies to map supply chains, assess risks, respond to identified harms, and undergo independent audits where applicable.
- **EU Conflict Minerals Regulation**: created due-diligence obligations for certain importers of 3TG into the European Union.

## Why It Matters

The core concern is not only the origin of minerals, but the **human rights and security impacts** linked to their extraction and trade, including violence, displacement, and exploitation in affected regions.

## Notes

“Conflict minerals” is a policy and compliance term; it does not imply that all mining in the region is illicit, nor that every supply chain link is directly controlled by armed groups.
