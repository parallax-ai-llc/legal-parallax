## Summary

Brazilian bank robber known as the 'Bandit Who Read Marx,' killed under mysterious circumstances.

## Career Timeline

| Year | Event |
|------|-------|
| Early 1970s | Became widely known in Brazil for high-profile bank robberies and escapes (as described in contemporary reporting and later accounts) |
| Mid-1970s | Arrested and held in prison; his case drew attention to police corruption and prison violence |
| 1975 | Killed in suspicious circumstances in prison |

## Background and Notability

- Lúcio Flávio is frequently cited in Brazilian true-crime writing as a symbol of the era’s blurred lines between organized crime, policing, and political violence.
- The nickname **"Bandit Who Read Marx"** reflects how he was portrayed: unusually articulate for a criminal celebrity and associated (at least in popular retellings) with reading political theory while incarcerated.
- Accounts of his life are often framed less as a single biography and more as a case study in Brazil’s mid-20th-century urban crime environment and the institutions surrounding it.

## Themes Commonly Highlighted in Accounts

- **Prison conditions and extrajudicial violence:** Narratives about his death are commonly used to discuss violence within prisons and allegations of death-squad activity.
- **Corruption and complicity:** Many retellings emphasize alleged relationships between criminals, informants, and elements of law enforcement.
- **Media mythmaking:** His story illustrates how criminals can become public myths—part fact, part sensational reporting—especially when details are contested.

## Death

- He died in custody in **1975**, and the circumstances have been widely described as suspicious.
- Later summaries (including encyclopedic sources) typically treat the case as unresolved in the public imagination, with competing explanations and allegations around responsibility.

## Legacy and Popular Culture

- His story has been adapted and reinterpreted in Brazilian media, contributing to his status as a lasting figure in the country’s crime and political-history narratives.
- Discussions about him often connect personal biography to broader institutional critique (police violence, corruption, and the carceral system).
