## Summary
The Isabella Stewart Gardner Museum heist on March 18, 1990, was the largest property theft in history. Two men disguised as police officers stole 13 artworks worth $500 million, including works by Vermeer and Rembrandt. The art has never been recovered.

## Background
- **Museum**: The Isabella Stewart Gardner Museum is a private museum in Boston founded by collector Isabella Stewart Gardner.
- **Collection**: The collection included major European paintings and works on paper displayed in galleries arranged to Gardner’s specifications.
- **Security context (1990)**: Like many museums of the period, Gardner relied on a mix of guards, alarms, and physical barriers that were not designed for an organized, targeted art theft.

## How the theft unfolded (high-level)
- **Entry method**: Two thieves arrived late at night and **posed as police officers** to gain entry.
- **Control of guards**: After entering, they restrained on-duty security personnel, giving themselves extended time inside the museum.
- **Duration**: The theft took place over **multiple hours**, allowing the thieves to move through galleries and remove works with limited immediate resistance.
- **Exit**: The perpetrators left with the artworks and have never been publicly identified in a way that led to recovery.

## Notable stolen works (selected)
- **Johannes Vermeer**: *The Concert* (one of the most famous missing paintings in the world).
- **Rembrandt**: *The Storm on the Sea of Galilee* and *A Lady and Gentleman in Black*.
- **Edgar Degas**: Multiple works on paper.
- **Other items**: A variety of additional paintings and smaller works were taken, totaling **13 pieces**.

## Investigation and theories (overview)
- **Federal involvement**: The case drew sustained attention from US law enforcement, including federal investigators.
- **Organized crime speculation**: Investigators and journalists have periodically linked the heist to organized crime networks in the Northeast, though definitive proof has been elusive.
- **No recovery**: Despite searches, tips, and public appeals, **none of the stolen works have been returned**.

## Impact
- **Financial**: The stolen collection has been valued in the **hundreds of millions of dollars**, often cited around $500 million.
- **Cultural**: The heist became a landmark case in art crime, shaping public perception of museum security and the black-market art ecosystem.
- **Ongoing visibility**: The museum has kept the missing frames and locations prominent, reinforcing the open nature of the case.

## Security and risk lessons
- **Social engineering**: The heist is frequently cited as an example of how impersonation and authority cues can bypass physical security.
- **Guard protocols**: Clear procedures for verifying identity and handling after-hours access requests are critical.
- **Defense in depth**: Redundant layers—trained staff, monitored alarms, controlled access, and rapid response—reduce the likelihood that a single failure enables a large-scale loss.
