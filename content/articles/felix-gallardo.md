## Identity and Naming
- This entry refers to **Miguel Ángel Félix Gallardo**, widely described in journalism and law-enforcement history as a central organizer of Mexico’s large-scale drug-trafficking networks in the 1980s.
- He is often associated with the **Guadalajara Cartel**, a trafficking federation linked to early consolidation of routes and payoffs that later fragmented into multiple groups.

## Background (High-Level)
- Born in **Sinaloa, Mexico**, a region long tied to drug production and smuggling corridors.
- Public accounts commonly describe an early trajectory through local power networks before rising as an organizer and broker among traffickers.

## Role in the Guadalajara Cartel
- Frequently portrayed as a **coordinator** more than a street-level enforcer: aligning producers, transport, money movement, and protection.
- His era is often characterized by the **federation model**: multiple trafficking families cooperating under negotiated rules rather than one monolithic hierarchy.
- Guadalajara (Jalisco) is cited in many sources as an operational hub during the cartel’s peak influence.

## Operations and Methods (Reported)
- Trafficking routes expanded through Mexico into the United States, with logistics spanning border plazas, storage, and distribution partners.
- Corruption and protection payments are repeatedly cited in accounts of how high-volume flows were maintained.
- Large marijuana cultivation and cross-border movement in the early 1980s is frequently mentioned, alongside later diversification.

## Law Enforcement Pressure and Turning Points
- The 1980s saw increasing bilateral pressure on Mexican trafficking organizations.
- A major historical inflection point in many narratives is the **1985 kidnapping and murder of DEA agent Enrique “Kiki” Camarena**; the broader crackdown that followed is often linked to the decline of the Guadalajara network.

## Arrest and Incarceration
- He was arrested in **1989** and sentenced in Mexico; public reporting widely notes long-term imprisonment.
- Later years are commonly described as continued influence through intermediaries, though details vary by source and period.

## Historical Significance
- Often cited as a bridge figure between earlier smuggling groups and the later, more fragmented cartel landscape.
- The post-Guadalajara fragmentation is frequently summarized as the rise of multiple regional organizations competing for routes and protection.
