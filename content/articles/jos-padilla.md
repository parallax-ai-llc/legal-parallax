## Summary

American citizen designated an enemy combatant for plotting to detonate a dirty bomb in the US.

Padilla became a prominent post-9/11 legal and civil-liberties case because the U.S. government used executive authority to hold him in military detention without a civilian criminal trial for several years, before ultimately prosecuting him in federal court.

## Key Points

- Arrested in 2002 at Chicago O'Hare International Airport and initially held as a material witness.
- Later designated an "enemy combatant" by the U.S. President and transferred to military custody.
- Ultimately tried in federal court and convicted in 2007 of terrorism-related conspiracy offenses.
- Early public allegations focused on a "dirty bomb" plot; the later prosecution did not charge him with attempting to build or detonate a radiological device.

## Detention and Legal Proceedings (High-Level)

- **Material witness detention (2002):** Held under a material witness warrant connected to post-9/11 investigations.
- **Military detention (2002–2006):** Classified as an enemy combatant and held in a military brig; this period became central to litigation over due process and executive power.
- **Federal criminal case (2006–2008):** Transferred to the criminal justice system, tried in civilian court, and sentenced after conviction.

## Why This Case Is Notable

- **Executive power vs. civilian courts:** The case is frequently cited in debates about the scope of wartime executive authority over U.S. citizens.
- **National security vs. civil liberties:** It is used as an example in discussions of detention standards, access to counsel, and the role of judicial review.
- **Narrative shift in allegations:** Public claims about a radiological "dirty bomb" did not match the ultimate charging decisions in federal court.
