## Summary
Murder, Inc. was the enforcement arm of the National Crime Syndicate, carrying out an estimated 400-1000 contract murders. Led by Lepke Buchalter and Albert Anastasia. Broken up by prosecutor Burton Turkus.

## Background and Context
- **What it was**: A loosely organized group of hitmen and coordinators linked to the broader **National Crime Syndicate** in the 1930s.
- **Why it formed**: Organized crime groups sought a more reliable, deniable way to handle enforcement—especially around labor rackets, debt collection, and internal discipline.
- **Where it operated**: Primarily **New York City** and surrounding areas, with influence and contracted activity tied to other cities through syndicate relationships.

## Organization and Key Figures
- **Louis "Lepke" Buchalter**: Commonly described as a principal organizer/beneficiary through control of rackets that used contract enforcement.
- **Albert Anastasia**: Often associated with enforcement and intimidation; later a major figure in organized crime.
- **Operational model** (high-level): recruiters/handlers → shooters → disposal/alibi logistics; the emphasis was on compartmentalization to limit exposure.

## Methods and Operational Characteristics
- **Contract killings**: Carried out for payment or to resolve disputes; estimates vary widely in popular accounts.
- **Tactics**: Use of firearms, close-range attacks, and rapid escape; avoidance of predictable patterns when possible.
- **Secrecy**: Reliance on intermediaries and coded communications; fear and internal discipline reduced leaks.

## Investigation and Downfall
- **Turning point**: Cooperation by insiders and associates gave prosecutors corroborated narratives and names.
- **Abe "Kid Twist" Reles**: His cooperation is frequently cited as a catalyst for indictments; his later death (fall from a hotel window while under police protection) became a notorious episode.
- **Burton Turkus**: As a special prosecutor, Turkus became closely identified with the cases that dismantled the group’s public image and operational capacity.

## Legacy
- **Cultural impact**: "Murder, Inc." entered American popular vocabulary as shorthand for organized, contract-based violence.
- **Law enforcement lessons**: Demonstrated the value—and risks—of witness cooperation in organized crime cases.
- **Historical caution**: Many details are filtered through testimony, sensational journalism, and later retellings; careful sourcing matters.
