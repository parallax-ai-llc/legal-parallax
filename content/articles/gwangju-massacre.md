## Summary

South Korean military killed hundreds of pro-democracy protesters in Gwangju over 10 days. The exact death toll remains disputed; estimates range from 165 to over 2,000.

## Context and Background

- **Political context:** The violence occurred amid political turmoil and demands for democratization in South Korea in 1980, shortly after the assassination of President Park Chung-hee (1979) and the subsequent consolidation of power by military leaders.
- **Immediate triggers:** Student and civilian protests in multiple cities intensified in May 1980; in Gwangju, confrontations escalated after security forces used force to disperse demonstrators.

## Key Events (High-Level)

- **May 18, 1980:** Protests and clashes begin in Gwangju.
- **Following days:** Fighting spreads; reports describe severe beatings and shootings by security forces and growing civilian resistance.
- **Late May 1980:** A period of relative control by citizen groups and negotiations is followed by renewed military action.
- **After roughly 10 days:** Security forces retake control of the city.

## Actors and Terms

- **South Korean security forces:** Commonly described in sources as military units and/or special forces deployed to suppress protests.
- **Protesters and citizens:** Students, workers, and residents participated; some accounts describe the formation of citizen militias after weapons were seized from local armories.
- **Naming:** The event is often referred to in English as the *Gwangju Uprising*, *Gwangju Massacre*, or *May 18 Democratic Uprising*; wording can reflect perspective.

## Casualties and Disputed Death Toll

- **Why disputed:** Chaos during the crackdown, differing accounting standards, and political constraints in the aftermath contributed to uncertainty.
- **Range of estimates:** Public estimates vary widely (including official counts and higher unofficial estimates). Many summaries cite **hundreds killed**, while other claims reach into the **thousands**.
- **Ongoing scholarship:** Historians and investigative bodies have continued to refine casualty figures and document individual cases.

## Aftermath and Legacy

- **Domestic impact:** Gwangju became a powerful symbol in South Korea’s democratization movement, shaping public memory and political activism.
- **Truth-seeking:** Later democratic governments and commissions investigated the events, and the episode has been formally recognized in South Korea’s historical narrative in various ways.
- **Commemoration:** Annual memorial events and educational efforts continue, particularly centered on May 18.
