## Summary

Frank Morris and the Anglin brothers escaped from Alcatraz using fake heads, a raft made of raincoats, and a drill made from a vacuum cleaner motor. Never found.

## Background

- **Location**: Alcatraz Federal Penitentiary, an island prison in San Francisco Bay, known for cold water, strong currents, and distance from shore.
- **Participants**: Frank Morris and brothers John and Clarence Anglin were housed in adjacent cells and were considered intelligent, persistent escape risks.

## Planning and Preparation

- The escape involved months of preparation, including enlarging ventilation openings behind the cells and using improvised tools.
- Dummy heads were crafted to resemble sleeping inmates, helping delay discovery during nightly checks.
- A raft and life vests were assembled from waterproof materials (commonly described as repurposed raincoats), with makeshift inflation and sealing methods.

## Escape Night (June 11, 1962)

- The men exited through the widened openings, moved through service corridors, and reached the roof area.
- They descended to the shoreline and launched the improvised raft into the bay during the late-night hours.
- A fourth inmate, Allen West, was associated with the plan but did not depart with the others.

## Investigation and Aftermath

- The escape triggered a major manhunt involving federal and local authorities.
- Physical evidence (e.g., homemade equipment and personal effects) was recovered, but definitive proof of survival was not established.
- The official case remained open for decades due to uncertainty over whether the escapees drowned or reached land.

## Legacy

- The 1962 escape became one of the most famous prison breaks in U.S. history and a central part of Alcatraz’s modern mythology.
- It has been widely covered in books, documentaries, and films, often emphasizing the ingenuity of the methods used.
