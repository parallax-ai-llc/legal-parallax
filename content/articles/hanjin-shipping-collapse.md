## Summary

Hanjin Shipping, the world's seventh-largest container line, collapsed leaving 540,000 cargo containers stranded at sea. The Cho family's mismanagement destroyed the company.

## Background

- **Hanjin Shipping** was a major South Korean container carrier and part of the broader Hanjin Group ecosystem.
- The company grew during decades of expansion in global containerized trade, competing against larger global alliances and operators.

## Contributing Factors (High-Level)

- **Industry overcapacity and low freight rates**: Persistent oversupply in container shipping compressed margins and raised the cost of refinancing.
- **High leverage and refinancing pressure**: Shipping is capital-intensive; large debt loads can become unsustainable when cash flow falls.
- **Corporate governance and strategic missteps**: Public reporting and later commentary emphasized management decisions that failed to stabilize liquidity.
- **Creditor and supplier confidence shock**: Once counterparties doubt payment ability, ships can be denied port services or refused unloading.

## What Happened in 2016

- In late August 2016, the company **filed for court receivership/bankruptcy protection**, triggering a rapid operational freeze.
- Many ports and service providers required **cash payment up front**, leaving vessels unable to berth or discharge.
- The disruption reportedly left **hundreds of thousands of containers** delayed across global routes, affecting retailers and manufacturers.

## Impacts

- **Cargo delays**: Time-sensitive goods (seasonal retail, components) faced missed delivery windows.
- **Supply chain cost spikes**: Shippers paid for re-booking, storage, and alternative transport; insurers and forwarders dealt with uncertainty.
- **Port congestion and legal complexity**: Arrest risks and jurisdiction-specific procedures complicated unloading and container release.

## Aftermath

- The collapse accelerated consolidation pressures in container shipping and strengthened the role of alliances.
- Assets, routes, and contracts were redistributed through sales and restructuring processes, while the brand effectively disappeared from global schedules.

## Lessons (Generalizable)

- **Liquidity matters more than scale** in cyclical, commodity-like industries.
- **Counterparty trust is operational oxygen**: once lost, ships, ports, and banks can halt a carrier faster than formal insolvency.
- **Governance and risk management** are decisive when an industry downturn persists beyond a typical cycle.
