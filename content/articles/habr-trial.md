## Summary

First trial of a former African head of state by an African court. Hissène Habré was convicted of crimes against humanity, war crimes, and torture.

| Year | Event |
|------|-------|
| 2016 | Habré convicted by African court |

## Context and Background

- **Who was on trial:** Hissène Habré, former President of Chad (1982–1990).
- **Core allegations:** Systematic repression by security services during his rule, including torture and political killings.
- **Venue and legal basis:** Proceedings were held in Senegal through a special mechanism known as the **Extraordinary African Chambers** (created within the Senegalese court system with African Union support).

## Key Legal Milestones

- **Victim-driven accountability:** The case advanced through sustained advocacy by victims and human rights organizations over many years.
- **International pressure:** Disputes over prosecution vs. extradition were central to the case’s long pre-trial phase.
- **Landmark nature:** The trial is widely cited as a precedent for **universal jurisdiction** and regional accountability for mass atrocities.

## Why It Matters

- **Regional first:** Often described as the first time a former African head of state was tried and convicted by an African court for international crimes.
- **Survivor testimony:** The record of victim testimony helped document patterns of abuse and establish responsibility.
- **Model for future cases:** The Extraordinary African Chambers are frequently referenced in discussions about hybrid courts and regional mechanisms.
