## Summary

An estimated 300,000 children are used as soldiers worldwide. Groups in Congo, Myanmar, Syria, and elsewhere kidnap and force children to fight, some as young as 7.

## Context and Background

### What the term generally refers to

In most reporting and legal contexts, "child soldier" can include anyone under 18 who is recruited or used by armed forces or armed groups in any capacity, including:

- Combatants and porters
- Cooks, messengers, guards, and spies
- Children used for forced labor or sexual exploitation linked to an armed group

The practice is documented in both international and non-international armed conflicts and can involve abduction, coercion, deception, or economic pressure.

### Common drivers

Factors frequently associated with child recruitment include:

- Collapse of local security and governance during conflict
- Poverty and loss of family livelihoods
- Displacement and separation from caregivers
- Lack of access to schooling and basic services
- Armed groups seeking inexpensive, controllable manpower

## Recruitment Methods and Use

### How recruitment occurs

Documented patterns include:

- Abduction from homes, streets, camps for displaced people, or schools
- Forced conscription and intimidation of families
- "Voluntary" enlistment under duress (survival, protection, food, or pay)
- Ideological or identity-based propaganda and peer pressure

### Roles children are forced or pressured to perform

Beyond frontline fighting, children may be used for:

- Carrying supplies and ammunition
- Acting as lookouts and couriers
- Manning checkpoints or guarding prisoners
- Intelligence gathering and reconnaissance
- Forced "wives" or sexual slavery in some armed groups

## Impacts

### Health and psychosocial consequences

Commonly reported harms include:

- Exposure to severe violence and risk of death or maiming
- Long-term trauma, depression, and anxiety
- Substance use and coercive "conditioning" practices
- Stigmatization and community rejection after return

### Community and security effects

Child recruitment can:

- Prolong conflict by increasing force size at low cost
- Weaken social cohesion and normalize violence among youth
- Create long-term reintegration burdens for families and institutions

## International Law and Accountability (High-Level)

Key international instruments commonly cited in discussions of child soldiers include:

- The Convention on the Rights of the Child (CRC)
- The Optional Protocol on the involvement of children in armed conflict (OPAC)
- The Rome Statute of the International Criminal Court (ICC), which treats conscripting/enlisting children under 15 or using them to participate actively in hostilities as a war crime under specified circumstances

In practice, accountability can involve domestic prosecutions, international tribunals, and sanctions regimes, though enforcement varies widely.

## Prevention, Demobilization, and Reintegration

Approaches described by UN agencies and NGOs often include:

- Preventing recruitment through protection, schooling access, and livelihood support
- Demobilization and release programs with monitoring and verification
- Family tracing and reunification where possible
- Community-based reintegration, education catch-up, and vocational training
- Specialized support for survivors of sexual violence and children with disabilities

A recurring challenge is ensuring that children are treated primarily as victims of unlawful recruitment rather than as perpetrators.

## Data and Measurement Notes

Global estimates (such as widely-cited figures in the hundreds of thousands) are typically presented as approximations because:

- Armed groups may conceal recruitment practices
- Access to conflict zones is limited and politically sensitive
- Definitions and age verification differ across reporting systems
