## Summary

Transparency International's annual ranking measures perceived corruption worldwide. Most corrupt countries consistently include Somalia, South Sudan, Syria, and Venezuela.

## What the CPI Measures

- The Corruption Perceptions Index (CPI) is an annual composite indicator published by Transparency International.
- It summarizes perceptions of public-sector corruption (as assessed by expert assessments and business surveys), rather than counting proven corruption cases.
- Results are typically presented as a score (cleaner ↔ more corrupt) and a rank among jurisdictions included that year.

## Scale and Presentation

- Since 2012, the CPI score is commonly reported on a **0–100** scale, where higher scores indicate **lower perceived corruption**.
- In earlier years, the CPI was commonly presented on a **0–10** scale; comparisons across the 2012 methodological break require care.
- Transparency International usually publishes:
  - a global table of scores and ranks,
  - regional breakdowns,
  - and narrative highlights about year-over-year movements.

## Methodology (High Level)

- The CPI is a **composite**: it aggregates multiple underlying data sources into a single score.
- Only sources that meet Transparency International’s inclusion criteria (coverage, quality, regularity, etc.) are used for a given year.
- Because it is perception-based, changes can reflect both:
  - real changes in governance and enforcement, and
  - shifts in how informed observers view a country’s institutions.

## How to Interpret the Index

- CPI rankings are **relative**: a country’s rank can change even if its score is stable, depending on other countries’ movements.
- Small year-to-year score changes may not be meaningful; emphasis is often placed on sustained multi-year trends.
- The CPI is best used as a **starting point** for analysis, combined with country-specific context and complementary indicators.

## Common Uses

- Policy discussions and governance reform agendas.
- Academic research and cross-country comparisons (with methodology caveats).
- Risk assessment in business and investment contexts.
- Advocacy and public communication about integrity and accountability.

## Limitations (Frequently Noted)

- Measures **perceptions**, not direct incidence of corruption.
- Coverage and source availability can vary by country and year.
- Methodological revisions (notably around 2012) complicate long-run comparisons.
- “Public-sector corruption” is a broad umbrella; the CPI does not pinpoint specific sectors (e.g., procurement vs. courts) without additional data.

## Related Concepts and Adjacent Measures

- Sector- or institution-specific governance measures (e.g., rule of law, government effectiveness) are often used alongside CPI.
- Transparency International also publishes related research and thematic reports that provide qualitative detail beyond a single score.
