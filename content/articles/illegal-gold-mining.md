## Summary

Illegal gold mining generates $20 billion annually, causing massive environmental destruction and mercury poisoning. In the Amazon, criminal groups use slave labor and destroy indigenous lands.

## What It Is

Illegal gold mining refers to gold extraction carried out without required permits, in prohibited areas (including protected forests and indigenous territories), or in ways that evade taxes, labor standards, and environmental rules.[^1] It spans small-scale “artisanal” operations through to organized networks that finance equipment, launder gold through supply chains, and corrupt local enforcement.

## Common Techniques

- **Alluvial mining and dredging:** Removing river sediments to capture gold, often increasing turbidity and changing waterways.
- **Mercury amalgamation (ASGM):** Mercury binds to gold; burning the amalgam releases **toxic mercury vapor** and leaves mercury in soils and waterways.[^2]
- **Hard-rock processing:** Crushing ore and using chemicals (sometimes cyanide in legal contexts; illegal use can occur) to recover gold.

## Environmental and Health Impacts

- **Deforestation and habitat loss:** Clearing forest for pits, camps, access roads, and fuel.
- **River and soil damage:** Erosion, sedimentation, and altered river courses; damaged fisheries and drinking water sources.
- **Mercury contamination:** Bioaccumulation in fish and exposure in mining communities; risks include neurological and developmental harm.[^2]
- **Long tail of cleanup:** Abandoned pits and tailings can persist for decades, with continuing contamination risks.

## Human and Governance Impacts

- **Labor exploitation:** Reports frequently describe debt bondage, unsafe work, and coercion in illicit mining areas.[^1]
- **Violence and criminal control:** Some sites are linked to armed groups and organized crime that control territory, logistics, and trading.
- **Gold laundering:** Illicit gold can be mixed with legal production and sold into formal markets through weak traceability systems.[^3]

## Geographic Hotspots (Illustrative)

- **Amazon basin (e.g., Brazil, Peru, Colombia):** Alluvial mining and dredging in remote river networks; high deforestation and mercury concerns.[^1]
- **West Africa (e.g., Ghana “galamsey”):** River pollution and farmland damage have driven repeated enforcement campaigns.[^1]
- **Central Africa (e.g., eastern DRC):** Conflict-affected areas where mineral revenues may intersect with armed actors.[^3]
- **Southeast Asia (e.g., Indonesia, Philippines):** ASGM with mercury use remains a major issue in some regions.[^2]

## Signals and Indicators

- Sudden growth of **dredges**, excavators, or fuel/mercury supply lines in remote areas
- **Spikes in sediment** in satellite imagery along rivers and forest frontiers
- Discrepancies between **export volumes** and plausible domestic production
- Local health advisories about **fish consumption** due to mercury levels

## Responses and Mitigation (High-Level)

- **Traceability and due diligence:** Stronger chain-of-custody controls, audits, and import checks for high-risk gold.[^3]
- **Alternative livelihoods and formalization:** Programs that help legitimate small-scale miners comply with rules and reduce mercury use.[^2]
- **Environmental enforcement:** Targeting machinery, fuel, and finance networks—not only miners—can be more effective than sporadic raids.
- **Mercury reduction:** Promoting retorts, safer processing, and mercury-free technologies where feasible.[^2]
