## Summary

Illegal gold miners (garimpeiros) have devastated the Amazon, contaminating rivers with mercury and invading indigenous territories. Under Bolsonaro, illegal mining surged 46%.

## Background and Terminology

- **Garimpo**: small-scale or informal gold mining; in the Amazon it often overlaps with illegal operations.
- **Garimpeiros**: people involved in garimpo mining.
- **Alluvial mining**: extraction of gold from riverbeds and floodplains, frequently using dredges and pumps.

## Why It Matters

- **Mercury pollution**: mercury is commonly used to form an amalgam with gold, and can enter waterways and food chains.
- **Deforestation and habitat loss**: mining sites clear forest, open roads/airstrips, and can expand secondary logging.
- **Indigenous territorial intrusion**: illegal operations have repeatedly been reported inside or near protected Indigenous lands, increasing health and security risks.

## Common Operational Pattern (High-Level)

1. Entry via rivers/roads, sometimes with improvised airstrips.
2. Rapid setup of dredges, pumps, hoses, and camp infrastructure.
3. Extraction and on-site processing (often including mercury use).
4. Gold is moved into regional trading networks where origin can be obscured.

## Documented Impacts (Representative)

- **Water quality** degradation (turbidity, sedimentation) affecting fisheries and drinking water.
- **Public health** concerns from mercury exposure (neurological and developmental risks).
- **Violence and coercion** risks tied to criminal networks operating along supply routes.

## Governance and Enforcement (Overview)

- Brazil has multiple enforcement bodies (environmental, federal police, and military support at times), but remote geography and economics make sustained enforcement difficult.
- Policy choices and enforcement intensity can correlate with changes in detected illegal mining activity.
