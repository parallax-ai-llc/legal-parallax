## Historical Context (Congo Free State)

- The Congo Free State (1885–1908) was the personal possession of King Leopold II of Belgium.
- The regime relied on concessionary companies and the *Force Publique* to extract ivory and rubber.
- Communities were coerced through hostage-taking, forced labor, mutilation, and killings to meet quotas.
- International criticism grew through missionary reports, photographs, and investigative writing.
- The Congo Reform Association (founded 1904) helped catalyze global pressure and documentation.
- In 1908, Belgium annexed the territory, ending Leopold II’s direct rule (without instantly ending abuses).

## Kurtz as a Literary Archetype

- "Kurtz" is best known as a fictional figure in Joseph Conrad’s *Heart of Darkness* (1899).
- The character is commonly read as an emblem of how unchecked power and distance from oversight enable brutality.
- Many interpretations treat Kurtz less as a single biographical person and more as a composite drawn from colonial reality.
- The name appears in later adaptations and cultural references as shorthand for "going native" and moral collapse.

## Related Historical Figures Often Mentioned in Comparisons

- **King Leopold II**: Architect of the Congo Free State’s extractive system and its propaganda apparatus.
- **Roger Casement**: British consul whose 1904 report documented atrocities and influenced reform efforts.
- **E.D. Morel**: Journalist/activist who exposed trade patterns consistent with forced extraction.
- **Léon Rom**: Congo Free State officer associated in historical accounts with macabre displays and violence.

## Why This Topic Matters

- The Congo Free State is a widely cited example of how economic incentives, weak oversight, and racist ideology can scale violence.
- It shaped later debates about humanitarian intervention, international advocacy campaigns, and colonial accountability.
- The "Kurtz" figure persists because it compresses a complex political economy into a memorable moral warning.
