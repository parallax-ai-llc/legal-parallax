## Summary

Nazi war criminal responsible for deporting 128,000 Jews to death camps, believed to have died in Syria.

## Background

- Brunner was an Austrian member of the SS who became one of Adolf Eichmann's key assistants in the deportation of Jews from Austria, France, Greece, and Slovakia during World War II.
- He is commonly described in historical summaries as a principal organizer of transports to Nazi killing centers, with reported responsibility for tens of thousands of deportations.

## Role in Nazi Deportation System (High Level)

- **Vienna (Austria)**: Participated in the forced emigration and deportation machinery aimed at removing Jews from Austria, later shifting fully to deportations.
- **France (notably Drancy)**: Associated with the administration of the Drancy internment camp and the coordination of deportation convoys.
- **Operational pattern**: Used bureaucratic control—registries, transport lists, intimidation, and confiscation—to enable mass deportations with minimal public visibility.

## Postwar Flight and Evasion

- After the war, Brunner avoided prosecution in the immediate postwar period and later relocated to the Middle East.
- He is widely reported to have lived in **Syria** under an alias and maintained long-term protection that made extradition or capture difficult.

## Attempts to Bring Him to Justice

- Multiple countries pursued investigations and legal actions related to Brunner's wartime activities.
- Public reporting and historical accounts note attempts to locate him in Syria over decades, but without a confirmed arrest.
- Some sources report that he was tried and convicted **in absentia** in France for crimes against humanity.

## Additional Timeline (Selected)

- **1930s–1940s**: Rose within SS structures tied to Jewish deportation policy and implementation.
- **1940s (WWII)**: Worked in territories under German control, linked to deportations from several European countries.
- **Post-1945**: Evaded capture; moved through escape networks before settling in Syria.
- **Late 20th century**: International attention increased as Nazi-hunter efforts and archival research expanded.

## Why He Matters (Historical Significance)

- Brunner is frequently cited as an example of a mid-to-senior level functionary whose effectiveness in administration and coercion directly enabled large-scale mass murder.
- His decades-long evasion illustrates the limits of postwar accountability when perpetrators found protection in jurisdictions unwilling to extradite.
