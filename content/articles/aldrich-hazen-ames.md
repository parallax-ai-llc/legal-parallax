## Summary

Duplicate consolidated.

Aldrich Hazen Ames was a Central Intelligence Agency (CIA) officer who became one of the most damaging U.S. counterintelligence failures of the late Cold War. While assigned to sensitive roles that dealt directly with Soviet intelligence targets, he provided information to the KGB (and later to the SVR) that compromised multiple U.S. operations and human sources.

His case is commonly cited in discussions of counterintelligence tradecraft, insider-threat detection, and the organizational challenges of distinguishing lifestyle anomalies from legitimate income.

## Key Points (High-Level)

- **Role**: CIA officer with access to information about Soviet intelligence operations and U.S. human sources.
- **Espionage**: Passed information to Soviet intelligence services during the final decade of the Cold War.
- **Damage**: Widely described as severe, including the compromise of sensitive operations and human assets.
- **Detection**: Became a focus of investigation amid unexplained operational failures and financial/lifestyle indicators.

## Why the Case Matters

- **Insider threat**: Demonstrates how trusted access can produce outsized damage when controls are weak.
- **Operational security**: Shows how the compromise of a small set of identifiers can cascade across multiple cases.
- **Institutional lessons**: Commonly used as a cautionary example for continuous evaluation, audits, and anomaly detection.
