## Summary

French poet who was also a convicted murderer and thief, sentenced to hanging before his sentence was commuted.

## Background

- Villon was active in mid-15th-century France, a period shaped by the aftermath of the Hundred Years' War and social upheaval in Paris.
- He studied at the University of Paris and was associated with student life and the city’s criminal underworld.

## Major Works

- **_Le Lais_** (also known as **_Le Petit Testament_**) — an early long poem structured as a mock bequest.
- **_Le Testament_** (also known as **_Le Grand Testament_**) — his best-known work, combining satire, personal reflection, and moral meditation.
- **Ballads and rondeaux** — shorter lyric forms that include famous pieces such as the “Ballade des pendus” (Ballad of the Hanged Men).

## Themes and Style

- **Mortality and judgment:** recurring reflections on death, punishment, and salvation.
- **Irony and dark humor:** blends repentance with self-mockery and social satire.
- **Street-level realism:** references to Parisian places, slang, and everyday hardships.
- **Literary craftsmanship:** formal virtuosity within fixed poetic forms, especially the ballade.

## Legacy

- Often cited as a major late-medieval French poet whose voice feels unusually personal and immediate.
- Became a touchstone for later writers interested in the intersection of lyric art, marginality, and autobiography.
