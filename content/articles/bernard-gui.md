## Summary

French Dominican inquisitor who conducted over 900 trials and wrote the manual for inquisitorial procedure.

## Historical Context

Bernard Gui worked during a period when the medieval Church used inquisitorial courts to investigate and prosecute heresy, especially in southern France where Cathar and Waldensian movements had been strong in prior decades.

## Roles and Reputation

- **Dominican friar and administrator:** A member of the Dominican Order (Order of Preachers), he held offices within the order and the Church.
- **Papal inquisitor:** He is best known for serving as an inquisitor in the early 14th century, especially associated with the Toulouse region.
- **Record-keeping:** Surviving registers and procedural writing make him a visible figure in modern historiography compared with many contemporaries.

## Major Work

### *Practica Inquisitionis heretice pravitatis* ("Practice of the Inquisition of Heretical Depravity")

Often described as a practical manual for inquisitors, this work is associated with:

- Standardized questioning and investigation practices
- Categorizing suspected groups and offenses
- Guidance on procedure and the handling of evidence and testimony

## What "900 Trials" Usually Means

Popular summaries sometimes cite that Gui conducted **hundreds** of inquisitorial proceedings. In many discussions, this refers to the volume of cases recorded in surviving documentation from his tenure rather than a single continuous courtroom docket in the modern sense.

## Legacy in Popular Culture

Bernard Gui is widely recognized outside academic history because a character bearing his name appears as an antagonist in Umberto Eco's novel *The Name of the Rose* (and its film adaptation). The fictional portrayal is often more dramatic than historical accounts.
