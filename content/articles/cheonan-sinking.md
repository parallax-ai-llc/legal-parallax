## Summary

North Korean submarine torpedo sank the South Korean naval corvette Cheonan killing 46 sailors. North Korea denied responsibility despite overwhelming international evidence.

## Background

- The ROKS *Cheonan* (PCC-772) was a South Korean Navy Pohang-class corvette operating in waters near Baengnyeong Island in the Yellow Sea.
- The incident occurred amid longstanding inter-Korean military tension and periodic maritime clashes around the Northern Limit Line (NLL).

## Incident (March 2010)

- The ship broke apart and sank during a nighttime patrol; 46 South Korean sailors died and several were injured.
- Survivors were rescued by nearby vessels; a prolonged salvage operation later recovered the wreck.

## Investigation and Findings

- South Korea organized a Multinational Joint Investigation Group (JIG) involving domestic experts and specialists from several partner countries.
- The JIG reported that a torpedo explosion caused the sinking and attributed the attack to North Korea; North Korea rejected the conclusion.

## International Response

- The incident prompted diplomatic efforts at the United Nations and sharpened regional security concerns.
- A UN Security Council presidential statement (2010) condemned the attack that led to the sinking without explicitly naming a perpetrator.

## Dispute and Public Debate

- While the South Korean government and many international partners accepted the JIG’s conclusion, some analysts and civic groups raised questions about evidence interpretation and alternative explanations.
- The event remains a recurring reference point in South Korean domestic politics and inter-Korean relations.

## Legacy

- The sinking contributed to changes in South Korean defense posture and rules-of-engagement discussions.
- Memorials and annual commemorations continue for the sailors who died.
