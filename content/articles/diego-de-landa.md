## Summary

Spanish bishop who destroyed virtually all Mayan manuscripts and tortured indigenous people during the Inquisition.

## Background

- Franciscan friar active in the Spanish colony of Yucatán (present-day Mexico) during the 16th century.
- Worked amid Spain’s broader campaign of Christianization that often treated indigenous religious objects and writings as “idolatry.”

## Notable Actions and Allegations

- Associated with an inquisition-style investigation in Yucatán that used coercion and torture against Maya communities.
- Best known for overseeing the destruction of Maya religious items and the burning of many indigenous books/codices in 1562.
- The event is often referenced as a major loss for pre-Columbian Maya written history, because only a handful of Maya codices survive today.

## Later Career

- After controversy over the 1562 actions, he was called back to Spain for review and later returned to Yucatán.
- Served as Bishop of Yucatán (appointed later in life), giving him substantial authority in the region’s colonial church hierarchy.

## Writings and Historical Impact

- Authored *Relación de las cosas de Yucatán*, a colonial-era account describing Maya society, religion, and practices as he understood them.
- The work includes a flawed but historically influential “alphabet” (often called the Landa alphabet) that later helped scholars in the decipherment of Maya script.
- His legacy is therefore dual: a figure linked to cultural destruction and abuse, but also a source text used by later historians and epigraphers.

## Legacy and Controversy

- Remembered in many modern accounts as a central perpetrator in the suppression of Maya culture.
- Cited in debates about the role of religious institutions in colonial violence and the archival loss caused by book burnings.
