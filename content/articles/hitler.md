- Consolidated power into a one-party dictatorship and assumed the title Führer in 1934.

## Key Context

- **Ideology and movement:** Hitler's politics centered on extreme nationalism, racial hierarchy, antisemitism, and anti-communism, articulated through Nazi propaganda and state policy.
- **Power consolidation:** After 1933, the regime used emergency decrees, police powers, censorship, and intimidation to eliminate opposition and create a totalitarian state.
- **Expansion and war:** German rearmament and territorial expansion (e.g., Austria and parts of Czechoslovakia) preceded the invasion of Poland and the escalation into a continent-wide war.

## Mass Violence and Crimes

- The Nazi state conducted systematic persecution and mass murder of Jews (the Holocaust), along with Roma, disabled people, political opponents, and other targeted groups.
- The regime's war aims and occupation policies involved widespread atrocities, forced labor, and starvation policies across Europe.

## End and Legacy

- Hitler's suicide in April 1945 marked the collapse of Nazi leadership as Allied forces occupied Germany.
- In the postwar era, Nazi leaders and institutions were prosecuted in international and domestic trials (e.g., Nuremberg).
- Hitler and the Nazi period remain central to scholarship on dictatorship, propaganda, genocide, and the causes and conduct of World War II.
