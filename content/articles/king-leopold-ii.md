## Context and Early Life

- Leopold II (r. 1865–1909) was the second King of the Belgians.
- He came to power during a period when European states were accelerating overseas imperial expansion.
- In Belgium, he promoted colonial acquisition partly as a means of increasing national prestige and economic opportunity.

## Imperial Project: The Congo Free State

- Leopold II pursued control of a territory in Central Africa that became known as the **Congo Free State** (État Indépendant du Congo).
- Although presented internationally as a philanthropic and “civilizing” enterprise, the administration was structured to extract resources.
- The Congo Free State was **Leopold’s personal possession**, distinct from the Belgian state until annexation.

## System of Extraction and Coercion

- The Congo economy under Leopold relied heavily on forced labor, particularly tied to the extraction of ivory and later rubber.
- Communities were subjected to quotas and punitive measures for non-compliance.
- Contemporary reports and later historical research describe widespread violence and severe human costs; estimates of deaths vary widely across sources.

## International Scrutiny and Reform Efforts

- By the late 19th and early 20th century, missionaries, diplomats, journalists, and activists brought attention to abuses.
- Prominent figures associated with documentation and advocacy included:
  - **E. D. Morel** (journalist and campaigner)
  - **Roger Casement** (British diplomat whose reporting helped catalyze international concern)
  - The **Congo Reform Association** (a major early human-rights advocacy movement)

## Transfer to Belgian Control (1908)

- Under sustained international and domestic pressure, control of the Congo was transferred from Leopold II to the Belgian state.
- In 1908, the territory became the **Belgian Congo**, ending Leopold’s direct personal rule.

## Legacy

- Leopold II’s legacy is sharply contested.
- In many historical assessments, his rule is inseparable from the Congo Free State’s system of coercion and mass suffering.
- Public debates over monuments, commemoration, and national memory have continued in Belgium and internationally.
