## Summary

Region in the Atlantic where ships and aircraft have allegedly disappeared under mysterious circumstances. While most incidents have conventional explanations, the mythology persists.

## Geography and Definition

The "triangle" is commonly described as an area in the western North Atlantic roughly bounded by Miami (Florida), Bermuda, and San Juan (Puerto Rico). The boundaries vary by source and there is no officially recognized or uniformly agreed-upon perimeter.

## Notable Incidents Often Cited

- **1918 — USS *Cyclops***: A U.S. Navy collier that disappeared with a large loss of life; frequently referenced in Bermuda Triangle discussions.
- **1945 — Flight 19**: A U.S. Navy training flight of five TBM Avenger aircraft that vanished; a rescue aircraft was also lost.
- **1948 — *Star Tiger***: A British South American Airways passenger flight that disappeared over the Atlantic.
- **1948 — Douglas DC-3 (NC16002)**: A commercial flight reported trouble and was never recovered.
- **1949 — *Star Ariel***: Another British South American Airways aircraft that disappeared in the region.

## Common Conventional Explanations

Many disappearances and accidents in the area are discussed alongside ordinary risk factors for busy ocean and air routes:

- **Weather and sea state**: Rapidly changing storms, squalls, and rough seas can overwhelm vessels and complicate search-and-rescue.
- **Navigation and communication issues**: Historical limitations in navigation aids and radio reliability, plus human error, can contribute to incidents.
- **Currents and drift**: The **Gulf Stream** can move debris and wreckage quickly, reducing the chance of recovery and increasing uncertainty.
- **Traffic density**: High volumes of maritime and aviation traffic increase the number of incidents simply by exposure.

## Mythology and Media Influence

Modern popular awareness of the Bermuda Triangle grew substantially in the mid-to-late 20th century through magazine articles and best-selling books that framed incidents as mysterious and sometimes linked them to paranormal explanations. Later investigations and skeptical treatments have argued that some popular accounts selectively cite cases, merge unrelated incidents, or omit relevant context.

## Key Claims vs. Evidence (High-Level)

- The area is **not** identified by major navigation authorities as uniquely hazardous compared with similar heavily traveled regions.
- Reported cases often have **incomplete records** (especially older maritime losses), which can leave space for speculation.
- Where documentation exists, many incidents are consistent with **ordinary causes** (weather, mechanical failure, navigation error).

## Notes

This topic is best treated as a blend of maritime/aviation history, popular culture, and the study of how legends form around real but explainable events.
