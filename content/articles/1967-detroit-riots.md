## Summary

One of the deadliest riots in US history killed 43 people when police raided an unlicensed bar. The National Guard and Army were deployed; 7,200 were arrested.

## Context and Causes

- **Housing and segregation**: Detroit’s rapid postwar growth and discriminatory housing practices contributed to crowded neighborhoods and deep racial segregation.
- **Policing and community tension**: Long-running friction between predominantly white police forces and Black residents, including complaints of aggressive enforcement and unequal treatment.
- **Economic pressure**: Deindustrialization and job loss pressures were emerging by the late 1960s, worsening inequality and neighborhood disinvestment.

## Trigger and Early Hours

- **Police raid (July 23, 1967)**: The uprising began after a police raid on an unlicensed after-hours bar (a “blind pig”) during a gathering celebrating two returning Vietnam veterans.
- **Escalation**: Crowds formed, property damage and looting spread, and arson became a major driver of destruction over subsequent days.

## Government Response

- **Local and state actions**: Detroit police were quickly overwhelmed; Michigan authorities mobilized the **National Guard**.
- **Federal deployment**: US Army troops were sent in after state request as violence intensified.
- **Curfew and stabilization**: Curfews and large-scale patrols were used to restore order; the crisis lasted roughly **five days**.

## Impact

- **Deaths and injuries**: 43 people were killed, with hundreds injured.
- **Arrests**: About **7,200** arrests were recorded.
- **Property destruction**: Thousands of buildings were damaged or destroyed; arson produced long-term scars in affected corridors.

## Aftermath and Legacy

- **National significance**: The Detroit uprising became a defining event of the “Long Hot Summer” era and a reference point in debates over civil rights, policing, and urban policy.
- **Investigations and reporting**: The **Kerner Commission** (National Advisory Commission on Civil Disorders) studied riots of the period and argued that the US was moving toward “two societies, one black, one white—separate and unequal.”
- **Urban change**: The event accelerated patterns of disinvestment and population movement already underway, shaping Detroit’s political economy for decades.
