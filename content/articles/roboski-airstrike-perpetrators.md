## Summary

Turkish military personnel who killed 34 Kurdish civilians including children in a border airstrike.

## Career Timeline

| Year | Event |
|------|-------|
| 2011 | Airstrike killed 34 Kurdish civilians |

## Incident Context

- **Location:** Roboski (Uludere), Şırnak Province, near the Turkey–Iraq border.
- **Victims:** 34 Kurdish civilians, including children, who were returning from cross-border trade.
- **Strike type:** Aerial bombardment conducted by Turkish military forces after the group was reportedly misidentified as militants.

## Accountability and Investigations

Public reporting frequently describes the **direct individual perpetrators as not formally identified in a way that produced criminal accountability**. Key high-level points commonly cited in summaries include:

- **Official framing:** Authorities characterized the strike as a mistaken targeting decision under counterinsurgency pressures.
- **Investigative outcomes:** Multiple investigatory and judicial processes were reported over subsequent years, but the case became widely known as **unresolved in terms of naming and convicting those responsible**.
- **Public controversy:** The incident remains a prominent example in debates over civilian protection, intelligence reliability, and accountability in state security operations.

## Why “Perpetrators” Are Often Listed as Unknown

In many datasets and short encyclopedic summaries, “perpetrators” are recorded as a collective (e.g., “Turkish military personnel”) because:

- decision-making and authorization occurred across **several layers** (intelligence, command, and execution), and
- no single publicly confirmed list of individuals is consistently cited as responsible in final legal outcomes.

## Naming / alternate references

- The incident is also widely referred to in English-language reporting as the **Uludere airstrike** or **Uludere massacre**, after the district name Uludere (near Roboski).
- In many databases, the "perpetrator" field is recorded collectively (state/military) because individual names are not consistently present in public, final judicial outcomes.

## Decision chain (high-level)

Even when the operators are not individually named, public discussions typically distinguish:

- **intelligence inputs** (how the group was identified),
- **command authorization** (who approved the strike), and
- **execution** (aircraft/crew carrying out the attack).

This is one reason "perpetrators" can remain ambiguous in open sources: responsibility is distributed across a chain of decisions rather than a single actor.

## Why the event remains politically salient

- The incident is frequently cited in Turkish domestic politics and Kurdish rights advocacy as a test case for civilian protection and state accountability.
- It is also referenced in broader discussions about airpower, border security, and the risks of misidentification in counterinsurgency environments.
