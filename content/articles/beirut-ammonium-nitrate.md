## Summary

2,750 tonnes of ammonium nitrate stored in Beirut's port for 6 years exploded with the force of a small nuclear bomb. Government officials knew of the danger but did nothing.

## What It Was

- **Material**: Ammonium nitrate (NH4NO3), a common industrial chemical used in fertilizers and some explosives.
- **Quantity**: Widely reported as **about 2,750 metric tonnes** stored at the Port of Beirut.
- **Hazard profile**: Not inherently explosive under all conditions, but can **detonate when contaminated, confined, heated, or exposed to intense fire**, especially in large masses.

## How It Ended Up in Beirut (High-Level)

- The stockpile is commonly traced to cargo from the ship **MV *Rhosus***, which was linked in reporting and official narratives to the presence of ammonium nitrate in Beirut.
- The cargo was **unloaded and stored** in a port warehouse rather than promptly re-exported or safely disposed of.
- Over time, the material remained in storage amid **administrative disputes and legal/ownership ambiguity**, contributing to prolonged risk.

## Warnings and Governance Failures

- Multiple Lebanese authorities and port-related actors were reported to have **received warnings** over the years about the danger of storing the material in an urban port.
- Failures often described in coverage and public discourse include:
  - **Diffusion of responsibility** across agencies.
  - **Inaction despite documented memos** and risk notices.
  - **Inadequate storage conditions** for a large hazardous stockpile.

## The Explosion (2020-08-04)

- The event is commonly described as a **major blast at the Port of Beirut** preceded by a **fire** in or near the storage area.
- The explosion produced a **large shockwave** causing extensive damage across Beirut and surrounding areas.
- Reported human impact includes:
  - **Over 200 deaths** (often cited as ~218 in early summaries; later counts are frequently higher depending on reporting date).
  - **Thousands injured**.
  - **Mass displacement** due to damaged housing.

## Immediate Impact

- **Infrastructure**: Severe destruction of port facilities and nearby neighborhoods.
- **Healthcare**: Hospitals faced surges of casualties while some facilities were themselves damaged.
- **Economy & logistics**: The port, a critical import hub, suffered major disruption.

## Investigations and Accountability (High-Level)

- The blast triggered domestic and international calls for **transparent investigation** and **accountability**.
- Public reporting has described:
  - Political and legal disputes about jurisdiction and responsibility.
  - Periodic halts and challenges affecting investigative progress.

## Why It Matters

- The Beirut blast is widely treated as a defining case study in:
  - **Catastrophic risk from improper hazardous-material storage**.
  - **Institutional inertia** and failure to act on known hazards.
  - The need for clear chain-of-command and safety enforcement in ports.

## Key Takeaways (Factual, High-Level)

- Large quantities of oxidizers like ammonium nitrate require **strict segregation, ventilation, contamination control, and fire prevention**.
- Risk governance requires **clear ownership**: someone must be empowered and obligated to remediate hazards.
- Warning systems and paperwork are not enough without **execution** (removal, neutralization, or safe re-handling).
