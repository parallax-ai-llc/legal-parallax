## Summary

Guards stole $282 million from the Dar Es Salaam bank in Baghdad, making it the largest cash bank robbery in history. The theft exploited the chaos of the Iraq War.

## Background and Context

- **Location and period:** The robbery took place in **Baghdad, Iraq**, during the instability and administrative disruption of the **post-2003 Iraq War** period.
- **Why the environment mattered:** Large cash movements, disrupted oversight, and reliance on armed guards for physical security created conditions where insider theft could be attempted at scale.

## What Happened (High-Level)

- **Insider involvement:** The incident is widely described as an **inside job** involving security personnel/guards assigned to protect the bank.
- **Method:** Rather than a violent smash-and-grab, reporting emphasizes the exploitation of **access and trust**, enabling removal of cash from the vault.
- **Scale:** The theft is commonly cited at **about US$282 million**, making it one of the largest (and often labeled the largest) cash bank robberies.

## Immediate Aftermath

- **Discovery and response:** The disappearance of funds was reportedly detected after the guards involved failed to report for duty, prompting an investigation.
- **Investigative constraints:** In a conflict zone, **jurisdiction, chain-of-custody, and enforcement capacity** can be limited, complicating recovery.

## Significance

- **Record-setting nature:** The event is frequently referenced as a benchmark case for **high-value cash theft**.
- **Security lesson:** It illustrates that the greatest risk to physical cash storage can be **authorized access** (insider threat) rather than external attackers.
- **Institutional impact:** Such incidents can accelerate reforms around **dual control, vault access logs, cash reconciliation, and guard rotation**.

## Notes on Reporting and Uncertainty

- **Amount and classification:** Public sources commonly repeat the figure and description, but precise verification can be difficult in wartime conditions.
- **Outcomes:** Widely available summaries do not consistently document full details on prosecution or recovery, and some aspects remain unclear in secondary reporting.
