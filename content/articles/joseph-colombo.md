## Summary
Joseph Colombo Sr. was boss of the Colombo crime family. He founded the Italian-American Civil Rights League. Shot at a rally in 1971, he survived but was left in a vegetative state until his death.

## Additional Context

### Early Life and Background
- Joseph Colombo Sr. was born in New York City and grew up in an Italian-American community.
- He became involved with organized crime in the mid-20th century, during an era when New York’s major Mafia families shaped much of the city’s underworld economy.

### Public Activism: Italian-American Civil Rights League (IACRL)
- In 1970, Colombo helped create the Italian-American Civil Rights League, presenting it as an anti-discrimination organization.
- The group drew significant media attention and held high-profile public rallies.
- Critics and law-enforcement officials argued the IACRL also served to challenge investigative focus on organized crime and to influence public narratives about Italian-American identity.

### 1971 Shooting and Aftermath
- On June 28, 1971, Colombo was shot during a public rally in Manhattan.
- He survived the attack but sustained severe injuries and remained incapacitated for the rest of his life.
- After the shooting, leadership within the Colombo crime family shifted as other figures assumed operational control.

### Legacy
- Colombo is remembered for the unusual combination of alleged Mafia leadership and overt public political-style organizing.
- The IACRL remains a notable case study in how ethnic-identity advocacy, media strategy, and criminal-allegiance allegations can intersect.

### Key Takeaways
- **Uncommon visibility:** Unlike many organized-crime leaders who avoided publicity, Colombo pursued public-facing activism.
- **Narrative strategy:** The IACRL leveraged claims of stereotyping to contest the language used by media and authorities.
- **Pivotal event:** The 1971 shooting effectively ended Colombo’s active role and reshaped the family’s internal power dynamics.
