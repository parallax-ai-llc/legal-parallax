## Summary
Vincent 'Mad Dog' Coll was a New York gangster notorious for accidentally killing a five-year-old boy during a street shooting. He was shot dead in a phone booth at age 23.

## Background
- **Birth name**: Vincent Coll (often reported as Irish-born; many summaries describe him as an Irish immigrant who grew up in New York).
- **Era and setting**: Prohibition-era New York, when bootlegging and rackets drove frequent armed conflict between gangs.

## Notability
- Coll became infamous after a **1931 street shooting** in which a **five-year-old child was killed** by gunfire intended for an underworld target. The incident intensified public outrage over gang violence and helped cement his nickname in the press.

## Criminal Activity (high-level)
- Worked as an **enforcer / gunman** in the New York underworld.
- Became involved in disputes tied to **bootlegging operations** and territorial control.
- Developed a reputation for **recklessness**, which made him both feared and a liability to allies.

## Key Events (selected)
- **Late 1920s**: Emerged as a young gunman during the peak of Prohibition violence.
- **1931**: Linked in contemporary reporting to a shooting that killed a child, drawing major attention from newspapers and law enforcement.
- **1932**: **Assassinated in a phone booth** in New York City, a style of hit associated with organized-crime retaliation and deterrence.

## Death
- Coll was shot and killed at **age 23** while using a public phone booth. His killing is commonly framed as the end of a short, highly visible career marked by escalating conflicts.

## Legacy
- Frequently cited as an example of how **public sympathy shifted** when bystanders—especially children—were harmed during gang warfare.
- Appears in many popular histories of Prohibition as a cautionary figure: a young enforcer whose notoriety accelerated both his pursuit by rivals and his vulnerability.
