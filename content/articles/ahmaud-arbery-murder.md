## Summary

Three white men chased and murdered Black jogger Ahmaud Arbery in Georgia. Despite video evidence, arrests didn't come for 74 days. All three were convicted of murder and federal hate crimes.

## Background and Context

- **Location**: Satilla Shores neighborhood near Brunswick, Glynn County, Georgia.
- The case became a major U.S. flashpoint about **racial violence**, **vigilantism**, and the treatment of Black victims in the criminal-justice system.
- Public attention escalated after a cellphone video of the shooting circulated widely online in May 2020.

## Key People

- **Ahmaud Arbery**: 25-year-old Black man who was running in the neighborhood when he was pursued and shot.
- **Gregory McMichael** and **Travis McMichael**: father and son who joined the pursuit and shooting.
- **William "Roddie" Bryan**: neighbor who joined the pursuit and recorded video that later became central evidence.

## What Happened (High-Level)

- Arbery was **followed and confronted** by the men in vehicles while he was jogging.
- The confrontation ended with Arbery being **shot and killed**.
- The defendants later claimed they suspected Arbery of burglary and attempted a citizen’s arrest; prosecutors argued the pursuit and use of force were unlawful and racially motivated.

## Investigation and Arrests

- Local handling of the case drew scrutiny because **arrests were delayed** for more than two months.
- After the video became public and the case drew national attention, state authorities became more directly involved and arrests followed.

## Trials and Outcomes

- **State trial (Georgia)**: the three men were convicted on charges including **malice murder** and other counts (verdicts in late 2021).
- **Federal case**: the defendants were also convicted of **federal hate crimes** and related charges in 2022.
- Sentences included **life terms**; the state convictions and federal convictions addressed different legal theories (state homicide offenses vs. federal civil-rights/hate-crime violations).

## Why the Case Matters

- The case is often cited in debates about:
  - the limits and misuse of **citizen’s arrest** laws (Georgia later changed its law in 2021),
  - how viral evidence and public pressure can affect accountability,
  - the intersection of **race**, **self-defense claims**, and **community vigilantism**.
