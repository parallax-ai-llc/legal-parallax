## Summary

See Red Brigades. Italian PM Aldo Moro was kidnapped by the Red Brigades in 1978 and held for 55 days before being murdered. His body was found in the trunk of a car in Rome.

## Background and Context

- **Aldo Moro** was a leading figure in Italy’s Christian Democracy (DC) and a former prime minister.
- In the late 1970s, Italy faced severe political violence during the **“Years of Lead”** (anni di piombo), with attacks carried out by both far-left and far-right extremist groups.
- The **Red Brigades (Brigate Rosse)** aimed to destabilize the state and present themselves as an alternative revolutionary authority.

## What Happened (High-Level)

- **Ambush (Rome, 16 March 1978):** Moro’s convoy was attacked; members of his security detail were killed, and Moro was abducted.
- **Captivity (55 days):** The Red Brigades held Moro in a secret location and issued communiqués, seeking prisoner exchanges and political concessions.
- **Government response:** Italian authorities largely maintained a “no negotiation” stance, while public and political debate intensified.
- **Killing and discovery (9 May 1978):** Moro was murdered; his body was left in a car in central Rome, widely interpreted as a symbolic message to the Italian political system.

## Key Actors

- **Red Brigades:** The perpetrating militant organization.
- **Italian government and political parties:** Managed the crisis response amid pressure to negotiate or hold firm.
- **The Catholic Church and civil society:** Played visible roles in appeals for Moro’s life and in public mobilization.

## Aftermath and Significance

- The event became a defining episode of Italy’s modern political history.
- It influenced debates about **counterterrorism policy**, **state legitimacy**, and **the limits of negotiation** with armed groups.
- The kidnapping and murder affected Italy’s political trajectory and the broader social climate of the late 1970s.
