## Summary

British consulting firm harvested data from 87 million Facebook users without consent to influence elections. Used in Trump 2016 campaign and Brexit referendum targeting.

## Career Timeline

| Year | Event |
|------|-------|
| 2018 | Scandal exposed by whistleblower |

## What It Did (High-Level)

- Built voter persuasion and turnout models by combining commercial data, survey/psychometric data, and digital targeting.
- Became most associated with Facebook data obtained via a third-party app and then used beyond the purposes users expected.
- Marketed the approach as “psychographic” microtargeting, though the real-world effectiveness of such methods has been debated by researchers and practitioners.

## How the Facebook Data Was Obtained (Commonly Cited Narrative)

- A researcher associated with Cambridge University built a Facebook app that paid users to take a personality survey.
- At the time, Facebook’s platform policies allowed the app to collect not only the user’s data but also some data from the user’s friends.
- The resulting dataset was later provided to Cambridge Analytica / its affiliates, triggering allegations of consent violations and policy breaches.

## Key Entities and People

- **Cambridge Analytica**: Political consulting firm at the center of the scandal.
- **SCL Group**: Parent/associated group involved in defense and political influence work.
- **Aleksandr Kogan / GSR (Global Science Research)**: Academic/contractor frequently referenced in reporting as the app developer.
- **Christopher Wylie**: Former employee who acted as a key whistleblower in 2018.
- **Facebook (Meta)**: Platform whose data and policies were central to the controversy; faced major regulatory and public scrutiny.

## Aftermath and Consequences

- Regulatory investigations and hearings expanded in the UK, US, and EU following the 2018 reporting.
- Cambridge Analytica announced it would cease operations in 2018 amid legal and reputational pressure.
- Facebook faced penalties and settlements in multiple jurisdictions, and tightened platform data access rules.
- The scandal accelerated mainstream awareness of privacy risks, data brokerage ecosystems, and election-adjacent influence operations.

## Why It Matters

- Demonstrated how “permissioned” platform access can still produce large-scale downstream misuse when data is shared onward.
- Became a reference point for modern privacy regulation discussions, including consent, data minimization, and platform accountability.
- Helped push product and policy changes around third-party app permissions, auditing, and transparency.
