## Summary

European explorers and colonial agents participated in and facilitated the trans-Saharan and Atlantic slave trades that transported millions of Africans into bondage over centuries.

## Historical Context (High-Level)

- **Trans-Saharan routes** connected West and Central Africa with North Africa and the Mediterranean world for many centuries, involving the trade of gold, salt, and enslaved people.
- The **trans-Atlantic slave trade** expanded dramatically from the 16th to the 19th centuries, forcibly transporting millions of Africans to the Americas.
- Enslavement was sustained by a network of **European commercial interests**, **colonial governance**, and **local and regional political economies** across multiple African regions.

## What “Slave Trade” Means in This Context

This article uses "slave trade" as a broad historical category referring to:

- the capture, coercion, or purchase of people into bondage,
- the long-distance transportation and sale of enslaved people,
- and the institutions (legal, commercial, and military) that enabled forced labor systems.

## Common Roles of Explorers and Colonial Agents

European exploration and colonial expansion did not always directly run slave-trading operations, but often:

- mapped routes and resources that later supported commercial extraction,
- negotiated treaties and alliances under unequal power,
- gathered intelligence that shaped imperial policy,
- participated in or benefited from systems of forced labor and coerced "recruitment".

## Impacts (Brief)

- **Demographic and social disruption:** families and communities were fractured by repeated raids, warfare, and displacement.
- **Economic transformation:** regions were pulled into external commercial systems that prioritized human trafficking and later cash-crop extraction.
- **Political consequences:** militarization and state formation patterns were influenced by slave-raiding and defense against it.
- **Long-run legacies:** racialized hierarchies, diaspora formation, and enduring inequalities in the Atlantic world.

## Notes on Attribution

The title and framing may suggest an individual-centric narrative. In many historical cases, however, the most reliable approach is to treat slave trading as a **system** involving multiple actors across time, rather than attributing sweeping responsibility to a single person without clear documentation.
