## Summary

Al-Qaeda operative who planned the USS Cole bombing and was subjected to CIA enhanced interrogation.

## Background

- Born in Saudi Arabia; reported to have spent time in Yemen and Afghanistan.
- Commonly described in public reporting as a senior al-Qaeda operative tied to maritime/ship-targeting plots.[^1]

## Role and Allegations

- Widely alleged by U.S. authorities to have had an operational role in the **USS Cole** bombing (2000) and other attempted attacks against U.S. and allied shipping in the Gulf region.[^2]
- Most public summaries emphasize planning/coordination rather than direct participation; details vary across sources and remain contested in court filings.

## Detention and Interrogation

- Captured in 2002 and held in CIA custody as a so-called “high-value detainee.”
- Subjected to “enhanced interrogation techniques” documented in U.S. government reviews, including waterboarding and other abusive methods.[^3]
- Later transferred to Guantánamo Bay along with other high-value detainees when the CIA detention program was wound down.[^1]

## Legal Proceedings

- Prosecuted in the U.S. military commission system at Guantánamo.
- The case has been shaped by prolonged pretrial litigation over classified evidence, the admissibility of statements obtained after torture, and questions about commission jurisdiction and procedure.[^2]

## Significance

- The al-Nashiri case is frequently cited in discussions of:
  - the legacy of the CIA detention and interrogation program,
  - the reliability and admissibility of post-torture evidence,
  - and the speed and legitimacy of the military commission process at Guantánamo.
