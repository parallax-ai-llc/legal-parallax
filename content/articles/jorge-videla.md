## Background and Rise

- Videla was a career Argentine Army officer who rose through the command structure during a period of severe political polarization, economic instability, and escalating violence involving state forces and armed groups.
- In March 1976 the armed forces removed the constitutional government and established a military regime that described itself as a "National Reorganization Process" (Proceso de Reorganización Nacional).

## Rule (197681): Governance and Repression

- As head of state and a leading figure of the ruling junta, Videla became internationally identified with the regime's campaign of repression commonly referred to as the "Dirty War".
- The period is widely documented as involving:
  - enforced disappearances (desapariciones) and secret detention centers
  - torture and extrajudicial killings
  - censorship and persecution of perceived political opponents
- Human-rights organizations and later Argentine court findings treated these practices as systematic and state-directed, forming the basis for crimes-against-humanity charges.

## Post-Regime Trials and Convictions

- After Argentina returned to democracy in 1983, senior junta leaders were prosecuted in the 1985 Trial of the Juntas (Juicio a las Juntas), in which Videla was convicted.
- In later years, shifting legal and political decisions affected accountability (including periods of legal limits and pardons), but major cases were reopened in the 2000s.
- Argentine courts ultimately imposed life sentences for crimes against humanity; Videla died while serving a prison sentence.

## Historical Significance

- Videla is commonly referenced in studies of:
  - military authoritarianism in Latin America during the Cold War era
  - transitional justice (how democracies investigate and prosecute prior state crimes)
  - the politics of memory in Argentina (truth commissions, trials, memorialization, and ongoing debates)
