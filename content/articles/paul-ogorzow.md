## Summary
Paul Ogorzow, the 'S-Bahn Murderer,' was a Nazi Party member who murdered eight women on and around the Berlin S-Bahn during WWII blackouts. He was beheaded after conviction.

## Background
- Ogorzow was born in 1912 in what was then the German Empire and later lived and worked in Berlin.
- He was employed by the Berlin S-Bahn (suburban rail system), which gave him familiarity with routes, stations, and rail-yard areas.
- Contemporary accounts describe him as a member of the Nazi Party; historians often emphasize that his crimes occurred amid wartime disruption rather than being politically motivated.

## Crimes on the Berlin S-Bahn
- The murders and assaults attributed to Ogorzow occurred during World War II, when Berlin used blackout conditions to reduce visibility during air raids.
- Many attacks were reported in or near S-Bahn stations, trains, and trackside areas, where low light and crowd movement could reduce the chance of identification.
- Sources commonly attribute **eight murders** to him, along with additional sexual assaults and attempted attacks.

## Investigation and Identification
- Police and rail authorities investigated a pattern of attacks connected to S-Bahn travel corridors.
- Ogorzow’s rail employment and access reportedly made him a suspect as investigators narrowed the pool to individuals with opportunity to move around the network.
- After arrest, he was tried and convicted; details of evidence vary by secondary source, but the case is generally presented as resolved through a combination of investigation and confession/admissions.

## Trial and Execution
- Ogorzow was convicted by a Nazi-era court during the war.
- He was executed in 1941 by beheading (a method used in Germany at the time for certain capital sentences).

## Historical Context
- The Berlin home front in 1940–1941 was shaped by air-raid alarms, blackout orders, and wartime policing.
- Blackouts and damaged infrastructure created environments where routine commuting became more dangerous and public fear amplified.
- The case is frequently cited in criminology discussions about how conflict conditions can increase victim vulnerability and complicate investigations.

## Notes on Sources and Naming
- He is often referred to in English-language media as the **“S-Bahn Murderer”** (or similar phrasing), referencing the transit system linked to the attacks.
- As with many wartime cases, some biographical and victim details are inconsistently reported across summaries; careful histories prioritize court documentation and contemporaneous records when available.
