## Summary

Tammany Hall boss who ran New York City's most corrupt political machine, stealing an estimated $200 million.

## Background

- William M. Tweed rose from working-class New York City politics into a central role in the Democratic Party’s Tammany Hall organization.
- His power was less about holding a single executive office and more about controlling nominations, patronage, budgets, and contracting decisions through allied officials.

## The “Tweed Ring” and How It Worked

High-level pattern of corruption commonly associated with Tweed’s machine:

- **Control of key offices:** Allies held positions that touched contracts, payments, and auditing (e.g., city treasury and comptroller functions).
- **Inflated invoices:** Public works and services were billed far above market rates, with the excess diverted to ring members and connected businesses.
- **Patronage and votes:** Jobs and favors were traded for political loyalty, helping maintain electoral dominance in many city wards.

## Exposure and Prosecution

- Public outrage intensified after a combination of **investigative journalism** (notably the *New York Times*) and **political cartoons** (notably Thomas Nast) made the allegations understandable and widely visible.
- Tweed became an emblem of “machine politics” in the Gilded Age: highly organized, locally effective, and deeply vulnerable to bribery and kickback schemes.

## Legacy

- Tweed’s downfall is often cited as a turning point for **urban reform movements** and the idea that sustained public oversight (press, courts, and watchdogs) can weaken entrenched patronage systems.
- In historical memory, “Boss Tweed” became shorthand for large-scale municipal corruption—especially in rapidly growing cities where infrastructure spending created opportunities for graft.
