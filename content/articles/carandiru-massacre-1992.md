## Summary

Brazilian military police stormed Carandiru Penitentiary killing 111 inmates to end a riot. Many were shot while cowering in their cells in one of the worst prison massacres in history.

## Background

- The Casa de Detenção de São Paulo, commonly known as Carandiru, was a major prison complex in São Paulo state.
- By the early 1990s it was known for severe overcrowding and poor conditions, a context often cited in accounts of the riot and police response.

## What Happened (2 October 1992)

- A disturbance/riot broke out inside the facility.
- Brazilian Military Police units entered the prison to regain control.
- The operation ended with 111 inmates killed, making it one of the deadliest prison incidents in Brazil’s modern history.

## Immediate Aftermath

- The scale of deaths drew intense national and international attention.
- Survivors and human-rights observers alleged that many inmates were shot after the situation was already contained, including inside cells.

## Investigations and Legal Proceedings (High-Level)

- The incident prompted investigations and long-running court proceedings.
- Over the years, trials focused on accountability for the killings and the conduct of the operation.
- Case outcomes and appeals have been a continuing feature of the public record, reflecting how complex and politically charged the event remained.

## Legacy

- In Brazil, “Carandiru” became shorthand for systemic prison failures (overcrowding, violence, weak governance) and the risks of militarized responses.
- The massacre is frequently referenced in discussions of public security policy, police use of force, and human-rights standards in custodial settings.
