## Summary

Elizabeth Short was found murdered and bisected in Los Angeles, her body drained of blood and meticulously posed. Despite hundreds of suspects, the killer was never identified.

## Case Background

- **Victim**: Elizabeth Short (1924–1947), an aspiring actress known posthumously by the nickname “Black Dahlia.”
- **Location**: Los Angeles, California.
- **Date discovered**: January 15, 1947.
- **Circumstances**: Short’s body was found in a vacant lot, posed and severed at the waist; the case became one of the best-known unsolved murders in U.S. history.

## Investigation (High-Level)

- The Los Angeles Police Department received extensive tips and confessions, many of which were later determined to be false or unreliable.
- The investigation considered a large number of potential suspects over time, but no suspect was conclusively charged.
- Media coverage was intense from the start and helped shape public perception of the case.

## Why the Case Drew Lasting Attention

- **Scene details**: The condition and placement of the body were unusually sensationalized in press reporting.
- **Publicity**: Newspapers amplified rumors and promoted the “Black Dahlia” moniker, creating a lasting cultural imprint.
- **Unresolved status**: The lack of an identified perpetrator continues to invite speculation, books, and re-examinations.

## Cultural Legacy

- The murder has been referenced in numerous works of true crime and fiction.
- It remains a frequently cited example in discussions of mid-20th-century media sensationalism and cold-case mythology.
