## Background

- Jean-Bédel Bokassa was a Central African military officer who rose through the armed forces before seizing power.[^1]
- He cultivated a personalist regime centered on loyalty to him, the security services, and patronage networks.[^1]

## Rule (Central African Republic)

- After taking power in 1966, Bokassa concentrated authority in the executive and relied heavily on the military.[^1]
- His government became associated with repression and human-rights abuses, alongside efforts at state-building and symbolic nation-making.[^1]

## Central African Empire

- In 1976, Bokassa transformed the republic into a monarchy, declaring the Central African Empire and himself Emperor Bokassa I.[^1]
- The 1977 coronation drew global attention for its cost and extravagance relative to the country's poverty.[^1]

## Downfall and Aftermath

- In 1979, Bokassa was deposed; the republic was restored and David Dacko returned to power with French support.[^1]
- Bokassa later returned to the country, faced trial, and served a prison sentence before being released.[^1]

## Why He Is Notable

- Frequently cited as an extreme example of post-colonial personal rule and political theatrics.[^1]
- His reign is remembered for the combination of authoritarian governance, cult-of-personality symbolism, and the international spectacle of the imperial period.[^1]
