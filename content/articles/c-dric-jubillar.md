## Summary

French suspect accused of murdering his wife Delphine in 2020. While technically awaiting trial, the case became one of France's most followed criminal investigations of the 2020s.

## Case Background

- **Disappearance:** Delphine Jubillar disappeared in December 2020 in the Tarn department in southern France. The case quickly drew national attention due to the lack of a body and the uncertainty around the timeline and circumstances.
- **Family context:** The investigation was widely reported as involving marital tensions and conflicting accounts of events around the disappearance, which became part of public discussion.

## Investigation (high level)

- **Search efforts:** Authorities conducted repeated searches and forensic work in the surrounding area, a common feature of “missing person” cases that later shift to homicide inquiries.
- **Digital and witness evidence:** As in many modern investigations, investigators reportedly relied on phone data, messaging, and witness statements to reconstruct movements and communications.
- **Evolving theory:** The case has been described as moving from a missing-person investigation to a suspected homicide case as evidence and prosecutorial theories developed.

## Charges and Legal Process

- **Arrest and charge:** Jubillar was arrested in 2021 and charged in connection with the disappearance.
- **Pre-trial detention:** He was held while awaiting trial, and procedural developments (appeals, investigative steps, judicial decisions) were followed closely by French media.
- **Presumption of innocence:** Reporting around the case often notes that suspects remain legally presumed innocent until convicted by a court.

## Public and Media Attention

- The disappearance became one of France’s most-followed criminal investigations of the decade, illustrating how high-profile cases can generate sustained public interest, extensive press coverage, and secondary narratives beyond the courtroom.

## Legal and Investigative Context (France)

- **Investigating magistrates:** High-profile French criminal cases can involve an investigating judge (juge d’instruction) overseeing parts of the inquiry, a structure that differs from purely prosecutor-led models.
- **Cases without a body:** “No-body” homicide prosecutions tend to rely on circumstantial evidence, consistency of timelines, digital traces, and corroborated witness statements.
- **Pre-trial publicity:** Extensive media attention can shape public narratives, which is why court proceedings and evidentiary standards are often emphasized in responsible coverage.

## Significance (Why It Drew Attention)

- **Uncertainty and timeline gaps:** Missing-person cases with unclear movements and limited physical evidence often generate prolonged public interest.
- **Procedural milestones:** Detention decisions, expert reports, and investigative acts become news in long-running French cases, keeping the story visible even before trial.
