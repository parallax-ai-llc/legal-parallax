Investigation revealing Dubai real estate bought with dirty money from criminals and sanctioned individuals worldwide.

## Background

- Dubai’s rapid growth as a global real-estate hub, combined with large cross-border capital flows, has long attracted scrutiny from anti–money laundering (AML) investigators.
- Real estate can be an appealing vehicle for illicit finance because it enables the conversion of cash or opaque funds into a tangible, appreciating asset.

## What “Dubai Leaks” refers to

- “Dubai Leaks” is commonly used to describe reporting based on a dataset of Dubai property ownership information.
- The reporting highlighted how high-value properties in Dubai were associated with:
  - politically exposed persons (PEPs),
  - individuals accused or convicted of serious crimes,
  - and persons subject to international sanctions.

## High-level findings (as reported)

- Ownership and beneficial-ownership opacity: public information about who ultimately controls a property can be limited, especially when intermediaries or corporate structures are involved.
- Cross-border risk: the reporting emphasized that the buyers/owners came from many countries, creating challenges for international enforcement and asset tracing.
- Due-diligence pressure points: attention focused on the roles of professional intermediaries (e.g., agents, brokers, service providers) and on the quality of customer due diligence.

## Why real-estate ownership data matters

- Property registers and ownership data can support:
  - investigative journalism,
  - civil society oversight,
  - law-enforcement asset recovery,
  - and compliance programs at banks and regulated firms.
- When data is incomplete or hard to access, investigators often rely on leaks, cross-referencing, and open-source records.

## Public impact and discussion

- The story contributed to a broader international debate about how major property markets can be used to park wealth and launder proceeds of crime.
- It is frequently cited alongside other leak-driven investigations that stress the importance of beneficial-ownership transparency.
