Hook-handed radical cleric convicted of terrorism charges in the US after preaching hate in London.

## Background

- **Also known as**: Mostafa Kamel Mostafa (as commonly reported in court and press coverage).[^1]
- **Public profile**: Became a prominent figure in the UK during the 1990s–2000s as a militant Islamist preacher associated with **Finsbury Park Mosque** in north London.[^1]
- **Notable physical features**: Often described in media as having a hook for a hand and an eye injury; these traits contributed to his recognizable public persona.[^1]

## Activities and Influence (High-Level)

- **Preaching and recruitment concerns**: UK authorities and journalists widely described his sermons and public statements as encouraging violent jihad and extremist causes.[^1]
- **Network effects**: His name became linked (in public reporting) to individuals who later took part in extremist violence or traveled to conflict zones, making him a recurring reference point in discussions of UK domestic radicalization.[^1]

## Legal Proceedings

### United Kingdom

- **UK prosecution**: Faced criminal cases in the UK in the early 2000s related to extremist materials and public speech acts; this period marked the beginning of sustained legal pressure and restrictions.[^1]
- **Loss of role**: Over time, official actions and community changes reduced his influence at Finsbury Park Mosque, which later underwent leadership and governance reforms.[^1]

### United States

- **Extradition**: After UK proceedings, he was extradited to the United States to face terrorism-related charges.[^1]
- **Conviction (2014)**: A US federal jury convicted him on multiple counts tied to terrorism-related conduct; he received a lengthy prison sentence.[^1]

## Significance

- **Symbolic figure**: His case is often cited as an example of how charismatic preachers can amplify extremist narratives and how states respond through surveillance, prosecution, and extradition.[^1]
- **Cross-border counterterrorism**: The UK→US sequence of investigations and extradition is frequently referenced in the context of international counterterrorism cooperation.[^1]
