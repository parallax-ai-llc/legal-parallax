## Identity and Alias

- **BTK** is the widely used nickname for **Dennis Rader**, a U.S. serial killer active primarily in and around **Wichita, Kansas**.
- The acronym is commonly expanded as **"Bind, Torture, Kill"**, reflecting how the offender described his pattern in communications.

## Overview

- BTK was responsible for **10 murders** (as charged and admitted) spanning **1974–1991**, with later re-emergence via letters in **2004–2005**.
- The case is frequently cited as an example of a long-running investigation that advanced through a combination of **forensic analysis**, **behavioral assessment**, and **suspect tracing** based on communications.

## Modus Operandi (High-Level)

- Target selection and attack methods varied, but public reporting commonly notes:
  - **Pre-attack planning** and stalking behavior.
  - Use of **bindings** and control of victims.
  - Attempts to **communicate with media/police** to claim credit and maintain notoriety.

## Investigation and Capture

- After years without confirmed activity, renewed letters in the mid-2000s prompted fresh investigative work.
- Public accounts describe investigators using **metadata and technical traces** from submitted materials (including a **floppy disk**) alongside traditional casework to identify Rader.
- Rader was arrested in **2005** and later provided detailed admissions in court.

## Prosecution and Sentence

- Rader **pleaded guilty** to **10 counts** of first-degree murder in Kansas.
- The court imposed **multiple consecutive life sentences**, resulting in imprisonment for the remainder of his life.

## Cultural and Historical Notes

- The BTK case has been referenced in discussions about:
  - The evolution of **forensic and digital evidence** in cold/long-term cases.
  - How offender communications can both hinder and help investigations.
  - Media ethics around coverage of violent crimes and copycat risks.
