## Summary

Korean Air VP Cho Hyun-ah forced a plane to return to the gate because her macadamia nuts were served in a bag rather than a bowl. She was convicted of aviation safety violations.

## Incident Details

- **Setting:** Korean Air Flight 086 (Airbus A380) at New York JFK, preparing to depart for Incheon.
- **Trigger:** The nuts were served in the original package (a bag) rather than plated in a dish, which Cho Hyun-ah reportedly considered improper for first-class service.
- **Escalation:** Cabin crew and the purser were reprimanded; the aircraft was ordered to **taxi back to the gate** and the purser was removed.
- **Why it mattered:** For an aircraft that has pushed back, ordering a return to the gate is treated as an operational and safety decision, not merely a customer-service complaint.

## People and Organizations

- **Cho Hyun-ah (Heather Cho):** Then a Korean Air vice president and daughter of the chairman of Hanjin Group.
- **Korean Air / Hanjin Group:** The airline and its parent conglomerate, whose corporate governance became a major part of public discussion after the event.
- **Cabin crew / purser:** Airline staff directly involved in the confrontation and subsequent reporting.

## Legal Aftermath (High Level)

- **Charges / findings:** South Korean authorities pursued the case as an **aviation safety** matter rather than a simple workplace dispute.
- **Court outcome:** Cho was convicted at trial and later received a reduced sentence on appeal; she served a period of detention before release.
- **Public record:** Specific charges, dates, and sentencing details vary across reporting; the Wikipedia summary provides a consolidated timeline.

## Cultural and Business Impact

- **"Nut rage" as shorthand:** The incident became an international symbol of perceived "chaebol" entitlement and abusive treatment of employees.
- **Reputational damage:** Korean Air faced sustained criticism and renewed scrutiny of service culture, internal controls, and executive accountability.
- **Governance debate:** The episode fed broader South Korean conversations about power concentration in family-controlled conglomerates and protections for workers.
