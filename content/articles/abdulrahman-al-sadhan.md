## Summary

Not a scientist; removing.

Abdulrahman al-Sadhan is a Saudi humanitarian aid worker (reported as a Red Crescent employee) whose case has been cited by multiple human-rights organizations as an example of Saudi Arabia’s use of the Specialized Criminal Court to prosecute peaceful expression.

In 2021, Amnesty International reported that he was sentenced to 20 years in prison followed by a 20-year travel ban, after proceedings described as involving serious due-process violations and allegations of torture used to extract a “confession”.

## Additional Context

### Detention and trial (high-level)

- Amnesty International described the proceedings as involving violations including allegations of torture and other ill-treatment, and said that a “confession” may have been extracted under coercion.
- The reported sentence included both a long prison term and a long post-release travel ban, a combination frequently highlighted by advocates as a mechanism of extended punishment.

### International attention

- Amnesty International categorized the case under themes including censorship/freedom of expression, detention, disappearances, torture, and unfair trials.
- The case has been referenced in broader discussions about the Specialized Criminal Court’s role in handling cases tied to speech and online activity.
