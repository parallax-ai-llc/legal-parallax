## Summary

US soldiers tortured and sexually humiliated Iraqi prisoners including forced nudity, electric shocks, and using dogs to attack detainees. Photographs became iconic symbols of war crimes.

## Context

Abu Ghraib prison, located near Baghdad, was used by Saddam Hussein’s regime and later repurposed by US-led Coalition forces after the 2003 invasion of Iraq. During the insurgency, it became a major detention site for Iraqi prisoners.

## What Happened (High-Level)

- Detainees were subjected to physical abuse, intimidation, and sexual humiliation by some US military personnel.
- Abuse was documented in photographs that later circulated widely.
- The scandal centered on misconduct within parts of the prison’s guard force and failures of supervision and accountability.

## Investigations and Accountability

- US Army investigations examined detainee abuse and command climate at Abu Ghraib.
- Multiple service members faced courts-martial and received sentences ranging from imprisonment to discharge.
- The events triggered broader scrutiny of detention practices in Iraq and Afghanistan, including interrogation policies and compliance with international humanitarian law.

## Global Impact and Legacy

- The images became a defining symbol of the Iraq War and damaged US credibility abroad.
- The scandal intensified debates over torture, prisoner treatment, and the responsibility of leadership for abuses by subordinates.
- Abu Ghraib is frequently referenced in discussions of war crimes, detainee rights, and military ethics.

## Key Terms

- **Detainee abuse**: mistreatment of prisoners in custody, including cruel, inhuman, or degrading treatment.
- **Command responsibility**: principle that commanders may be responsible for crimes committed by subordinates if they knew or should have known and failed to prevent or punish.
- **International humanitarian law**: laws and norms governing conduct during armed conflict, including protections for prisoners.
