## Summary

British activist who exposed the horrific conditions in British concentration camps during the Boer War.

## Background

- Born in Cornwall, England, and became involved in social welfare and relief work.
- Best known for humanitarian advocacy during the Second Boer War (1899–1902).

## Boer War Concentration Camps (1900–1902)

- Visited British-run camps in South Africa where Boer civilians—especially women and children—were held.
- Documented overcrowding, inadequate food, poor sanitation, and disease outbreaks.
- Reported her findings to British authorities and the public, helping trigger political scrutiny.

## Impact and Controversy

- Her reports contributed to the establishment of official inquiries and reforms to camp administration.
- Faced criticism and hostility from parts of the British establishment for publicizing the abuses.
- Became an early, widely cited example of modern humanitarian whistleblowing tied to wartime civilian policy.

## Later Years

- Continued peace and relief work in Europe, including during and after World War I.
- Remained active in public advocacy and correspondence on humanitarian issues until late in life.

## Legacy

- Remembered for emphasizing the human cost of war on non-combatants and for insisting on public accountability.
- Frequently referenced in discussions of civilian internment, war ethics, and the history of human-rights campaigning.

## Key Facts (High-Level)

- Primary historical association: exposing conditions in Boer War concentration camps.
- Method: first-hand observation, documentation, and public reporting.
- Enduring theme: protection of civilians and humanitarian oversight during conflict.
