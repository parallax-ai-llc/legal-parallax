## Summary
The Hatton Garden safe deposit burglary was carried out over Easter weekend 2015 by a group of elderly thieves. They drilled through a vault wall and stole £14 million in valuables. The gang, mostly in their 60s-70s, was caught and convicted.

## Background
- **Hatton Garden** is London’s historic jewelry district, known for businesses handling high-value gems and precious metals.
- The target was a **safe deposit facility** used by small businesses and private clients, concentrating many independent boxes in one vault.
- The burglary took place over the **Easter holiday weekend**, a period when staffing and foot traffic were reduced.

## Perpetrators (overview)
- The core group were older, experienced criminals, which led to widespread media coverage describing them as an “elderly” gang.
- Public reporting and court coverage commonly reference named participants such as **Brian Reader**, **Terry Perkins**, **Danny Jones**, **Carl Wood**, and **Hugh Doyle**, along with additional alleged associates.
- Investigators relied on **forensic traces** and **telecommunications evidence** rather than eyewitness identification.

## Modus operandi
- The group gained access to the building and worked through the night to reach the vault area.
- They used **industrial power tools** to drill through thick structure and the vault wall, then forced entry into the safe deposit area.
- Valuables were taken from many individual deposit boxes; early estimates varied before converging on a headline figure of **~£14 million**.

## Investigation and arrest
- The case became a high-profile Metropolitan Police investigation due to the scale and symbolism of the target.
- Authorities reported recovering some property and tools, and later arrests followed as evidence linked suspects to the scene.
- Media coverage emphasized how small mistakes (e.g., trace evidence) can unravel carefully planned property crimes.

## Trial and sentencing (high level)
- Several members of the group were **convicted** in UK courts; sentencing reflected both the scale of loss and the organized nature of the burglary.
- Some proceeds were never recovered, and courts issued orders related to confiscation where applicable.

## Impact and legacy
- The burglary prompted renewed discussion about **security standards** for private vaults and safe deposit providers.
- It became a frequent reference point in British true-crime media, partly due to the unusual demographics of the offenders.
- The incident is often cited as a modern example of a “classic” heist: planning, specialized tools, and a concentrated high-value target.
