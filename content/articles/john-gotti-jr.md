## Summary
John A. Gotti Jr. was the acting boss of the Gambino family after his father's imprisonment. He faced four racketeering trials, all ending in mistrials. He claims to have left organized crime.

He is the son of Gambino boss John Gotti (“The Teflon Don”) and became a prominent figure in U.S. organized-crime reporting during the late 1990s and 2000s due to repeated federal prosecutions and high media attention.

## Background
- **Family**: Son of John Gotti (Gambino boss) and Victoria Gotti; the Gotti family became one of the most publicly recognized names associated with the American Mafia in the late 20th century.
- **Organization**: The **Gambino crime family** is historically described by U.S. law enforcement as one of New York City’s “Five Families,” with a long-running structure of crews, captains, and a boss/administration.

## Role in the Gambino Family (reported)
- **“Acting boss” label**: Multiple press and law-enforcement accounts described Gotti Jr. as an *acting boss* at points after his father’s incarceration, though such titles can be contested and are difficult to verify independently.
- **Public profile**: Unlike many organized-crime figures who avoided attention, the Gotti name drew sustained media coverage, which in turn amplified the visibility of related investigations and trials.

## Federal Prosecutions and Trials
- **Racketeering focus**: The repeated trials centered on alleged racketeering activity (often prosecuted under **RICO**, the Racketeer Influenced and Corrupt Organizations Act), a statute frequently used in U.S. organized-crime cases.
- **Outcome pattern**: Across four major racketeering trials in the 2000s, juries failed to reach unanimous verdicts, resulting in **mistrials** rather than convictions or acquittals.
- **Aftermath**: Following the fourth mistrial, prosecutors ultimately chose not to pursue another retrial, bringing that particular sequence of high-profile courtroom battles to a close.

## Public Statements and Later Life
- **Claim of leaving organized crime**: Gotti Jr. has publicly stated that he left organized crime, often dated to the late 1990s in reporting.
- **Ongoing interest**: Even after the major trials ended, the Gotti family remained a frequent reference point in discussions of modern U.S. organized crime, media spectacle, and the legal challenges of proving complex conspiracy cases.

## Notes on Sources and Reliability
- Organized-crime history is frequently reconstructed from **court filings, trial testimony, investigative reporting, and law-enforcement statements**.
- Terms like “boss,” “acting boss,” and “underboss” are sometimes used differently by journalists, investigators, and insiders; the same figure may be described with different roles across sources.
