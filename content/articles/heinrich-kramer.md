## Context

- Kramer was a Dominican friar and papal inquisitor active in the late 15th century within the Holy Roman Empire.
- His notoriety is closely tied to disputes over the legitimacy and procedure of witch prosecutions, particularly in German-speaking regions.

## Malleus Maleficarum ("Hammer of Witches")

- First published in 1487, the *Malleus Maleficarum* argued for the reality of witchcraft and offered a framework for identifying, interrogating, and prosecuting alleged witches.
- The work blended theology, demonology, and legal procedure, presenting witchcraft as both a spiritual threat and a prosecutable crime.
- Although often treated as an “official” guide, its authority varied by place and time; it was influential largely because it was widely copied and reprinted.

## Methods and Controversy

- Kramer is frequently associated with aggressive prosecution tactics and a strong presumption of guilt in witchcraft cases.
- Historical accounts describe conflicts with local church authorities and civic leaders over evidentiary standards and the conduct of trials.

## Impact and Legacy

- The *Malleus* contributed to a broader European discourse that helped normalize witch-hunting ideas, even though later witch persecutions had multiple political, social, and religious drivers.
- Kramer’s name became a shorthand in modern historiography for the harshest currents of late-medieval and early-modern demonology.
