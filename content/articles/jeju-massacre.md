## Summary

South Korean government forces killed an estimated 30,000 residents of Jeju island to suppress a leftist uprising. One of the worst massacres in Korean history, suppressed for decades.

## Context and Causes

- **Post-liberation polarization (1945–1948):** After Japanese rule ended in 1945, Korea experienced sharp political conflict, labor unrest, and competing visions for governance in the South.
- **Approaching separate elections (1948):** Tensions rose around the plan to hold elections in the South, which many on the left opposed as cementing division.
- **Local dynamics on Jeju:** Jeju had active political organizing and deep grievances (including policing and economic hardship), which interacted with national-level ideological conflict.

## Main Parties and Institutions (high level)

- **Insurgent/leftist forces:** Armed groups associated with leftist politics engaged in attacks and intimidation, including targeting local officials and police.
- **South Korean security forces:** Police and military units conducted counterinsurgency operations; historical accounts describe widespread abuses against civilians.
- **Paramilitary youth groups:** Some accounts note participation by right-wing youth organizations in violent repression.

## What Happened (expanded overview)

- **April 3, 1948:** A major outbreak of violence began, with coordinated attacks reported against police and state-linked targets.
- **Counterinsurgency escalated:** Government response expanded beyond armed actors; broad “screening” and punitive operations were carried out in multiple communities.
- **Civilian impact:** The event is widely described as involving mass killings, village destruction, and displacement. Estimates of deaths vary by source.

## Aftermath and Legacy

- **Long suppression of discussion:** Public recognition and open debate were limited for decades, with survivors and families facing stigma.
- **Official investigations and apologies:** South Korea later undertook formal investigations and state acknowledgments; the massacre is now recognized as a major historical tragedy.
- **Memorialization:** Jeju hosts memorial sites and annual remembrances that focus on victims, reconciliation, and historical education.

## Notes on Numbers and Dating

- **Death toll estimates:** Commonly cited figures include “around 30,000,” but the range differs across studies and official reports.
- **End date:** Some timelines extend beyond early 1949 because arrests, reprisals, and related violence continued; summaries often use approximate endpoints.
