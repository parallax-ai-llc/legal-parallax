## Summary
Helmer 'Pacho' Herrera was the fourth leader of the Cali Cartel, running its New York operations. He surrendered in 1996 and was assassinated in prison two years later.

## Background (High-Level)
- Herrera was a Colombian narcotics trafficker associated with the Cali Cartel, one of the dominant drug-trafficking organizations in Colombia during the late 20th century.
- He is commonly referenced by the nickname **"Pacho"** in press and law-enforcement reporting.
- The Cali Cartel is often contrasted with the Medellín Cartel in style and operations, with Cali generally described as more businesslike and networked.

## Role in the Cali Cartel
- Frequently described as one of the cartel’s principal leaders (often listed as the **"fourth"** senior figure alongside the better-known Rodríguez Orejuela brothers and José Santacruz Londoño).
- Reported responsibilities included oversight of key international distribution and logistics, including **New York-linked** trafficking routes.
- As with many large trafficking groups, control was typically exercised through intermediaries, shell entities, and compartmentalized cells to reduce exposure.

## Surrender, Imprisonment, and Death
- Herrera **surrendered** to authorities in the mid-1990s (commonly reported as **1996**) amid an intensified Colombian crackdown on cartel leadership.
- He remained in custody and was later **killed in prison** (reported as **1998**).
- His death is widely characterized as an assassination connected to organized-crime rivalries and prison violence.

## Context and Significance
- The takedown and surrender of senior Cali Cartel figures marked a major shift in the Colombian drug-trafficking landscape, with trafficking activity fragmenting into smaller and more dispersed groups.
- Herrera’s case is frequently cited in discussions of:
  - leadership decapitation strategies,
  - prison security and cartel influence behind bars,
  - how trafficking networks adapt after major organizational shocks.
