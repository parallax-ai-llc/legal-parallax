## Summary

German papal inquisitor whose ruthless persecution of heretics was so extreme he was assassinated.

## Role and Historical Context

Konrad von Marburg (c. 1180–1233) is remembered primarily as an early papal inquisitor in the Holy Roman Empire, active during a period when the papacy was formalizing procedures to investigate and punish heresy.

### Background

- He was a German cleric associated with reform-minded circles in the early 13th century.
- He operated in a climate of heightened concern about heterodox religious movements and lay preaching.
- His activities predate the later, more institutionalized inquisitions that developed in subsequent centuries.

### Appointment and Authority

- Konrad acted as a papal inquisitor, empowered to pursue suspected heresy.
- Papal backing gave him unusual leverage over local elites, including the ability to summon and interrogate suspects.
- His reputation rests on the severity and speed of his proceedings rather than on surviving detailed trial records.

### Approach to Investigations

- Contemporary and near-contemporary accounts portray his methods as exceptionally harsh.
- Accusations of relying on denunciations, broad suspicion, and limited due process are common in later narratives.
- The combination of coercive pressure and wide investigative scope created intense local resentment.

## Controversies

### Relationship with Local Nobility

- His campaigns brought him into conflict with regional nobles and clergy.
- The perception that he targeted prominent figures, or threatened them with prosecution, increased political stakes.

### Assassination (1233)

- In 1233, Konrad was killed, commonly attributed to retaliation for his uncompromising persecution.
- The assassination became part of the larger story of the risks faced by investigators who challenged entrenched power.

## Legacy

- He is often cited as an example of early inquisitorial zeal taken to extremes.
- Historians treat him as a revealing case of how anti-heresy policy could collide with local politics and legal norms.
- Popular memory tends to reduce him to a symbol of inquisitorial brutality, though the broader institutional context mattered.

## Notes

- Dates for his birth are approximate in many secondary summaries.
- Specific details of individual cases are less prominent in modern overviews than the general characterization of his severity.
