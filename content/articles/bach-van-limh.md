### Wildlife Trafficking Overview (Ivory & Rhino Horn)

Wildlife trafficking is a transnational organized crime that often involves:

- **Sourcing**: poaching or illicit acquisition in biodiverse regions (including parts of Africa and Southeast Asia)
- **Aggregation**: consolidation through brokers and storage locations
- **Transport**: concealment in containerized maritime shipments, air cargo, or overland routes
- **Laundering**: falsified paperwork, misdeclared commodities, and use of front companies
- **Distribution**: wholesale buyers and retail networks in destination markets

**Ivory** and **rhino horn** are frequently cited commodities in enforcement reporting due to high value, demand-driven markets, and the role of international transport corridors.

### Common Trafficking Routes and Methods (High-Level)

While routes change in response to enforcement, widely documented patterns include:

- Movement from **source regions** → **transit hubs** (ports, free-trade zones, logistics centers) → **destination markets**
- Use of **container shipping** for bulk quantities and **air baggage/courier networks** for smaller shipments
- Concealment in legitimate trade flows via misdeclaration, mixed cargo, and falsified documentation (method varies by case)
- Reliance on intermediaries who handle **procurement, logistics, and corruption risk**

### Legal and Regulatory Framework

Key international mechanisms relevant to ivory and rhino horn trafficking include:

- **CITES** (Convention on International Trade in Endangered Species of Wild Fauna and Flora), which regulates international trade in listed species and their derivatives
- National criminal laws that implement CITES obligations and impose penalties for possession, transport, import/export, and conspiracy/organized crime activity

Enforcement actions are commonly led by customs, wildlife authorities, police, and cross-border task forces, sometimes supported by international coordination.
