## Summary
Joseph 'Joe Bananas' Bonanno was one of the original Five Families bosses. He attempted to take over the Commission in 1964 ('Banana War'). He retired to Arizona and wrote an autobiography.

## Background (High-Level)
- **Origins**: Born in western Sicily, a region central to early 20th-century Mafia networks.
- **Migration**: Joined the wave of Sicilian migration to New York, where Sicilian and Neapolitan groups competed and merged.

## Role in the Five Families Era
- **Commission-era boss**: Known as one of the original “Five Families” bosses identified in post-1931 New York.
- **Power style**: Often described (in secondary sources) as favoring diplomacy, alliances, and long-term positioning rather than constant public violence.

## The “Bonanno War” (1960s)
- **Core issue**: A bid to reshape Commission influence and family leadership arrangements.
- **Consequence**: Prolonged instability in and around the Bonanno organization, with defections, leadership disputes, and intensified law-enforcement attention.

## Later Years and Legacy
- **Retirement**: Spent later decades largely outside New York’s day-to-day Mafia politics.
- **Autobiography**: *A Man of Honor* became notable as a rare first-person Mafia-boss narrative, but is treated cautiously by historians because it is self-interested and selective.
- **Historical significance**: Frequently cited in overviews of organized crime as a case study in Commission politics, intra-family conflict, and the limits of boss authority.

## Notes
- The nickname “Joe Bananas” appears widely in popular accounts; it is not consistently used in formal contemporary records.
- Many details of leadership transitions and Commission decisions are reconstructed from a mix of testimony, investigative reporting, and later histories.
