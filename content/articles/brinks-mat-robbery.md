## Summary
The Brink's-Mat robbery occurred on November 26, 1983, when armed robbers stole gold bullion worth £26 million from the Brink's-Mat warehouse at Heathrow Airport. Much of the gold was never recovered. It has been called 'the crime of the century.'

## Background
- The target was a secure warehouse facility associated with precious-metals storage and handling at Heathrow Airport.
- The robbery became notable not only for the scale of the theft, but for how difficult it proved to trace and recover the proceeds.

## What was stolen
- Contemporary reporting described the haul as primarily **gold bullion** (often summarized as roughly **three tonnes**), with additional valuables also reported (e.g., cash and/or diamonds).
- The gold’s estimated value at the time was about **£26 million**, making it one of the largest bullion thefts recorded in the UK.

## Modus operandi (high level)
- Armed offenders entered the premises during early-morning hours and overcame staff/security through intimidation and violence.
- The operation relied on inside knowledge of security routines and storage arrangements, which later became a focus of investigators.

## Aftermath: laundering and recovery challenges
- A significant portion of the bullion was believed to have been **melted and re-cast**, complicating identification.
- Proceeds were associated in public accounts with wider criminal finances, illustrating how a single large theft can “seed” long-running illicit networks.
- Only part of the stolen value was recovered, contributing to the case’s enduring notoriety.

## Investigation and prosecutions (overview)
- The case drove long, multi-agency investigative efforts and produced multiple prosecutions over time, including for handling and money-laundering related offences.
- Rather than a single, clean resolution, the legal outcomes unfolded across years as participants and intermediaries were identified.

## Why it matters / legacy
- Often referred to as a defining UK organized-crime case of the late 20th century.
- Highlighted vulnerabilities in **airport-adjacent logistics** and high-value storage operations.
- Became a common reference point in discussions of:
  - asset recovery limits,
  - precious-metals traceability,
  - and the long tail of proceeds-of-crime investigations.
