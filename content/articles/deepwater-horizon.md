See Tony Hayward and BP. The 2010 explosion and oil spill in the Gulf of Mexico. Not a person.

## What It Was

Deepwater Horizon was an offshore mobile drilling unit (MODU) operated by Transocean and leased to BP for deepwater exploration. It was equipment and an industrial workplace, not an individual person, but it became a global symbol of catastrophic operational failure in high-risk energy infrastructure.

## What Happened (High Level)

- The incident began with a loss of well control during operations at the Macondo well.
- Gas and hydrocarbons reached the rig, leading to an explosion and sustained fire.
- The rig ultimately sank, leaving the well uncontrolled until containment and capping efforts succeeded.

## Impact and Aftermath

### Human Impact

- 11 workers died in the explosion.
- Many others were injured or affected by the prolonged emergency response.

### Environmental Impact

- Large-scale oil release affected marine ecosystems, coastal wetlands, and fisheries.
- Cleanup and remediation efforts continued for years and involved multiple federal, state, and private actors.

### Economic and Legal Consequences

- The event led to extensive civil claims and significant settlement costs.
- Criminal and civil enforcement actions targeted corporate safety, environmental compliance, and reporting failures.
- Regulators tightened oversight of offshore drilling, including well-control standards and safety management expectations.

## Relationship to Related Entities

- **BP**: The well operator and leaseholder; central decision-maker for the Macondo drilling program.
- **Transocean**: Owner/operator of the rig; responsible for rig operations and certain safety systems.
- **Halliburton (commonly cited)**: Provided cementing services for the well; involved in later investigations and litigation.

## Why It Appears Here

This entry is framed as an "Environmental Criminal" because the rig is commonly used as shorthand for a disaster whose consequences included environmental harm and subsequent legal actions. Strictly speaking, the rig itself is an asset; accountability in investigations typically focused on organizations, executives, and operational decision chains.
