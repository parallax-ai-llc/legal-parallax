## Summary
Susumu Ishii was the kumicho of the Inagawa-kai, the third-largest yakuza syndicate. He was involved in a major financial scandal involving Tokyu Corporation stocks that contributed to Japan's asset price bubble collapse.

## Background
- Raised in the Yokosuka/Kamakura area; his family reportedly ran a small noodle shop.[^1]
- Assigned to a kaiten unit in the Imperial Japanese Navy during World War II but did not participate in a suicide mission.[^1]

## Role in the Inagawa-kai
- Commonly described as a senior leader who rose to become the Inagawa-kai’s kaicho/kumicho (leadership titles vary by translation and period).[^1]
- Japanese and international media coverage in the late 1980s often highlighted him as an unusually wealthy, finance-oriented yakuza boss.[^1]

## Business & finance (high-level)
- Reported to have used loans, real-estate deals, and investment structures to build a large asset base during the bubble years.[^1]
- Associated reporting described significant overseas investments (e.g., hotels and other properties) and extensive domestic financial relationships.[^1]

### Tokyu / securities-industry scandal (context)
- Under Ishii’s leadership, reporting linked his group to large-scale stock transactions and borrowing connected to major brokerage houses.[^1]
- The public revelation of these links led to senior resignations at major firms and became a well-known example of how organized crime intersected with late-bubble finance.[^1]

## Legacy & interpretation
- Frequently cited as a case study in:
  - the late-1980s Japanese bubble economy,
  - the vulnerability of financial institutions to reputational and compliance risk,
  - and the globalization of capital flows tied to organized crime networks.[^1][^2]
- Accounts of his funeral note exceptionally large attendance, reflecting his prominence and the Inagawa-kai’s scale in that era.[^1]
