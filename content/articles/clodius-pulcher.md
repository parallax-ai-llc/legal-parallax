## Summary

Roman politician who used armed gangs for political violence and desecrated religious rites.

## Background

- Born into the patrician Claudian gens, he later adopted the more plebeian-style spelling "Clodius" as part of a political strategy.
- Operated in the late Roman Republic during an era of intense factional competition, street violence, and contested legitimacy of institutions.

## Key Episodes (High Level)

### Bona Dea scandal (-62)

- Clodius was accused of entering the women-only rites of Bona Dea, an event held in the house of Julius Caesar while Caesar was pontifex maximus.
- The incident became a major political-religious scandal; Clodius faced prosecution and was ultimately acquitted amid allegations of jury bribery and political pressure.

### Tribune of the plebs and legislation (-58)

- After being transferred to plebeian status (via adoption), Clodius secured the tribunate and used it to pursue popular measures and to attack rivals.
- He helped drive the exile of Cicero, who had ordered executions during the Catilinarian conspiracy and was vulnerable to retrospective political-legal retaliation.

### Street politics and violence

- Clodius is closely associated with the organization and use of armed gangs to intimidate opponents, disrupt assemblies, and influence elections.
- His rivalry with Titus Annius Milo escalated into sustained street fighting, illustrating how extra-legal violence could substitute for formal politics.

## Additional Career Milestones

- Associated at various points with leading figures and factions of the period (including Caesar, Pompey, and the senatorial opposition), though his alliances were often opportunistic.
- Sought further offices after the tribunate; electoral contests in this period were frequently accompanied by public disorder and contested outcomes.

## Death and Aftermath (-52)

- Clodius was killed in a clash on the Appian Way involving Milo and their followers.
- His death triggered severe unrest in Rome; a riot led to the burning of the Senate House (Curia Hostilia).
- The subsequent trial of Milo became a famous political-legal episode, with Cicero delivering (and later revising) a defense speech, *Pro Milone*.

## Legacy

- A prominent example of how religious scandal, populist legislation, and organized street violence could be leveraged as political tools in the late Republic.
- Frequently cited in discussions of the Republic's institutional breakdown prior to the civil wars and the emergence of autocratic rule.
