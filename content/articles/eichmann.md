## Summary

Duplicate consolidated.

Adolf Eichmann was a senior SS officer and one of the principal administrators involved in organizing the deportation of Jews from across German-occupied Europe during the Holocaust.[^1] After World War II he escaped Allied custody, lived under an assumed identity in Argentina, and was later captured by Israeli agents, tried in Jerusalem, and executed in 1962.[^1]

## Career Timeline

| Year | Event |
|------|-------|
| 1932 | Joined the Nazi Party and the SS (Schutzstaffel).[^1] |
| 1938–1939 | Worked in SS offices dealing with Jewish emigration policy following the annexation of Austria and other territorial expansions.[^1] |
| 1941 | Became head of the RSHA section responsible for “Jewish affairs and evacuation” (commonly associated with deportation logistics).[^1] |
| 1942 | Participated as a secretary/administrator at the Wannsee Conference where coordination of the “Final Solution” was discussed.[^1] |
| 1944 | Played a key role in coordinating deportations of Hungarian Jews during the German occupation of Hungary.[^1] |
| 1945 | Fled at the end of the war; subsequently avoided long-term detention and disappeared from Europe.[^1] |
| 1950s | Lived in Argentina under an alias, working in ordinary civilian jobs while remaining in hiding.[^1] |
| 1960 | Captured in Argentina by Israeli agents and taken to Israel.[^1] |
| 1961 | Tried in Jerusalem for war crimes, crimes against humanity, and related charges; the trial received global attention.[^1] |
| 1962 | Executed after conviction; his case became central to later debates about bureaucracy, responsibility, and obedience in mass atrocity.[^1] |

## Context and Significance

- **Administrative role:** Eichmann is widely described as a key bureaucratic figure in the Nazi apparatus responsible for deportation logistics—transport, coordination between agencies, and implementation of policy decisions.[^1]
- **Public record:** Much of the historical outline of his activities, postwar flight, and capture is consolidated in widely cited public sources, including contemporary trial records and secondary histories.[^1]

## Capture, Trial, and Legacy

### Capture and extradition controversy
- Eichmann was captured in Argentina by Israeli operatives in 1960 and brought to Israel for trial. The operation prompted diplomatic dispute about sovereignty and jurisdiction, but the trial proceeded in Israel.[^1]

### The Jerusalem trial
- The 1961 trial featured extensive witness testimony and documentary evidence about the Holocaust and deportations, contributing to broader public awareness of survivors’ experiences in the postwar period.[^1]

### Enduring debates
- **Individual vs. system:** Eichmann’s case is often discussed in relation to how personal agency operates inside large administrative systems.
- **“Banality of evil” discourse:** The trial and its interpretations influenced influential postwar writing about moral responsibility and ordinary bureaucratic participation in atrocity (often discussed alongside Hannah Arendt’s reporting and analysis).[^1]
