## Summary

South Korean military and police killed an estimated 100,000-200,000 suspected communists and their families at the start of the Korean War. Mass graves were covered up for decades.

## Background and Context

- The **National Bodo League** (also translated as *Bodo Federation*) was created in **1949** by the South Korean government as a re-education and monitoring program for people considered left-leaning or connected to communist groups.
- After North Korea invaded in June **1950**, South Korean authorities feared that detainees and registrants could support the invading forces or organize internal uprisings.
- The killings are widely described as a **mass political massacre** carried out by South Korean security forces in the early weeks of the war.

## What Happened (High-Level)

- Large-scale executions were carried out against:
  - Bodo League registrants and detainees
  - other suspected leftists or political prisoners
  - in some cases, relatives of the accused
- The violence occurred across multiple regions and involved local police, military units, and affiliated groups, with significant variation by locality.

## Scale and Estimates

- Commonly cited estimates range from **tens of thousands** to **over 100,000** victims; many summaries cite **~100,000–200,000** as an upper-range estimate.
- Exact numbers remain uncertain due to wartime chaos, incomplete records, and decades of suppression or non-disclosure.

## Aftermath and Public Reckoning

- For decades, discussion and investigation were limited in South Korea, especially during periods of authoritarian rule.
- In the 2000s, South Korea’s **Truth and Reconciliation Commission** (TRCK) investigated numerous wartime incidents, including mass killings linked to political prisoners and suspected leftists.
- The massacre remains a sensitive topic in South Korea’s historical memory and debates about responsibility, documentation, and commemoration.

## Key Takeaways

- The Bodo League Massacre is primarily associated with **state-led anti-communist repression** at the start of the Korean War.
- The episode illustrates how wartime security policies can escalate into **mass violence** when due process collapses.
- Ongoing historical work focuses on identifying victims, locating burial sites, and clarifying chains of command.
