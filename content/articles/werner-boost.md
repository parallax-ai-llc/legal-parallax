## Summary
Werner Boost, the 'Doubles Killer,' murdered couples in the Düsseldorf area in the 1950s. He drugged victims' drinks at nightspots then killed them in remote locations. He was beheaded.

## Background
- Born in 1928 and active as an adult in post-war West Germany.
- Reported to have spent time around nightlife venues in and around Düsseldorf, where he could approach strangers without drawing immediate suspicion.

## Crimes and Modus Operandi
Boost was widely reported to target couples rather than individuals.

Commonly described elements of the pattern:
- **Approach**: He met victims at nightspots (bars/dance halls).
- **Control method**: Drinks were allegedly **drugged** to incapacitate victims.
- **Crime scene selection**: Victims were then taken to **remote locations**, reducing the chance of intervention or witnesses.
- **Victim type**: Couples out on an evening date, making the crimes especially alarming to the public.

## Investigation (High Level)
- A series of attacks on couples created a recognizable investigative pattern (time, setting, victim profile).
- Law enforcement attention focused on links between nightlife encounters and later discoveries in isolated areas.
- Contemporary reporting and later summaries describe him as eventually **identified, arrested, and prosecuted**.

## Trial and Sentence
- Boost was convicted in West Germany for the murders associated with the “Doubles Killer” case.
- He received a death sentence, and historical summaries note that the execution method was **beheading**.
- Date of execution commonly cited: **1956-06-06**.

## Historical Significance
- The case is frequently cited in German true-crime histories because it combined:
  - public-facing victim selection (couples in nightlife settings),
  - alleged use of drugs to disable victims,
  - and the post-war West German criminal justice context (including capital punishment).
