Edinburgh murderers who killed 16 people to sell their bodies for anatomical dissection.

## Names and Identity

- The "Burke and Hare" case refers to **William Burke** and **William Hare**, who committed a series of murders in **Edinburgh** in **1828**.
- The murders were committed to supply cadavers to the medical-anatomical market, where legal access to bodies was limited.

## Historical Context

- In the early 19th century, demand for bodies for anatomical teaching and research outstripped lawful supply.
- This shortage supported a gray market of "body-snatching" (grave robbing) and, in extreme cases, murder to obtain fresh bodies.

## Method (High-Level)

- Victims were typically killed without obvious external injury, a method contemporaries associated with the later term "burking."
- Bodies were then sold as anatomical subjects to an Edinburgh anatomist connected to the medical school economy.

## Investigation and Trial (Overview)

- The pattern of deaths and the appearance of repeated sales eventually drew suspicion.
- The case resulted in a prosecution in which one perpetrator cooperated with authorities, while the other faced conviction and execution.

## Aftermath and Legacy

- The scandal became a widely cited example in debates over how to regulate the procurement of cadavers for medical study.
- The episode is commonly discussed in connection with the broader reform movement that culminated in the **UK Anatomy Act (1832)**.

## Related People and Institutions

- **Edinburgh medical community**: the local educational and anatomical infrastructure that created strong demand for cadavers.
- **Anatomists and students**: the downstream consumers of the bodies (often discussed in public controversy over medical ethics and sourcing).

## Key Takeaways

- The Burke and Hare murders illustrate how legal constraints and market incentives can produce severe criminal outcomes.
- The public reaction helped shape modern expectations around consent, oversight, and lawful sourcing in medical education.
