## Summary
Heriberto Lazcano Lazcano was the supreme leader of Los Zetas, the most violent Mexican drug cartel. A former Mexican special forces soldier, he was killed by marines in 2012. His body was stolen from a funeral home.

## Background
- **Name**: Heriberto Lazcano Lazcano (often referred to by nicknames such as *El Lazca*).
- **Region**: Commonly associated with northeastern Mexico, especially Tamaulipas, and later operational influence in multiple states.
- **Profile**: Frequently described in journalism and government reporting as a former elite soldier who applied military tradecraft to organized crime.

## Role in Los Zetas (high level)
- **Origins of Los Zetas**: Widely reported as an armed group originally formed from military deserters who became enforcement for the Gulf Cartel, later turning into an independent organization.
- **Operational style**: Accounts emphasize the use of disciplined cells, heavy weapons, intimidation, and territorial control, contributing to the cartel's notoriety for extreme violence.
- **Leadership**: Lazcano is typically identified as a senior commander and later top leader (or one of the top leaders) during the period when Los Zetas expanded beyond a protective wing into a multi-market criminal enterprise.

## Public notoriety and law-enforcement focus
- Mexican and U.S. agencies publicly listed him among priority targets during the peak years of cartel violence.
- Reporting often notes that his military background became part of the broader narrative of cartel “militarization” in Mexico.

## Death and aftermath
- **2012 firefight**: Mexican marines reported killing Lazcano during an operation in Coahuila.
- **Body theft**: Subsequent reports that his body was stolen from a funeral home became an internationally covered episode, illustrating the fear, corruption risk, and spectacle surrounding major cartel figures.

## Context (why he is historically notable)
- Los Zetas became a symbol of the shift from trafficking-focused organizations to groups that also relied heavily on territorial control, extortion, kidnapping, and mass intimidation.
- The trajectory from special-forces service to cartel leadership is frequently cited in discussions about institutional weakness, corruption pressures, and the spillover of tactical expertise into organized crime.
