## Summary

US airstrike destroyed a Médecins Sans Frontières hospital in Kunduz, killing 42 people including patients and staff. MSF called it a war crime; the US called it a mistake.

## Career Timeline

| Year | Event |
|------|-------|
| 2015 | Hospital bombed; 42 killed |

## Background

- Kunduz is a city in northern Afghanistan that saw intense fighting in 2015 as Taliban forces briefly captured the city and Afghan/U.S. forces attempted to retake it.
- The Médecins Sans Frontières (MSF) trauma center in Kunduz was one of the few facilities in the region capable of providing advanced emergency surgery.
- MSF reported that it had repeatedly communicated the hospital’s GPS coordinates to U.S. and Afghan authorities as part of standard deconfliction practices.

## What Happened (High-Level)

- In the early hours of 3 October 2015, a U.S. AC-130 gunship carried out an extended strike on the MSF hospital compound.
- The hospital building was heavily damaged and burned; MSF and other accounts describe staff calling for the attack to stop during the strike.
- U.S. officials later stated the strike was not intentionally directed at a medical facility and attributed the event to failures in process, communication, and targeting.

## Casualties and Immediate Impact

- MSF and widely cited reports list 42 deaths, including patients and medical staff.
- The trauma center’s destruction removed a critical source of emergency care at a time of active conflict in the region.
- The incident amplified concerns about protection of medical services in war zones and the reliability of deconfliction mechanisms.

## Investigations and Accountability

- The U.S. military conducted investigations that described a chain of errors and rule violations; disciplinary actions and administrative measures were reported.
- MSF called the strike a war crime and urged an independent international investigation, including via the International Humanitarian Fact-Finding Commission (IHFFC).
- Public reporting also covered compensation to victims and support to MSF, alongside continuing debate over whether the response met expectations for accountability.

## Why It Matters

- The strike became a major case study in modern armed conflict for:
  - the protection of hospitals and medical personnel under international humanitarian law,
  - the risks of urban combat and close-air support under contested, time-pressured conditions,
  - the operational limits of “no-strike lists” and coordinate-based deconfliction.

## Notes on Terminology

- “War crime” is a legal characterization that typically depends on intent and contextual factors; MSF used the term to emphasize the gravity of the incident.
- Official U.S. statements emphasized mistake and systemic failure rather than deliberate targeting.
