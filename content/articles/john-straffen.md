## Summary
John Straffen was Britain's longest-serving prisoner, spending 55 years incarcerated. He murdered three young girls in the early 1950s. Found unfit to plead initially, he escaped and killed again within hours.

## Background
- Straffen is primarily known through the public record of his crimes and subsequent custodial history in the UK.
- Contemporary reporting and later criminology summaries often cite him for the length of his incarceration rather than for any public role or professional career.

## Criminal Case (High-Level)
- **Victims**: The case involved the murders of multiple young girls.
- **Time period**: Early 1950s.
- **Public impact**: The murders, escape, and subsequent reoffending were widely reported and contributed to public concern about custodial security.

## Legal & Custodial Status
- **Fitness to plead**: He was initially found unfit to plead, which affected how the courts could proceed at the time.
- **Secure detention**: He was held in high-security settings under mental health / secure custody arrangements and later remained under prison custody.
- **Duration**: He spent roughly five and a half decades in custody, making him one of the UK's longest-incarcerated individuals.

## Escape and Aftermath
- Straffen escaped custody during the period of his early detention.
- He committed another murder shortly after the escape, a fact that has remained central to summaries of the case.
- The episode is frequently referenced in discussions of mid-20th-century detention security and risk assessment.

## Legacy and Notes
- The label "Britain's longest-serving prisoner" is commonly attached to Straffen in secondary sources.
- His case is often discussed in criminology context as an example of how mental health findings, detention settings, and security lapses can intersect.
