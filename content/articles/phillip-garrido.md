## Summary
Phillip Garrido kidnapped 11-year-old Jaycee Dugard in 1991 and held her captive for 18 years, fathering two children with her. Discovered in 2009. Sentenced to 431 years.

## Key Facts (High-Level)
- **Victim**: Jaycee Lee Dugard (kidnapped at age 11).
- **Duration**: Approximately **18 years** in captivity.
- **Location**: California/Nevada region; captivity associated with Garrido’s residence in **Antioch, California** (backyard/enclosed area reported in accounts).
- **Outcome**: Garrido pled guilty and received a de facto life sentence; Dugard and her children were recovered alive.

## Background
- Public reporting describes Garrido as an American male with a history of criminal convictions prior to the 1991 kidnapping.
- Accounts note repeated contact with systems (criminal justice/parole) before and after the offense, with debate about oversight failures.

## The 1991 Kidnapping
- Dugard was abducted while walking to a school bus stop.
- The kidnapping was widely reported as involving **force** and immediate concealment.

## Captivity (1991–2009)
- Dugard was held in isolation and under coercive control.
- Two children were fathered by Garrido during the period of captivity.
- Public summaries often describe the confinement area as a concealed/controlled space on Garrido’s property.

## Discovery and Arrest (2009)
- Garrido came to law-enforcement attention after appearing with Dugard and the children.
- Subsequent investigation identified Dugard and confirmed the long-term abduction, leading to Garrido’s arrest.

## Trial and Sentencing
- Garrido pled guilty to multiple felony counts related to kidnapping and sexual assault.
- The sentence—**431 years to life**—is commonly cited to reflect the severity and multiplicity of offenses.

## Aftermath and Public Impact
- The case prompted broader public discussion about:
  - **Victim recovery and long-term trauma**
  - **Parole supervision and oversight**
  - **Missing-person investigations** and the possibility of prolonged captivity
- Jaycee Dugard later published memoirs and participated in advocacy/public education on survivor experiences.
