## Key Facts

- Full name: **Henry Clay Frick** (1849–1919), U.S. industrialist and art patron.
- Best known for leadership roles in **coke and steel** (notably in partnership with Andrew Carnegie) and for his role in the **1892 Homestead Strike**.

## Early Life & Entry into Industry

- Raised in western Pennsylvania in a region shaped by coal, iron, and emerging heavy industry.
- Built early wealth through **coke production**, an essential fuel for blast furnaces in iron and steelmaking.

## Carnegie Steel & Business Role

- Became a central executive figure in **Carnegie Steel**, overseeing operations and labor policy during a period of rapid expansion.
- Known for a hard-driving management style focused on cost control, output, and industrial scale.

## Homestead Strike (1892)

- As a top executive at the Homestead Works, Frick is widely associated with the decision to bring in **Pinkerton agents** during the strike.
- The confrontation led to significant violence and deaths, becoming one of the most famous and consequential U.S. labor conflicts of the era.
- The episode is frequently cited in labor history as a turning point in public perceptions of corporate power and union conflict.

## Later Life, Art Collecting, and Legacy

- Accumulated a major private art collection; after his death, portions became the core of **The Frick Collection** in New York City.
- Remembered with a dual legacy: major contributions to industrial growth and philanthropy, alongside enduring criticism tied to labor repression.
