## Summary
Wolfgang Přiklopil kidnapped 10-year-old Natascha Kampusch in 1998 and held her prisoner in a cellar for over eight years. She escaped in 2006. He killed himself the same day by jumping in front of a train.

## Context and Background
- Wolfgang Přiklopil was an Austrian man known primarily for the abduction and long-term captivity of Natascha Kampusch.
- In most public accounts, details of his early life and work history are comparatively sparse compared to documentation of the crime and its aftermath.

## The 1998 Abduction
- Kampusch was abducted in Vienna in March 1998 while walking to school.
- She was transported to Přiklopil’s residence, where he maintained a concealed cellar space used to imprison her.

## Captivity (1998–2006)
- Kampusch was held for over eight years in a small underground cellar area.
- Reporting has described severe isolation, coercive control, and restrictions on movement, contact, and daily life.
- Over time, she was also brought into the living areas of the house under supervision, while still being prevented from leaving freely.

## Escape and Immediate Aftermath (2006)
- In August 2006, Kampusch escaped when an opportunity arose during a routine moment outside the immediate confinement setting.
- Police quickly confirmed her identity and the location associated with her captivity.
- Přiklopil died by suicide shortly after the escape, jumping in front of a train.

## Legal and Investigative Notes
- The case involved extensive police investigation after the escape, including searches, forensic work, and reconstruction of the confinement environment.
- Because the perpetrator died the same day, there was no full criminal trial that would have produced a complete evidentiary public record in court.

## Public, Media, and Cultural Impact
- The abduction and escape became one of the most widely reported criminal cases in Austria in the 2000s.
- It influenced public discussion about missing-person investigations, offender profiling, and how long-term captivity victims are supported after rescue.
- Kampusch later published accounts and participated in interviews that shaped public understanding of the case.
