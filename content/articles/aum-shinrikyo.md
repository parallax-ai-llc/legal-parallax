## Background

- Aum Shinrikyo (Japanese: オウム真理教) was founded in Japan in the 1980s by Shoko Asahara (Chizuo Matsumoto).
- It blended elements drawn from Buddhism, Hindu concepts, apocalyptic Christianity, and Asahara’s own teachings.
- The group cultivated a doomsday worldview that framed violent acts as a means of hastening or surviving an expected apocalypse.

## Ideology and Practices (High-Level)

- Promoted a hierarchical, guru-centered structure in which Asahara held ultimate spiritual authority.
- Reported practices included intensive communal living, recruitment of educated members, and strict internal discipline.
- Advanced an “ends justify the means” logic in which harm to outsiders could be rationalized as spiritually beneficial (as described in public reporting).

## Notable Actions and Key Events

- 1994: Sarin attack in Matsumoto, Nagano Prefecture (often cited as a precursor to 1995).
- 1995: Tokyo subway sarin attack, killing and injuring civilians and triggering a major national security response.
- Post-1995: A series of raids, arrests, and prosecutions targeted leadership and operational networks.

## Aftermath and Legacy

- The organization was dismantled in its original form, but successor groups emerged; the best-known is Aleph.
- The case became a reference point in Japan for domestic terrorism, cult dynamics, and chemical weapons preparedness.
- Public debate intensified over surveillance, civil liberties, and the regulation of high-control religious organizations.

## Related People and Entities

- Shoko Asahara: founder and principal leader.
- Aleph: successor organization commonly cited in reporting on the group’s remnants.
