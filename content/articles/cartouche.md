## Summary

French highwayman who led a large gang of thieves in Paris before being broken on the wheel.

## Career Timeline

| Year | Event |
|------|-------|
| 1721 | Broken on the wheel in Paris |

## Historical Context

Cartouche (often identified as **Louis Dominique Garthausen**) became notorious in early-18th-century Paris, a period marked by post-war economic strain, dense urban poverty, and an active underworld that included theft rings, fences, and highway robbery.

## Notability

- **Urban banditry at scale:** Sources describe Cartouche as leading a relatively large and organized group rather than operating as a lone robber.
- **Paris-centric reputation:** His crimes and pursuit are closely associated with Paris and its environs.
- **Punitive spectacle:** His execution by **breaking on the wheel** reflects the era’s use of public, exemplary punishment for violent or high-profile criminals.

## What “Broken on the Wheel” Means

Breaking on the wheel (the “Catherine wheel” punishment in some accounts) was a form of capital punishment in which the condemned was tied to a frame or wheel and subjected to bone-breaking blows; it was intended to be both lethal and publicly deterrent.

## Legacy

Cartouche’s name persisted as a shorthand for a daring thief in later retellings. Like many early-modern criminals who became famous, later popular accounts can blur the line between documented events and sensationalized storytelling.
