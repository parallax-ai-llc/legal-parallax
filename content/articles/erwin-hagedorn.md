## Summary

East German serial killer executed at age 17 for murdering three children, one of the youngest executed in Cold War Germany.

## Background

- Hagedorn was an East German (GDR) teenager whose case became widely known because of his young age at sentencing and execution.
- He is commonly described in secondary sources as one of the youngest people executed in post-war Germany.

## Crimes and Victims (high-level)

- In 1972, three children were murdered in the Eberswalde area (Brandenburg).
- The killings involved sexual violence and resulted in a large-scale investigation by East German authorities.

## Investigation

- The case drew significant police resources and public attention within the GDR.
- Hagedorn was identified as the perpetrator and arrested later in 1972.

## Trial, Sentence, and Execution

- He was tried in the German Democratic Republic and convicted of the murders.
- The court imposed the death penalty; he was executed by shooting in 1972 at age 17.
- His execution is frequently cited as an extreme example of the GDR’s use of capital punishment, including against minors.

## Historical Context

- Capital punishment existed in the GDR until it was abolished in 1987.
- Documentation around death-penalty cases in the GDR has often been discussed in the context of secrecy and later archival research after German reunification.
