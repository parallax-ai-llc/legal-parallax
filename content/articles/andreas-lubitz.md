## Summary

Germanwings co-pilot who deliberately crashed his aircraft into the Alps killing all 150 aboard.

## Career Timeline

| Year | Event |
|------|-------|

## Background

- Trained as a pilot in Germany and later worked as a first officer (co‑pilot) for Germanwings, a low-cost subsidiary of Lufthansa.[^1]
- Public reporting after the crash described a history of mental health treatment and medical leaves; investigators later emphasized the importance of evaluating fitness-to-fly and reporting pathways for aeromedical concerns.[^1][^2]

## Germanwings Flight 9525 (2015)

- On 24 March 2015, Germanwings Flight 9525 departed Barcelona for Düsseldorf with 144 passengers and 6 crew (150 total on board).[^1]
- During cruise, the captain left the cockpit; the co-pilot remained alone and the cockpit door was locked, preventing re-entry.[^1]
- The aircraft began a controlled descent and impacted terrain in the French Alps; there were no survivors.[^1]

## Investigation (high level)

- French investigators (BEA) concluded the crash was deliberately caused by the co‑pilot, based on cockpit voice recorder evidence and flight data.[^1]
- The case became a widely cited example in aviation safety discussions about cockpit access protocols, pilot mental health, and how medical information is handled across employers and regulators.[^2]

## Aftermath and policy discussion

- Several airlines and regulators revisited cockpit-occupancy rules (e.g., requiring two authorized persons to be present in the cockpit), though approaches varied and were later re-evaluated by different carriers and jurisdictions.[^1][^2]
- The crash prompted renewed attention to:
  - Confidential reporting and support systems for pilots
  - Standardized aeromedical oversight and information sharing (within privacy constraints)
  - Organizational culture that encourages early help-seeking without stigma
