## Summary

French colonial officer who co-led the murderous Voulet-Chanoine expedition that massacred villages across West Africa.

## Context

- Chanoine served as a French colonial officer in West Africa during the late 19th century.
- He became best known as the second-in-command (adjutant) to Captain Paul Voulet on the 1898–1899 Voulet–Chanoine expedition, an operation launched from Dakar toward the Lake Chad basin.

## The Voulet–Chanoine Expedition (high-level)

- Purpose (officially): extend French control inland and help connect French territories across West Africa toward the Chad basin.
- Composition (as described in common summaries): a small cadre of European officers supported by West African troops and a much larger number of auxiliaries and porters.
- Conduct: the expedition gained notoriety for extreme violence against civilian populations, including village massacres and looting.

## Breakdown of Command and Death

- The mission’s violence and refusal to follow directives led French authorities to attempt to replace or arrest the commanders.
- In mid-1899, the commanders’ position collapsed amid confrontation with French superiors and growing dissent among their own soldiers.
- Chanoine was killed in July 1899 during the mutiny that ended the expedition’s original leadership.

## Reputation and Historical Assessment

- In modern historical writing, the expedition is frequently cited as a severe example of colonial brutality in the period.
- Chanoine’s legacy is therefore tied less to conventional military achievement and more to the documented atrocities associated with the mission.

## Notes

- Contemporary accounts and later histories often describe both Voulet and Chanoine as unusually ruthless even by the standards of colonial conquest.
- Chanoine is commonly identified as the son of General Charles Chanoine, a prominent French military figure and politician.
