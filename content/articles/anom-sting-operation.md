## Summary

FBI secretly ran the ANOM encrypted phone platform used by criminals, reading 27 million messages. Operation Trojan Shield led to 800 arrests across 16 countries.

## Background

- ANOM was an encrypted messaging system created and operated in secret by U.S. and Australian law enforcement as part of **Operation Trojan Shield**.
- The platform was positioned to criminal networks as a hardened “secure phone” alternative after other illicit encrypted-device providers were disrupted.

## How the Platform Worked (High-Level)

- ANOM devices were modified smartphones configured to run a messaging app marketed as end-to-end encrypted.
- In reality, messages sent through ANOM were routed in a way that allowed law enforcement to obtain plaintext copies and analyze them.
- Investigators used the message stream to map networks, identify logistics, and coordinate synchronized arrests and searches.

## International Coordination

- The operation was coordinated across multiple jurisdictions, with information sharing supporting parallel enforcement actions.
- Public reporting describes the Australian Federal Police (AFP) as a major partner in the rollout and investigative phase.

## Reported Outcomes

- **Messages collected:** ~27 million (as widely reported).
- **Arrests:** ~800 across ~16 countries (initial wave).
- **Seizures (reported):** large quantities of illicit drugs, firearms, and cash linked to organized crime investigations.

## Significance

- ANOM is often cited as an example of an “intelligence-led” approach where law enforcement ran the communications infrastructure to generate evidence.
- It demonstrated the scale advantages of targeting distribution channels and trust networks (who introduces a “secure” device) rather than trying to break encryption directly.

## Notes & Caveats

- Public figures (device counts, countries involved, seizure totals) vary by source and by the time of reporting; official tallies evolved as cases progressed.
- Details of technical implementation and evidentiary handling are not fully public, both for operational security and because many cases were (or remain) in court.
